FOREWORD


 

The Owl of Minerva, a previous book, explores the theme of Western and modern philosophical thought, from the break with the medieval worldview that came about with the emergence of scientific thought and the philosophy of Descartes, to the present day.

The Owl had two main objectives. First, to offer the possibility of following the thread of the evolution of modern philosophical thought in order to understand its internal dynamics, and second, to show what, in our opinion, have been the great impasses facing modern thought—those questions that, in different ways, haunt it, without being adequately resolved, throughout its journey—and which end up becoming what I then called its "breaking points."

In El Búho, we argued that the Western world has entered a profound crisis that questions the basic assumptions from which we gave meaning to life and constructed our identity. We pointed out, then, the need to move toward overcoming such assumptions and opening ourselves to a different understanding of the human phenomenon.

We maintain that we have the necessary foundations to articulate this new conception. These are provided by the contributions of philosophers such as Nietzsche, Wittgenstein, and Heidegger, among others. But it is not only from philosophy that this questioning has taken place. From the field of science, and particularly from those sciences that incorporate a systemic view, our old assumptions have also been challenged. In this latter field, we highlight the important contribution of biologist Humberto Maturana, the cornerstone of the interpretation we offer in this text.

The aim of this new book is to propose a specific and particular articulation of all these contributions and, based on them, to offer a new integrative conception of the human phenomenon. This new interpretation claims to go beyond the assumptions of modern thought and even seeks to transcend the very foundations of what we call "the metaphysical program" that has dominated Western thought for 25 centuries, since Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle. We call this interpretation, the central proposal of this book, "the ontology of language."

Our proposal is not an isolated case. It is part of a broad emerging movement that is taking place in the most diverse fields of culture. In this sense, it recognizes strong affinities with developments that have taken place and continue to take place in philosophy, the humanities, literary criticism, the natural sciences, and so on. It is also consistent with the profound changes that are developing in our economic, political, and social relations and that are translating into the emergence of a new historical scenario. But it is perhaps in the field of technology, and particularly new communication technologies, that we see the most important challenges that force us to rethink what it means to be human.

Each historical stage questions the meaning of humanity and offers certain answers and interpretations. As discussed in El Búho, each new interpretation of the human phenomenon is reflected, in one way or another, in certain forms of common sense. This core set of assumptions that constitute what seems obvious to us, that place where our questioning stops, is based on what we refer to here as a particular "ontology." That is, an interpretive matrix about the meaning of the human phenomenon. Well, we maintain that for 25 centuries our interpretations of the meaning of the human have developed within the parameters established by the metaphysical thinking that originated in ancient Greece. The different changes in our interpretations, and the consequent modifications of our common sense, which have taken place during this long period, have occurred within certain assumptions that today need to be questioned.

The ontology of language represents an effort to offer a new interpretation of what it means to be human, an interpretation that claims to be outside the parameters of the metaphysical program that has long served as the basis for the way we view life. In this sense, it is not only a postmodern proposal, but also a post-metaphysical one.

And of all the different influences that feed into this proposal, which are many, the most important, as will become clear from reading the text, are undoubtedly those of Nietzsche and Maturana. They represent the fundamental axis from which we orient our gaze. Two very different thinkers, coming from very different fields—the first a philosopher, the second a biologist—separated in their intellectual production by a century, but both obsessed with better understanding the phenomenon of human life. Both, moreover, with proposals that are not always easy to understand in their implications, not suitable for metaphysical ears.

From the point of view of my personal history, I must acknowledge the great influence that my encounter with Fernando Flores has had on what I propose today. I met Flores in the 1960s when we were both involved in the university reform movement that we, as students, had unleashed at the Catholic University.

In the mid-1980s, while working on research on the concept of work, a mutual friend suggested that I examine some of Flores' essays in which he related work and language. This proved to be a very stimulating experience, as it allowed me to glimpse a different and powerful approach to addressing multiple social phenomena. Flores developed an approach that articulated contributions as diverse as those of Heidegger, Searle, and Maturana, resulting in connections that I personally had never seen before. I perceived his ideas as an important and original contribution.

This experience led me to the United States, where I worked for more than two years at one of Flores' companies. This proved to be a decisive learning experience and is reflected in my current thinking, as well as in many of the topics developed in this book. The reader will find many ideas that come from Fernando Flores' thinking or are inspired by material produced during this period by him and some of his companies. I am very grateful to Flores for this learning and influence.

I would also like to express my gratitude to Fernando Flores and his company Business Design Associates, Inc., owners of the copyrights to works on which several sections of this book are based, for kindly allowing me to make use of them. I regret not having made this acknowledgment explicitly in the first editions of this work.

The main concern of Ontology of Language lies in the field of ethics. Strictly speaking, it is a book about the ethics of human coexistence. With this, we point in two different directions. First, it refers us to the great theme of the meaning of life, which we consider to be the fundamental challenge of our time. Second, it leads us to the problems related to the construction of new forms of coexistence in a globalized world, which prevents us from seeing the Other in a way that is very different from what we were accustomed to.

The topic of social coexistence leads me to another topic that I also feel is necessary to address. My formal training was in the discipline of sociology. Despite this, I cannot help but recognize that I have never felt like a traditional sociologist, a practitioner of the "sociological craft" as it is conceived within the discipline. From very early on, strictly speaking from my early training at university, I always sought to build bridges between sociology and philosophy. My impression, however, is that at one of those crossings, some years ago now, I stayed on the other side, on the shore of philosophy. Since then, I have not returned to sociology. In a way, therefore, I see myself as a sociologist in exile, someone who, without being a philosopher by formal training, has for some time now taken up residence in the territories of philosophy.

In this regard, I would like to anticipate something I mention in the final chapter of this book. My judgment is that sociology has not yet recognized a fundamental aspect of the social phenomenon. I want to summarize it in the following thesis: For human beings, the social is constituted in language. Every social phenomenon is always a linguistic phenomenon. Until sociologists recognize this, I think that the power of their interpretations and their work will inevitably be limited. I hope this book will serve to show why I hold this view.

Before concluding this prologue, I would like to thank some of the people who have made the writing and publication of this book possible.

First, I must thank all my students from the "Mastering the Art of Professional Coaching" courses, offered in the United States for several years now, and from "El Arte del Coaching Profesional" (The Art of Professional Coaching), the Spanish version of the former, offered to students in Latin America and Spain. I have always insisted that the work I have done with the students in these two courses has been my main laboratory and a constant source of questions and inquiries. No book could have replaced this experience. My students have been a constant stimulus to expand, deepen, and revise many of my positions. There are countless cases in which a question asked in one of our lectures led me to visit places I had never imagined before. There are also many occasions when a student showed me something I had not noticed. This book has been greatly enriched by situations of this kind.

I must also thank Sally Bendersky, who led the Spanish translation of my materials, originally written in English, which we used in the Spanish version of our courses. I used Sally's translation as the basis for writing this book. Although the final text differs in many ways from those early translations, Sally's contribution was very helpful to me, and she is still very much present in the body of this text.
