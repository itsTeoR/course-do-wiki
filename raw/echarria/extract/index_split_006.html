BASICS OF THE ONTOLOGY OF LANGUAGE

We are participating in a fundamental historical transformation: a new and radically different understanding of human beings is emerging. This is one of those special events in history that have the power to reconfigure what is possible and to change the future.

We are on the threshold of a new historical era. And those who are the first to understand the nature of this important change may be able to embark on paths that others will find more difficult and gain advantages that will eventually allow them to become pioneers and leaders in their respective fields.


 

The birth of metaphysical drift

History is often conceived as the sequence of traces left by events that occurred in the past. However, we know that not all events have the same impact on the future, nor have all moments in history been equally fruitful in producing new possibilities. Some have been more important than others, and very few have been capable of opening up long historical periods for humanity.

Long ago, one of these great historical transformations took place in ancient Greece. Around 700 BC, a new form of communication was invented: the alphabet. This event had far-reaching historical consequences, creating the conditions from which a particular type of human being emerged: Western men and women.

The invention of the alphabet gave rise to fundamental changes in society. Our notions of education, wisdom, and social coexistence were all profoundly transformed. New social practices emerged. Poets were soon forced to cede the education of youth to philosophers. Democracy was invented. However, perhaps the most important changes took place in a less visible area: in the transformation of our "mental" categories, in the way human beings think about themselves and the world.

Before the invention of the alphabet, human beings lived in what we call a "language of becoming." Language and action were then closely linked. It was naturally recognized that speech had the power to make certain things happen—things that might not have happened if someone had not said what they said. Poets, who were then responsible for education, fulfilled their role of teaching by telling epic stories or fables, narratives about actions performed by humans, heroes, and gods. In this way, people learned about piety, love, or treachery through the actions of the characters in these stories. For example, wisdom was gained by acting in the manner of those whom the community, through these stories, considered wise, as in the case of Ulysses. Courage was learned by acting according to those whom the community, in their stories, considered brave, such as Achilles.

The alphabet separated the speaker, language, and action. This was a major change. Once a text was written, it seemed to speak for itself, and the speaker was no longer necessary to hear it.

At the same time, there was a shift from a language of action to a language of ideas. Reflection began to supplant the role previously played by the narration of events. With the advent of the alphabet, our way of thinking about things changed. We then began to ask ourselves "what is wisdom?" and "what is courage?" independently of the actions undertaken by a Ulysses or an Achilles. We began to talk about them as ideas, not as traits characteristic of the actions of the wise and the brave. It was now possible to leave epic tales behind. The figure of Ulysses was no longer needed to talk about wisdom. The epic tale was gradually replaced by the treatise. Wisdom ceased to be the way the community characterized the actions of a particular character such as Ulysses. Now the character of Ulysses was conceived as an illustration of the idea of wisdom. With the interest in the question "What is...?" (which manifests itself in the questions "What is wisdom?" or "What is courage?") the emphasis is placed not on actions but on the "being" of things. This marked a departure from the "language of becoming" of the past and a transition to a new form of language: "the language of being."

This was a fundamental transformation and a great historical achievement. On the basis of this new "language of being," the forces of reflection and rational thought were unleashed. Philosophy was invented, and later, scientific thought. Interest in the art of accurate thinking developed logic. Logical principles showed us the path of valid thinking, how to move from one idea to another to reach the truth and avoid falsehood. Thus was born rationality, the hallmark of Western thought.

Very soon, the power of thought became evident. It allowed us to transform the world and destroy our enemies. It gave us the illusion that we were dominating nature. It was as if, having eaten from the tree of knowledge, we felt like gods.

Blinded by our success, we made one type of action—thinking—the queen of all actions and separated it from all others, considered "inferior." The distinction between theory and practice was complete. Thought, we said, was unique and could not be compared to anything else. Everyday actions were treated as subordinate, particularly when they were not driven by thought. We even forgot that thought itself is an action.

One of the most important aspects of this historic revolution was the fact that we also changed our understanding of ourselves, our understanding of human beings. A human being, we postulated, is a rational entity. Reason, we argued, is what makes us human, different from other species. If we want to know a human being, we must begin by knowing their mind: the place where thought resides. That is where the soul resides, what makes us who we are.

Possessed by this new "language of being," we began to ask ourselves about the being of everything, including ourselves. From the moment we moved away from the perspective of "becoming" and separated language from action, every time we asked ourselves "what is wisdom" or "what is a human being?", we sought answers that would tell us what was immutable, what was unchangeable, whatever the object of our questions. We assumed that being was the non-contingent, that which eluded historical becoming, that which always remained the same.

When we directed this question about being toward ourselves, since we had assumed that our soul was one and the same throughout our lives, we also assumed that our being was the hidden side of ourselves and that it did not change. We assumed that we all possessed an unchanging soul from the moment of our birth. Few challenges could rival the one inscribed on the walls of the temple of Apollo at Delphi: "Know thyself." From the moment we assumed that we were a given and unchanging, the best we could do to live a better life was to begin to know who we were, to know our being.

Confident in our success, we assumed that reason had no limits, that we could begin to know everything and completely master our natural environment and our relationships with others through reason. It was only a matter of time. The linear progress of rational thought, itself an expression of the predominance of the line in written language, became our measure for adopting, as time went on, a linear understanding of time itself.

It did not take us long to assume that we could explain anything. Reason was the key to grasping the essence of things. We assumed that things are what they are according to their essence. We also came to postulate, as Isaiah Berlin rightly pointed out, that for all genuine questions there is one and only one true answer and that these answers are in principle accessible only through rational thought. We assumed, therefore, that human beings were capable of knowing, through reason, the true being of everything around them.

The emphasis on the being of things led us to minimize the role played by language. We saw ourselves as rational beings, endowed with an immutable soul, surrounded by a wide range of entities whose beings we could decipher—and eventually control—through the power of reason. Language played little or no role in the constitution of ourselves and the world. It only allowed us to describe how things are, their being. We assumed, therefore, that being preceded language.

For a time after the invention of the alphabet, this new understanding of ourselves and the world evolved slowly. For a certain period of time, there was a transitional phase in which very different conceptions coexisted. It was only after the emergence of a group of philosophers we call metaphysicians, led by Plato, a disciple of Socrates, and Aristotle, a disciple of Plato, that a coherent synthesis based on these new assumptions crystallized. This soon became the predominant social vision. In some ways with Socrates, but certainly with Plato and Aristotle, a whole historical period had begun, a period we call the metaphysical drift. Current common sense is largely based on metaphysical assumptions, originally generated by these metaphysicians of ancient Greece.

We have changed our understanding of many things since the early Greeks. We cannot say that we think the same way they did. However, we postulate that despite all these changes, we have maintained, on the whole, the basic metaphysical assumptions developed in early Greece. Our historical development has taken place without breaking with these main assumptions, with this basic common understanding of what it means to be human. We have evolved "within" this metaphysical drift that began in ancient Greece. In this sense, we are faithful heirs of these early Greek metaphysicians.


 

Descartes and the modern world

Descartes' philosophy—known as Cartesianism—has been the most influential of modern times. Despite some opposing views, modernity developed within a Cartesian framework, accepting the main assumptions formulated by Descartes. However, upon examining these assumptions, we realize that they are faithful to the ancient Greek tradition of understanding human beings as rational beings.

Descartes' philosophy is a historical expression of the impetus given to literacy—originally developed with the advent of the alphabet—through the invention of another major change in the way we communicate: the printing press.

With the printing press, the initial separation between the speaker, language, and action that had been produced by the invention of the alphabet was deepened and extended to all levels of society [2]. With the printing press, books became easily purchasable items, which had profound social consequences, allowing for the emergence of the school system, the social expansion of literacy (reading and writing skills), and the democratization and extension of rationality to all corners of social life. On a different level, the invention of the printing press allowed for the development of the sphere of individual privacy and intimacy and the expansion of romantic love.

In Descartes' philosophy, thought is once again the basis for understanding human beings. Thought always takes precedence. Thought, Descartes posits, makes us the kind of beings we are. It is because we think, says Descartes, that we can conclude that we exist: "I think," he tells us, "therefore I am." Thought is the basis of being. Reason is what makes us human.

We postulate that important developments—many of which have taken place in recent decades—are bringing the metaphysical drift to an end. A radically new understanding of human beings is emerging. We call this new understanding of human beings the ontology of language.


 

A new historical landscape

We are once again facing a revolution of paramount importance in the way we communicate with others. Long ago, in ancient Greece, the invention of the alphabet gave rise to a new mode of communication based on the ability to read and write. Something similar has happened in recent decades. We have been facing a transformation of the utmost importance in our mode of communication, as a result of significant technological innovations and the emergence of electronic language.[3]

The emergence of electronic language encompasses a process that includes a profusion of communication media, including the old telegraph and gramophone, the telephone, telex, radio, television, cinema, video and VCR, photocopier, and fax machine, along with innovations in computer hardware and software. Today we see how these different means of communication converge and integrate with each other in different multimedia configurations. As a result of electronic language, the world has changed, becoming the "global village" that Marshall McLuhan spoke of more than thirty years ago. Distance, which was always a relevant factor in the way human beings organized their lives, is becoming increasingly irrelevant.

This new language has already changed—and will continue to change—the way we live together. On the other hand, it has changed the way change occurs in human life. Today, change has become a permanent aspect of life. Nothing stays the same for too long. In fact, the predominance of "being" is once again (and under very different circumstances) being replaced by that of "becoming."

These new historical conditions are pushing the "metaphysical observer" that we have been for so long to its very limits. Metaphysics is approaching its historical exhaustion. As in ancient Greece, this change in the way we communicate with each other, based this time on the emergence of electronic language, is also profoundly affecting the way we think about ourselves and the world.

The metaphysical fortress has also been shaken from within, through the emergence of new conceptions, new thoughts, new theories. Important conceptual developments have occurred in fields as diverse as philosophy, the biological sciences, linguistics, and the so-called human sciences. In one way or another, these developments challenge the old postulates that constitute the core of the metaphysical program.

In the field of philosophy, we consider that there are three major developments that challenge the metaphysical program. The first, and perhaps the most important, is the philosophy of Friedrich Nietzsche. Nietzsche has given us the strongest critique of the understanding of the human soul. He was the first philosopher to place himself outside the metaphysical framework and to seriously question its basic assumptions. The second is Martin Heidegger's existential phenomenology and his critique of the assumptions of Cartesianism, according to which human beings are primarily rational beings. The third corresponds to the second period of Ludwig Wittgenstein's philosophy, which has offered us a radically new understanding of language.

Each of these philosophers has been followed by others whose contributions cannot be ignored. But it was the aforementioned who opened the doors that others would cross. Based on these new contributions, philosophy as a whole has been greatly disrupted during this century. This process has been called "the linguistic turn," as language seems to have taken the place of privilege that reason occupied for centuries.

Important developments have also taken place in the field of biological sciences. We have seen, for example, how, at the level of theoretical biology, it has been postulated that the basic feature that distinguishes the human species from others is human language. This postulate was established by Ernst Mayr in the early 1960s. However, it is above all in the area that has evolved from systemic thinking that the relationship between human beings and language has been explored even more deeply. In this domain, the valuable contribution of Chilean biologist Humberto Maturana is noteworthy.

Finally, it is also worth mentioning the important achievements in the fields of systemic psychology, anthropology, sociology, and linguistics. In each of these disciplines, the common thread has been the recognition of the importance of language in understanding human life.

The ontology of language attempts to bring all these different—and often seemingly contradictory—developments together into a coherent unity and synthesis. It aims to create a basis from which we can observe human phenomena from a non-metaphysical perspective.

What do we mean by "ontology"?

We borrow the term "ontology" from a very specific tradition and give it a particular meaning. It is very important that we understand this in order to avoid using the term in a metaphysical sense. For the ancient Greeks, who coined the term ontology, it meant our general understanding of being as such. Greek ontology was, therefore, framed within the metaphysical program. Thus, if we take the term ontology in its ancient classical sense, we find ourselves trapped in the ancient metaphysics from which we precisely want to distance ourselves and with which we seek to break.

Our use of the term ontology stems—in the double sense of emanating from and departing from—the tradition inaugurated by the German philosopher Martin Heidegger. For Heidegger, ontology is related to his investigation of what he called Dasein, which we can summarize as the particular way of being that we humans have. In this sense, ontology refers to our generic understanding—our interpretation—of what it means to be human. When we say that something is ontological, we are referring to our interpretation of the constituent dimensions that we all share as human beings and that give us a particular way of being.

In this sense, ontology, our understanding of what it means to be human, does not necessarily imply the adoption of a metaphysical perspective. We can have a metaphysical ontology, as the Greek metaphysicians did, but we can also generate non-metaphysical ontologies, as claimed by the ontology of language.

Let us establish an initial postulate in relation to what we call ontology. We maintain that, whatever we do, whatever we say, it always reveals a certain understanding of what is possible for human beings and, therefore, an underlying ontology. Every time we assert something, whatever it may be, what is said rests on assumptions about what is possible for human beings, even if it is merely the assumption that, as human beings, we are capable of asserting what we are saying.

Take an example. When we say "the apple is red," we are assuming that, as human beings, we can determine what color the apple is. Every action, every utterance, presupposes a judgment about what is possible for us as human beings. Therefore, every time we act, every time we say something, not only is the object on which we act or to which we refer when we speak manifested, but also a certain interpretation of what it means to be human and, therefore, an ontology, in the sense that we confer on the term.

If the above is accepted, it follows that any postulate we make about being "in general," or about beings other than human beings (as involved in the classical sense of the term ontology) is ultimately based on an underlying understanding of being that formulates that postulate. Consequently, ontology as we have defined it, in terms of understanding what it means to be human, lays the foundation for the ancient notion of ontology as a general understanding of being.

This can be summarized in the following terms: Every statement made by an observer tells us about the type of observer that observer considers themselves to be. This is a fundamental principle in our approach to the subject. We may not realize that when we speak or act we are revealing these underlying ontological assumptions, but we do so nonetheless.

Once we understand this, we realize that, in order to make any statement, we implicitly make a statement about who we are as human beings. Our understanding of what it means to be human is the cornerstone of everything we do. This allows us to reiterate, therefore, that an ontology, as an interpretation of what it means to be human, precedes any other postulate about how other things might be. It is the primary interpretation (even if it is an implicit interpretation) from which other interpretations are made.

This same principle, in a modified version, the postulate that everything we do, whatever it may be, reveals our judgment of ourselves, is the basis of one of the most powerful uses of the ontology of language: the practice of ontological coaching, a topic on which we will not go into further detail because it would take us away from the subject at hand.


 

The ontology of language: basic postulates

We are situated in this profound transformation through the development of a new understanding of human beings that we call "the ontology of language." We know, however, that we are part of a much broader movement, a movement that incorporates different approaches and is given different names.

As we have already mentioned, very important changes are taking place in almost all fields of human life—in philosophy, the natural sciences, the humanities, politics, spirituality, the arts, new ecological concerns, etc.—developments that have great affinities and links with each other. In this regard, we see the "ontology of language" as one of many developments that are moving in a similar direction and that very often share similar assumptions and sensibilities.

If we had to condense the core of the ontology of language into a small set of premises, we would be inclined to rescue three basic postulates and three general theses or principles. These contain, we believe, what is fundamental to this particular way of interpreting human beings. They represent what we would call the basic framework of the "clearing" in which a new observer of human phenomena is constituted.

We will refer to this notion of "clearing" at another time. Suffice it to say now that it is a term borrowed from Heidegger ("die lichtung"), to which we confer a strong Heraclitean meaning. With it, we simply allude to the basic conditions through which language constitutes a particular observer of the world and of human phenomena.

The three basic postulates of the ontology of language will be discussed in this section. They are as follows:

1. We interpret human beings as linguistic beings.

2. We interpret language as generative.

3. We interpret human beings as creating themselves in and through language.

Let us now examine each of these postulates in greater detail.

First Postulate

: We interpret human beings as linguistic beings

The first and most important of these postulates refers to human beings. It posits that language is, above all, what makes human beings the particular kind of beings that they are. Human beings, we argue, are linguistic beings, beings who live in language. Language, we postulate, is the key to understanding human phenomena.

It is important to avoid a reductionist interpretation of this postulate, which restricts the complexity of human phenomena to language and therefore disregards other non-linguistic dimensions of human existence. We are clear that human beings are not only linguistic beings and that, therefore, language does not exhaust the multidimensionality of the human phenomenon. Moreover, we maintain that human existence recognizes three primary domains, from which any other domain of human phenomena can be derived. Without going into detail on this topic at this time, it is important to identify these three primary domains. They are: the domain of the body, the domain of emotionality, and the domain of language.

Each of these domains encompasses different phenomena that cannot be reduced to another without sacrificing the specificity of the phenomena to which each gives rise. The autonomy of these three primary domains does not preclude close relationships of coherence between them. This implies that the phenomena that take place, for example, in the emotional domain (e.g., emotions) are coherent with those that we can detect at the level of the body (e.g., postures) and language (what is said or heard). These relationships of coherence enable the possibility of carrying out "reconstructions" of the phenomena specific to each domain through any of the other two.

If we recognize three primary domains, of which language is only one, why then do we postulate the priority of language? Why do we maintain that what makes us who we are, as human beings, is language? Because it is precisely through language that we give meaning to our existence, and it is also through language that we are able to recognize the importance of non-linguistic existential domains.

Even when we point to the domains of the body and emotionality (the two primary non-linguistic domains), we can only do so through language, a form of conferring meaning; all forms of comprehension or understanding belong to the domain of language. There is no place outside of language from which we can observe our existence. It is precisely through the mechanism of "linguistic reconstruction," mentioned above, that we gain access to non-linguistic phenomena.

Human experience, what for human beings represents the experience of existence, is realized through language. Language represents for human beings, in Nietzsche's words, a prison from which they cannot escape; or, in Heidegger's words, the dwelling place of their being. Human beings inhabit language.


 

Second Postulate

We interpret language as generative


 

The consequences of the above postulate, in the sense that we are linguistic beings, can only be fully understood to the extent that we are able to radically modify our traditional conception of language.

For centuries, we have considered language to be an instrument that allows us to "describe" what we perceive (the outside world) or "express" what we think or feel (our inner world). This conception made language a fundamentally passive or descriptive capacity. Language, it was assumed, allowed us to talk "about" things. Reality, it was assumed, preceded language, and language was limited to "giving an account" of it. We have called this, in some places, an "accounting" conception of language.

Our second postulate takes on the task of questioning the traditional conception of language. Supported by advances made in recent decades in the field of the philosophy of language, this postulate recognizes that language not only allows us to talk "about" things: language makes things happen. This second postulate abandons the notion that reduces language to a passive or descriptive role. It maintains that language is generative. Language, therefore, not only allows us to describe reality, language creates realities. Reality does not always precede language; language also precedes reality. Language, we postulate, generates being. We will have the opportunity to return to this point.

It is important to note, however, that we are not saying that everything that exists only exists in language. We are not denying the "existence" of a so-called "external reality" independent of language. But we cannot speak of such an "external reality" as external and independent of language. Everything we speak of is, by definition, within the domain of language. How could we speak of what is external to us without thereby denying this very "external-ness"?

As we have argued above, the way in which an external reality (whose existence, we insist, we cannot deny) "exists for us" remains linguistic. Once something becomes part of our lives, once external reality "exists for us," it ceases to be external, and the way it exists for us is in language. Therefore, let us repeat: we are not saying that language generates everything that exists. This would make silence and nothingness the same. And obviously they are not. We cannot argue that what we do not talk about does not exist.

By positing that language is generative, we are arguing that language is action. As we stated earlier, we maintain that through language, we not only talk about things, but we alter the spontaneous course of events: we make things happen. For example, when we propose something to someone or say "yes," "no," or "enough" to someone, we intervene in the course of events.

Just think of the countless occasions when a person, a group, or a country changed direction and altered their history because someone said what they said. In the same way, we recognize that history (individual or collective) could have been so different from what it was if someone had remained silent, if they had not said what they said. Language, we argue, is not a passive tool that allows us to describe how things are. Language is active; through it, we participate in the continuous process of becoming.

Just as we argued that in order to fully understand what was involved in our postulate that human beings are linguistic beings, it was necessary to modify our understanding of language, so too do we now argue that in order to understand what is involved in the premise that language is action, we must also modify our traditional conception of action. This task, however, we will undertake later. It is important, however, to recognize that at its core, the ontology of language rests on a modification of the meaning of three terms: human beings, language, and action.

By arguing that language is action, we are pointing out that language creates realities. We see this in many ways. By saying what we say, by saying it in one way and not another, or by not saying anything at all, we open or close possibilities for ourselves and, often, for others. When we speak, we shape the future, our own and that of others. Based on what we said or were told, based on what we kept quiet, based on what we heard or did not hear from others, our future reality is shaped in one way or another.

But in addition to intervening in the creation of the future, human beings shape our identity and the world we live in through language. The way we operate in language is perhaps the most important factor in defining how we will be seen by others and by ourselves. We will soon discover how personal identity, ours and that of others, is a strictly linguistic phenomenon, a linguistic construction. The same is true of the world we live in. Populated by entities, relationships, actions, and events, our world is constituted in language. Different worlds emerge depending on the type of linguistic distinctions we are capable of making, the way we relate them to each other, and the type of language games we operate in.

Recognition of the previous postulate leads us directly to the third postulate. This is presented as a natural conclusion of what has been said so far.


 

Third Postulate

: We interpret that human beings create themselves in and through language.


 

Once these first postulates are united, a new understanding of human beings emerges. In our tradition, it is normally assumed that each individual is born with a particular way of being; that each person, therefore, has a permanent, fixed, or immutable way of being (often called the "soul"). Life, from this perspective, is a space in which this way of being, which we are presumably endowed with from birth, is revealed and unfolds. Life, therefore, allows us to discover who we really are.

The ontology of language takes a radically different position. It argues that life is, on the contrary, the space in which individuals invent themselves. As Nietzsche tells us, in human beings the creature and the creator are united.

Subject to biological and natural, historical and social conditioning, individuals are born with the possibility of actively participating in the design of their own way of being. Human beings are not a determined or permanent way of being. They are a space of possibility towards their own creation. And what makes this possible is precisely the generative capacity of language. Based on the aforementioned conditioning, individuals have the capacity to create themselves through language. No one is a predetermined, given, and immutable form of being that does not allow for infinite modifications.

It is an interpretation that allows us to confer meaning on ourselves as human beings in a powerful way. Above all, this interpretation allows us to gain mastery over our own lives by playing an active role in designing the kind of being we would like to become. This is the promise that the ontology of language makes to us for the future.

What deserves to be emphasized at this point, however, as it is a frequent source of misunderstanding, is that language is not, as we have seen, the focus or main concern of the ontology of language. Its main interest is human beings. This distinguishes the ontology of language from disciplines such as linguistics and the philosophy of language. Although it has been strongly influenced by both, the ontology of language has a different object of study. Linguistics and the philosophy of language have language as their main concern. The focus of the ontology of language is human beings.


 

A non-metaphysical understanding of human beings

Human beings, we reiterate, do not have a given, fixed, immutable being. To be human is to be in a permanent process of becoming, of inventing and reinventing ourselves within a historical drift. There is no such thing as a predetermined human nature. We do not know what we are capable of being, we do not know what we can become. As Shakespeare wrote: "We know what we are, but we do not know what we might be." Our being is indeterminate, it is an open space pointing towards the future.

An ontological understanding of ourselves can never give us a concrete and definite answer to the question of what it means to be human. Our being is a field open to design. What an ontological approach can give us are only some general distinctions that serve as parameters for defining a basic structure of possibilities in this open process of becoming. It can provide neither more nor less than that.

This general structure of possibilities, which we all share as human beings, is what Martin Heidegger called Dasein, the "being in the world" that we are. Ontology is the inquiry into Dasein. Heidegger's philosophy provided us with an important starting point. However, it was only late in his intellectual development, when most of his ontological work was already done, that Heidegger opened himself to the recognition that, in order to understand what it means to be human, we must turn to language. Human beings, Heidegger recognized, dwell in language.

Human beings invent themselves through language. We are a linguistic construct that, viewed from a metaphysical perspective, seems to oscillate between reality and fiction. A kind of linguistic bubble.

Perhaps the two most important philosophers who have viewed the human soul in this way were Heraclitus and Nietzsche. Heraclitus lived in ancient Greece long before the emergence of metaphysicians. When we read Plato and Aristotle, we realize that Heraclitus was their main opponent. Metaphysics, in a way, was an attempt to prove Heraclitus wrong. If we want to overcome it, it may be time to return to his teachings.

Nietzsche has long been considered a kind of philosophical outcast, a pariah, a sacrilegious thinker, an iconoclast. Very few people really understood what he was saying. Many felt that Nietzsche contradicted himself too often. He came to philosophy through philology, a discipline concerned with language and, in his case, the study of the languages and literature of the ancient Greeks and Romans. This allowed him to come into contact very early on with the work of the pre-Socratics, the philosophers who lived before Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle. Once Nietzsche came into contact with the thought of Heraclitus, he understood that it offered a totally different perspective from that offered by the metaphysical program. He declared Heraclitus his mentor.

Both Heraclitus and Nietzsche understood that, in order to understand human beings, we cannot focus solely on their "being," but must also look at what they are not, at the space in which current forms of being are transcended and the process of becoming is participated in. In this process of becoming, we must make room for both being and non-being, for this cycle that brings together being and nothingness, this eternal recurrence of one and the other. According to Nietzsche, being human can be seen as a process in which we are constantly fleeing from nothingness, while at the same time being driven towards it, towards the "meaninglessness" of our lives, and induced to constantly regenerate meaning for ourselves.

We are, as Heraclitus said, in a process of constant flow, never remaining the same, changing continuously, like a river. And, like a river, we cannot understand who we are if we only focus on our side of being. A river always involves this tension between fullness and emptiness, between being and non-being. If we only look at the fullness, we no longer have a river, but a lake, a pond, or even a swamp. If we only look at the emptiness, we also no longer have a river, but a dry canal, without movement, without life of its own.


 

Being, truth, and power: the role of the observer

It is important to clarify the form used to present our three basic postulates. We had two different ways of presenting them. We could have said, for example, that our first postulate is that "human beings are linguistic beings." We chose, however, not to do so. If we had said it that way, we would have assumed—without saying so—that human beings can postulate what human beings are like. We would have assumed, therefore, that we can account for and access the "being" of things (in this case, human beings). In fact, we hold the opposite view. We believe that we can never say how things really are: we can only say how "we" interpret or consider them.

Therefore, instead of asking how things are, we chose to talk about how we interpret them to be. It is important not to forget, as Maturana always reminds us, that everything said is always said by someone and, as far as possible, not to hide the speaker behind the way things are said. This is a trap that language constantly sets for us, allowing the speaker to hide behind what they are saying.

We also warn the reader that the care we have taken on this occasion to recognize the interpretive nature of what we maintain will often be sacrificed in the text in order to facilitate reading. Our language bears the imprint of metaphysical roots, and using it against this often compromises easy communication.

Recognition of this leads us to the first thesis or general principle of the ontology of language. We can formulate it as follows:


 

First principle

:

We do not know how things are.

We only know how we observe them

or how we interpret them.

We live in interpretive worlds.

At first glance, this might seem like an innocent principle, without major consequences. However, a closer look reveals that it is loaded with dynamite.

Indeed, if we maintain that we cannot know how things are, this implies that we must abandon any claim to access to the truth. For what else is truth but precisely the claim that "things are" as we say? We maintain that truth, in our ordinary language, refers to a judgment we make about a given linguistic proposition that attributes to it the ability to account for "how things are."

Being and truth are two fundamental and mutually dependent pillars of the metaphysical framework. Truth and access to being are two ways of referring to the same thing. If, therefore, access to being (to how things "are") is blocked, any claim to access to truth is simultaneously blocked. Nietzsche, speaking about the relationship between thought and being, argues: "Parmenides said: 'One cannot think of non-being.' Placing ourselves at the opposite extreme, we say: everything that can be thought is, with certainty, fictitious."

If we examine the postulate that affirms our ability to access the being of things and, therefore, the truth, we immediately encounter multiple difficulties. Let us take, by way of example, a few situations.

Maturana has argued convincingly about the difficulties we encounter when we assume that our perceptions correspond to the entities that populate our external world. Our perceptions, he points out, result—and cannot but result—from the conditions of our own biological structure and not from the features of the disturbing agents in our environment. In other words, human beings do not have biological mechanisms that allow us to have perceptions that correspond to how things are. The senses, therefore, do not provide us with a faithful representation of how things are, regardless of the observer who perceives them.

Does this imply a denial of what philosophy has called "the external world"? Does this mean that we must deny the existence of an environment and that which populates it? Obviously not. Denying that we can know how things are does not imply denying their existence, whatever they may be. It is only a matter of denying that we can know them as they really "are," regardless of who observes them.

It is important to recognize that modern logic has long since distanced itself from the notion of truth related to our ability to apprehend the "being" of things. For modern logic, questions of truth are limited to ensuring internal consistency between different propositions. This implies that we can only speak of truth within certain systems of propositions. What we cannot do is ensure the truth of the system as such, since every system of knowledge rests on assumptions that are not part of the system in question, and the system of which those assumptions are a part rests, in turn, on assumptions that, again, do not belong to that system, and so on. Truth, therefore, is simply a logical game of internal coherences within a "given" system. In this context, to say that something is true is only to maintain that it is consistent with other propositions that we accept as valid. This shows the circularity of knowledge, as recognized by hermeneutics.

Questioning the ability of human beings to access truth immediately raises two significant shifts. The first implies that the center of gravity in matters of knowledge shifts from the observed (the being of things) to the observer. Knowledge reveals as much about the observed as it does about the observer. We could perfectly well say: tell me what you observe and I will tell you who you are. This is precisely one of the central premises of the discipline we have christened "ontological coaching." It rests on the ability to observe what someone says with the purpose not only of knowing what is being talked about, but also of knowing (interpreting) the soul (understood as the particular way of being) of the speaker.

We maintain that what we have just pointed out represents one of the most brilliant insights of Nietzsche's philosophy. He always seeks to establish the connection between interpretations and the interpreter, between what is said and who says it (the speaker). Nietzsche always seeks the thread of Ariadne that allows us to leave the labyrinth of knowledge, where the minotaur of truth dwells, and enter the open space of life. And in order to understand life, Nietzsche is forced to recognize the fundamental role that emotions play in it. Nietzsche has been the great philosopher of life and the emotional world. It is not for nothing that he has paused to examine the consequences that result from human beings' fear of the experience of nothingness (nihilism) and the central role that resentment has played in our history (in our interpretations and practices).

The second shift has to do with the criteria for discerning between conflicting interpretations once we have questioned our ability to access the truth. A first aspect to recognize in this direction is that if we accept the postulate that we cannot know how things are, this means that we cannot maintain that what we postulate can be considered truth. This would obviously amount to contradicting and therefore invalidating the very postulate we are making. Unfortunately, there is no dialectical recourse that can resolve this contradiction. We must therefore ask ourselves: If we cannot sustain that this postulate is true, what is the point of doing so?

This question is closely related to another: if, as we say, we cannot postulate truth, does that mean that everything we say or support is the same? Does it mean that everything is equally permissible? Does it mean that any proposition, any interpretation, is equivalent to any other? In other words, is truth the only criterion we have for discerning between different propositions or interpretations? Is truth the only game available? Or to put it another way, what is the price we must pay if we choose to sacrifice the assumption that human beings are capable of accessing truth? What do we lose? What do we gain?

In fact, although these latter questions may seem prosaic or utilitarian, they are the ones we feel most comfortable with. This is because they place the problem in the very terrain where, from our perspective, such questions need to be answered: the terrain of pragmatics. We maintain that by sacrificing the criterion of truth, we are not deprived of other criteria of discernment for discriminating between different interpretations. In a nutshell: not all interpretations are equal. What allows us to discern between different interpretations is the judgment we can make about the power of each of them.

The issue of power is one of the major themes of the ontology of language, as it was in Nietzsche's philosophy. We must note, however, that in order to properly understand the question of power, it will be necessary to make some developments that we have not yet made. This does not prevent us, however, from pointing to what is involved when we speak of power.

Language, we argue, is not innocent. Every proposition, every interpretation, opens and closes certain possibilities in life, enabling or inhibiting certain courses of action. This is what we mean when we talk about the power of different interpretations: their ability to open or close possibilities for action in the lives of human beings. This is the most important criterion we can use to choose one interpretation over another.

When we question our ability to access the truth, a spontaneous objection we encounter is to point to science as an obvious refutation of what we maintain. We cannot deny the fact that we are indeed accustomed to considering that what science does is precisely to reveal how things are. Its power seems to lie in this. But this is only one interpretation of what science does, however accustomed we may be to it. Maturana and Varela suggest a very different interpretation, which has the great merit of dispensing with the invocation of truth. They argue that what distinguishes scientific explanations from other types of explanations is that the former are explanations that allow the phenomena they explain to be regenerated. What differentiates scientific explanations, therefore, is their generative power.

What has been said in this section allows us to argue that the interpretation we call the ontology of language can open up possibilities for action and intervention that other interpretations cannot offer. Considering human beings as linguistic beings (and based on the successive reinterpretations we make from this first postulate), we claim that we open up possibilities for intervention in life that are closed in other interpretations.

Human beings have spent too much time disputing the truth of our interpretations. The only thing that is really at stake is the power that results from these interpretations, the capacity for action to transform ourselves and the world we live in. In his Eleventh Thesis on Feuerbach, Marx pointed out that philosophers had until then only devoted themselves to interpreting the world, when what matters is transforming it. The capacity to transform the world, we reply, is associated with the power of our interpretations.


 

Relationship between action and being

The human tendency to search for meaning, which we referred to earlier, manifests itself in language through the invention and adoption of stories about ourselves and the world. We have argued that what we are, our personal identity, is a linguistic construction, a story we make up about ourselves, about the direction of our lives in the future, and about our place in a community and in the world. We have said that both what we postulate ourselves to be and what we postulate the world we live in to be are linguistic constructions.

In reviewing the postulates put forward here, based on a view of language as action and, therefore, as a generator of reality and being, we can now establish an important thesis about human beings. We will call it the second principle of the ontology of language.


 

Second principle

:


 

Not only do we act according to how we are,

(and we do),

we also are according to how we act.

Action generates being.

One becomes according to what one does.

By moving from being to action, we allow ourselves to enter the process of becoming and avoid getting trapped in the metaphysical assumption that being is immutable. Being is only a moment in the process of becoming, and only one side of this same process. The other side is that pole of tension that confronts being with its dissolution and with the possibilities of transformation. It is what we will call nothingness.

We maintain that it is not possible for us to get rid of the notion of being and that a non-metaphysical interpretation of human beings is forced to live with this notion and can only, in order to undermine its metaphysical weight, enable a procedure to recognize the precariousness of all forms of being and their capacities for dissolution and transformation. The notion of nothingness allows us to create this procedure, which places the notion of being in tension and from which the priority of the process of becoming emerges. Within the cycle of becoming, being and nothingness are two sides that complement each other.

The metaphysical program privileges a relationship that goes from being to action. Behind every action, it assumes that there is always a being, a subject, that is revealed through the action performed. Our actions reveal our way of being. We do not question this. We understand the importance of observing human actions in order to understand what humans are like. This is one of the basic principles of ontological coaching, within which we can carry out what we call an "ontological interpretation." By observing the way an individual acts (and let's not forget that language is action), ontological coaching can interpret that person's way of being.

But at the same time, we postulate that this is only one side of the equation. Our actions not only reveal who we are, they also allow us to transform ourselves, to be different, to become. This is a second principle of ontological coaching and enables us to perform what we call "ontological intervention." By enabling a person to act in a certain way (and, again, let's not forget that language is action), ontological coaching allows them to move in a certain direction and leave behind their old ways of being.

Action, therefore, is not only the manifestation of a certain being unfolding in the world, it is also the possibility for that same being to transcend itself and become a different being.
