ON HUMAN LANGUAGE

We have said that human beings, as individuals, are linguistic beings, beings who live in language. In this section, we will explore, first, the nature of language, particularly human language, and second, the relationship between language and the individual. Once we have done this, we will return to the topic of the ontology of language.


 

LANGUAGE

Language as a consensual domain

We normally understand language as an individual capacity, as a property of a person. We say, then, that individuals have a capacity for language. This, as we can see, gives precedence to the individual with respect to language. It implies that it is the individual who speaks and listens. It assumes the individual as a precondition of language.

We oppose this view. We postulate, on the contrary, that individuals—not as particular members of a species, but as we have identified human individuals, that is, as persons—are themselves constituted in language. This implies that we give precedence to language over the individual. And this, as we shall see, is not a trivial postulate. Of course, we are not denying that, once constituted, the individual speaks and listens and therefore has the aforementioned capacity for language. But by taking the individual as already constituted, in order to derive language from it, we close off the possibility of understanding its own process of constitution as an individual.

It is clear that certain biological conditions must be met for a human being to be able to speak. As biologist Humberto Maturana—on whom we will rely heavily in this section—always insists on reminding us, we can only do what our biology allows us to do; we cannot exceed the limits of our biological capabilities. Without the particular structure of the human nervous system, and without the developed senses with which humans are equipped, we would not have the ability to hear and speak in the way that we do. But language is not generated by our biological capacities. "Wolf children" (children raised in the wild by wolves rather than humans), who have all these biological capacities, do not develop what we know as human language. Language, we postulate, is not developed by an isolated human being. Language arises from social interaction between humans. Consequently, language is a social phenomenon, not a biological one.

It is in the interaction between different particular human beings—even before we can speak of a process of individualization in which we constitute ourselves as persons—that a fundamental precondition of language appears: the constitution of a consensual domain. We speak of consensus wherever the participants in a social interaction share the same system of signs (gestures, sounds, etc.) to designate objects, actions, or events in order to coordinate their common actions. Without a consensual domain, there is no language. Once we accept this, we can no longer consider language as an individual property. The consensual domain is constituted in interaction with others in a social space.

A linguistic world of linguistic entities

It is important to note, however, that we are talking about language through and from language, and we cannot avoid this, which implies a trap that we must be wary of. Signs, objects, events, and actions are constituted as such in language. As such, they do not exist on their own.

We often quote Gertrude Stein as saying that "a rose is a rose is a rose." But a rose is not a rose independently of language. Whatever it is, it is what it is for us in language. Whatever it is in itself, independently of language, we do not know. A rose is a rose for us, for those who see it as a rose in a given consensual domain. It is a rose only as an entity designated in language, an entity that results from a linguistic distinction, which separates the entity, as an entity, from the rest.

An object is always a linguistic relationship that we establish with our world. Objects are constituted in language. As such, they always bear our own human mark and always say something about us. A cup of tea is only a cup of tea for us, not for the fly we see perched on it. And the fly is only a fly for us, not for the spider we see approaching it. And so we can go on ad infinitum. We insist: there is no other way than language; outside of language there is no place we can stand. Human beings live in a linguistic world.


 

Language as coordination of coordinated actions

Again, following Maturana, we propose that a consensual domain, an important factor in many forms of communication, is still not sufficient to produce the phenomenon of language. We speak of language only when we observe a particular type of communication. Many species communicate. Whenever we see members of a species coordinating common actions, we speak of communication.

However, we say that there is language—and by this we are suggesting a certain convention for referring to language—only when a particular type of coordination of actions occurs: when we observe members of a species coordinating the coordination of behavior. Language, as a phenomenon, is what an observer sees when they see a consensual coordination of the coordination of actions—when the members participating in an action coordinate the way they coordinate the action together. Language, we argue, is recursive coordination of behavior.

Let's take an example that can illustrate how these two systems of coordination of actions—involved in recursive coordination—work together. I say to my son Tomás: "Could you open that door, please?" He replies: "Okay." Let's examine what has happened. I made some sounds that we take as shared signs in an already established consensual domain. Based on the shared meanings attached to these sounds, Thomas responded to my question. In doing so, he also made some sounds, sounds different from mine. Again, we both gave these sounds a particular meaning that results from our shared consensual domain. So far, what we have done is very much like a dance. We have coordinated action at the level of sound production.

We can note the great difference that exists for those who share this consensual domain compared to those who do not. To strangers to this consensual domain, what Tomás and I have done sounds very much like gibberish, an unpredictable string of sounds. However, this is not the case for us. Rather, we are dancing together in a well-orchestrated consensual domain. Through the sounds we utter, we both hear words that designate objects in our common world, and we hear a question and an answer, and so on.

The dance does not end here. In addition to this first level of coordination of actions, another dance, another level of coordination of actions, can be expected to take place. Tomás may go to open the door. Once again, this action is not arbitrary. It arises directly from what happened at the first level of coordination of actions, when we were both uttering sounds. It would have been strange, for example, if he had gone to open the window. Or if he had done nothing at all. Once he has opened the door, Tomás can expect me to say "Thank you," to which he could reply "You're welcome." If instead he said "puppies" to me, I would probably find it strange again.

The above example shows us that language arises from the generation of a consensual domain that is produced in social interaction. But is this interaction between Tomás and me exceptional? Not at all. We humans are not the only species that have developed this dual pattern of action coordination that we call language. This is a trait that we often find in other species. A classic example of this is the oscillating dance of the bee, decoded in 1945 by the German biologist Karl von Frisen. Birds have also developed language systems. Through the action of singing, they coordinate other actions. Mammals also exhibit well-structured language systems. A typical example of this is the language of dolphins.


 

The recursive capacity of human language

The main difference between the linguistic capacity of humans and that of other living species is, first, our ability to encompass a very large number of consensual signs and, especially, to create new ones. There is a second important factor that differentiates human language from that observed in other species. We call it the recursive capacity of human language.

We have said that language is the recursive coordination of behavior. We now say that human language is recursive language. This means that we, as humans, can turn language back on itself. We can talk about our speech, about our linguistic distinctions, about our language, about the way we coordinate our coordination of actions. And we can do this over and over again. We can say, for example, "What do you mean by this?" or "Which door are you talking about?"

This recursive capacity of human language is the basis of what we call reflection and is the basis of human reason. If we view reflection as the metaphysicians did, as an individual property, as the activity of an individual faculty they called "the mind," severed from any connection to language, and if we accept that this reflection—which, as we have said, is the basis of reason—is a typically human trait, we can understand why the metaphysicians came to characterize human beings as rational beings. Reason, however, is a function of language. We are rational beings because we are linguistic beings living in a linguistic world.

Structural and historical conditions for the emergence of human language

How is it possible that human beings developed this special capacity for language? This is a question that can be answered in at least two ways: structurally and historically.

When we answer it in a structural way, we must examine the biological conditions that allow us to operate in language. This leads us to examine the structure of our nervous system and our vocal and auditory organs. The pioneering work of biologist Norman Geschwind has been pivotal in the study of the relationship between language and the brain. Philipp Lieberman has made important contributions in the field of the physiology of speech.

In the sense of a historical or evolutionary explanation, allow me to share a hypothesis once suggested to me by my friend, the biologist and population geneticist Carlos Valenzuela. He suggested that the triggering of evolutionary factors leading to the emergence of language in humans could be due to the enlargement of the buttocks in females. This is a physical trait that distinguishes humans from other primates.

Valenzuela speculated that the female human buttocks could have been the triggering factor that contributed to altering our physical balance and facilitating an upright posture. The latter allows humans to free their hands for gathering food and making tools. In turn, larger buttocks may have contributed to the expansion of the female pelvis, allowing for the birth of children with greater cranial capacity and, therefore, larger brains. With a narrower female pelvis, as is the case with other primates, these children simply could not be born. Living beings with larger brains have the neurological capacities necessary for more developed forms of language.

It should be clarified, however, that these types of explanations do not really account for the emergence of language itself. They only explain the development of the biological conditions necessary to make it possible. As we said before, language is not an individual ability, but an evolutionary trait that, based on specific biological conditions, arises from social interaction. Once the biological capacities are in place, we need social interaction as a breeding ground for the emergence of language.

THE INDIVIDUAL AS A LINGUISTIC CONSTRUCT


 

Individuals as social phenomena

We have postulated that human beings are linguistic beings. Let us examine the meaning of this postulate. An important aspect of what we are saying has to do with the fact that the individual, in terms of the person we are, is a linguistic phenomenon. As individuals, we are a type of living being that, as a condition of its own existence, lives constrained by its ability to generate meaning in its life, always interpreting itself and the world to which it belongs. We do this as soon as we operate in language.

The way we give meaning to our lives is obviously linguistic. It suffices to ask someone "who are you?" to recognize that what we get back is a narrative, a story in which we "narrate" who we are. Our identity is directly associated with our ability to generate meaning through our narratives. By modifying the narrative of who we are, we modify our identity.

One could object to what we are saying and argue that we cannot confuse the story we have of ourselves (or of others) with the subject to whom the story in question refers. One thing, one could contradict us, is the story, and quite another is the subject of the story. But the point is precisely that the individual cannot be separated from their story. That narrative is constitutive of who the individual is, since it is in the narratives we make of ourselves and others that we generate who we are. People with different narratives about themselves are different individuals, even though they may have gone through very similar experiences. We are the narrative that we and others tell about ourselves. We reiterate that by modifying that narrative, we modify who we are.

This, however, does not exhaust our understanding of individuals as linguistic beings. The main problem with this is that it remains an individual understanding of the individual and, as such, does not take sufficient account of the social nature of language. A second important point must therefore be made in this regard.

We postulate that, as individuals, we are what we are because of the linguistic culture in which we grow up and our position in the system of coordination of behavior (i.e., language) to which we belong. In this sense, the individual is not only a linguistic construct, but also a social construct. Although we belong to a tradition that tends to separate the individual from the social and interprets the two as opposite terms of a polarity, we postulate that the individual is a social phenomenon.

Let us examine this postulate in detail. The way we give ourselves meaning and the way we act in life is not arbitrary, and it is not possible for us, as individuals, to transcend it completely. The ways in which we confer meaning and act are based both on history and on the current practices of the community to which we belong.

The stories we tell about ourselves and others are constructed from a background of narratives and stories historically generated by the community to give itself meaning. We, as individuals, are always constituted within and from the background of those meta-narratives that we call historical discourses. If we want to better understand an individual, we must know the historical discourses from which they are constituted. It is within the principles of coherence of these historical discourses that we can grasp the coherence that makes a human being the individual they are.

Language, however, goes beyond our ability to tell stories; it goes beyond discourse. Language, we have said, is a system for coordinating behavior and is present in our actions. The production of narratives is only one way, albeit a very important one, of acting in life. There are many other ways of facing life that are not always included in the stories we tell about ourselves. And each community develops its own ways of facing life, of doing things. We call these ways of doing things, the way the community does them, social practices.

The way people behave in one community is often very different from the way people behave in another community. Even when dealing with similar issues (such as matters related to family, intimacy, death, work, etc.), the way we deal with them is very different from one community to another. That is why we have different customs, different ways of courting, eating, living as a family, and so on. Our coordination of behavior changes from one community to another. The French, the Chinese, the Somalis, the Mexicans are all different because they belong to different language systems. They belong to different historical discourses and social practices that arise precisely from different "breeding grounds" and, consequently, from different "cultures," giving rise to different types of individuals. Different linguistic cultures produce different individuals.

As the members of the Xhosa people of South Africa say, "I am because we are." Individuals are generated within a given linguistic culture, within a given system of behavioral coordination, within a given language, within a community. Once we grasp the language of the community, we can better understand the individual.


 

Individuals are constituted as such from the place that human beings occupy within broader linguistic systems.

We have already discussed the importance, for the individual, of those meta-narratives we have called historical discourses. They constitute an important aspect of a community's language. But they are only one particular element within that language. Viewed as a whole, language is the system of coordination of actions maintained by a community and, as such, is embedded in its social practices, in the way its members interact with each other. We know that not all French, Somali, or Mexican individuals are alike. There are strong individual differences between them. Of course, some of these differences can be attributed to biological factors, since we all have genetic differences that arise as distinct biological predispositions. However, it is not only in the field of biology that we can base individual differences, since the individual is always a linguistic entity, a meaningful unit within a larger language system. Biological differences, whose importance we do not deny, acquire their meaning and significance within the language system.

A language system is not a uniform space. On the contrary, it is a diversified interaction structure in which each member of the community plays a different role. In the system of behavioral coordination, not everyone occupies the same place or performs the same actions. The language system is a structure of relationships, and the position of each member of the community within this structure is an important aspect to consider in the process of individualization, in the constitution of individuals as individuals.

We are what we are based on the relationships we establish with others. The individual is constituted as the sum of their relationships with others. Individualities will differ depending on whether we are the employer or the employee, the parent or the child, the eldest child, the middle child, or the youngest child, the actor or the spectator, and so on. One of the main merits of systemic psychology has been precisely to recognize the role of social systems (especially the family) in shaping the process of individualization.

History provides us with very good examples of people who became famous precisely because of the position they occupied in the social structure. A classic example is that of Thomas Beckett during the reign of Henry II in 12th-century England. Beckett had been Henry's great childhood friend and, therefore, was the person Henry chose, once he became king, to succeed the Archbishop of Canterbury when he died, in the midst of a great dispute between the Church and the Crown. By appointing Beckett as the new Archbishop, Henry believed he had resolved the conflict in his favor. However, in his new position, Beckett became a different individual. His behavior is no longer that of the King's old friend, but rather what Beckett interprets as befitting the highest authority of the Church in England. His new position, therefore, makes him the great enemy of the actions undertaken by the English Crown. Beckett is murdered at the doors of his cathedral by knights of the Crown, presumably sent by Henry.


 

The mutual relationship between linguistic systems and individual behavior

A basic principle of the systemic approach is the recognition that human behavior is shaped by the structure of the system to which the individual belongs and by the position he occupies in that system. When the structure of the system changes, individual behavior can also be expected to change. What was once done may not be done again, and/or what seemed impossible in the past may suddenly become possible for the same members of the system. This is something that often goes unnoticed. We do not realize how the systems to which we belong make us who we are.

We often notice, for example, that some companies seem to recruit capable people while others seem to do the opposite. The difference, however, often lies not in recruitment but in the different behavioral systems that these companies have. Different management systems, for example, generate different types of individuals, who are capable (or not) of doing things in very different ways and, therefore, of becoming (or not) increasingly capable. What has been said about companies is equally valid in relation to any other social system (e.g., family system, educational system, health system, government system, etc.).

Based on the above, we could easily fall into adopting a narrow structural deterministic approach that would postulate that what we are as individuals is determined by the structure of the systems to which we belong. While this is, on a certain level, a valid postulate, it needs to be balanced, on another level, by an opposite postulate. We cannot forget that, while the system conditions what we are as individuals, it is no less valid that we, as individuals, are the creators of that same system.

How can this be possible? If we accept that individuals are social phenomena, how can they also be the designers of their social space? There is no contradiction here. Once constituted as individuals, due to the recursive capacity of human language, we are able to observe ourselves and the system to which we belong, and to go beyond ourselves and those systems. We can become observers of the observer that we are and we can act according to our possibilities for action.

Our capacity for reflection allows us to speculate, to engage in conversations with others (and with ourselves) about new possibilities, to take risks and invent—freeing ourselves from our attachments to ourselves and our social environment.

The phenomenon of leadership sheds light precisely on this human capacity to intervene in the design of our social environments and, in doing so, to also intervene in the design of many other individuals. And leadership, we postulate, is based on a set of specific linguistic abilities. It is one of the clearest manifestations of the generative capacity of language.

We said earlier that we not only act according to how we are, but we also are according to how we act. The time has come to complement that thesis with another that takes us from the relationship between being and action to the polarity between the individual and the social group (or system) to which he or she belongs. Based on the above, we recognize, therefore, that human beings are historical beings, beings who live and operate within the framework of certain historical conditions.

Not everything is possible for an individual. On the contrary, what is possible is always a given historical movement, under specific social conditions and influenced by them. Individuals cannot do anything. They operate within the limits of what is historically possible for them. And what is historically possible for an individual depends on the language systems to which they belong.

Even if individuals transcend what is historically given, even if they invent new possibilities, even if they generate new historical realities, and even if they project themselves into the future, they do so as a result of what is historically possible for them. This brings us to the third principle of the ontology of language.

Third principle

:


 

Individuals act according to the social systems to which they belong. But through their actions, even though conditioned by these social systems, they can also change those social systems.

Only our narrow individualistic ideology can blind us to the powerful effect of social systems on our formation as individuals. If we want to create more effective systems, as the Japanese are doing in the field of business and enterprise, we must open ourselves to a new examination of the relationship between individuals and social systems.

Human beings are at the intersection of two very different systems. On the one hand, we are a biological system and are determined by our biological structure. It is at this level that we can locate our biological capacity for language. But, as we have already pointed out, our biological capacity for language does not generate language. Language arises from social interaction, from living together with one another.

Therefore, we constitute ourselves as individuals from the system of relationships we maintain with others. Individuals are components of a larger social system, the language system. Their position within that system is what makes them the particular individuals they are. The emphasis, however, should not be placed on the social system or its individual components. It is in the relationship between the social system and the individual, between the whole and its parts, that the dynamics of becoming occur. The social system constitutes the individual, just as the individual constitutes the social system.


 

RETURN TO THE ONTOLOGY OF LANGUAGE

The vision presented here, the ontology of language, is not just another interpretation of the human being. It challenges the basic assumptions held for centuries by the metaphysical program. It represents a serious attempt to go beyond the basic dogmas of metaphysics and brings to an end what we have called the metaphysical drift. It does so in several ways.

First, it replaces the traditional "language of being" with a new "language of becoming." To do this, it introduces the notion of "nothingness" as a fundamental principle. Nothingness, in this context, should not be confused with passive nihilism. It does not stop at the pessimistic recognition of the meaninglessness of human life, but becomes the driving force that allows human beings to create and regenerate a meaning of life, necessary to cope with our existence.

Secondly, the ontology of language distances itself from the metaphysical concept of truth, a concept that marks the primary objective of rational thought. On the contrary, it assumes that only more or less powerful interpretations exist: narratives that can open and close different possibilities for human beings. Power, not truth, is the fundamental kriterion (a Greek word designating the standard of a valid judgment). Possibilities do not exist on their own; they are not independent of the individuals for whom they are possibilities.

The search for those abstract universals (those truths sought by metaphysicians) capable of standing on their own, independently of human beings, is futile. As Protagoras—who, along with Heraclitus, is another of the thinkers against whom the early metaphysicians write—proclaimed, "Man is the measure of all things." The distinction between true and false propositions—an important distinction—only makes sense within the shared background of a community.

Thirdly, the ontology of language achieves what had been destroyed by the metaphysical program: unity between the speaker, language, and action. It recognizes that everything said is always said by someone, reestablishing what we have called the broken link between language and the speaker. It posits that language is action and, therefore, avoids the separation between the two, particularly between thought and action. Finally, it posits that action (which encompasses language) generates being and that it therefore constitutes the individual who speaks (the speaker) and the one who acts (the actor). As Nietzsche pointed out, "the actor is a fiction, the fact is everything."

While replacing the importance of "being," upheld by metaphysicians, with a renewed understanding of "becoming," the ontology of language places action at the center of its argument. Connecting language with action produces a new understanding of human action. We now become observers of those actions—linguistic acts—that we had not seen as actions in the past, and, moreover, we are also able to reconstruct human action in linguistic terms, thus taking a very important step in our capacity for design.

However, the greatest strength of the ontology of language lies in the interpretation it provides of the individual—that is, of the phenomenon of the human person—and their world. By treating both the individual and their world as linguistic constructions, this new approach offers an enormous expansion of human possibilities. Let us examine some fields in which this expansion becomes evident.

The first is the field of human suffering. Suffering, we postulate, is a linguistic phenomenon. That is what differentiates it from pain. Pain is due to biological reasons. When we suffer, on the other hand, we do so based on our interpretations of ourselves, others, and the events in which we participate in our lives. Without language, there would be no suffering. At the same time, by intervening through conversation in our judgments and interpretations (narratives), we are able to deal effectively with suffering. This is how we relieve ourselves of suffering, although we rarely notice that this is what we are doing.

It should be noted that by recognizing the linguistic nature of the person, we can intervene in a much more powerful way. The ontology of language, when used in the practice of ontological coaching, guides us to the place where we must seek the source of suffering and, consequently, to what needs to be transformed in order to alleviate it. The ontology of language does not offer to eliminate suffering, since suffering is constitutive of human life. But it shows us that there is much suffering that we can avoid once we understand how it is generated in language.

In a related field, we postulate that the ontology of language offers us a powerful tool for dealing with one of the most salient features of our time: the crisis of meaning (of life) that we face today. We live in a postmodern world, a world characterized by the progressive exhaustion of the power of metanarratives, those primordial social discourses of the past, from which we derive meaning for our lives. Human beings are always trying to make sense of themselves and their world. This represents a kind of "original sin," an original condition that arises from the fact that we are linguistic beings. We cannot live without giving meaning to ourselves and the world.

For centuries, we have relied on fundamental social discourses—whether religious, political, philosophical, etc.—to generate the meaning we needed to continue living. These meta-narratives answered questions about why life, ourselves, and the world had meaning. The weakening of these fundamental discourses and their growing inability to generate on their own the full meaning of life that we need is a feature of our times. This historical feature, called the postmodern condition, leaves us in a recurring crisis of meaning, where the religious, political, philosophical, and other answers of the past cannot provide us with solutions as effectively as they once did. Many of today's social ills have been responses, often desperate responses, to the meaning we lack.

One of the greatest contributions of the ontology of language is the competence it offers people to invent and regenerate meaning in their lives. The ontology of language confronts us with the fact that we cannot always expect life to generate, by itself, the meaning we need to live it. But at the same time, it shows us how we generate meaning through language: by constantly inventing stories and through action that allows us to transform ourselves as people and transform our world. The ontology of language allows us to take full responsibility for our lives. It allows us to choose the actions that will lead us to become the person we have chosen to be. It is a fundamentally important tool in the design of our lives, ourselves, and the world.

Finally, let us say that there is—in our opinion—another important contribution of this new understanding of human beings. In the past, when we were shaped by those metanarratives that we took for granted, when we thought that truth existed and was within our reach—when we did not possess it—we lived in war, killing each other, destroying our natural environment, the condition of our existence. Today, we cannot avoid recognizing a dark side to the assumptions associated with the metaphysical idea of rationality.

In a global world, in a world like ours today, in which we face the rapid dissolution of the nation-state, in which all human beings on the planet have become economically interdependent, and in which we have accumulated such incredibly destructive power, the metaphysical approach does not allow us, within its boundaries, to relate to one another with mutual respect. A new understanding of human beings is needed if we are to survive. An understanding that allows us to observe and accept our differences, and to devise better ways to bridge those differences and live together. In the ontology of language, there is room for hope that this will happen.

As we said at the beginning, the ontology of language is not just a development that arises solely from the realm of ideas. There have been important historical conditions that have facilitated the emergence of the ideas that represent its theoretical center. The emergence of new forms of communication—which we have called electronic language—constitutes, in our opinion, one of those fundamental historical forces pointing in that direction. We therefore see the ontology of language as capable of filling a historical need in addressing many of the issues that challenge the world today (cultural, political, economic, individual, etc.).

In this sense, the ontology of language is a good example of the result of the conditions of our current social systems, as well as representing an immense resource for dealing with the deficiencies of those systems and contributing to their necessary future transformations.
