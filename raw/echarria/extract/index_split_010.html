BASIC LINGUISTIC ACTS

[5]


 

BACKGROUND

As we have previously pointed out, according to our traditional conception, language describes reality. When I say, for example, "it was sunny on Tuesday," or "this is a wooden table," or "my computer has a 40 MB hard drive," these phrases are normally understood as descriptions of the properties of different objects (last Tuesday, this table, my computer). When I say "I'm sorry" or "thank you very much," these phrases are understood as descriptions of feelings.

Our common sense takes it for granted that language describes the state of things. This conception assumes that reality is already there long before language, and that language simply describes it, "talks about" it. The role of language is assumed to be passive. Language always comes late, when reality has already been established, has already taken its place. Therefore, reality comes first, then language. The role of language seems to be to give an account of what exists.

This is a very old interpretation of language, whose origins date back to the ancient Greeks. It is so old that we usually forget that it is an interpretation. Moreover, we even come to think that this interpretation is, in fact, a description of what language is and, therefore, a faithful representation of its own "reality."

This long-held interpretation has been seriously questioned since the second half of this century with the emergence of a branch of philosophy called the philosophy of language and, more particularly, since the late contributions of the Austrian philosopher Ludwig Wittgenstein.

The philosophy of language soon argued that when we speak, we not only describe an existing reality; we also act. Language, it was argued, is action. Let's take an example. When we say "Congratulations" to someone, we are not describing a congratulation, we are actually giving it. We are actually performing the act of congratulating. British philosopher J.L. Austin was the first to highlight this active quality of language or, in his own words, the "performative" nature of language. He realized that even when we describe, we are "making" a description and, therefore, we are acting.

Another important advance was made by the American philosopher John R. Searle, who proposed—in a way that reminds us of the inquiries of the ancient Greek sophists, and particularly Gorgias—what he called a taxonomy of speech acts. According to Searle, when we speak, we perform a limited and specific number of actions. He called these actions "speech acts." We will call them linguistic acts, since these acts can also be performed nonverbally.

Searle argued that, regardless of the language we speak, be it Spanish, English, or Chinese, we will always perform the same limited and specific number of linguistic acts. We may not know how to speak Chinese, but we know, according to Searle, that when Chinese people speak, they perform the same type of actions as Mexicans, English people, or Russians. All human beings, regardless of the language we speak, make statements, make declarations, make requests, and so on when we speak. These linguistic actions are universal. We find them in all languages, regardless of the specific language spoken. Searle's proposition is of the utmost importance. We can now observe language and distinguish the different actions we perform when we communicate.

Thanks to these contributions—and others that are not relevant here—a generative and active interpretation of language has gradually replaced our old passive interpretation of language, which restricted it to its descriptive nature.


 

LINGUISTIC ACTS

The presentation of the different linguistic acts that we will make below is simultaneously based on and departs from the proposal made by John R. Searle. We therefore insist on pointing out that our treatment of linguistic acts does not correspond to that of the American philosopher, but rather represents an elaboration based on his proposal.


 

Assertions and declarations


 

When we observe speech as an action, or rather, as an action that always establishes a link between words on the one hand and the world on the other, we might ask ourselves the following question: when we speak, which takes precedence? The world or words? In other words, which of the two—the word or the world—drives the action? Which could we say "commands"? These questions have the merit of leading us to establish an important distinction: sometimes, when speaking, the word must conform to the world, while at other times, the world conforms to the word.

In the first case, when we can argue that the word must conform to the world and that, therefore, the world drives the word, we speak of assertions. When the opposite occurs, when we can point out that the word modifies the world and that, therefore, the world must conform to what is said, we speak of declarations. The importance of this distinction is that it allows us to separate two different types of actions that take place when speaking: two distinct linguistic acts. Having made the distinction, let us now examine each of its terms separately.


 

a) Statements

Statements correspond to the type of linguistic act that we normally call descriptions. In fact, they appear to be descriptions. However, they are propositions about our observations. We believe it is important to clarify this point.

We are careful not to say that statements describe things as they are, since, as we have postulated, we never know how they really are. We only know how we observe them. And since human beings share, on the one hand, a common biological structure and, on the other, the tradition of distinctions of their community, it is possible for them to share what they observe.

When our biological structure is different, as is the case with color-blind people, for example, we cannot make the same observations. What is red to one person may be green to another. Who is right? Who is wrong? Who is closer to reality? These questions have no answer. We can only say that these individuals have different biological structures. Red and green only make sense from the point of view of our species' sensory ability to distinguish colors. The distinctions between red and green only tell us about our ability to react to the external environment; they do not tell us about external reality itself.

Human beings observe according to the distinctions we possess. Without the distinction "table," I cannot observe a table. I can see differences in color, shape, texture, etc., but not a table. Eskimos can observe more distinctions of white than we can. The difference between us and them is not biological.

Our traditions of distinctions are different. Therefore, the question "How many shades of white are there really?" only makes sense in the context of a particular tradition of distinctions.

Similarly, we cannot talk about Tuesday, Madrid, and sun without the distinctions Tuesday, Madrid, and sun. Someone who does not have these distinctions cannot say, "It was sunny last Tuesday in Madrid." Who is right? Who is wrong? Who is closer to reality? The person who has the distinctions? Or the person who does not? These questions only make sense to people who share the same set of distinctions. From this point of view, it is valid to say that we live in a linguistic world. Statements are always made within an already established "space of distinctions."

As human beings, we can share what we observe, so we assume that this is how things really are. We think that if what I observe appears to be the same as what my neighbor observes, then things must be as we both observe them. But this conclusion is obviously debatable. Even if my neighbor and I share the same observations, we cannot say that we observe things as they really are. We can only conclude that we share the same observations, that we observe the same thing. Nothing more. The only description we make is that of our observation, not a description of reality.

However, based on this common capacity for observation, human beings can distinguish between true and false statements. This is one of the most important distinctions we can make when dealing with statements.

It should be noted, however, that the distinction between true and false only makes sense within a certain "space of distinctions" and, therefore, only under certain social and historical conditions. It does not refer to "Truth" (with a capital letter) as an apprehension of the "being" of things. The distinction between true and false is a social convention that makes coexistence in community possible.

A true statement is a proposition for which we can provide a witness. A witness is any member of our community (with whom we share the same distinctions) who, because they were in the same place at the same time, can corroborate our observations. When we say "It was sunny last Tuesday in Madrid," we will call this statement true if we can prove that someone with whom we share common distinctions, having been there last Tuesday, shared what we observed.

Statements can not only be true, they can also be false. A false statement is a proposition subject to confirmation, but which any witness, anyone who was there at the time, could refute. The linguistic act of saying "It rained last Tuesday in Mexico City" is a statement, even though this fact can be refuted by others who were there that day. If it is refuted, it will remain a statement, but a false one.

Not all statements, however, can be separated in practice into true or false. Sometimes they cannot be confirmed because the conditions necessary for their corroboration do not exist.

Weather forecasts are good examples. If someone says, "It's going to rain tomorrow," they are making a statement. It is a proposition that is subject to confirmation. However, we will have to wait until tomorrow to determine whether that statement is true or false. In the meantime, its quality will be undecided. As a general rule, statements about the future are undecided.

When we make statements about the past, something similar can happen. If we say, for example, "It snowed in Bariloche on April 10, 1415," this is a statement. Theoretically, it can be corroborated. Moreover, it is still a type of proposition in which the word must fit the world and, therefore, it is a statement. In practice, however, we will not find anyone who was present there at that time, and there are no records with witness observations. The quality of this statement (whether true or false) will also remain undecided.

Every time we perform a linguistic act, we make a commitment and must accept social responsibility for what we say. Speaking is never an innocent act. Every linguistic act is characterized by different social commitments. In the case of statements, the social commitment is related to the need to effectively establish that the word meets the requirement of conforming to our observations about the state of the world.

Therefore, when we affirm something, we commit ourselves to the truthfulness of our statements before the community that listens to us. We assume a social responsibility for their truthfulness. In other words, we commit ourselves to the possibility of providing a witness to corroborate our observations or, failing that, to comply with any other procedure that is accepted as evidence in the community to which we belong.

When we make statements, we are talking about the state of our world and, therefore, we are talking about a world that already exists. Statements have to do with what we normally call the world of "facts."


 

b) Declarations

Very different from statements is another type of linguistic act called a declaration. When we make declarations, we are not talking about the world; we are creating a new world for ourselves. The word generates a different reality. After what was said has been said, the world is no longer the same as before. It has been transformed by the power of the word.

Let's take a classic historical example. When a group of people gathered in Philadelphia in July 1776 and, representing the 13 English colonies in North America, presented the world with a text that began by saying: "When, in the course of human events, it becomes necessary for one people to dissolve the political bonds which have connected them with another...", they were not talking "about" what was happening in the world at that moment. They were creating a new world, a world that did not exist before the Declaration of Independence of the United States of America was made.

Declarations do not only happen at very special moments in history. We find them everywhere throughout our lives. When the judge says "Not guilty!"; when the referee says "Out!"; when the officer says "I now pronounce you husband and wife"; when we say at home "It's time for dinner"; when someone starts a new company; when a boss hires or fires someone; when a teacher says "Pass"; when a mother says to her child "Now you can watch TV"—in all these situations, declarations are being made. And in all these cases, the world is different after the declaration. The act of making a declaration creates a new reality.

In each of these cases, words transform the world. Once a statement has been made, things are no longer as they were before. In each of these instances, the world is rearticulated according to the power of the word. Each of them is an example of the generative capacity of language. These are concrete situations in which we can recognize the limitations of our traditional conception, which conceives of language as a fundamentally passive instrument.

Declarations bring us closer to what we commonly associate with the power of the gods. They are the clearest expression of the power of words, of the fact that what is said becomes reality; that reality is transformed according to the will of the speaker. It is not surprising, therefore, to note how, in our Judeo-Christian tradition, it is maintained that in the beginning there was only the word and that it was precisely the word, as Genesis tells us, that created the world through successive declarations. "Let there be light," God declared, and there was light.

Declarations are not related to our shared capacities for observation, as was the case with affirmations. They are related to power. We only create a different world through our declarations if we have the capacity to enforce them. This capacity may come from force or may have been granted to us as authority. Force compels us to bow to a declaration and obey it because we want to avoid the risk of disintegration. Authority is the power that we or the community grants to certain people to make valid statements. Both force and authority are expressions of power.

Let us return to our first example, the United States Declaration of Independence. When the British learned of it, they obviously did not accept it immediately. For them, it was not a valid declaration but an act of arrogance by some of the Crown's subjects. And King George III made this clear to them. However, as history has shown, the British did not have enough power to oppose that declaration and, in the end, had to accept it. This declaration was valid because those who made it had the power to ensure its enforcement and validity.

The case of an official performing a marriage ceremony is different. In this case, we have granted an official the authority to make the declaration. If someone without authority were to say, "I now pronounce you husband and wife," we would not take what that person says seriously. The world would not change after that declaration. However, the world does not remain the same as before—not for the couple getting married nor for the community in which the marriage takes place—when the declaration is made by an official vested with the authority to make it.

Declarations are not true or false, as statements were. They are valid or invalid according to the power of the person making them. This is a fundamental distinction when dealing with declarations.

A declaration implies a different kind of commitment than an assertion. When we declare something, we commit ourselves to behave consistently with the new reality we have declared. The official who performed the ceremony, for example, cannot later say that he did not really mean what he declared, without suffering the consequences of inconsistent behavior.

When we make a declaration, we also commit ourselves to the validity of our declaration. This means that we claim to have the authority to make such a declaration and that it was made in accordance with socially accepted norms. Authority is generally limited to specific social norms. The person who has been granted the authority to make a particular statement must usually meet certain requirements in order to do so. A jury, for example, has the authority to return a verdict of not guilty, but in order to do so, it must comply with clearly established social norms.


 

c) Some fundamental statements in life

We have said that in order to make certain statements, it is necessary to have the proper authority. Without such authority having been granted, these statements are not valid and, therefore, are not effective. However, there is a wide range of statements that do not require a social grant of authority, but are associated with the dignity of the human person. Just as a dignitary, by occupying a position of authority, has the power to make certain statements that society reserves only for some, in the same way, every human person has the power to make certain statements in the sphere of their own personal life, and insofar as they exercise that power, they establish their dignity as a person. A society of free men and women is precisely that society which recognizes and socially sanctions the rights related to human dignity. This is directly related to the recognition that the individual, by the simple fact of being an individual, has the authority to make certain declarations.

Below we will refer to a set of statements that belong to this sphere of personal authority. We will limit ourselves to only six, aware that we could add several more.

The statement of "No"


 

Saying "No" is one of the most important statements an individual can make. Through it, they establish both their autonomy and their legitimacy as a person and, therefore, it is the statement in which we commit our dignity to the greatest degree. As individuals, we have the right to refuse to accept the state of affairs we face and the demands that others may make of us. This is an inalienable right that no one can take away from us. On many occasions, however, the price of saying no is high, and it is up to each of us to decide whether or not to pay it. But even if the price is high, as individuals we can continue to exercise our power to say no. Many of our heroes, many of our saints, are people we admire because they were willing to pay with their lives for the exercise of this right.

There are two important social institutions that rest on the social recognition of the right of individuals to say no: democracy and the market. Both rest on the right of the individual to choose, and every right to choose is ultimately based on the right to say no. Obviously, these are not the only social institutions in which this right is manifested, nor are we arguing that we cannot recognize limitations on it. But analyzing this would take us away from the topic at hand.

Beyond heroes and saints, democracy and the market, we want to highlight the importance of saying "No" in everyone's daily life. Every time we feel we should say "No" and don't, we see our dignity compromised. Every time we say "No" and it is ignored, we feel we have not been respected. This is a statement that defines the respect we have for ourselves and that others will have for us. It is a statement that plays a decisive role in shaping our relationships with our partners, friends, co-workers, children, and so on. Depending on how we exercise our right to say "No," we define one way or another of being in life. Moreover, we also define one way or another of living.

The declaration of "No" can take different forms. It does not always manifest itself by saying "No." Sometimes, for example, we recognize it when someone says "Enough!", thereby declaring their willingness to no longer accept what they have accepted until then. It therefore refers to a process in which we have participated and which we have decided to end. We also recognize "No" when someone says "This is not acceptable to me" and, in doing so, sets a limit for the other person regarding what we are willing to allow.


 

The declaration of acceptance: "Yes"

"Yes" does not seem to be as powerful as "No." After all, life is an open space for "Yes." It is, as computer specialists would say, the statement that operates "by default." As long as we do not say "No," it is normally assumed that we are saying "Yes."

However, there is one extremely important aspect of "Yes" that is worth highlighting. It refers to the commitment we make when we say "Yes" or its equivalent, "I accept." When this happens, we put the value and respect of our word on the line. Given that we maintain that we are linguistic beings, beings who live in language, the importance we attach to the value we place on our "Yes" will be understood. Few things more seriously affect a person's identity than saying "Yes" and not acting consistently with that statement. One area where this is decisive is in the realm of promises. We will discuss this further below.


 

The declaration of ignorance

It would seem that saying "I don't know" is a statement of little consequence. Some might even argue that it is not a declaration, but an affirmation, and in some cases it can indeed be considered as such (when, for example, the community—whatever it may be—consensually establishes criteria that clearly define for its members who knows and who does not know). However, this is not always the case, and in fact, it is often impossible to reach such a consensus.

Experience shows us how often we tend to operate on the assumption that we know, only to discover how ignorant we really were. One of the crucial problems with learning is that very often we do not know that we do not know. And when that happens, we simply close off the possibility of learning and approach a field full of opportunities to learn new things as if it were already familiar territory. Anything new that is said to us is therefore trapped in what is already known or in premature disqualification. How many times have we found ourselves exclaiming, "I know about this!" or "This is the old story of..." only to realize much later that we were listening assuming we knew about it, and discover that we had closed ourselves off to a possibility of learning. And there are those who may die without us ever convincing them that they don't know.

Saying "I don't know" is the first link in the learning process. It involves crossing that threshold where, at least, I know that I don't know and, therefore, I open myself up to learning. Having made that first statement, I can now say "I will learn" and, as a result, create a space where I can expand my possibilities for action in life. Our ability to open ourselves early to learning, through the statement "I don't know," represents one of the most powerful driving forces in the process of personal transformation and creation of who we are.


 

The declaration of gratitude

As children, we are taught to say "Thank you," and we often view this teaching as a habit of good manners, a formality that facilitates coexistence with others. We do not always recognize everything that this small statement contains. Of course, we can say "Thank you" without it meaning much, although, we insist, saying it is never insignificant. But we can look at the statement "Thank you" as an opportunity to celebrate all that life has provided us and to acknowledge others for what they do for us and what they mean in our lives.

In this context, we cannot fail to recognize the generative power of the action we perform when we say "Thank you." When someone fulfills their commitment to us to our complete satisfaction and we say "Thank you," we are not only acknowledging that fulfillment, we are also building our relationship with that person. Failure to do so can undermine that relationship. No matter what type of relationship it is, whether romantic, friendly, or work-related, thanking those who fulfill our needs or who take our concerns to heart and act accordingly allows us to take care of the other person and address their own desire to be recognized for what they do and to receive our appreciation for the attention we have benefited from. By not expressing gratitude, we can generate resentment, and those who went out of their way to serve us and be there for us end up saying, "And they didn't even say thank you." It is very possible that in the future, if they can avoid it, we will not be able to count on their help again.

But it is not only people; life itself is a reason for gratitude and celebration for all that it provides us. Saying "Thank you to life," as Violeta Parra's beautiful song does, for example, is a fundamental act of regeneration of meaning, of reconciliation with our existence, past, present, and future. It should come as no surprise, therefore, that some societies have the celebration of a day of thanksgiving as one of their main activities. By declaring our gratitude, we not only take a stance "in front" of others and "in front" of life. In doing so, we participate in the creation of our relationships with them and in the construction of our own lives.


 

The declaration of forgiveness

Under this heading, we include three different declarative acts, all of them associated with the phenomenon of forgiveness. Just as we previously highlighted the importance of the declaration of thanks, we must now examine its reverse. When we fail to fulfill what we have committed to or when our actions, without intending to, hurt others, we must take responsibility for it. The way we normally do this is by saying "I'm sorry." This is a declaration.

In Spanish, however, we often express the declarative act of forgiveness in the form of a request. We say "I ask your forgiveness" or "I apologize." In doing so, we make the declaration "I'm sorry" by the person who takes responsibility for the actions that hurt the other person dependent on the declarative act of the injured party saying "I forgive you." Both acts are extremely important, and we believe it is necessary not to subsume the first in the second.

The importance of keeping them separate is that it allows us to recognize the effectiveness of saying "I'm sorry" regardless of the response we get from the other person. In other words, what we are pointing out is that we cannot make our responsibility for our own actions dependent on the actions of others. The other person's forgiveness does not exempt us from our responsibility. Having said "I'm sorry," even if the other person does not forgive us, is in itself of greater importance, and the world we build is different—regardless of what the other person says—depending on whether or not we have declared it. Obviously, in many cases, saying "I'm sorry" may be insufficient as a way of taking responsibility for the consequences of our actions. Often, in addition to forgiveness, we have to take responsibility for repairing the damage done or compensating the other person. But that does not diminish the importance of declaring forgiveness.

The second declarative act associated with forgiveness is, as we anticipated, "I forgive you," "I forgive them," or simply "I forgive." This act is obviously very different from saying "I'm sorry." We will also refer to it when we address the issue of resentment. However, allow us to make some clarifications in this regard.

When someone fails to fulfill a promise or behaves toward us in a way that contravenes what we consider to be legitimate expectations, we will very likely feel affected by what has happened. This is even more so if, after the event, the person responsible does not take responsibility for the consequences of their actions (or omissions). We may quite legitimately feel that we have been victims of injustice. And in thinking this way, we will justify our resentment towards the other person, especially to the extent that we have placed ourselves on the side of good and the other person on the side of evil. Therefore, we consider that we have every right to be resentful.

What we may not realize, however, is that by falling into resentment, we have placed ourselves in a position of dependence on the person we hold responsible. That person may well have forgotten what they did. However, our resentment will continue to bind us, like slaves, to that other person. Our resentment will eat away at our peace and well-being and will probably end up coloring our entire life. Resentment makes us slaves to those we blame and therefore undermines not only our happiness but also our freedom as individuals.

Nietzsche was the great philosopher on the subject of resentment. When he talks about it, he associates it with the image of the tarantula. Resentment, Nietzsche tells us, is the emotion of the slave. But be careful. It is not that slaves are necessarily resentful people. Often they are not, as the example of Epictetus shows us. The point is that those who live in resentment live in slavery. A slavery that may not be legal or political, but which is undoubtedly a slavery of the soul.

Forgiveness is not an act of grace for those who have hurt us, although it can also be that. Forgiveness is a declarative act of personal liberation. By forgiving, we break the chain that binds us to the perpetrator and keeps us as victims. By forgiving, we take charge of ourselves and resolve to end an open-ended process that continues to reproduce the harm that was originally done to us. By forgiving, we recognize that not only the other person, but also ourselves, are now responsible for our well-being.

When we talk about forgiveness, the issue of forgetting often arises. Some people say, "I don't want to forget" or "I feel I have an obligation not to forget." Whether or not to forget is not something we can resolve through a declaration. In a way, it does not depend entirely on our will. Forgiveness, however, is an action that is in our hands.

The third declarative act associated with forgiveness is, this time, not saying "I forgive you," nor forgiving others, but forgiving oneself. Strictly speaking, this is a form of forgiveness, and therefore what we have said about forgiving others applies to forgiving oneself. The difference this time is that we assume the role of both victim and perpetrator.

One of the difficulties we encounter in relation to forgiving ourselves comes from holding a metaphysical conception of ourselves that assumes that we are a certain way and that this way is permanent. Therefore, if we did something irreparable, it speaks to who we are, and we can only carry the guilt for the rest of our lives. This interpretation does not allow for the recognition that in the past we acted under different conditions than those we find ourselves in today. Without allowing us to evade responsibility for our actions and preventing us from taking responsibility for what we did, this stance fails to recognize that having done what we did then and recriminating ourselves for the consequences of those actions transforms us, and that the person who recriminates themselves is usually already someone very different from the person who did what we regret.

Forgiving oneself has the same liberating effect we mentioned earlier, and doing so is a manifestation of love for oneself and for life itself.


 

The declaration of love

The last declaration we want to talk about in this section is when one person says to another, "I love you." Without examining what love is from a linguistic point of view on this occasion, it is important to note that it refers to a particular bond, a type of relationship, between two people. Given the aforementioned recursive capacity of language, we can also talk about self-love, referring precisely to the type of relationship we have with ourselves.

A common assumption is that love exists and that saying "I love you" merely describes what is already there. Based on this assumption, we sometimes hear people say, "What's the point of telling you I love you? It doesn't change anything." It may not change the emotion one feels for the other, but saying it or not saying it is not indifferent to the relationship we build with the other, particularly when that other is also a human being. Declaring "I love you" or "I care for you" participates in the construction of my relationship with the other and is part of the creation of a shared world.

It is important to examine our personal relationships based on bonds of affection—such as our relationship with our partner, our children, our parents, our friends, etc.—and ask ourselves how often we express our affection for each other. We should also ask ourselves what difference it would make to the other person to hear this declaration. It is important not to forget how speaking—and, therefore, also remaining silent—creates our world. As I write, I remember the English film "Remains of the Day," which I saw a few days ago. The central theme of the film is precisely the absence of declarations of love. In it, we see what happens to two people who were unable to say "I love you" to each other.

d) On the relationship between affirmations and declarations

So far, we have identified two linguistic acts, affirmations and declarations. It would seem, however, that both are on the same level and only differ according to who conducts the word-world relationship. There would be no problem with this, were it not for the fact that it omits an aspect that we consider important to highlight. Declarations represent the primary linguistic act par excellence. That is to say, the one that creates the conditions for the emergence of the others. Without denying the distinction we have made between assertions and declarations, it should be recognized that in order to have assertions, we need a declarative space in which they are constituted.

Let us take an example to illustrate this. Saying "Today is Thursday" is an assertion. For all members of a community who share the way we name the days, it is a proposition that can be considered true or false. The person making this statement does not intend to change anything by making it. However, for this statement to be made, there had to be a moment when, by declaration, the convention of naming the days in a certain way was established, allowing us to say "Today is Thursday."

We said earlier that statements give account of our observations and that these operate within a space of distinctions. What we are pointing out now is that every "space of distinctions," a condition of statements, is strictly speaking a "declarative space."

The fact that statements, like other linguistic acts that we will examine below, result from (and are therefore derivative phenomena of) the power of declarations does not mean that in their concrete operation they cannot be recognized as different linguistic acts, distinct from the very declarations that make them possible.

A common mistake made when comparing assertions with statements is to assume that, given the transformative power of the latter, assertions are unimportant. Furthermore, that assertions are unrelated to our ability to intervene and transform the world. This is wrong. We can only intervene in the world that we are able to recognize, and our capacity for observation is decisive for the proper exercise of our capacity for intervention. My assessments of what is possible in the world in terms of my actions are based on the statements I can make about it.

Let's look at an example. Someone tells me, "The stock market has just experienced a 100-point drop." This is obviously a statement. But based on the fact that I know this, there are many actions I can take (sell, buy, etc.) that I would not have considered if I had not had access to that statement. Similarly, to give another extreme example, if someone tells me, "The house is on fire!" I probably wouldn't think to flee or try to save some things if this statement had not been made.

At the same time, many statements may have no significance in terms of my possibilities for action. Therefore, not every statement is equally important to me. This leads us to a second distinction regarding statements. Earlier, we said that they could be distinguished as true or false. Now we can establish that we can also distinguish between relevant and irrelevant statements, depending on their relationship to our concerns. Knowing the weather forecast for tomorrow in Sudan will probably be irrelevant if I am going to be in Bogotá all day tomorrow. However, it is not the same if I have to travel to Sudan tomorrow and the purpose of my trip depends greatly on the weather. An important skill in life is knowing how to distinguish between relevant and irrelevant statements and how to generate the former.

Statements reflect the world we live in and, therefore, our ability to make statements speaks to the size and character of our world. A provincial person is characterized by a very limited ability to make statements and, consequently, by a very limited world. The concept of world, in this sense, is not geographical. It is an existential distinction and is related, among other things, to our ability to make statements about events. In order to develop the topic of the relevance of our statements in greater depth, however, it is necessary to introduce two additional distinctions. These are the distinctions between concerns and judgments.


 

The distinction of "concern"

At this point, it seems appropriate to introduce the distinction of concern [6]. As will be seen later, it occupies a central place in our interpretation. Human beings act, and one of the domains of our action is language.

One way to understand what we call concern is to ask the question, why do we act? or why do we speak? The answers we give to these questions, insofar as they fall within the realm of what psychology would call "motivations," correspond to what is indicated by the distinction of concern. Later on, we will also see why we do not use the terms "motivations," "purposes," or "intentions," which are commonly used in this regard.

We maintain that when human beings act, we are "taking charge" of something. As the philosopher Martin Heidegger pointed out, human existence is, for human beings, a matter that they need to take charge of and, therefore, one that they have to "attend to." We maintain that human beings cannot rest, as other living beings do, in the total innocence of existence, in simply letting it flow.

For us, human beings, existence challenges us, and in order to maintain it, we must often take a position with respect to it and, because of this, we are often compelled to modify the spontaneous course of events. We do the latter through action. Therefore, we conceive of action as an exclusive dimension of human existence. Only in a figurative and radically different sense can we speak of action in relation to other living beings or to the elements and forces of nature.

This condition of existential anguish, peculiar to human beings, is interpreted by the Judeo-Christian tradition as an expression of a fall that arises from God's punishment for "original sin." Human beings pay for the audacity of having wanted to participate in the process of creation. This is symbolized by having given in to the temptation to eat from the tree of good and evil, the tree of values and, ultimately, the tree that nourishes the meaning of life. By eating from the tree of good and evil, human beings lose innocence as a condition of their existence.

This heartbreak, inherent in human existence, is therefore expressed at an even deeper level than that of everyday action, which leads us to worry about our food, shelter, and other needs of this kind. An essential part of taking charge of and caring for our existence is also the imperative to give it meaning.

Human beings require meaning in life as a condition of our existence. This seems to be the other side of the power we have to participate in the process of our own creation. When we are unable to give meaning to life, given that we are active beings, we have the option of ending it. Suicide, as Albert Camus pointed out, seems to be a typically human phenomenon.

All of the above is only conceivable because human beings are linguistic beings. There would be no way to account for this dimension of taking charge of and attending to our existence, nor for the ontological imperative of giving meaning to life, if we were not beings who live in language and if human language did not have the capacity for its own recursivity. Nor could we talk about human action in the way we do, except insofar as we are linguistic beings.

From what we have just said, it seems to us that the most significant thing is the following: human beings act as a way of attending to our existence. The distinction of concern arises, therefore, in response to this need to point out what a particular action takes care of or, as we said earlier, the answer to the question: why do we act?

The distinction of concern therefore presupposes that there is something that leads us to act, to intervene in the course of events and not let them flow spontaneously. It expresses the assumption of a certain dissatisfaction, a certain uneasiness, a certain preoccupation (all of which we call "concern"), which prompts us to act, to "occupy ourselves" with doing. With the distinction of concern, therefore, it is being postulated that actions are not justified in themselves, but insofar as they take care of something. The meaning of human action compels us to transcend the domain of action itself and seek deeper existential roots.

We would like to make some final points before closing this section. The answer to the question, why do we act? and, therefore, what we designate as "concern" will always be a matter of interpretation. Strictly speaking, we never know why we act as we do, although we suspect that certain interpretations may lead us to act in one way and not another. The distinction of restlessness does not provide us with a solid foothold from which action acquires meaning. From the interpretation we are proposing, we have to get used to the fact that we will not find any solid foothold. Every time we think we have found one, we will discover that it dissolves.

This means that we cannot give priority to concern over action either. This has been one of the great errors of rationalism, which assumed that reason precedes action, even though such reason often eludes us. From our perspective, we say that while we accept that certain interpretations lead to certain actions, it is no less effective, we argue, that actions also generate interpretations capable of giving them meaning. The relationship between action and concern can be established in both directions. We live in interpretive worlds.


 

The dance of promises: on requests and offers.

Promises are linguistic acts that differ from assertions or declarations, although they too, like assertions, function within a "declarative space." Promises are, par excellence, those linguistic acts that allow us to coordinate actions with others. When someone makes a promise, he or she commits to another person to perform some action in the future.

When someone promises me that he or she will perform a certain action in the future, I can make commitments and perform actions that would previously have been impossible. If my boss tells me, "I'm going to give you a $700 bonus next Friday," or a coworker tells me, "I'll be at tomorrow's meeting," I can now act on the basis of those promises. In the first case, I can, for example, make plans to go skiing, knowing that I will have the money to do so; or, in the second case, I can promise my client a response to his request because the meeting has been called to discuss his issue.

Promises imply a clear mutual commitment. If I promise something to someone, that person can rely on it and expect me to fulfill the conditions of my promise. This is not only a personal commitment but also a social one. Our communities, as a fundamental condition for social coexistence, are concerned with ensuring that people keep their promises and generally punish those who do not. Much of our social life is based on our ability to make and keep promises. Our extensive contract law is a resource through which we socially reinforce the obligation for people to fulfill their promises. In less formal settings, the breach of a promise gives us the right to make a claim.

Nietzsche once said that human beings are animals that make promises. Promises are constitutive of human existence, as are other linguistic acts. Because of this ability to make promises, we can increase our capacity for action; we can achieve things that would not have been possible without the ability to coordinate our actions with those of others. We need only look around and observe our world to see that much of what we see rests on the ability of human beings to make promises. We realize that our work, our marriage, our education, our political system, and so on, were created because there were people who made promises to others.

The act of making a promise comprises four fundamental elements:

- a speaker

- a listener

- an action to be carried out (i.e., certain conditions for satisfaction),

- a time factor.

A more detailed analysis of promises, however, will show us that other elements are also necessary. We will not deal with them on this occasion.

It is interesting to note that when we make a promise, there are actually two different processes involved: the process of making the promise and the process of fulfilling it. The promise, as a whole, requires both. The first process, that of making a promise, is strictly communicative and therefore linguistic. The second process, that of fulfilling the promise, may or may not be communicative.

Let's look at some examples. It is easy to accept that making promises is something we do in a communicative dance with another person (or even with ourselves). If my promise, for example, is to say hello to someone, the process of fulfilling the promise will also be communicative. If, on the other hand, the promise is to deliver a package to someone, fulfilling it will undoubtedly involve more than just communicative actions. It will require the physical action of delivering the package.

Both processes, however, have their respective end points. And the promise as a whole is completed when the process of fulfillment is completed. It is important not to lose sight of this. These two processes usually take place in different time periods. The process of fulfillment generally begins once the promise has been made. However, the promise as a whole is only completed when its fulfillment has been completed.

Promises, as we said, are linguistic actions that allow us to coordinate actions with each other. For this to happen, it is necessary, both when making the promise and when fulfilling it, that both parties agree on what is being promised.

Promises always involve a conversation between at least two people. This makes them a very special type of linguistic act. An individual can make an assertion or statement in the context of a private conversation with themselves. Even when we say to ourselves, "I promise to start exercising on Friday," we can always turn that promise into a statement such as "I am going to start exercising on Friday." In our internal conversations, apparent promises can always be turned into statements. We cannot distinguish between the two. To make the distinction of a promise, we say that we need an "other," another person who commits to us, or we commit to them, to perform an action.

When we make an assertion or a statement, we assume the existence of someone who is listening. Even in our private conversations, we act simultaneously as the speaker and the listener. If, when conversing with another person, we make an assertion or a statement, the "other" plays their expected role in the conversational dance by simply listening. In the linguistic dance of promises, more is expected of this "other" than merely listening. Both participants in the conversation are expected to go beyond simply listening.

Therefore, making a promise requires at least two people acting together in a conversation. A promise is not a single linguistic act; it involves two linguistic actions, two different linguistic movements. These two linguistic actions can be either the action of offering a promise and accepting it, or alternatively, the action of asking for a promise and accepting it. These are the two fundamental ways of performing the dance of promises.

Let us now take the first process involved—the process of making a promise—and leave the process of fulfillment in abeyance. This first process is not completed when the promise is offered by the speaker, but when it is accepted by the listener. If someone says, "I promise to visit you tomorrow," that person has not yet made a promise. He or she has simply offered one. The other person may well respond, "I'm sorry. I won't be home tomorrow." If this happens, no promise has been made yet. In this example, both parties did not agree to make the promise. A promise is like a buckle: it needs two sides to close.

The same is true of the second process, the one related to the fulfillment of a promise. This is also not closed when the promisor considers that he has fulfilled the conditions of satisfaction that were stipulated when the promise was made. If a waiter brings a dessert and says to the customer, "Here is the blackberry pie you ordered," this is not enough to complete the promise. It is only when the customer examines the conditions of satisfaction of the promise—the blackberry pie—and accepts them as those agreed upon that the fulfillment of the promise is complete. This usually happens when the customer receives the plate and says "Thank you," which is equivalent to a declaration of acceptance.

The fulfillment of a promise, therefore, is only complete when the conditions of satisfaction are met and, again, when the listener declares their satisfaction. Before that happens, it is a pending promise, waiting for the moment when the conditions of satisfaction will be met.

Since making promises requires mutual consent between the parties, we can proceed through two different actions to reach this consent. Both are initial moves by a speaker toward obtaining mutual agreement with their listener and thus being able to make a promise. These two actions are requests and offers. We cannot make promises without requests or offers, and both are opening actions toward the realization of a promise.

Requests and offers differ because they place the concern that will be addressed by the action involved in the eventual fulfillment of the promise, if it is made, on different people. Similarly, the person who will be responsible for fulfilling the promise will be different.

When the process of making a promise begins with a request, we understand that the requested action, if accepted, will be carried out by the listener to satisfy a concern of the speaker. However, when this same process begins with an offer, we understand that the offered action, if accepted, commits the speaker and that it takes care of a possible concern of the listener.

To emphasize, we can say, therefore, that the action of making a promise involves two linguistic movements: a request or offer plus a declaration of acceptance, commonly made by another person.

Requests, as we have pointed out, are linguistic movements to obtain a promise from the listener. A request can be refused, and if this happens, no promise has been made. A request implies an anticipated acceptance of the promised action. The declaration of acceptance of a request completes the action of making the promise. If someone says, "Can you give me a mint?" and the listener responds, "Yes, of course. Take one," we do not expect the first person to say "No, thank you." If the conditions of satisfaction of the requester are clear and if the acceptor agrees to grant them, the declaration of acceptance of the promise is implicitly agreed upon, as a condition of consistency understood in the request. This is one of the commitments we make when we make a request.

Questions are a type of request. In them, the conditions of satisfaction are another linguistic act that addresses a concern of the person asking the question. If I ask someone, "Is it hot outside?" and that person responds, "Yes," the response includes both acceptance of the request and immediate satisfaction of the required conditions of satisfaction.

Offers are conditional promises that depend on the listener's statement of acceptance. When we make an offer, we have not yet promised anything. As with requests, offers can also be refused, and if this happens, no promise has been made. However, if they are accepted, the promise must be fulfilled. Acceptance of the commitment to perform the offered action is taken for granted as part of the consistency of the offer. If we say, "Can I offer you a drink?" and the other party says, "Okay, thank you," we cannot say, without being inconsistent, "I won't give it to you." If this happens, the listener can legitimately make a claim.

Since requests and offers are basically opening moves to obtain promises, they comprise the same basic elements that we have identified in promises. They include a speaker, a listener, some conditions of satisfaction, and a time factor.

Given the importance of promises in our lives, it is worth pausing to examine how competent we are at making them and identifying the types of problems we usually encounter. The four elements mentioned above allow us to refer to the four most important problems.

The first relates to who we identify as the speaker, as they are the ones who initiate the process (as we know, in the dance of promises, both interlocutors participate in the conversation). A common problem relates to people who do not know how to make requests or offers. And here we have two situations. The first relates to people who simply do not ask or offer in certain areas of their lives or under certain circumstances.

There are those who, for example, knowing how to ask within the family, tend not to do so at the office, or with certain friends, or when, for example, they make love. They expect others to discover, almost as if by magic, what concerns or matters to them. They often fall into resentment, blaming others for not keeping promises they never dared to ask for. Not asking not only conditions a certain identity and results in a particular way of being, but it is also a factor that defines the kind of life we can expect. We insist on one of our basic postulates: it is not that, being who we are, we do not ask; rather, not asking makes us who we are and gives us a corresponding way of life. If we start asking where we do not, we transform our way of being.

In the same way, there are also those who do not make offers and, as a result, assume a passive role in presenting themselves as a possibility for others. If these others do not "discover" them, they are doomed to go unnoticed as valuable resources for others. They, therefore, do not take responsibility for making themselves recognized for what they are worth, but remain subject to the accident of discovery by others. Again, this has profound repercussions on the identity and ways of life they can access.

There are also those who believe they are making requests or offers that are not usually heard as such. Some think, for example, that saying they do not like something is equivalent to asking for it to be changed. Obviously, this is not the same thing, and often things will remain as they were, simply because no specific and clear request was made.

From the listener's side of a request or offer, problems can also arise. Particularly when we do not know how to accept offers or refuse requests. How many times, for example, do we say "Yes" to a request that we feel we should not have accepted? What price do we pay in terms of identity, self-esteem, and dignity when we are unable to say "No"? How does this manifest itself in our relationships with others? What consequences does it have in our lives?

Let us now move on to the element related to the committed action and its conditions of satisfaction. We are now faced with a situation in which a request or offer was made and accepted. However, what the speaker understands to have been promised turns out to be different from what the listener understood. Both, however, operate under the assumption that the promise was made and will be fulfilled. Both, therefore, will take action based on that assumption, only to find out later that what they expected to happen will not happen. Those who expect the promised action to be fulfilled will see their expectations frustrated, as will those who discover that what they did to fulfill the promise does not produce the expected satisfaction. What is the cost in productivity, personal well-being, and identity that will result from a situation like this? How often do we face situations like this?

Finally, let us examine what happens when, this time, a promise is made with clear conditions for satisfaction but no clear date for fulfillment. A promise that does not clearly specify the time by which it must be fulfilled is not a promise. Those who expect it to be fulfilled are not in a position to rest assured that the promise will be kept, since it is not known when that might happen. Moreover, since the time of fulfillment is not specified, there is no room for complaint, since it can always be argued that at some point, sooner or later, the promise will be fulfilled. A promise that does not specify the time factor is not binding and, therefore, strictly speaking, cannot be considered a promise. Is it necessary to ask about the consequences of this situation?

When we make a promise, we commit ourselves in two areas: sincerity and competence. Sincerity, in this context, is the judgment we make that the public statements and commitments made by the person who made the promise are consistent with their private statements and commitments. Competence relates to the judgment that the person who made the promise is in a position to effectively carry it out, so as to provide the agreed conditions for satisfaction.

When either of these two factors, sincerity or competence, is lacking, trust is affected. We normally say that we trust someone who has made a promise when we judge that person to be sincere and competent in making it. Distrust arises from our judgment that the person making the promise lacks sincerity and/or competence and that, therefore, we cannot be sure that it will be fulfilled.

These commitments involved in promises give them great power in social life and make them one of the pillars of our ability to coordinate actions.

As we have already seen, one of the main differences between linguistic acts is that they involve different social commitments. These are assumptions we make when we listen to what we say and for which we as speakers are responsible. As we said earlier, when we speak, we are not innocent. We are always responsible for the social commitments implicit in our linguistic acts.


 

FUNDAMENTAL SPEECH ACTS

1. Assertion

2. Declaration

4. Offer + Declaration of acceptance

3. Promise

5. Request + Declaration of acceptance

When I make an affirmation, I commit to the truthfulness of what I affirm. When I make a declaration, I commit to the validity and appropriateness of what is declared. When I make a promise, a request, or an offer, I am committing to the sincerity of the promise involved. And when I commit to fulfilling a promise, I am also committing to having the competence to fulfill the stipulated conditions of satisfaction.

This does not mean that we cannot break our commitments. Of course we can and do. However, this will affect our communication with others, and we must expect varying degrees of sanctions for not fulfilling them.


 

THE GENERATIVE ROLE OF LANGUAGE

We should be able to observe how far we are from our traditional understanding of language. The conception of language as descriptive and passive has been replaced by a different interpretation, which sees language as action and, as such, as a powerful force that generates our human world.

As we pointed out earlier, in our traditional conception, reality came first and then language. In the new conception, language generates reality. Any reality that exists beyond language cannot be spoken of by language. Language can only point in its direction, speak of it without touching it, or, as Wittgenstein recommended, pass over it in silence.

We replace the old descriptive interpretation of language with what we call the generative interpretation. Because language is not passive; because language is action, it constantly generates new realities. We, as human beings, live in linguistic worlds, and our reality is a linguistic reality. We create the world with our linguistic distinctions, with our interpretations and narratives, and with the ability that language gives us to coordinate actions with others.
