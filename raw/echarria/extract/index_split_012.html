ON JUDGMENTS

' [7]


 

The assumption that language describes reality leads us to commonly consider the statement "IBM is a computer company" to be of the same type as "IBM is the most prestigious company in the computer industry." Indeed, they look very similar. From the point of view of their formal structure, both attribute properties to IBM; both seem to be describing IBM. The only difference seems to be one of content: the properties they refer to are different. In one case, we are talking about the property of being "a computer company" and in the other, of being "the most prestigious company in the computer industry."

The same thing happens when we talk about people. We often treat the statements "Isabel is a Venezuelan citizen" and "Isabel is a very efficient executive" as equivalent. We continue to assume that both propositions refer to Isabel's properties or qualities and therefore describe her.

We are not unaware that, from the point of view of content, we normally make a distinction. We tend to say that the first proposition refers to what we call "facts," while the second implies a "value judgment." We thus recognize that the second represents an opinion and that, when it comes to opinions, unlike facts, we cannot expect the same degree of agreement. This difference in content, however, is not profound enough to radically differentiate the way we treat facts, values, or opinions.

For centuries, we have treated these statements in a similar way. We have considered the statement "John is six feet tall" to be equivalent to "John is good." Therefore, we have investigated what is good (or what is fair, wise, beautiful, true, etc.) in the same way that we might investigate what it means to be six feet tall, assuming that when we talk about values we are referring to an objective measure, independent of who is speaking. Many of our conceptions of good and evil, justice, wisdom, beauty, truth, etc., are based precisely on the assumption that we can treat them objectively, independently of the observer making the assertion.

Many of the questions that have preoccupied philosophy derive precisely from the fact that these assertions have not always been differentiated. Bertrand Russell once said that most philosophical problems have their roots in logical or grammatical errors. These are some of them. Ludwig Wittgenstein reiterated a similar idea when he argued that philosophical problems arise when language "goes on vacation." Many metaphysical labyrinths that have confused us for centuries have been caused by a failure to make a clear distinction between these two types of assertions.

It is worth noting that this is not an issue related exclusively to abstract philosophical questions. It permeates our daily lives and is present in all areas of our lives: in our personal relationships, at work, in the way we structure and listen to the news, and so on. We commonly fail to completely separate statements such as "Carolina holds the position of General Manager in our company" from "Carolina is incompetent at leading meetings," or "Carlos was twenty minutes late for Tuesday's meeting" from "Carlos is unreliable," or "David has a red Ford" from "David is a bad driver."

We propose the need to make a clear distinction between these statements. To do so, however, we must abandon the old interpretation that language describes reality. Only by starting from the admission that language is action can we notice the profound difference between them all.

We maintain that these assertions, regardless of their formal similarities, imply two different actions. These are two different linguistic acts, and this difference can only be noticed when we look at language as action.

Why do we say that these are different actions? Because the speaker who makes these assertions is committing to something very different in each case when they say one or the other. We say that speech is not innocent; that every time we speak, we commit ourselves in one way or another to the community in which we speak, and that all speech has practical effectiveness insofar as it changes the world and what is possible. This postulate has allowed us to say that there are five fundamental linguistic acts: assertions, declarations, promises, requests, and offers. To the extent that they imply different social commitments, as well as different forms of intervention, they can be classified as distinct actions.


 

Recap on assertions

If we examine the statements mentioned above, we will recognize that all those we have placed first ("IBM is a computer company," "Isabel is a Venezuelan citizen," "Carolina holds the position of General Manager in our company," "Carlos was twenty minutes late for Tuesday's meeting," "David has a red Ford") are assertions.

And as we stated previously, statements are those linguistic acts in which we describe the way we observe things. The language of statements is a language that is subject to an already existing world. In this case, the world leads and the word follows. The language of statements is the language we use to talk about what happens: it is the language of phenomena or facts.

As we know, statements can be true or false. Statements are, therefore, those linguistic acts by which we commit to providing evidence of what we are saying, if requested. We commit that, if someone was in that place at that time, that person could theoretically corroborate what we are saying. This is what we call a witness. If we say, "The product was shipped to you last Friday," and we are asked, "Why do you say that?" we cannot answer, "Because I say so." When we make a statement, we are expected to be able to provide evidence that what we are saying is true.

Statements operate within a certain space of social consensus. We agree, for example, to talk about time using the same distinctions—years, months, weeks, days, hours, etc.—or to talk about height based on a shared scale of measurement. Saying "six feet tall" is a clear measurement within a given community. But talking about height in terms of feet is not at all clear in communities that use the metric system.

Different communities develop different social consensuses by which they accept something as true or false. These consensuses are binding on all members of the community. Every community creates a consensual "declarative space" in which its members can make statements. These consensuses can be reached by agreement or be the result of tradition.

Typical consensuses are those that can be observed, for example, in scientific or professional communities. Within the scientific community, a statement is accepted as true if it complies with the rules and procedures defined by shared scientific methods. Within the legal community, what is accepted as evidence may vary from country to country or even from state to state within the same country, as is the case in the United States.

Therefore, what is considered true in one community may change according to the prevailing social consensus. What is true or false, therefore, is always a matter of social consensus. As these consensuses change over time, so does what people consider true or false. A statement is always a statement within, and for, a particular community at a given moment in history.

Judgments

If we look at the list of statements used as examples, we see that all those in second place belong to a type of action very different from assertions. Here, the speaker's commitment is not to provide evidence. The formulation of this type of statement does not imply that anyone who was there at the time necessarily agrees with us. Here we accept that one may disagree with what we are saying.

Let's go back to the previous examples. Each of the statements "IBM is the most prestigious company in the computer industry," "Isabel is a very efficient executive," "Juan is good," "Carolina is incompetent at running meetings," "Carlos is not to be trusted," and "David is a bad driver" can be legitimately challenged. There is room for someone to say "I consider Apple to be the most prestigious company in the computer industry" or "Isabel is just an average executive" or "I think Juan is truly despicable," etc. Therefore, the social commitment we make when making these assertions is very different from the one made in statements.

Similarly, the practical effectiveness of the word is very different in each case. The institution of awards offers us a good example of what we are pointing out. When Miss Venezuela is announced as the most beautiful of the contestants and is awarded the title of Miss Universe, when was she the most beautiful? Was it when she was declared as such, naming her Miss Universe? Or was she before this judgment was made? We must recognize that from the point of view of the physical attributes of the contestants, there are no major changes in them before and after the announcement. However, before this announcement was made, Miss Venezuela, being what she was, was not the most beautiful of the contestants. She only becomes so when someone makes the judgment that she is. And as a result of this judgment, the world changes; it changes for those who accept the judgment and obviously changes for Miss Venezuela. From then on, her identity, both for herself and for others, is different. What we have seen in this example is exactly the same as what happens with all those assertions that we have placed in second place.

We call these second assertions judgments. And judgments belong to the class of basic linguistic acts that we have called statements. As we know, statements are very different from assertions. They generate new worlds. Unlike assertions, when we make a statement, words guide and the world follows.

Judgments are like verdicts, as are statements. With them, we create a new reality, a reality that only exists in language. If we did not have language, the reality created by judgments would not exist. Judgments are another important example of the generative capacity of language. They do not describe something that existed before they were formulated. They do not point to qualities, properties, attributes, etc., of a particular subject or object. The reality they generate resides entirely in the interpretation they provide. They are entirely linguistic.

When we say, for example, "This meeting is boring," where does "boring" reside? Let's compare these two statements: "Alejandra is persevering" and "Alejandra has brown hair." Could we say that perseverance belongs to Alejandra in the same way that brown hair belongs to her? What a statement says about someone is different from what a judgment says.

Judgment always lives in the person who makes it. If a community has given someone the authority to make a judgment, it can be considered a valid judgment for that community. However, even when that happens, even if we have given someone authority, we can always have a different opinion. We can bow to that person's judgment. We may even decide to set aside our own judgment for practical reasons. But it will still exist. Judgments do not bind us like statements—there is always room for disagreement.

Judgments are statements, but not every statement is necessarily a judgment. Many statements are made solely by virtue of the authority we confer on others (or ourselves) to make them. Even when people explain to us why they made such statements (and even when certain expected steps precede them), what makes them valid is not the reasons given or the procedures in place. Rather, what makes them valid is the authority that has been conferred on the person making them. In many cases, when asked why we have made certain statements, we could simply say, "Because I can and because I have the power to make them."

We can give various examples of this kind of statement: when an executive hires someone in their company; when they decide to redesign a product; when a judge hands down a sentence; when a referee calls a foul; and so on. In all these cases, what matters is the power to make them. Ultimately, they make these statements because they have the power to do so and they exercise it.

In the United States, there is an anecdote about a well-known baseball umpire that illustrates this point. After the game, the umpire is having a beer with some friends, who praise his performance. One of them says, "Every time there are 'balls' and 'strikes,' he calls them by name." "No," replies a second, "if there are 'balls' and 'strikes,' he just calls them according to how he sees them." The umpire reacts by saying, "You're both wrong. We distinguish 'balls' from 'strikes,' but they don't exist until I name them." This anecdote illustrates that, in the case of statements, "they don't exist" until they are made.

As we recognized earlier, when we make a statement, we commit to its validity. This means that we claim to have the authority to make that statement. Statements, as we know, can be valid or invalid, depending on the power of the person making them. When we declare something, we are implicitly committing to having the authority to do so. The social commitment involved in a statement is therefore very different from that involved in an assertion. This is precisely what distinguishes them as different linguistic acts.

Judgments, as we have said, are a particular type of statement. As with statements, their social effectiveness lies in the authority we have to make them. This authority is most clearly demonstrated when it has been formally granted to someone, as is the case with a judge, an arbitrator, a teacher, a manager, and so on. Very often, however, this authority is granted without any formal act. Children do this with their parents. There is no formal act by which they grant their parents the authority that they exercise over them.

People, however, are constantly making judgments, even when they have not been granted authority.

When they communicate their opinions to others, those who listen to them can always dismiss them, based on the fact that they have not granted the authority to accept those judgments as valid.

If I am walking down the street and someone approaches me and says, "I don't like the way you walk," I will probably respond, "Mind your own business," which is equivalent to saying, "I have not given you the authority to make that judgment." However, if I am in the military and a superior officer approaches me and says the same thing, or if it happens in a ballet class, my response will probably be very different. In this case, it is clear that I have given authority to a senior officer or ballet teacher to make such observations. Therefore, judgments, as with any statement, can be "valid" or "invalid," depending on the authority of the person making them.

Judgments, however, require an additional social commitment that is not necessary for all statements. The commitment is that judgments are "grounded" in a certain tradition. Judgments are therefore not only valid or invalid, depending on the authority granted to the person making them; they are also "grounded" or "ungrounded" according to how they relate to a particular tradition, that is, how they relate to the past.


 

Judgments and the structure of temporality


 

To understand what grounded and ungrounded judgments are, we must first investigate the phenomenon of judgment and examine how it relates to human time. When we make a judgment such as "Miguel is an effective speaker," what are we doing?

First, we are, in the present, issuing a verdict about Miguel. There is, therefore, the act of making the judgment in a particular present. We are declaring that Miguel is a certain way. This present will serve as a dividing line. People who think about Miguel after hearing this opinion may well think differently about him. When judgments are made about people, they contribute to shaping their identity. Judgments are an important component of people's identity. But this does not only happen with people. With our judgments, we affect the identity of companies, countries, and so on.

Second, when we make a judgment, we are referring to the past. To say "Miguel is an effective speaker," we must have heard Miguel speaking on more than one occasion. We must have observed, for example, how people were motivated when he spoke to them.

This is precisely what distinguishes judgments from other statements. When we make certain statements, the implicit social commitment involved is that we have the authority to make them. However, when we make a judgment, in addition to the commitment of authority, people assume that this judgment is based on observations of actions performed in the past.

If you ask me, "Have you seen Miguel speaking?" and I answer "No," people will naturally be suspicious. If you ask, "But have you heard this about Miguel from people who have observed him speaking?" and I answer "No" again, we can anticipate your disapproving response. This shows that when we make a judgment, people understand that we have committed to "grounding" that judgment in actions we have observed in the past. They will assume that I have observed Miguel speaking not once, but probably several times. The "grounding" of judgments has to do with how the past is brought into the present when judgments are made.

Third, judgments also speak about the future. When we make a judgment, we are implying that, based on actions observed in the past, certain actions can be expected in the future. Judgments allow us to anticipate what may happen later. This is one of the functions of judgments, which highlights their importance in life. Through judgments, particularly in the case of "well-founded" judgments, we can enter the future with less uncertainty, with a greater sense of security, knowing what to expect and thus narrowing the range of possible future actions. Judgments help us design our future. They operate like a compass, giving us a sense of direction regarding what to expect in the future. They allow us to anticipate the consequences of our actions or those of others.

If we are responsible for having our company present one of our new products to an audience and we judge that Miguel is an effective speaker, we might be inclined to trust that judgment and have Miguel make the presentation. We would not ask Pedro, whom we judge to be a poor speaker. Judgments allow us to move into the future more effectively.

Sometimes we dismiss our judgments because they seem less solid than statements and because they always involve the possibility of disagreement. There are no true judgments. We hear people say, for example, "But this is just an opinion," as if identifying it as an opinion somehow diminishes its value. We don't realize the important role judgments play in our lives and how useful they are in guiding us toward the future.

That is why we make them all the time. Human beings are incessant generators of judgments. We make them all the time and about practically everything we observe. Every time we encounter something new, we begin to make judgments almost automatically. For example, every time we are introduced to someone, we make countless judgments. Or every time we arrive at a new place. We are like machines constantly making judgments. Nietzsche warns us that one of the distinctive features of human beings is that they are judging animals.

The key to judgment is the future. If we weren't concerned about the future, there would be no need for judgments. Who would care how people behaved in the past? Who would be interested in what happened under similar circumstances? It is when we assume that the past can guide us into the future that we make judgments. We make judgments because the future worries us. We make them because we have learned (we have the judgment) that what has already happened can be used to enlighten us about what is to come.

Because of their strong connection to the past, judgments are, by nature, often extremely conservative. They are based on assumptions that need to be examined with caution. When we make judgments, we are assuming that the past is a good guide to the future. We are assuming that because something happened again and again in the past, it could happen again in the future. We know from experience that this is often a fair assumption. Human life is full of recurrences, of things that happen over and over again.

However, we all know that the past is only one of the factors that must be considered when dealing with the future. Anything that has happened in the past does not necessarily have to happen in the future. Many factors can make the future very different. Moreover, there are two particular circumstances in which we ourselves, through our actions, participate in making the future different—learning and innovation.

Learning allows us to take actions that we could not take in the past. Because of our ability to learn, someone who was a very poor speaker in the past can become a very effective speaker in the future. Our ability to learn therefore allows us to challenge those judgments about ourselves. The possibility of learning also makes us open to revising judgments about others, since we learn from the past and can modify our behavior.

In addition to learning, we also have the ability to invent new actions, design new recurrences, and introduce new practices. We call this ability innovation. It allows us to participate in the creation of the new.

Since the future may be different from the past, we must be open enough to treat our judgments as temporary signals that we will subject to constant review. This ability to regularly reexamine our judgments is a fundamental skill for strategic design. When we talk about strategy, we are referring to a way of thinking about the future and designing our actions that takes into account the fact that the future is generated through interaction with others and that these others can modify their judgments and therefore their actions according to, among other factors, the judgment they have about the judgments we may have about them. Recursion plus recursion plus recursion, in a process that is theoretically open to infinity and where there is always room for one more turn.

Leaders and those who are generally responsible for designing the future know how to take full advantage of judgments to guide themselves amid the uncertainties of the times ahead. At the same time, they must avoid becoming prisoners of their judgments or of the past that those judgments bring with them. They must accept that new situations can arise.

It is valuable to understand how judgments connect the past, present, and future (what we call the structure of temporality). Affirmations, for example, do not usually have the ability to carry the past into the future. Although affirmations may seem stronger as statements, they are less flexible in terms of moving through the structure of temporality. When we say, "Carlos was twenty minutes late for Tuesday's meeting," we have no knowledge of how he will behave in future meetings. However, when we say, "Carlos is unreliable," we are saying something about what can or cannot be expected of him in the future.

Here we find an interesting area of intersection between statements and judgments. Saying "Carlos was twenty minutes late for Tuesday's meeting" is a statement. Saying "Carlos is unreliable" is a judgment. Saying "Carlos will be twenty minutes late for next Tuesday's meeting" is again a statement. It is, indeed, one of what we have called indecisive statements, since we will not be able to corroborate it until Tuesday. We maintain that the latter is a statement and not a declaration because it is a situation in which the word must conform to the world and not the other way around. This is the criterion we have chosen to draw the line between statements and declarations.

However, when we make indecisive statements, it is also appropriate, as with judgments, to ask ourselves what basis leads us to make them. Every prediction is a statement, but we can also use the past to anticipate the future. This obviously does not deny the fact that predictions can be made without any basis in the past. Predictions by fortune tellers or religious prophecies are often made without invoking any basis.

One of the consequences of not distinguishing between statements and judgments is that it would lead us to treat judgments as if they were statements. When we do this, we restrict our possibilities for action and fail to take advantage of what judgments provide.

Let's take an example. When I say "Carolina is incompetent at leading meetings" and we treat this assertion as if it were a statement, we may fail to see that being incompetent at leading meetings is not a quality of Carolina but a verdict we make in language, based on her past actions. By not seeing it as a judgment, we may take it as an immutable quality of Carolina, as solid as a statement. At the same time, we project it into the future, as we normally do with judgments. We assume that this is the way Carolina is and that she will continue to be that way in the future.

What we miss in this example is the connection between judgments and action. We do not see that by changing our actions, we allow judgments about ourselves to change as well. If someone holds the above judgment about Carolina (and even if Carolina herself holds that judgment about herself), we might say, "Why should we even consider Carolina to lead meetings, if we know she is incompetent?" By doing this, we nullify the possibilities for learning and innovation. We have taken the worst of the world of statements and judgments. In doing so, we have transferred the past to the future and eliminated the possibility of changing the past and creating a different reality.

We have argued that not only do we act according to who we are, but we also are according to how we act. We have said that action generates being (second principle of the ontology of language). Furthermore, we have postulated that judgments represent the core of people's identity (we will discuss this topic later). We have just pointed out that judgments are based on past actions. It is therefore reasonable to conclude that to the extent that we modify our actions (as happens, for example, as a result of learning), we modify our identity: we transform our being.


 

How judgments are founded

Having examined the relationship between judgments and the structure of temporality, we are now in a position to examine how we base our judgments. We call the way in which the past can be used to formulate judgments that effectively support us in dealing with the future the foundation. Foundations, therefore, connect the three instances of the structure of temporality: past, present, and future.

We have said that the future is the key to judgments. We formulate judgments because of our concern for the future. We will therefore begin by examining the effects of the future on the foundations of our judgments.

We divide the process of forming a judgment into five basic conditions:

1. We always make a judgment "for or about something." We always envision a future in which our judgment will open or close possibilities. Depending on the judgment we make, some actions will be possible, others will not. When we make judgments about behavior, such as when we say "Carlos is not to be trusted" or "Isabel is a very efficient executive," we do so because of an action we anticipate in the future. This action gives meaning to the judgment.

The "for or about something" is an essential dimension of judgments. If we say, for example, "David is a bad driver," the judgment will be very different if the action we are imagining is the Indianapolis 400 or delivering Domino's pizzas.


 

2. Every time we make a judgment, we are assuming that it is being compared to a set of behavioral standards for judging the performance of individuals, which allow us to evaluate the effectiveness of their actions. One person may say "Barbara is a fast reader" and another that she is "a slow reader" not only because their observations of Barbara are different, but also because the standards by which they make their judgments are different.

However, we do not only evaluate people's actions and behavior. We also judge their appearance, the days of the week, the height of mountains, and so on. These are not judgments of behavior. We can say, for example, "Nicholas is thin," "Monday was a beautiful day," "We are standing in front of some huge mountains." All of these are judgments. The standards used to make these judgments come from particular traditions that tell us what to expect and, therefore, from social expectations. If Nicolas' weight is lower than what is normally expected, we will make the judgment that he is "thin." If we are not used to seeing mountains around us, we will judge that the ones we are seeing at that moment are huge, while other people who are used to seeing taller mountains will find them quite low. The consideration of a "nice day" can be very different for people living in different climates.

As we make judgments in relation to certain standards, a polarity is often established. If we are faced with a distinction for which we can produce the exact opposite, we can suspect that we are faced with a judgment. Distinctions such as good and bad, fast and slow, competent and incompetent, friendly and surly, effective and ineffective, beautiful and ugly, and so on, are all used to make judgments.

Judgments are historical, since the standards we use to make them change over time. What we considered a fast car has changed over time. People involved in the world of sports are accustomed to seeing how standards change—for example, how behavior considered outstanding in the past often becomes only good or even average a few years later. The same is true of aesthetic judgments. The standards used to consider something beautiful have changed significantly throughout history. In the world of fashion, we see how standards sometimes change in just a few months.

Although it is often not perceived, most standards are social. We generally assume that since we are the ones making the judgments, we do so according to our own standards. In a sense, this is true—we make them according to standards that we possess. What we commonly fail to see is that these standards were not produced by us but belong to the community and correspond to specific historical circumstances. When we judge someone to be arrogant, or something to be exciting, we assume that it is we who are speaking. We are. But behind us, our community and our tradition are also speaking.

We have said that "most" standards are social. By this we mean to acknowledge that sometimes certain individuals rise above or fall below the standards maintained by their communities and contained in their traditions. They bring with them standards that were not available in their community. This is what leaders and innovators often do. Napoleon, for example, is well known for introducing new standards in military action. Many innovations are generated simply by examining existing standards and exploring the possibility of establishing new ones.


 

3. When we make a judgment, we generally do so within a particular domain of observation. When we evaluate behaviors, these domains of observation correspond to domains of action. When we do not evaluate behavior (such as "This is a powerful offer" or "This is the most beautiful painting in the exhibition"), we are talking only about domains of observation.

We speak of domains when we can identify stable areas of interest in which we specify the possibility of recurring breaks. The distinction of domain does not, therefore, point to an existing entity. We do not see domains around us. They are social consensuses or conventions that we adopt because we believe they help us act more effectively. This allows us to talk about domains such as "driving vehicles," "running meetings," "family," "work," and so on.

When we make a judgment, what we normally do is render a verdict based on certain observations. This judgment is limited to the particular domain in which the observations were made. They determine whether the judgment is more or less well-founded, but it is always limited to the particular domain of observation.

If, for example, someone systematically breaks their promises to pay us back money we have lent them, we have good reason to say that this person is unreliable in the domain of money. However, we often extend our judgments beyond our domain of observation. Based on the judgment that someone is untrustworthy in matters of money, we may assume that that person is unreliable in relation to their work or family responsibilities. The generalized judgment is unfounded even if the judgment in the domain of money is well founded. Therefore, an important factor to consider when making judgments is to confine them strictly to the domain of observation from which they were made.


 

4. Judgments are grounded by providing statements about what we are judging. When we have statements that allow us to measure against some standard in a particular domain of observation, we can generate a judgment.

Statements, therefore, play an important role in the process of grounding our judgments. If we are unable to provide statements, we cannot ground our judgments. When asked why we say "Isabel is an efficient executive" and we respond "Well, because she has a strong sense of leadership and has brought about very positive changes in the company," what we have done so far is to exchange one judgment for another. We have not yet established the first one.

On the contrary, if we are asked why we say "Catalina is very competent at leading meetings" and we respond: "In the last five meetings she has led, all the items on the agenda were addressed, as shown in the meeting reports. This has never happened in the past" or "Since she has been in charge of leading these meetings, her department has been spending less time in meetings and receiving fewer complaints from its customers," or "Roberto, Tina, and Pablo, who also participate in these meetings, have informed me that they all want Catalina to continue leading them," we may want to know more about these meetings before endorsing the initial judgment. but we must recognize that it sounds different when we introduce statements to support our judgments than when we add new judgments about them. By introducing statements, we build confidence in that judgment. This is an important factor in the skill of supporting judgments.

Depending on the judgment we make, more or fewer statements will be needed to support it. If we say "Pamela is thin" and add "She weighs 15 pounds less than the average weight for her height and age," that statement should be sufficient to support the judgment. However, when we base judgments on behavior, pointing to a single instance and relying on a single statement may not be enough. If I say, "You can't count on Alberto to be on time for appointments," and when asked why, I reply, "The last time we met, he was fifteen minutes late," some might think that this is not enough to take my judgment very seriously.

5. The number of statements we are able to provide to support a judgment does not guarantee that we consider it well-founded. We may generate even more statements when trying to support the opposite judgment. For this reason, we ultimately recommend reviewing the basis for the opposite judgment when supporting a particular judgment.

For example, if we want to base the judgment "Pedro is boring in meetings," we should also examine the basis for the judgment "Pedro is not boring in meetings." We may well discover that, although we can produce several instances (statements) in which Pedro has been boring in meetings, there have been many more instances in which he has been quite entertaining. Can we reasonably say that the judgment "Pedro is boring in meetings" was well-founded? No, we cannot. This was not a well-founded judgment.

We often consider a judgment about ourselves or others to be well-founded (based on observations made in a given number of instances), only to realize later that there were many more instances pointing to the opposite judgment.

To recap, then, we can say that the following conditions are required to make a judgment well-founded:

1. the action we project into the future when we make it,

2. the standards held in relation to the projected future action,

3. the domain of observation within which the judgment is made,

4. the assertions we provide regarding the standards held, and finally,

5. the fact that we find insufficient grounds to support the contrary judgment.

We call judgments that do not satisfy these five conditions "unfounded" judgments.

What makes judgments different from assertions, that is, what makes them different actions, are the social commitments that both imply. When we make an assertion, we commit to providing evidence. This means that if someone brings a witness, that person will agree with what we say. When we make a judgment, we commit, first, to having the authority to make that judgment and, second, to providing grounds for that judgment.


 

The double-sided nature of judgments

Every action reveals the type of being that performs it. This is what the second principle of the ontology of language recognizes when it begins by emphasizing that "we act according to how we are." As long as this does not lead us to close off the possibility of transformation of being, of the process of becoming, it is important to establish this first relationship between action and being.

Therefore, every time we say something (insofar as speaking is acting), we reveal who we are in some way. Even when we try to deceive others about who we are, to the extent that the effort to deceive is manifested, we reveal the kind of person we are and what leads us to try to deceive others about who we are.

This ability of language to reveal the speaker's being, which is valid for all linguistic actions, is particularly characteristic when we examine judgments. We commonly think that when we make a judgment, we are only judging what the judgment is about. We do not always perceive how much of ourselves is revealed when we make it. Precisely because a judgment is not a description of our observations of facts or phenomena, because what the judgment says is not "out there," there is no other linguistic act that allows us, as judgments do, to reveal our soul (our way of being) in greater depth.

Judgment, we argue, has two sides. It is like the god Janus. One face looks toward the world, the other looks toward the being that we are. If someone says, for example, "Foreigners are dangerous," I will not know much about foreigners, but I will know something about the person who makes that judgment. Similarly, if someone says, "The sky is wonderful," I will know something about the sky, but I will know much more about the person who has that judgment. We insist that, whether we are aware of it or not, judgments always speak about those who make them. A fundamental aspect of the discipline of "ontological coaching" is learning to treat the judgments that people make as windows into the human soul.

The great precursor of this profound insight into the human soul was Friedrich Nietzsche. For Nietzsche, every thought deserves to be examined not only in itself for what it involves as such. Nietzsche never forgets that every idea is always expressed by someone who, in expressing it, reveals who they are. And in particular, they reveal the emotions from which such ideas are expressed. In one of his most beautiful paragraphs—part of a self-critical effort directed at one of his earliest works, The Birth of Tragedy—examining the relationship between the eager search for truth, characteristic of the metaphysical program, and life, Nietzsche writes: "Is the determination to be so scientific about everything a kind of fear or a form of escape from pessimism? A subtle last resort against... the truth? And in moral terms, a kind of cowardice and falsehood? In amoral terms, a ruse? Oh Socrates, Socrates, was that perhaps your secret? Oh enigmatic ironist, was that perhaps your... irony?"

Judgments and the mastery of ethics

We previously argued that one of the most important functions of judgments was their ability to guide our actions toward the future. Judgments allow us to reduce the uncertainty with which we inevitably enter the gates of temporality. But that is not the end of their important role in human life.

Human beings, we have argued, cannot live without giving meaning to existence. This is our basic condition of existential anguish. Once thrown into life, we cannot just let ourselves be carried away by it, like someone who lets themselves be carried away by the current of a river. To live it, we have to give it meaning.

As we pointed out earlier, one of the features of our current historical phase is the great crisis of meaning that human beings face. This is what Nietzsche calls "nihilism," which he perceives as the great challenge of his philosophy to overcome.

Well, we define ethics as the field in which we take a position on the meaning of life and where we generate, in the words of Ludwig Wittgenstein, "that which makes life worth living, or the right way to live." [9] Ethics therefore appears to be related to the world of values (i.e., to what has value in life), as well as to the distinction we make between good and evil and the way we conceive of what it means to "live well," that "right way of living," or living with meaning.

The world of values, which in our definition corresponds to the domain of ethics, is one of Nietzsche's main concerns, which he identifies with the sphere of morality. For Nietzsche, human beings are moral beings par excellence, beings who cannot do without the imperative to give meaning to their lives and, therefore, to give them value. For Nietzsche, human existence represents a permanent moral challenge, a challenge to define what is or is not given value and, consequently, what is or is not capable of giving meaning to life.

Well, it is in the realm of judgments that human beings wage the battle for the meaning of life. It is at the level of judgments that the meaning or meaninglessness of existence is defined. Hence Nietzsche warns us that without evaluations and, therefore, without the ability to make judgments, the core of existence would be empty.

Judgments provide human beings not only with certain basic parameters through which existence will pass (defining what is just, beautiful, true, good, etc., as well as all their opposites). They also provide the direction from which individuals transform themselves and enter the future.

From this perspective, it is difficult to find anything that possesses the degree of importance that judgments have in human life. Judgments represent the fundamental core of human existence. They compromise life itself. Affirmations, with all their pretensions of being capable of touching the truth, only serve judgments, particularly those that constitute us as the human beings we are and from which we sustain our lives.

It is interesting to note that the subject of virtues and vices, which for a long time was the predominant way of addressing the concern for "good living," rests entirely on the subject of judgments. When we speak of virtues, we are referring to those judgments that, within a particular community, are selected as those that ensure the best coexistence among its members, as well as the moral ideals associated with human beings. Vices, on the other hand, typify the opposite: a class of actions that contravene proper social coexistence and are associated with the degradation of life.


 

Judgments and ways of being

From the above, some aspects related to judgments arise that have a direct impact on each person's personal life and, particularly, on our ways of being. We will refer specifically to three particular dimensions that we will address below.

The first refers to those people who are characterized by living according to the judgments of others and who, therefore, do not constitute the center of the judgments that govern their own existence. This defines what we call the condition of inauthenticity. Those who live in this condition delegate to others the authority to make the judgments that matter to them. Nothing makes them happier than receiving a positive judgment from others. Nothing depresses them more than receiving negative judgments.

The logic of their actions is therefore fundamentally oriented toward pleasing others, who acquire, almost indiscriminately, the authority to make judgments that obviously affect the inauthentic person. Their lives, therefore, become directed by forces they do not control and that are the result of the various judgments they receive.

Given that it is inherent in judgments that they may be discrepant on the same issues, living in inauthenticity often translates into a permanent condemnation to suffering, as it is impossible to satisfy everyone around them. A single negative judgment is enough to affect the emotional stability of the inauthentic person.

The second dimension we would like to mention is that of treating judgments as statements, without distinguishing between the two. For those who operate in this way, the consequences are often rigidity/intolerance and the closure of multiple learning possibilities. For them, judgments do not represent the possibility of differing points of view and even greater diversity and enrichment. A different judgment is treated as an error, as falsehood. The legitimate space for disagreement is transformed into a potential space for confrontation. To the extent that I consider my judgments to be true and those of others to be false, I relegate others to the realm of evil or even the diabolical. We have created the ground for fundamentalism and intolerance.

By treating judgments as statements, I also close off space for transformation. I tend to treat judgments as permanent traits. I shut down the possibilities for learning and, therefore, restrict the plasticity of life. We live within what we call a metaphysical attitude toward existence.

The third dimension refers to those who live without being able to distinguish between well-founded judgments and unfounded judgments. The consequences are permanent disappointment with regard to their expectations and great difficulty in designing the future. They fail to understand why things do not turn out the way they want them to and compare themselves to others without understanding why success is so elusive to them.

They live in magical interpretations and life is generally a mystery to them. Their own inability to make well-founded judgments can lead them to resentment, since they experience both the successes of others and their own failures as injustices. When they do not fall into resentment, they fall instead into euphoria or fictitious optimism. The inability to form judgments translates into an unfounded way of life.

Beyond good and evil


 

If we understand authenticity as the condition of living by one's own judgments, of becoming the one who sets the standard for one's own actions, it is not enough to simply avoid letting life be governed by the judgments of others. It is not enough to determine who issued the judgment that governs our behavior. Issuing a judgment does not ensure belonging. The important thing is to establish to whom the judgment really belongs. It is not enough for me to be the one who issues a judgment if, in doing so, I am only uncritically endorsing judgments that I have found available within my community.

Most human beings, even when they are the ones who make the judgments that define their actions, tend to do nothing more than repeat the judgments they find at hand, without examining them critically, without "judging the judgment" they pronounce.

Our spontaneous judgments are inauthentic. They are made within the multiple automatisms that we carry as social beings. It is surprising, however, how much authority we confer on our spontaneous judgments to resolve fundamental issues in our lives. And although we may experience them as our own judgments, they do not really belong to us. Rather, such judgments possess us. To the extent that they govern our behavior, we are still captive to the condition of inauthenticity. We are not yet truly free human beings, nor are we masters of our own lives.

The human being who manages to access their full potential for freedom is the one who subjects their existence to the rigor of authenticity, who learns to judge judgments, to evaluate evaluations, to examine the values at hand. This forces them, therefore, to transcend many of the inherited forms that demarcate good and evil and to take responsibility for creating that demarcation anew for themselves. In some cases, the new demarcations may coincide with the old ones; in others, they will involve new lines, new distinctions, the invention of new values. Whatever the case may be, the free human being is one who has subjected their values to critical judgment and can conclude that their judgments belong to them and not them to their judgments.


 

Judgments and human suffering

Judgments are the root of human suffering. All suffering is contained in linguistic packaging, and central to this is the role of judgments. To better understand what we mean, it is important to distinguish the phenomenon of pain from the phenomenon of suffering.

By pain, we mean a phenomenon that has fundamentally biological roots and affects our nervous system. If I put my hand in the fire, I will feel pain. This experience can be explained entirely within the dynamics of the nervous system.

By suffering, we mean something different. Here we are no longer dealing with a phenomenon that I can circumscribe to the domain of biology, although biology will be affected by it, as it always is by everything that happens to us. When we speak of suffering, our tradition alludes to a kind of pain of the soul. And we do not find this objectionable, as long as we are willing to recognize that what we have called the soul for centuries is a phenomenon of a linguistic nature.

Suffering, unlike pain, arises from the interpretations we make of what happens to us and, more particularly, from the judgments on which those interpretations are based. Let's take a few examples. When someone close to us dies or when a relationship that matters to us ends, what happens to us is completely different from the experience of being punched in the stomach or burning our hand. What we feel then and identify as suffering does not have its source in my biology—although, we insist, it will be compromised—but in the judgment I make about the meaning of these events.

I can interpret the two examples given as a loss, as a closing of possibilities in my life. And in doing so, I am making judgments. Both events, in themselves, do not necessarily entail suffering. We can well imagine different circumstances in which, as a result of someone's death and the end of a relationship, what is generated may be peace and relief.

If suffering, therefore, rests on the judgments I make about what happens, it means that there is a huge field of intervention for treating human suffering. By modifying the judgments I make about what happens to us, we can find an effective mechanism to relieve ourselves of suffering.

What we are pointing out is not profoundly original. What we are saying has been recognized for many centuries, particularly by the Stoic philosophers. Among them, we should highlight the figure of Epictetus, a Greek slave who lived in the second half of the first century and early second century AD, during the Roman era, and with whom Emperor Hadrian had long conversations.

Epictetus' teachings have come down to us through the compilation made by his disciple, Flavius Arrianus. Among Epictetus' recommendations, the following stands out: "It is not what has happened that upsets a man, since the same thing may not upset another. It is his judgment of what has happened." In a different context, Epictetus points in a similar direction: "Do not forget that it is not the man who insults you or strikes you who debases you, but your own judgment that this man insults you. Therefore, when someone irritates you, be sure that it is your own opinion that has irritated you. And commit yourself not to be carried away by external impressions, since once you gain time and postpone your reaction, you will more easily become the master of yourself."

Allow us to make two caveats before closing this section. First, we are not arguing that it is necessary—or even possible—to eliminate all forms of human suffering. Suffering plays a decisive role in human existence. But it is also true that not all forms of suffering play a positive role in life, and there is much suffering that would be better eradicated. Second, in insisting on the linguistic nature of human suffering, we do not mean to imply that we should not pay attention to the other primary domains of human existence that are not linguistic: the physical and the emotional. Both are significantly involved. We cannot deny the emotional dimension of suffering, just as we cannot ignore the fact that our judgments about what happens may be conditioned by biological factors. But to the extent that we accept that judgments represent the substrate of all forms of suffering, we must necessarily give priority to the domain of language.


 

A matter of trust

At the end of this section on human speech, let us take a look back and, from the perspective of judgments, reexamine some of the things already said.

When discussing different linguistic acts, we argued that one way to differentiate between them was to examine the different social commitments we made when performing each one. We argued, therefore, that when we make a statement, we commit ourselves to the truthfulness and relevance of what we say. That when we make a declaration, we commit ourselves to making our subsequent behavior consistent with what we have declared, as well as to the validity of what we declare. That when we make a judgment, in addition to committing ourselves, as in any declaration, to its consistency and validity, we commit ourselves to the judgment being well-founded. When talking about promises, requests, and offers, we argued that we commit ourselves to the sincerity of what we promise or are going to promise, as well as to our ability to carry out what we promise.

Well, all these conditions, all these commitments involved in speaking, involve judgments that we make in each case. When we speak of true or false, relevant or irrelevant, valid or invalid, consistent or inconsistent, well-founded or unfounded, sincere or insincere, competent or incompetent, we are making judgments about the speaker or what they say. We are using language to judge speech. It seems that we have no way of breaking the chains of language. We can only go round and round inside it an indeterminate number of times. There is no way out of the labyrinth of language.

All these judgments lay the foundation for a judgment that is the cornerstone of all forms of coexistence with others: the judgment of trust. Without trust, I have no chance of building a stable relationship with others. Without trust, relationships with our partners, our parents, our children, our coworkers, our business associates, our teachers, and so on are undermined. No human relationship can develop properly when there is no trust.

Well, this condition, which is essential to our lives and the possibilities within them, results from a judgment we make about others (and that others, in turn, make about us). Given the recursive capacity of language, we can even talk about self-confidence, or the confidence we have in ourselves. As an expression of judgment, trust is a strictly linguistic phenomenon. It is only insofar as we are linguistic beings and, in that capacity, beings capable of making judgments, that trust as a phenomenon is constituted.

If we accept that trust is constituted on the basis of a judgment, we must ask ourselves what kind of judgment we are referring to. To explore this question, it may be useful to start with those particular judgments that were related to the commitments we made when speaking, when performing different linguistic acts. For if we see all these judgments as associated with the phenomenon of trust, they may well clear the way for us to reach a unified understanding of trust as a phenomenon.

Let us therefore briefly reexamine the different linguistic acts. Let us look at assertions. Will someone who is known for making false assertions inspire our trust? Will we be willing to take action based on assertions made by such a person? Can we therefore argue that there is a relationship between making untrue assertions and trust? Similarly, will we trust someone who makes assertions that are irrelevant to what we consider relevant?

Let us now examine declarations. Will we trust someone who behaves in a way that is inconsistent with what they declare? Or someone who makes declarations on matters for which they have no authority? Do we see any relationship between such behaviors and trust? And in the particular case of judgments, will we trust someone who is known for not basing their judgments on evidence? For example, will we follow their advice?

Let us now turn to the cluster of linguistic acts that form promises, requests, and offers. Can we trust someone who makes promises without being sincere, without intending to fulfill what they promised? Will we be willing to rely on such promises? And what will happen, in terms of trust, if we find that someone promises to do something for which they are not competent? Will we put ourselves in their hands?

In addition to the domains of sincerity and competence, there is a third domain in which trust is also compromised when we make promises. This is the domain we call reliability. We speak of reliability in relation to someone's general competence, not in terms of fulfilling what they specifically promised, but simply in terms of keeping their promises. These are people who may have been sincere when they made a promise and who have the competence to do what they promised. However, since there is usually a time lag between the moment the promise is made and the moment it is fulfilled, sincerity does not guarantee fulfillment, and these people—for a variety of reasons—turn out to have a history of non-fulfillment. Will we trust someone like that?

Trust, therefore, is a judgment that is compromised in each and every linguistic act we perform. Depending on how we perform in them, others will have more or less trust in us. Depending on how others perform, we will have more or less trust in them. Our impeccability in respecting the commitments involved in each linguistic act is the basis that allows us to build the trust that others have in us.

But can we say that behind the respect for the different commitments involved in each linguistic act there is a single phenomenon that is revealed in these particular commitments? In other words, are all these particular cases of the same general phenomenon? And if so, what is this phenomenon?

Our answer is yes. We believe that all these cases are expressions of the same phenomenon. Respect for each of these particular commitments is an expression of something global that comes into play in each of these cases. We are referring to respect for others, the respect that we show each other as people in social coexistence. Humberto Maturana calls this love. We prefer the term respect. We are, however, talking about the same thing.


 

Towards an ethic based on respect

What is respect? It is a phenomenon that, as with various judgments, we can distinguish in two different domains of human existence: in the domain of language and in the emotional domain. For now, we will limit ourselves to what relates to respect as a phenomenon of the domain of language.

As a linguistic phenomenon, we say that respect is the judgment of acceptance of the other as a being different from myself, legitimate in their way of being and autonomous in their capacity to act. It therefore implies the acceptance of the difference, legitimacy, and autonomy of the other in our coexistence. It therefore implies a willingness to grant the other a space of full and reciprocal legitimacy for the pursuit of their concerns.

When, based on particular instances linked to the ethics of the different commitments that result from different linguistic acts, we manage to situate ourselves in the unitary phenomenon associated with mutual respect, we find that trust is not only manifested as a judgment we make with respect to the actions of speech, but also in every form of behavior in which we enter into relationship with others.

Trust, we insist, is constituted as a judgment and is, therefore, a strictly linguistic phenomenon. But the judgment that constitutes trust can be made both about linguistic actions and about any other human action. Let's look at an example. If I am walking down the street at night and I see someone approaching me in a manner that I can describe (in my "judgment") as suspicious, I can say that I do not trust that person and, based on that, choose to cross to the other side of the street, which is better lit and has more people.

That person who seemed suspicious to me didn't even open their mouth, and I had already made my judgment of distrust. They had not made any statements that I could consider false, no invalid declarations, no promises. My judgment of distrust is based simply on the assumption that such a person might not recognize me as someone who occupies a legitimate space in our mutual coexistence and who might, as a result, violate my integrity as a person.

This brings us to a fundamental point in the ontology of language. It is the recognition that the ontology of language is based on a certain ethic of coexistence, founded on mutual respect. Mutual respect, as Maturana points out, is not only a precondition for language itself, but for all forms of social coexistence, from which language itself emerges.

Once we understand the impossibility of completely separating the sphere of ideas from the world of life, once we accept that human beings are moral beings, beings who make judgments and, in doing so, generate values and give meaning to their existence, once we have accepted the above, we cannot isolate what we hold from the domain of ethics.
