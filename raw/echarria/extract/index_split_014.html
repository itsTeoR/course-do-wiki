LISTENING: THE HIDDEN SIDE OF LANGUAGE

[10]

Human communication has two facets: speaking and listening. It is generally thought that speaking is more important, as it seems to be the active side of communication, while listening is often considered passive. It is assumed that if someone speaks well enough (loud and clear), they will be heard well. Based on this interpretation, listening is generally taken for granted and rarely examined as a problematic issue.

However, a new common sense about the importance of listening is emerging. People are beginning to accept that they are poor listeners. They recognize that they often find it difficult to hear what others are saying and that they have difficulty making themselves heard in the way they would like. This phenomenon occurs in all areas of our lives.

For example, the issue of listening has become a major concern in our personal relationships. It is common to hear the complaint, "My partner doesn't listen to me." Without a doubt, ineffective communication is one of the leading causes of divorce. When people talk about "incompatibility" with their partner, it is listening, once again, that is at the center of their concerns.

In the business world, effective listening has become a top priority. Peter Drucker wrote in a recent book:

"Too many (executives) think they are wonderful with people because they talk well. They don't realize that being wonderful with people means 'listening' well."[11]

Tom Peters emphasizes that one of the main reasons for the poor performance of American management is the fact that managers do not listen to their employees, their customers, or what is happening in the market. Peters recommends "becoming obsessed with listening."[12] The problem, of course, lies in how to do this. What does it mean to know how to listen?

We maintain that as long as we hold on to our traditional concept of language and communication, we will find it difficult to grasp the phenomenon of listening. Furthermore, we will not be able to develop the skills required to listen more effectively.


 

Listening as a determining factor in human communication

If we examine communication closely, we realize that it rests primarily not on speaking but on listening. Listening is the fundamental factor of language. We speak to be heard.

Effective speaking is only achieved when it is followed by effective listening. Listening validates speaking. It is listening, not speaking, that gives meaning to what we say. Therefore, listening is what drives the entire communication process.

It is surprising to realize how little attention we have paid to the phenomenon of listening. If we look for literature on this subject, we will find that it is very scarce. The few things that have been written are generally of dubious quality. For centuries we have taken listening for granted. We normally assume that in order to listen to other people we only have to expose ourselves to what they say—we must be with them, talk to them, ask them questions. We assume that by doing this, listening will simply happen. We are not saying that this is not important or necessary. What we are saying is that it is not enough.


 

The fallacy of information transmission

The prevailing understanding of communication today is based on the notion of information transmission. This is a notion inherited from communication engineering and developed by C. Shannon, among others. It deals with communication between machines—that is, between a transmitter and a receiver (as in radio transmission processes). This framework, despite its usefulness in technical transmission issues, proves deficient when used to understand human communication. The notion of information transmission hides, precisely, the problematic nature of human listening.

This happens for at least two reasons. First, because it says nothing about one of the main aspects of human communication—the question of meaning. (We will return to this topic later.) For now, let's just say that when a machine sends information to another machine to, for example, reproduce a sound or image, or execute a command, the meaning of the message sent is irrelevant. We can speak of successful communication as long as our television screen obtains a clear and stable image of what is happening in the studio. We do not ask ourselves whether the image received makes sense to the television.

When we deal with human communication, the issue of meaning becomes paramount. We cannot address it without considering how people understand what is said to them. The way we make sense of what is said is constitutive of human communication. It is also a fundamental aspect of the act of listening. The notion of information transmission only operates as a metaphor when used in human communication. However, it is a poor metaphor, distorting the phenomenon it purports to reveal.

Second, our traditional approach to human communication assumes that human beings communicate with each other in an instructive manner. Instructive communication occurs when the receiver is able to reproduce the information being transmitted to them. But human beings, as biologist Humberto Maturana has argued, do not have the biological mechanisms necessary for the process of information transmission to occur in the way described by communication engineering. Human beings, like all living beings, are closed systems. They are "structurally determined units." This means that what happens to them in their communicative interactions is determined by their own structure and not by the disturbing agent.

Humans do not possess a biological mechanism that allows them to "reproduce" or "represent" what is "really" happening in their environment. We do not have a biological mechanism that allows us to say that our sensory experience (seeing, hearing, smelling, tasting, touching) "reproduces" what is "out there."

We do not see the colors that are out there; we only see the colors that our sensory and nervous systems allow us to see. Similarly, we do not hear the sounds that exist in the environment independently of us. The sounds we hear are those predetermined by our biological structure. Disturbances in the environment only select predetermined reactions from our structure. Environmental disturbances only "trigger" our responses within the space of possibilities that our human structure allows.

We can therefore point out that there is a "critical gap" in communication between saying (or speaking) and listening. As Maturana says: "The phenomenon of communication does not depend on what is delivered, but on what happens to the receiver. And this is a very different matter from 'transmitting information.'"

We can conclude, then, that we say what we say and others hear what they hear; saying and listening are different phenomena.

This is a crucial point. We normally take it for granted that what we hear is what has been said, and we assume that what we say is what people will hear. We commonly do not even bother to check whether the meaning we give to what we hear corresponds to that given by the speaker. Most of the problems we face in communication arise from the fact that people do not realize that listening differs from speaking. And when what has been said is not heard as expected, people fill this "critical gap" with personal stories and judgments about what other people are like, creating even deeper problems in communication.


 

Listening is not hearing

So far, we have differentiated between speaking and listening. Now we need to differentiate between hearing and listening. Hearing is a biological phenomenon. It is associated with the ability to distinguish sounds in our interactions with an environment (which may be another person). Hearing is the biological ability that some living species possess to be triggered by environmental disturbances in such a way that they generate the sensory domain called sound.

Certain environmental disturbances generate, in some organisms, what we call the phenomenon of hearing. And these same disturbances may not generate it in other organisms. We know, for example, that dogs hear some disturbances that humans do not hear. This is because they have a different biological structure. Organisms belonging to the same species share the same biological structure and are normally triggered in a similar way by the same disturbance.

Listening is a totally different phenomenon. Although its root is biological and rests on the phenomenon of hearing, listening is not hearing. Listening belongs to the domain of language and is constituted in our social interactions with others.

What differentiates listening from hearing is the fact that when we listen, we generate an interpretive world. The act of listening always involves understanding and, therefore, interpretation. When we attribute an interpretation to a sound, we move from the phenomenon of hearing to the phenomenon of listening. Listening is hearing plus interpreting. There is no listening without an interpretive activity involved. Herein lies the active aspect of listening. When we observe that listening involves interpreting, we realize that listening is not the passive dimension of communication that it was supposed to be.

The interpretive factor is so important in the phenomenon of listening that it is possible to listen even when there are no sounds and, consequently, even when there is nothing to hear. Indeed, we can listen to silences. For example, when we ask for something, the other person's silence can be heard as a refusal. We also listen to gestures, body postures, and movements to the extent that we are able to attribute meaning to them. This is what allows the development of languages for the deaf. Silent films also provide a good example of how we can listen when there are no sounds. Hearing and listening, we insist, are two different phenomena.


 

From a descriptive understanding to a generative understanding of language

We normally think that we hear words. Our ability to organize words into larger units allows us to hear sentences. Our ability to organize sentences into even larger units allows us to hear accounts, narratives, stories. But ultimately, everything seems to boil down to words.

In our traditional interpretation, words label, name, or refer to an object, an event, an idea, and so on.

We are told that the meaning of a word is its connection to what it refers to. Since we cannot always point to the object, event, idea, etc., to which the word refers, the meaning of a word is commonly established by means of a definition. The definition provides meaning to the word by using other words that refer to it. If we do not know the meaning of a word, we consult a dictionary. There, each word is shown alongside other words. In a dictionary, meaning lives in a universe of words.

The above interpretation is consistent with the old assumption that language is a passive instrument for describing reality. We say that this interpretation produces a narrow understanding of the phenomenon of listening. We support a different interpretation of language. For us, language is not just an instrument that describes reality. We maintain that language is action.

We say that when we speak, we act, and when we act, we change reality; we generate a new one. Even when we describe what we observe, as we obviously do, we are also acting; we are "making" a description, and this description is not neutral. It plays a role in our horizon of possible actions. We call this the generative capacity of language—since we maintain that language generates reality.

Based on the above premise, we generate a different understanding of what the phenomenon of conferring meaning is. Ludwig Wittgenstein said that "The meaning of a word is its use in language." But to point to the "use" of a word is, of course, to point to the actions in which that word is brought to hand, in a way that makes sense. We maintain that if we want to grasp the meaning of what is said, we must examine the actions involved in speaking. When we listen, we do not only hear words, we also hear actions. This is key to understanding listening.


 

The actions involved in speaking

When we speak, we do not normally perform one action, but three different types of actions relevant to the process of human communication. These three types of actions were originally distinguished by the British philosopher J.L. Austin.

At the first level is the act of articulating the words we say. This is the action of saying what we say. Austin called these "locutionary acts." Saying, for example, "I'll be busy tomorrow" is a different action from saying "I don't feel like it." These are not just different sounds, nor are they just different words; they are also different actions. As such, they generate different listening and different consequences in our coordination of actions with others.

At a second level, there is the action involved in saying what we say. Austin called these "illocutionary acts." Both expressions mentioned above can be, for example, ways of refusing the request "Could you attend our meeting tomorrow?" Both are negatives to this request and, as such, imply the same action and are heard as the same (that is, as negatives), regardless of the fact that both negatives are heard differently, because of their differences at the locutionary level. Our taxonomy of basic speech acts—namely, assertions, declarations, requests, offers, and promises—operates at this second level.

Finally, according to Austin, there is a third level of action involved in speech. Austin called this third level "perlocutionary acts." Here we are not concerned with what was said (first level), nor with the actions of making a request, an offer, a statement, etc. (second level), but with the actions that take place because something was said, those that occur as a consequence or effect of what we say. Thus, for example, a particular illocutionary act can amaze, convince, annoy, etc.

Following Austin, therefore, we can say that when we listen, we hear the three levels of action. First, we hear the level of what was said and how it was said. Second, we hear the level of the action involved in what was said (whether it is an assertion, a statement, a request, an offer, or a promise). Third, we hear the level of the actions that our speech produces. In this interpretation of language, words are tools that allow us to look at all these levels of action.

However, all this is still insufficient to fully understand listening. So far, we have seen how the actions of speaking affect listening. We have recognized that language is action, based on the recognition that speaking is action. We maintain that this still corresponds to a partial understanding of the active and generative nature of language. What is missing is to go beyond the formula "speaking => action" and discover the active nature of listening.

Let's examine some examples. If I ask a customer, "Can I call you next week to continue this conversation?" and he replies, "Okay," I might well hear, in addition to his acceptance, "He is interested in my product." If I ask Emilia, "What are you doing on New Year's Eve?" and she replies, "I'm staying home," I might hear, "Emilia wants to avoid the stress of social activities." If my son asks, "Dad, can you give me fifty dollars?" I might hear, "He's planning to go out with his girlfriend."

Obviously, this is not what was said, but it is what I heard. Let's not forget that we say what we say and hear what we hear. In all these examples, what we heard was simply not said, but that does not mean we heard wrong. On the contrary, we may be listening quite effectively. We postulate that this part of listening, which goes beyond speaking, is a fundamental aspect of effective listening. Moreover, it is a fundamental aspect of the phenomenon of human listening.

Certainly, what we hear may sometimes be valid and sometimes not. Where is the difference? How can we increase our ability to listen more effectively? To answer these questions, we must make some other distinctions that will take us beyond the actions directly involved in communicative interactions.


 

The assumption of "intention" to give meaning to our actions.

We have said that when we listen, we do not only hear the words that are spoken; we also hear the actions implied in the speech. And we have just seen that listening to these actions is only part of what we hear. Listening to the actions implied in the act of speaking is not enough to ensure effective listening. What is missing? What else does listening include?

Every time we hear an action, we usually ask ourselves two basic questions. The first is: Why is the person performing this action? The second is: What are the consequences of this action? Depending on how we answer these questions, the same action can be heard in very different ways. We will deal with the first of these questions here and in the next section.

When we hear an action, we not only identify it, we also answer, in one way or another, the question "why" the action is being performed. Or, to put it another way, "what" leads someone to say what they say. How do we do this? What assumptions do we make when we answer the question?

Traditionally, we address the question of "why" an action is performed, assuming that "behind" it there are what we call "intentions." We assume that we will normally find an intention behind a person's action. Actions appear as responses to a purpose, a motive, or an intention. And these intentions are assumed to reside in our consciousness or mind.

This assumption (which dates back to the time of the ancient Greeks) is one of the foundations of the rationalist tradition. Rationalism assumes that there is generally a conscious intention or goal behind every action. The rationalist tradition seeks people's "reasons" for acting the way they do. An action is considered rational if it corresponds to the conscious intentions we have set for ourselves in carrying it out. From this perspective, one of the basic factors that makes an action meaningful is its intention. Therefore, one of the ways we make sense of an action is by discovering the "true intention" behind it. An action that is consistent with its "reason" or "true intention" is a rational action. But, we ask ourselves, does it make sense to postulate the existence of something like a "true intention" behind an action?


 

The solution offered by Freud

This problem was also presented to Sigmund Freud, and it is perhaps interesting to examine how he approached it. Freud began by making two important contributions to this problem. The first was to point out that human beings often act without conscious intentions—without a clear knowledge of what they are doing and why they are doing it. The second is that even when they believe they know why they are doing what they are doing, the reasons they give can be legitimately challenged. This is precisely part of the therapist's job. The therapist allows himself to challenge the patient's "reasons" and offer different "reasons."

So far, we have no problem with Freud. On the contrary, what he is proposing seems coherent to us, and we consider his position, up to this point, to be an important contribution. However, we disagree with the next steps taken by Freud as a way of generating an interpretation that is consistent with his postulate that individuals' awareness of the reasons for their actions (their intentions) is unreliable.

Since we cannot rely on conscious intentions to understand human behavior, Freud suggested the existence of another entity—the unconscious. Unconscious intentions are those that supposedly reside in the unconscious and can be "discovered" by the therapist.

Let us examine the nature of the solution offered by Freud. He does not challenge the postulate that we act on the basis of intentions. Having recognized a problem in the traditional interpretation, he sought to resolve it within the framework of some of the assumptions accepted in his time. One of these is the assumption we call the primacy of the mind or consciousness, which forms another of the pillars of the metaphysical program [13].

Since consciousness, according to Freud, is not capable of explaining some of our actions, we can only assume that there must be another entity, similar in rank to consciousness but different from it in that it is not associated with conscious phenomena. What better, then, than to assume the existence of a kind of "other mind," with the difference that this one is not conscious. Now, instead of one mind, we have two: one conscious and one unconscious. In doing so, we remain within the tradition that uses the mind as the explanatory principle of human action.

With this solution, there is also no need to question the interpretive framework that has traditionally nourished our concept of intention. Instead of questioning the concept of intention, Freud expands it. He postulates that in addition to our conscious intentions, we also have unconscious intentions. We do not agree with Freud's solution to this problem.


 

Questioning the concept of intention

One of the problems with the assumption of intentions is that it involves splitting each action in two—the action itself and the action that leads us to act. Since the action that leads us to act is an action in itself, it can be divided into two again, the action that leads us to act, by itself, and the action that leads us to the action, which leads us to act, and so on in infinite regression.

In doing so, the person acting is also divided into two—the person revealed by the actions they perform and the person who is supposedly deciding to act. And we enter again into an infinite regression, since deciding to act is, in itself, an action that someone supposedly does. This is known as "the humunculus fallacy" (a Latin word meaning little man), in which we assume that behind each person there is another little person at the helm.

The idea that every action involves a self that performs it is similar to the idea that every time we see an arrow flying, there must be an archer who shot it. If there is an action, we assume that an agent or a person (the archer) performed it. Humanity has been trapped in this assumption for a long time. If something happened, we assumed that there was necessarily someone who made it happen. Rain, thunder, illness and recovery, harvests—even winning the lottery—were all actions performed by invisible individuals. Many of the gods that humans have given themselves throughout history were invented based on this assumption.

Separating the action from the person (the self) may have originated in the way we speak. We normally say "I wrote this letter" or "I accept your offer," denoting a "I" behind the action of writing the letter or accepting the offer. The limitations of our language often cause highly sophisticated philosophical problems.

It is interesting to note that one of the strengths of scientific thought is that, from its inception, it freed itself from the assumption that there is a person creating phenomena. The anecdote goes that some time after publishing his masterpiece on the structure of the universe, the French astronomer Laplace met Napoleon. The anecdote notes that after congratulating him on his work, the emperor asked, "Mr. Laplace, how could you write such a long work without mentioning, even once, the Creator of the Universe?" To which Laplace replied, "Sire, je n'ai pas eu besoin de cette hypothèse" (Your Majesty, I had no need for such a hypothesis).

Friedrich Nietzsche was, once again, one of the first thinkers to observe the fact that we perform this strange operation of separation that we have described above. Nietzsche writes:

"... [language] understands and misunderstands that all doing is conditioned by an agent, by a 'subject'. (...) in the same way that people separate lightning from its flash and conceive of the latter as an action, as an action of a subject called lightning. (...) But such a substrate does not exist; there is no 'being' behind the action, the act, the becoming; the 'agent' has been fictitiously added to the action, the action is everything."

Like Nietzsche, we postulate that "the agent is a fiction, doing is everything." We maintain that the action and the subject (the "I") that performs the action cannot be separated. In reality, it is the actions that are performed that are permanently constituting the "I." Without actions there is no "I," and without "I" there are no actions. The arrow, the bow, and the archer in this case are generated simultaneously. The flying arrow is constituting the archer. We are who we are according to the actions we perform (and this includes the acts of speaking and listening).

Albert Einstein took a similar position. In a lecture he gave in England on the methodology of theoretical physics, he said that if we want to understand what a scientist does, we should not rely on what he would tell us about his actions. We should limit ourselves to examining his work. This is also one of the central positions of the epistemology developed by the French philosopher of science, Gaston Bachelard.

When we act (and also when we speak and listen—that is, when we are in conversation), we are constituting the "I" that we are. We do this both for ourselves and for others. Our actions include both our public and private acts, as well as our public and private conversations. But making a separation between public and private acts (or public and private conversations) is very different from separating the "I" from its actions.

The very notion of intentions falls apart when we oppose separating the person from their actions. It is therefore irrelevant to look for new kinds of intentions to understand human behavior, as Freud did. It is the very assumption of intention that must be replaced. The question is: Can we do without it? Can we make sense of human behavior without presupposing an intention behind the action?

From intentions to concerns

We propose a completely different interpretation. We say (inspired by the philosophy of Martin Heidegger) that every time we act, we can assume that we do so to take care of something. As we pointed out earlier, we call this something, whatever it may be, a concern. We can therefore say that an action is carried out to address a concern. We say that a concern is the interpretation we give to what we take care of when we carry out an action. Therefore, it is what gives meaning to the action. If we cannot attribute a concern to an action, it loses meaning. So far, this is very similar to the old rationalist conception that speaks of intentions. Are we not simply calling intentions concerns? Are we not using different names to talk about the same thing? We argue that we are not. What makes the difference between concern and intention is this: we are not saying that there is an intention "behind" an action, we are not saying that intentions guide our actions, we are not saying that the mind is guiding our actions.

We maintain that a concern is an interpretation that gives meaning to the actions we take. It is a narrative we construct to give meaning to our actions. Instead of looking for "reasons" to act the way we do, we have narratives, "stories." Moreover, our "reasons" are nothing more than "stories" we construct for ourselves. We fabricate some stories after performing actions and others before doing so. What we used to call intentions are nothing more than stories, that is, interpretations that give meaning to our actions.

But, once again, what is the difference? At this point, the question of listening becomes important. We postulate that the place where we should look for concerns is not "behind" the action, nor in people's minds, but in listening to what this action produces. When we observe people's actions and when we listen to them speak (and now we know that speaking is an action), we give them meaning by constructing stories about what the actions are about. We say that concerns are not rooted in the action itself or in the mind or consciousness of the person acting, but in how we interpret (or listen to) them.

As such, a concern is always a matter of interpretation and reinterpretation. No one owns concerns; no one has the final authority to determine the "true concern." Not us, when we talk about the meaning of our actions, nor the therapist when they offer us their interpretations. Everyone has a right to their own interpretations, to their own stories about their actions and those of others. The fact that we have stories about our own actions does not make them true.

Certainly, some interpretations may be better or worse founded, more or less valid, or more or less powerful. Depending on the interpretation we hold, certain possibilities will open up to us and others will close. This allows us to support or refute some interpretations. We are not saying that, because they are interpretations, they are all equal. Different stories create different worlds and different ways of life. Our stories are by no means trivial.

Concerns are interpretations of the meaning of our actions. They are stories that are capable of conferring meaning insofar as they answer the question of what it is that action takes care of. Just as the meaning of words refers to the actions we perform with them, the meaning of actions refers to the interpretations we construct through language, with the power of words. There is no escape from the networks of language.

The point we wish to emphasize, however, is that these interpretations—these stories—reside in the listening to actions. Concerns are different from intentions, since they do not reside in the speaker but in the listener. And since we are capable of listening to and observing our own actions, we can also attribute meaning to them. Since we are capable of listening to possibilities for action, we can also attribute meaning to actions that have not yet been carried out. When we do this, people commonly speak of intentions. We propose speaking of concerns. What we have called intentions is thus shown to be a particular case, a subset, of what we have designated with the distinction of concern.

When we listen, therefore, we listen to people's concerns. We listen to why people do the actions they do. This is what allows me to hear that my son wants to go out with his girlfriend when he asks me for fifty dollars. This is what allows me to hear that someone wants to get rich when they say they want to go into business. And this is what allows me to hear that my wife might be upset when she tells me she doesn't want to go to the movies with me. No one said what I heard, but I heard it anyway.

When we listen, we are not passive receivers of what is being said. On the contrary, we are active producers of stories. Listening is not, as we often assume, the passive side of communication—it is completely active. People who know how to listen are people who allow themselves to constantly interpret what the people around them are saying and doing. Those who know how to listen are good narrative builders, good story producers.

To listen, we must allow others to speak, but we must also ask questions. These questions allow us to understand the facts, make informed judgments, and construct coherent stories. Good listeners do not immediately accept the stories they are told. They often challenge them. They are not satisfied with a single point of view. They are always asking for another opinion, looking at things from different angles. Like weavers, they produce stories that, step by step, allow us to distinguish more clearly the threads of events.

By shifting from intentions to concerns, we radically change the center of gravity of the phenomenon of listening. By moving away from the assumption that listening is passive, we can now view listening as an action to be performed—as an action that can be designed—and as an action based on specific skills that we can learn. Replacing intentions with concerns brings about a Copernican shift. The search for people's "true intentions" no longer makes sense.

When we listen, we also construct a story about the future

When we listen, we do not remain neutral and indifferent observers. We are reconstructing the speaker's actions and inventing stories about why they said what they said (that is, we are answering the question "what is the speaker taking responsibility for when they speak?").

However, there is an additional aspect that also plays a part in our listening. Human beings are necessarily committed to the world in which we live. We know that what will be possible for us in life depends not only on us, but also on what happens in the world to which we are bound and which we always carry with us. One of Heidegger's great contributions has been to postulate that we cannot separate the being that we are from the world within which we are. The primary phenomenon of human existence is "being-in-the-world," or, as Heidegger also calls it, Dasein. It is only from this recognition that we can proceed to examine each of the terms (being and world) of this primary unity.

Because of our indissoluble relationship with the world, everything that happens in it concerns us. A basic ontological dimension of human existence is a permanent concern for what happens in the world and for what changes it.

By recognizing that speaking is acting and, therefore, an intervention that transforms the world, we also recognize another crucial aspect of listening. To the extent that speaking is action, all speech has consequences in our world. All speech is capable of opening or closing possibilities for us. All speech has the potential to change the future and what we can expect from it.

When we listen, therefore, we do so from our current commitment to the world. We cannot help but ask ourselves, "What are the consequences of what is being said?" "How does what is said alter the course of events?" "How is the future affected by what is being said?" "How is the world being reshaped by what is being said?" But above all, "How do the transformations generated by speech affect my concerns?" "What opportunities and dangers do these transformations entail?"

We often believe that what is said will not significantly change our world. When we make this judgment, we may adopt a neutral attitude toward what was said. But our ability to listen to something neutrally always comes from our degree of commitment to the world. Commitment is primary; neutrality is always a derivative.

Listening transcends, goes beyond our ability to reconstruct the actions involved in speaking. This happens not only because we invent stories about the speaker's concerns, as we examined earlier, but also because we make judgments and construct stories (about those actions) in terms of their consequences for our future. There is no listening that is not based on the listener's future. Here the question is not what future the speaker envisions when speaking. This is resolved by asking a question about the speaker's concerns. What is at stake here is how the listener hears that those actions will affect their own future.

When we converse, we dance a dance in which speaking and listening are intertwined. Everything one says is heard by the other, who constructs two kinds of stories. One is about the speaker's concerns when they say what they say, and the other is about how what was said will affect the listener's future (their own concerns). Both parties are doing this at the same time. The German philosopher Hans-Georg Gadamer, a disciple of Martin Heidegger, has called this "the fusion of horizons." Gadamer wrote:


 

"...the fusion of horizons that occurs in understanding is the real achievement of language... the nature of language is one of the most mysterious questions that man can reflect on. Language is so strangely close to our thinking, and when it operates it is such a tiny object that it seems to hide its own being from us."[14]

What is interesting to note, however, is that this "fusion of horizons" (or "fusion of histories," as we would call it) occurs in the listening of both parties. Each party brings not just one story to the conversation. Both contribute two—one about the concerns of their interlocutor and the other about themselves. In the act of listening, both parties produce this "fusion of horizons." The way the fusion takes place in each of them is never the same.

The basic matrix of listening

It is important to pause for a moment and recapitulate the path we have traveled. This will give us an opportunity to gain an overview, make some additional clarifications, and move the argument to a different terrain.

What we have argued so far is that the phenomenon of listening arises when, based on our biological ability to hear sounds, we are also able to refer those sounds to a consensual domain in which, for a given community, they are illuminated with meaning. Within a community, those strange sounds that its members emit open up the possibility of coordinating actions together and coordinating the coordination of their actions.

The sounds emitted cease to be mere noises and become words, sentences, narratives. Their exchange constitutes the world of conversation. Listening, therefore, involves going beyond the ability to hear sounds as sounds and being able to interpret their meaning by virtue of their reference to the consensual domain. Listening, we have said, is hearing plus interpreting. The decisive factor in this equation is interpretation.

The great contribution of linguistics has been to study language as a system of signs and, therefore, of sounds that refer to certain referents and are subject to certain rules of functioning among themselves. The great contribution of the philosophy of language has been to overcome the traditional descriptive conception of language and the recognition of language as action and, therefore, with the capacity to transform the world.

The ontology of language seeks to bring our understanding of it into the realm of a different understanding of human existence. Its perspective, therefore, is existential. It is within this context that we are analyzing the phenomenon of listening.

So far, we have postulated that listening, once rescued from an interpretation that confines it to a dynamic within a system of signs, refers to three different spheres: the sphere of action, the sphere of concerns (which give meaning to action), and the sphere of the possible (defined by the consequences of the actions of speaking). Let us briefly return our gaze to each of these three spheres.


 

a) The realm of action

A first way in which we give meaning to speech (and which, therefore, constitutes listening) is related to identifying the actions involved in speech. In this regard, we used Austin's proposal to talk about three types of actions involved: locutionary, illocutionary, and perlocutionary actions. Locutionary acts, we said, are those related to what is said. Illocutionary acts are related to what is done when saying what is said (affirming, declaring, asking, offering, and promising). Perlocutionary acts are related to the effects on the other person that result from saying what was said (indignation, persuasion, tenderness, etc.).

To these three types of actions identified by Austin, we can add two more types that may also be involved in speaking. To illustrate them, let's take the example of someone who says, "Fernando, I ask you to apologize to Veronica."


 

The overall action involved. Beyond the actions indicated by Austin, we can recognize that this request may, in addition to being such, be part of a broader action. In this case, this request could be part of the action of making a complaint, within which Fernando is asked to apologize to Veronica. What is important to recognize here is that illocutionary acts can be components of more complex global actions. While we can reduce illocutionary acts to a limited number of them, the global actions that can be performed with them are infinite. It depends on the language games that one is able to invent.


 

The associated action. This same example shows us the possibility of a fifth type of action that may also be involved in speaking, particularly when I make requests and offers and thus participate in the game of promises. This is, obviously, the action that is the subject of the promise. In this case, the action of apologizing to Veronica. We will call this the action associated with the illocutionary action. As we have said before, this is an action that may or may not be linguistic.


 

b) The realm of concerns

We have also argued that in addition to listening to the different types of actions we have indicated above, when we listen we generate an interpretation of what the speaker is taking responsibility for when speaking. This is what we have called concern.

Let us examine the importance of knowing how to listen in the realm of concerns. Living together with others depends, to a large extent, on our ability to listen to each other, to take care of each other's concerns. This is related to caring for and looking after each other. One way of doing this is by fulfilling the requests that others make of us.

However, this is not the only way. A good interpersonal relationship (whether between partners, parents and children, at work, with our clients, etc.) depends to a large extent on our ability to take care of the other person before they ask. We achieve this by listening to the other person's concerns and making them our own. In this way, we try to prevent the other person from reaching the point where they have to ask. We know that when someone asks, it is already too late in a way. Using a distinction that we will introduce later, we will say that transparency has already been broken. The person who asks has already made the judgment that something was missing, that there is a dissatisfaction that needs to be satisfied (which is manifested, precisely, in asking for such conditions of satisfaction).

In any relationship based on care and effort to seek the satisfaction of the other, the moment of asking implies that we are late, that the other has already reached the point of dissatisfaction. In many circumstances, this cannot be avoided. There is no marriage, for example, that is so perfect that the spouses are not forced to make requests of each other. But the less we have to ask to achieve satisfaction, the better the relationship will be.

The same can be said about our relationship with our customers. The less the customer has to turn to us to find satisfaction, the better the product we sell them will be. Good service, however important it may be, is no substitute for a product that does not require service. Knowing how to listen to the customer's concerns, so that we can then take care of them, is a determining factor in our ability to produce quality.

Any job that is characterized by providing logistical support to the work of others (take that of a secretary, for example) is achieved not only through the proper fulfillment of requests. Above all, it is achieved through the ability to take care of the concerns of those being served, even before they lead to requests. The most important thing is to maintain transparency in the work of others. The less you have to ask, the better.

In many cases, because we are able to listen to the concerns of others, we may be in a position to take care of them in ways that they cannot even imagine and, therefore, offer them conditions of satisfaction that they would not know how to ask for. This is what a good salesperson normally does. They manage to show us how a particular product addresses concerns that we did not have before talking to them. It is not that we did not know we had them, but rather that they were there. (One might ask, where?). They only appear when I accept the interpretation proposed by the salesperson.


 

c) The realm of the possible

If we accept that speaking is acting, we recognize that speaking changes the world, the state of things, and that, therefore, speaking has consequences. In other words, speaking rearticulates the world as a space of possibility. After someone says something, new possibilities emerge and old possibilities cease to exist. What was once closed opens, and what was open closes. Because we had, or perhaps did not have, a certain conversation, our lives take one direction or another. Because someone told us—or perhaps did not tell us—something; because we said—or perhaps did not say—something, we become a different person.

When we listen, therefore, we can observe how the world, and others within it, are transformed by the power of language. By listening, we can ask ourselves about the consequences of what was said, how it relates to our concerns, and the new actions that are now possible based on what was said. We can ask ourselves about the new opportunities that arise from speaking, about the new threats that arise, and about the actions that allow us to deal with both.

Great politicians, great businesspeople, great teachers, great salespeople, great negotiators—in short, all those who deal with people and are effective in what they do—know how to listen to how speech changes what is possible. It is very likely, however, that if we ask them what they understand by language, their answers will not recognize its generative nature. But in their concrete work, that recognition exists. They are people who do not fail to notice that once something has been said, possibilities emerge or fade away.

This leads us to recognize the power of conversations. We have all had the experience of coming out of a conversation and recognizing that the world is different, that doors have opened or closed, that we can enter spaces that were previously forbidden to us, or that something very valuable was broken during the conversation.

In a conversation, what one person says changes what is possible for the other, allowing them to say what they would not have said before. This saying, in turn, changes what is possible for the first person, who now discovers the possibility of saying something they had never thought of before, and so on. Therein lies the great power of conversation.

Great seducers are masters in the art of changing what is possible through conversation. The same is true of great creators. They know the importance of entering into dialogue, either personally or through their works, with those who can stimulate this dynamic process of opening up possibilities. What we are pointing out allows us to understand how certain social circumstances can generate virtual microcultures in which, due to internal conversational dynamics, multiple creative bursts occur in those who participate in them.

Cases like these abound in human history. We see them, for example, in Pericles' Greece, in the Italian Renaissance, in Vienna at the beginning of the century, and so on. We see them in smaller circles, such as the Bloomsbury Group in England, or around a figure or event of importance that gives rise to a "school," and so on. This often happens in an academic institution or in the context of a particular corporation.


 

d) The realm of the human soul

There is no doubt that if someone knows how to listen in the three areas mentioned above—actions, concerns, and possibilities—we can say that we have a person who is competent in the art of listening. However, the three areas mentioned do not exhaust all the possibilities related to it.

We want to open up a fourth realm that is particularly important for the discipline of "ontological coaching," to whose birth and development we are committed. Although we will not address the topic of "ontological coaching," which would exceed the purpose we have set for ourselves on this occasion, we cannot fail to recognize the realm to which we will refer.

We have argued through the second principle of the ontology of language that "not only do we act according to how we are (and we do), but we also are according to how we act." From this principle, we will focus on what is stated in the first part: we act according to how we are. If we also remember that we have postulated that speaking is action, we can therefore conclude that we are also arguing that we speak according to how we are. In other words, we are postulating a relationship between speaking and being.

In making this connection, it is important to issue a warning. We are not suggesting that being, the "subject" or the "I," precedes and takes priority over speaking.

This is precisely the assumption that, as we saw, Nietzsche strongly questions when he warns us that "the agent is a fiction, doing is everything." What we are arguing is that in speaking, as an important form of acting, the being that we are is constituted.

We will have the opportunity to investigate the phenomenon of the human person and examine how we constitute ourselves as persons in language. For now, what interests us is to recognize this relationship between speaking and being and, based on it, to show how it creates a particular realm of listening.

When we speak, we reveal who we are, and those who listen to us can not only hear what we say, but also hear the being that is constituted by saying what we say. Speaking not only creates us, but also makes us known, opens us up to others, who, through listening, have a key to access our way of being, to what we call the human soul. As we said, this is the type of listening that is characteristic of "ontological coaching." It is a kind of listening that transcends what is said and seeks to access the "being." It is precisely in this sense that it is "ontological" listening.


 

Openness: the fundamental posture of listening

So far, we have examined what we consider to be the fundamental components of the phenomenon of listening. We say that these are the basic processes that take place when listening occurs. There is, however, one question we have not yet mentioned: what is needed for listening to occur?

This question may seem strange. Since we postulate that human beings are linguistic beings—that is, beings who live in language—we recognize that both speaking (verbal language is only one form of speaking) and listening are constitutive of every human being. If we accept that language is constitutive of human beings, it seems superfluous to ask about the conditions necessary for listening. We might well say, "It just happens—human beings are thrown into listening."

Three reasons, however, make this question about the conditions for listening interesting. First, there is an empirical reason. Through it, we recognize that, in addition to the fact that we are animals that listen, our ability to do so is not the same. Some people listen better than others. It may therefore be worthwhile to inquire about the conditions behind these differences.

Second, this question makes sense on a deeper level. Even though we are thrown into listening, we can still look at the phenomenon of listening as something we can try to explain—something we might want to understand. In doing so, we contribute to dissolving one of the many mysteries surrounding language.

Third, by examining the conditions of listening, we turn it not only into a particular aspect of human life, but also into a domain for learning and design. Therefore, this has a practical side. By identifying the conditions required for listening, we can intervene at that level and improve our skills for effective listening.

Having said that, we realize that this same question about the conditions required for listening can be answered on two different levels. One level looks at the phenomenon of listening as arising from a fundamental human disposition. The other divides this basic disposition into several segments or domains that can be treated independently of each other. In this section, we will examine listening as arising from a fundamental human stance in life.

We postulate that the act of listening is based on the same ethics that constitute us as linguistic beings. That is, on mutual respect, on accepting that others are different from us, that in such difference they are legitimate, and on accepting their ability to take action autonomously from us. Mutual respect is essential to listening. Without accepting others as different, legitimate, and autonomous, listening cannot occur. If this is not present, we can only project our own way of being onto others. Instead of doing that, when we listen, we place ourselves in a position to accept the possibility that other ways of being exist, different from our own.

We previously argued that, when we speak, we open ourselves up to the possibility of exposing who we are. That when we speak, we make our soul accessible. That there is a particular openness towards the other in this. Well, this same openness must also be present, although this time in a different way, as acceptance, in the listener.

H-G. Gadamer has once again seen the importance of openness towards others as the fundamental aspect of the phenomenon of listening. He says:

"In human relationships, what is important is... to experience the 'You' as truly a 'You,' which means not overlooking what they have to say and listening to what they have to tell us. To achieve this, openness is necessary. But it exists, ultimately, not only for the person one listens to, but rather, every person who listens is fundamentally an open person. Without this kind of mutual openness, genuine human relationships cannot exist. Staying together always means, too, being able to listen to each other. When two people understand each other, it does not mean that one "understands" the other in the sense of "scrutinizing" them. Similarly, listening to and obeying someone does not simply mean that we blindly agree to the other's wishes. We call such a person a slave. Openness to the other, therefore, includes the recognition that I must accept some things that go against me, even when no one asks me to.

Humberto Maturana expresses this same point of view when he argues that "accepting the other as a legitimate other" is an essential requirement of language. If we do not accept the other as a legitimate other, listening will always be limited and communication between human beings will be obstructed. Every time we reject another, whether a partner, a customer, an employee, a competitor, a country, etc., we restrict our ability to listen. We produce the fantasy of listening to the other while we are basically listening to ourselves. In doing so, we close ourselves off to the possibilities that others are generating.

What circumstances affect this openness, considered a fundamental requirement for listening? Every time we question the legitimacy of the other; every time we consider ourselves superior to the other on the basis of religion, gender, race (or any other factor we may use to justify positions of egocentrism, ethnocentrism, chauvinism, etc.); every time we claim to have privileged access to Truth and Justice; every time we presume that our particular way of being is the best way to be; every time we forget that we are only one particular observer within a beam of infinite possibilities for observation; each of these times, our listening suffers.

One of the great merits of democratic practices lies in the fact that they are based on the principle of difference, legitimacy, and the autonomy of the other (regardless of who or what that other may be). This makes the ability to listen effectively one of the keys to political success. Likewise, the practices of open and competitive markets, beyond their limitations, also place the skill of effective listening as a condition for success.


 

The ontological being and the person: a way of being that allows for infinite ways of being


 

What we have said so far should provoke an objection. At the beginning of this document, we strongly opposed the notion that we could understand the phenomenon of listening based on the concept of information transmission. We argued that this notion ignores the fact that human beings are "structurally determined units," closed systems, that is, systems that cannot represent what is happening in the environment in which they operate. However, we have come to suggest that a fundamental condition of listening is the attitude of "openness." Therefore, we could reasonably ask, how can this openness occur if we are supposed to be closed systems?

In truth, we cannot open ourselves in the sense that listening to another tells us how that other really is. We can never know how people and things really are. We are even a mystery to ourselves. As we have insisted time and again, we only know how we observe and interpret them. Listening is part of this capacity for observation and interpretation. When we listen to others, we open ourselves to them by inventing stories about them based on our observations. But these will always be our own stories. The distinction of openness only makes sense within the recognition that human beings are closed systems.


 

What, then, does "openness" mean? To answer this question, we must take a short detour. Being human means sharing a particular way of being, the human way of being. This way of being is what differentiates us from other beings, whether they be animals, things, events, etc. We can therefore say that all human beings share the same way of being, the one that makes us human.

In this sense, each human being is the total expression of the phenomenon of being human. We cannot speak of human beings who are only half human, or partially human. Even in the case of individuals who may be missing some of their limbs, this does not make them any less human. The human condition is not constituted in the domain of our biology, but in that of language. Hence, we say that language makes us who we are as human beings. Every human being, therefore, is a complete human being, insofar as they are human. This does not deny that, as part of the human condition itself, being human means a journey through existence from a fundamental and permanent incompleteness.

What Martin Heidegger's philosophy has done is precisely to explore this common mode of being that all human beings share, which he called Dasein. His main concern was to reveal the particular way of being of human beings. Much of his philosophy is directed toward this inquiry.

We will call this way of being shared by all human beings their "ontological being." This is an arbitrary distinction. Therefore, when we speak of "ontological being," we are speaking of the way of being that all human beings have in common.

We will not develop here what Heidegger argued was constitutive of human beings and, therefore, constitutive of their "ontological being." This would take us beyond the scope of this work. However, among the elements that Heidegger pointed out, we can reiterate his postulate that human beings are beings whose very being is a relevant matter for them. To be human means to take permanent responsibility for the being that one is. It is in this sense that we maintain that ontological being is always torn by a fundamental sense of incompleteness.

Human beings do not have a fixed essence. What is essential in them (in the sense of a generic and, therefore, ontological trait) is that they are always in the process of becoming, always in a process of becoming. This makes time a primary factor for human beings.

At the same time, however, within this common form of being that makes us human, we have infinite possibilities for fulfillment. The way we take care of ourselves, the way we deal with feeling incomplete, makes us very different individuals. We call the different ways in which different individuals realize their common form of being (as human beings) "personhood." As individuals, we are, on the one hand, all equal in terms of our "ontological being" (generic), since we share the basic forms of being that make us all human, and, on the other hand, we are different "persons." We all solve the riddles of life in different ways.

We postulate that the phenomenon of listening is based on these two fundamental dimensions of human existence—"ontological being" and "person." We are able to listen to each other because we share a common way of being, and in this respect, everyone else is like us. Our "ontological being" allows us to understand others, since any other human being is a possible path to the realization of ourselves, of our own being. However, at the same time, we are different "persons." We do not take care of, we do not attend to our common being in the same way. It is because we are different that the act of listening becomes necessary. If we were not different, why listen in the first place? If we were not different, the act of listening would be superfluous. But because we share the same ontological condition, listening becomes possible.

These are, we say, the basic requirements for listening. Therefore, since we are closed systems, two fundamental movements must be made. On the one hand, we must distance ourselves from "ourselves," from that particular way of being that differentiates us from other individuals. In doing so, we accept the possibility that there are other particular ways of being, other "persons," different from our own. This is what Gadamer refers to when he speaks of "openness."

On the other hand, we must affirm the fact that we share a common way of being with the person who is speaking to us. We must grant ourselves full authority insofar as we are a valid expression of the general phenomenon of being human. This is what allows us to understand the actions of other people, to understand people who are different from us. It is from this common ground that we interpret others, that we construct our stories about the actions that others perform. Every other person is the reflection of a different soul in the background of our common being.

Why, when we read good literature, do we perfectly understand characters as diverse as Shakespeare's Othello, Flaubert's Emma Bovary, Dostoevsky's Prince Myshkin, Charlotte Brontë's Jane Eyre, and Scott Fitzgerald's Gatsby? Why does this happen if the characters are so different from us? Moreover, how is it possible that all these authors could achieve such mastery and understanding of their characters? To understand them and to be able to write about them, we distance ourselves from the "person" we are in order to delve into our "ontological being," into our shared being with others. We strip ourselves of what makes us a particular individual and observe others from what we have in common with them. Classical literature is that which manages to penetrate more deeply into our ontological being and thus rises above the more contingent dimensions of the human soul.

The phenomenon of listening, therefore, involves two different movements. The first takes us out of our "person," that particular way of being that we are as individuals. The second affirms and brings us closer to our "ontological being," to those constitutive aspects of being human that we share with others. We can only listen to others because their actions are possible actions for us, actions that we ourselves could perform. Walt Whitman, I note, pointed in this direction when he wrote, "I am large, I contain multitudes." We contain the possibilities of any other human being. Terence, in Roman times, had one of his characters say, "Homo sum: humani nihil a me alienum puto" ("I am human: nothing human is alien to me").

Areas of observation for developing effective listening

Having already identified the fundamental attitude and requirements necessary to generate the phenomenon of listening, we can now move forward and examine other specific domains related to human communication.

Communicative interaction is like a dance. It involves coordinating actions with another person. Therefore, just as we do when performing a dance step, when I say something to someone, that person will usually respond within the space of possibilities created by what I said. For example, if I ask for something, my interlocutor will normally respond by accepting, refusing, or postponing their promise, making a counteroffer, and so on. Their actions in this conversational dance are very well defined by the movements already executed in that conversation. We call this the context of the conversation.

The context of the conversation is one of the factors that conditions our listening. Anything that is said is heard within the context of the conversation we are having. Usually, this defines what we expect to hear. If we make a request, we will hear any response given as an acceptance, rejection, postponement of the commitment, and so on.

Let's take some examples. If I ask Carlos, "Can you pass me the pepper?" and he replies, "I'm reading," we will probably hear that response as "No." But if I ask Carlos, "What are you doing?" and get the same response, "I'm reading," the meaning of the response will be very different. Silence, for example, can sometimes be heard as rejection, acceptance, ignorance, shyness, etc., depending on the context of the conversation. Even a "no" can sometimes be heard as a "yes," depending on the context. Many people have difficulty observing the context of a conversation. This creates all kinds of problems in their communication.

Another important factor that affects our listening is the emotional state of the conversation. Emotional state is a distinction through which we recognize a predisposition (or lack thereof) for action. If I happen to be in a bad mood, say I'm angry, I probably won't be available for certain conversations or to take certain actions that those conversations entail. Likewise, if I'm resigned, I probably won't hear the possibilities that a person in another emotional state is able to hear.

We are always in one emotional state or another. Depending on that state, the world and the future will seem different to us. Our emotional state colors the way we see the world and the future. It also colors what we hear. In some cases, there will be actions that our emotional state prevents us from hearing. In many other cases, the meaning we give to certain actions and the possibilities we see as a result of them will be completely different if the emotional state is different.

If we want to listen effectively, we must first learn to observe our own emotional state when we talk and, secondly, the emotional state of the person we are talking to. No matter how clearly we express ourselves or how clear all the actions involved in that conversation are, we will not be heard as we expect if our emotional state is not right for the conversation. Let's not forget that we say what we say and hear what we hear.

It is not only important to observe people's emotional state when we engage in conversation. The conversation itself is constantly generating changes in the emotional states of those participating in it. What we say, when we say it, and how we say it provokes different emotional states in the listener.

Different conversations have different emotional states, and we can change them by changing the conversation. Can you observe the different emotional states in the conversations mentioned above? ("Can you pass me the pepper?"—"I'm reading"; versus: "What are you doing?"—"I'm reading"). Different emotional states produce different listening. To communicate effectively, we must become good observers of the emotional state of a conversation.

There are many ways to judge people's emotional states. What they say usually reflects it. The way they speak allows us to hear how they see the world and what their position is regarding the future. However, in addition to conversations, we can also judge people's emotional states by observing their bodies. We have said that emotional state implies a readiness for action. This readiness is usually shown in the postures our body adopts. An expert in bodywork once commented that the way we stand reveals the way we "stand in the world." Our physical postures are also ways in which our soul, our way of being, manifests itself.

Another factor that should be mentioned is our personal history. People hear what is said to them differently, depending on their personal experiences. You may have done the exercise of reading a poem to a group of people and then asking them what they heard. We are often surprised to see how differently each person heard the same poem, what it evoked in different individuals. The same sentences, the same words, read to all of them at the same time, evoke different images, different memories, different emotions, different worlds.

Our personal history plays an important role in determining not only who we are but also who we will be in the future. We always listen from that history. The present inherits from the past concerns, possibilities that we accept and deny, and much more. Our history of personal experiences is reactivated in our ability to listen in the present. This personal history opens or closes our listening. It is one of the main filters that we always have with us when we communicate.

If we want to communicate effectively, it is important to ask ourselves how our personal history might be affecting the way we listen, and how the personal history of the person we are talking to might affect their ability to listen. Let's not forget that we speak to be heard. Therefore, we must always speak in such a way that we can judge that the person we are talking to will be able to hear what we want to say.

When we speak, we are not only coordinating actions with others. We are also participating in creating an identity with the people who listen to us. Anything we say contributes to creating this identity in the public domain. When we speak, as we have already pointed out, people not only listen to the actions included in the discourse, but also make judgments and develop stories about the person speaking. These judgments cover very different domains. They may say, for example, that this person is creative, that one is selfish, that one is responsible, and so on.

There is one domain, however, which, as previously acknowledged, is particularly important in how we are heard: the domain of trust. We say that this is an important domain because trust directly affects the credibility of what we say and, consequently, how we are heard. If others do not trust us, or if we do not trust the person we are talking to, the critical gap between what we say and how it is heard widens. When we say, for example, "I'll pay you two hundred dollars on Tuesday," we will be heard very differently depending on whether or not they trust us.

What we are saying, therefore, is that every time we speak, we are constructing our identity in the ears of others, and that this identity will affect how we are heard in the future. Conversely, every time we listen to someone, our listening is also affected by the identity that person has for us. The mutual identity that people have with each other affects how they listen to each other.

When we listen, we do so not only as individuals, but also from our historical background. Human beings are historical beings and, as we originally pointed out, even our own individuality is the product of particular historical conditions. As individuals, we are the embodiment of our historical background. When we talk about ourselves as historical beings, it is useful to make a distinction between two particular subdomains: historical discourses and social practices.

Historical discourses are those meta-narratives, meta-stories, or meta-explanations that generate collective identities. A historical discourse normally precedes the individual who adheres to it; it is there when they are born. We can speak, for example, of Christian, Buddhist, or Islamic historical discourses, in the same way that we can speak of French, American, or Japanese discourses. We can talk about regional historical discourses (Galician, Andalusian, Cordoban, Buenos Aires) or ethnic discourses (Korean, Jewish, African American, Hispanic, etc.). Science, romantic love, medicine, etc., can also be seen as historical discourses.

Historical discourses are important for understanding the phenomenon of listening because they are fields of meaning generation. Something said is heard very differently depending on the historical discourses that we are. The concerns and problems we have as human beings, for example, are usually constituted in the historical discourses that people embody. The same event may be a problem for a Muslim and go unnoticed by an American. Some historical discourses are contained in a fundamental text or group of texts (the Bible, the Talmud, the Koran, the Declaration of Human Rights, etc.).

When people come from similar or complementary historical discourses, these discourses can become completely transparent to them and become part of their common sense, that space where things seem obvious and we stop asking questions. Since they tend to listen in a very similar way, they do not normally observe their historical discourses. However, when a relationship develops between people with very different historical discourses, what was once transparent breaks down and severe communication problems often arise.

Unless we recognize that our different ways of listening stem from our different historical discourses and manage to build bridges of communication, we will end up blaming each other for something that, strictly speaking, precedes us as individuals and for which we have little responsibility. We are not responsible for being the embodiment of the historical discourses that constitute us. However, we can take responsibility for recognizing them, accepting them in their particularity, reevaluating them, and designing bridges that allow us to accept the difference, legitimacy, and autonomy of those who come from a different historical discourse.

The second component of our historical background corresponds to our social practices. The main difference between historical discourses and social practices is that, while the former take the form of narratives, social practices are simply recurring ways of acting. They are specific ways of coordinating actions that we have come to use repeatedly throughout history. A social practice is an established way of trying to address our concerns.

Social practices typically define the entities that are relevant to addressing a concern. They specify the actions that must, can, or cannot occur when we address a concern, as well as the conditions of satisfaction that those actions must meet so that we can judge that the concern was addressed effectively.

In various societies, individuals often greet each other as an expression of recognition and acceptance. However, the way in which they address this concern of recognition and acceptance tends to be very different. This is not because there is a meta-narrative about the meaning of shaking hands, bowing, or kissing, but because of the existence of different social practices. There is not necessarily a narrative that explains why things must be done in a certain way. They are done that way simply because that is how things are done in that community. It is the result of a particular historical drift that imposed a certain way of behaving.

If we behave in a way that is not accepted by a particular society, the way we are heard could be very damaging to us. The Czech novelist Milan Kundera recounts in one of his books how he heard a declaration of love from a woman who worked at his publishing house in Paris. This was, however, the result of the fact that the social practice of closing a letter was very different in France and Czechoslovakia.

Listening is not a simple phenomenon. Many factors influence the way we listen and the way we are listened to. Based on our old understanding of language, it is difficult, however, to grasp everything that is at stake when we listen. We maintain that in a world as diverse as ours, listening has become a matter of vital importance in ensuring not only effective communication and personal success, but also coexistence itself. Today, we have an imperative to learn to listen better in order to be able to live together in harmony. This has even become a matter on which the very survival of humanity is at stake.
