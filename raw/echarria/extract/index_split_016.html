HUMAN ACTION AND LANGUAGE

[16]

This chapter will deal with the subject of action, or more specifically, human action. Within it, we will address different topics. All of them, however, will have as their common thread their reference to the subject of action.

We will begin by addressing some of the assumptions underlying our traditional conception of human action, and we will examine the criticism directed at them by the German philosopher Martin Heidegger. In this context, we will introduce the distinctions of transparency and rupture and examine the linguistic nature of ruptures. We will then analyze human action in relation to language. This will lead us to recognize that all human action has an interpretive component that arises from language.

Next, we will take action as a domain and introduce various distinctions within it that will allow us to recognize different types of actions. Finally, we will take a particular type of human action, what we call social practices, and—approaching the interpretation proposed by Wittgenstein—we will show how these can be reconstructed linguistically.

We have argued that the ontology of language offers a different interpretation of the human phenomenon by referring it to language. We have noted, however, that in order to fully understand this interpretation, it is necessary to reinterpret what we understand by language and open ourselves to an understanding that conceives it as generative and posits that language is action. But we have also noted that our interpretive circle will not be complete until we reinterpret action itself. Only then will we be in a position to understand why we say that action constitutes us as the kind of person we are and will become. Human beings, language, and action are three fundamental pillars of the ontology of language. In this chapter, we will examine the third of these: human action.


 

Our "traditional conception" of human action

Our "traditional conception," our common sense, rests, among other things, on two assumptions that we have inherited from the philosophy of Descartes, and which have served as the basis for modern Western thought. In one way or another, the interpretation offered by Descartes in the 17th century has underpinned almost all of our partial interpretations in the Western world. Descartes' basic postulates became unquestioned assumptions of our common sense. Western human beings became Cartesians even though we did not know who Descartes was or were unfamiliar with his philosophy. This is because the linguistic culture within which we constitute ourselves as individuals assumed his postulates to be valid. [17]

The two assumptions we refer to are the following: the one that maintains that every subject is exposed to the presence and immediacy of the world of objects that surrounds them

and the one that defines human beings as eminently rational in their actions in the world. We will examine each of them separately.

The first of these assumptions is based on an original dualism. Its starting point assumes that, when examining human existence, we must recognize, from the outset, two irreducible substances: the thought or reason that constitutes us as subjects (what Descartes calls the res cogitans) and the physical substance that constitutes objects (res extensa), within which is our body and the totality of natural objects.

This allows us to interpret human existence as originally and primarily constituted by a given subject, surrounded by a world of objects that are also given. The presence of these objects is not questioned. They are there in the world, naturally present, capable of being perceived directly by the senses, insofar as they are not hidden.

This does not deny that the subject's perception of the objects of the world is not without problems and that, for example, such perception is not free from distortions. On the contrary, our traditional conception makes the subject-object relationship its main obsession, its great problem. Hence, for modern philosophy, doing philosophy has been fundamentally about doing epistemology: determining the possibility that the subject can achieve a faithful representation of objects and, to the extent that such a possibility is accepted (there are those who deny it), determining the conditions that guarantee it. The great theme of modern philosophy has therefore been the theme of knowledge.

The second assumption of our traditional conception has been to maintain that what defines the subject, what characterizes us as human beings, is thought, consciousness, reason, our capacity for deliberation. Human beings, it is argued, are thinking beings, rational beings. This second assumption is particularly important for understanding human action, since it is precisely in the realm of action that conceiving of ourselves as rational beings has perhaps one of its most important consequences.

The assumption that reason is what constitutes and defines us as the kind of beings we are leads us to a rationalist understanding of human action. It assumes that consciousness, reason, or thought, however we refer to it, precedes action.

It assumes that human beings act consciously, guided by reason. It follows, therefore, that all human action is rational action. This implies that there is no action that does not have its reason and, consequently, that is not preceded by the reason that leads to it. Reason, therefore, drives action.

The concept of reason becomes so important within the parameters of this interpretation that, even when experience seems to show us that this assumption is debatable, and we seem to observe unconscious, irrational actions where there was no prior deliberation, this assumption is not questioned and we seek the unconscious reasons that lead us to act the way we do. Thus, the assumption is maintained, appearances aside, and the primacy of reason is upheld: it continues to drive human action.

The assumption of rationality has been extremely powerful within Western culture and undoubtedly needs to be seen as an undeniable historical contribution. Behind it, for example, science and technology and multiple ways of life have developed that are expressions of the power of human reason.

Very soon, however, perhaps because of its own success, reason ceased to be conceived as that which characterized human beings, transforming them into subjects; reason was established as the guiding principle of the universe. Science seemed to demonstrate that nature behaved rationally, according to precise laws that were there, waiting to be discovered. What science did was therefore interpreted as an effort to find the reasons behind all phenomena. Leibniz proclaimed, "Everything has a reason" or, in other words, "Nothing is without reason." Hegel, somewhat later, would maintain the identity of reality and reason: everything real is rational, and everything rational is real.


 

Reason and language

Let us pause here for a moment and ask ourselves: Are we not facing a situation in which, in Wittgenstein's words, language seems to have gone astray, and where, instead of trying to solve the problems that have arisen, it may be necessary to "dissolve" them?

Let us reflect on what we have been saying. We will do so based on the interpretation we have developed so far. We have postulated that human beings are linguistic beings, beings who live in language. We have also argued that human language is characterized by its recursivity, by its ability to turn back on itself. In this recursive view, human beings gain a mastery of meaning that questions not only life in its concrete flow, but human existence itself. Language transforms us into ethical beings, insofar as we cannot escape the imperative to give meaning to our existence.

This same recursivity transforms us into reflective beings, capable of questioning ourselves, interpreting ourselves, and seeking explanations. As beings with the capacity for reflection, we are beings who think, who seek "reasons" to account for everything that is part of our experience. As beings with the capacity for reflection, we also reflect on the way we reflect and seek more effective ways of doing so. We have called the latter "rational" thinking. From this perspective, therefore, it makes perfect sense to recognize ourselves as rational beings. What remains to be examined is the role conferred on reason and the type of relationship established between it and language.

Some of the questions about the general meaning of existence can be addressed through "rational" thinking; others apparently cannot, and we respond to them through faith, contemplative experience, and even silence. Always through language, insofar as what is at stake is the problem of meaning.

What we would like to emphasize is that, until now, we have not separated the distinctions between reason and rationality from the capacities that language gives to human beings. From the ontology of language, reason is a type of human experience that derives from language. Language is primary. In what has been said, we have maintained an indissoluble relationship between language and reason. Within this perspective, reason is a particular case within the general domain of language. Reason is one of the possible language games that we humans are capable of as linguistic beings.

For the rationalist approach, however, and Descartes is a good example, this is not the case. To the extent that no attention is paid to language, the distinction of reason takes on a different meaning. It is no longer a particular competence of human beings as linguistic beings, but a distinction that is not only seen as autonomous from language, but also from human beings themselves. Reason becomes an autonomous principle that not only defines what we are as human beings, but also governs the cosmos, which governs reality. This allows us to say, for example, as Hegel does, that everything real is rational.

Science and philosophy are no longer seen as purely rational, insofar as they represent particular interpretive activities carried out by human beings from and within language. Science and philosophy appear to be rational because they are committed to finding the reasons for what is real, to the search for truth, for the essence of things, for Being itself. Truth appears as the reason for reality. Both truth and reason exist independently of human beings. Reason, therefore, has ceased to be an attribute of our ability to interpret (reflect) and ensure the coherence of our interpretations. It has become dissociated from the interpretive capacity of human beings.

From our perspective, it makes no sense to say that the world is rational. The rational belongs only to the domain of our explanations. Natural phenomena do not "have reasons." How could they have them if they do not "live" in language? The reason for a phenomenon does not belong to the phenomenon, but to the human beings who forge an explanation that seeks to account for that phenomenon. Reason is always related to the observer and not to the observed.

Just as we question reason as a hidden principle behind natural phenomena, in the same way, from our perspective, it makes no sense to speak of "unconscious reasons," as this again places reason behind the phenomenon (this time behind the human phenomenon). The reason for a phenomenon, we insist, does not belong to the phenomenon, but to its explanation.

There are no reasons behind phenomena. There is only the capacity of human beings to provide explanations to give meaning to the phenomenal events to which they are exposed. In response to Leibniz's postulated principle that "Everything has a reason," we reply: human beings, as linguistic beings, have the capacity to provide reasons for everything that happens. Reason does not refer to a cosmic attribute, but to a capacity of human beings as linguistic beings.


 

Heidegger's critique of Descartes


 

One of the most profound critiques of Cartesian thought developed during the 20th century was that proposed by Martin Heidegger. According to Heidegger, it is wrong to proceed as Descartes did, separating being and world, subject and object, res cogitans and res extensa. The primary phenomenon of human existence, according to Heidegger, is what he calls Dasein, or being-in-the-world. As we argued earlier, there is no being that is not in the world, nor a world that is not for a being. In a fundamental sense, both are constituted simultaneously and by reference to each other. The very possibility of speaking of being and world separately, as subject and object, is derivative of this primary phenomenon of being-in-the-world.

Heidegger strongly questions the two assumptions we have just referred to: that of the natural presence of the world and that which supports the primary nature of reason and, as a consequence, its determining role in understanding human action.

According to Heidegger, the rationalist interpretation of both human beings and human action represents a flagrant misrepresentation of what actually occurs. Philosophers, Heidegger argues, have taken their own philosophical practice—which is characterized by being conscious, thinking, reflective, rational, deliberative, and so on—as a general model of human action and have assumed that all human action is equivalent to that performed by the philosopher when doing philosophy. This, according to Heidegger, produces a great distortion. The primary way of acting of human beings, including philosophers themselves, is not that which takes place in philosophical activity. Philosophical activity represents a type of derivative action, which emerges as a result of the dynamics of a very different type of primary action.


 

The distinction of transparency

Heidegger posits that what we will call transparency—non-reflective, non-thinking, non-deliberative activity, action with a minimum threshold of consciousness—constitutes the basis and primary condition of human action.

When, for example, we walk, climb stairs, hammer a nail into the wall, type on the computer, talk on the phone, ride a bike, eat at the table, cook, and so on, we do so in transparency. This means that we are not paying attention to every step we take when walking or every movement we make with our hands when typing on the computer. Nor do we anticipate the movement we will make next. Transparent action does not follow the assumptions offered by the interpretation of rational action.

We act without being clearly aware of the pavement we walk on, the steps we climb, the hammer, the nail, and the wall, the computer screen and keyboard, the telephone receiver, the fork we hold in our hand when eating and the plate containing the food, or the pot we stir when cooking. Our attention is usually focused elsewhere.

If we are driving our car in the morning on the way to the office, the steering wheel, the pedals, even the other cars moving around us, seem to be transparent to us. Almost as transparent as the windshield in front of us, which we do not see as a windshield. Our mind is not focused on them; rather, we think about the conversation we had a few minutes earlier with our partner at the breakfast table, the phone call we will make once we get to the office, or the meeting scheduled for after lunch. We pass one intersection after another, one traffic light after another, as if we did not see them. If, upon arriving at the office, we tried to remember the route, we would most likely not be able to do so or would only remember a few details.

Does this mean we are not seeing them? Obviously not. If we had not seen the traffic lights, we would not have stopped, as we did every time there was a red light. It is clear that we did not commit any offense. We moved in tune with the world around us, without stopping to think about it. We flowed in it, like when we dance without paying much attention to the steps we are taking.

When we are in this state, in the transparency of the flow of life, not only are we not thinking about what we are doing, we are also not in a world governed by the subject-object relationship. We are in a state that precedes the constitution of that relationship. When, then, does thinking about what we do emerge? When do we enter into a relationship with the world in which we conceive of ourselves as subjects and perceive objects?


 

The distinction of rupture

We maintain that deliberation, the awareness of what we are doing, only emerges when this flow of transparency is interrupted for some reason: when what we call a break occurs. A break, we might say, is an interruption in the transparent flow of life.

Let us return to some of the previous examples and examine some situations in which transparency is broken. If we stumble while walking, we will suddenly notice the pavement that was previously transparent to us. If, while climbing a ladder, one of the steps makes a strange creaking sound or gives way, that step will appear to us as an object. If, while typing on our computer, one of the keys does not do what we expect it to do, the keyboard will also emerge as an object that commands our attention. If, when picking up a piece of meat with my fork, it jumps off the plate, we will probably stop paying attention to the conversation taking place at the table to worry about the piece of meat, the fork, and the plate. If, on our way to the office, we find that every traffic light we approach is red, it is very possible that after stopping at the first few, the ones that follow will be very visible to us, and we will begin to wonder how it is possible that they are not synchronized.

What was previously transparent to us now emerges in our field of attention, we become aware of it, and it occupies our thoughts. Only then is our action governed by the standards of rational action. From the moment transparency is broken, we establish the subject-object relationship and begin to think about how to restore the lost transparency. The model of rational action, therefore, is a bridge that connects situations of transparency and arises when there is a break in transparent action.


 

Conditions for generating a break

Here we encounter an interesting phenomenon: What is it that "produces" the break? How does it occur? When, for example, does an additional red light at a traffic light produce a break in our drive to the office? And why that red light and not the previous one? Or two red lights further ahead?

We maintain that every break involves a judgment that whatever is happening, whatever it may be, does not meet our expectations of what should happen. A disruption, therefore, is a judgment that what has happened alters the expected course of events. Let's pause to examine what we have said. The first thing we highlight is our interpretation that every disruption constitutes a judgment. Therefore, if we did not have the capacity to make judgments, we would not have disruptions.

Let's take an example. We are driving, we have our dog in the back seat, and we get a flat tire. This will represent a break. When we drive, we don't do so expecting to get a flat tire, although obviously that possibility cannot be ruled out. But what we expect is to be able to get from one place to another using tires that are in good condition. As long as that happens, the tires will be transparent to us. But then one of them goes flat, and we are forced to stop the car and worry about changing the tire. This is obviously a break for us, but not for the dog accompanying us. And it is not a break because the dog has no judgment about the meaning of what has happened, nor does it have any judgments about what was expected to happen.

Transparency is therefore broken because of the judgments we make about what is happening, based on a judgment about what is normal to expect. The break, therefore, can be reconstructed linguistically as a judgment that says, "What happened was not what I expected," even though what we actually say when we realize that the tire is flat is something different. As such, every break is associated with a transformation of our judgments about what is possible.

When we talk about our judgments about what is possible, it is useful to introduce a distinction. Obviously, getting a flat tire was not something I conceived of as impossible. It is within the range of things I know can happen when I drive. However, within what I know to be possible, there is also a smaller range of possibilities that relates to what I expect to happen. This defines what is possible in the realm of my immediate expectations and on the basis of which I project my existence and define my actions. I operate in life betting that within the broader range of possibilities, what I expect will happen. When someone, for example, makes us a promise, we know that it could be revoked before the date of fulfillment or that it could even happen that they do not fulfill it. However, unless I have good reason to distrust the person who made the promise, we normally define our expectations on the assumption that the promise will be fulfilled. If this does not happen, even though the possibility of non-fulfillment was not excluded, we will experience a breach.


 

Negative and positive breaks

Every breach, therefore, modifies the realm of possibility and transforms our judgment about what we can expect. This transformation can take two directions. Sometimes, as in the examples given, breaches will restrict what is possible. Because we had a flat tire, I will lose the chance to be at the beginning of the party I was going to. As we can see, in addition to judging that what happened is a breach, we will make a negative judgment about the breach itself. We experience it as a negative breach.

However, transparency is also broken because something happens that expands our possibilities. This allows us to talk about positive breaks. If I receive the news that my proposal was selected as the best of all those submitted, this will obviously interrupt the transparency I was experiencing and therefore constitutes a break. What is now possible will expand. But this will be a very different break from the previous one. So will my experience of the situation.


 

Breaks reside in the observer

A common question we are asked is why we talk about breaks and not problems. After all, almost everything we have said seems to refer to problems. Not everything, however. One of the advantages of the distinction of break is that it allows us to recognize that they can be both negative and positive. The term problem usually carries a negative connotation. We normally avoid having problems. We will not necessarily avoid having breaks.

However, the main reason for choosing the distinction of break refers to the fact that, by introducing a new term, it avoids contamination with the assumptions of our traditional conception. This does not happen with the distinction of problem. By introducing the distinction of break, we make explicit and emphasize the recognition that it resides in the judgment of an observer. When we talk about problems, on the other hand, we normally assume that they exist on their own, independently of the observer. The distinction of break, therefore, allows us to differentiate ourselves from that tradition.

This implies, for example, a desire to differentiate ourselves from approaches based on "problem-solving" models. For these approaches, given that problems exist "outside" the observer, the only course of action left to the observer is to take charge of "solving" them. Therefore, little attention is paid to the conditions that "define" a problem as such. On many occasions, examining the process of defining a problem is more important than solving it. As we have insisted, many problems do not need to be "solved," but rather "dissolved."

Every problem is always a function of the interpretation that underpins it and from which it is classified as a problem. This interpretation should not always be taken for granted and should be open to discussion. In doing so, what previously appeared to be a problem may well disappear. Similarly, what was originally defined as a problem, when the interpretation that underpins it is modified, may now appear as a great opportunity.

This opens up the possibility of observing the observer who makes a judgment about the judgment that generates the breakdown as a breakdown. There is no reason to take such a judgment for granted. We can ask ourselves, for example, "Why is this problem a problem for me?" In doing so, we cease to be possessed by our spontaneous judgments. We can also ask ourselves, "How might it be possible to transform this problem into an opportunity?"

But we can also do something else. Since the observer, whether they know it or not, is the one who constitutes a situation of breakdown and, therefore, the one who generates it, it is not necessary to wait for breakdowns to "happen" to us, even though they will happen to us, whether we like it or not. Now we can also design the declaration of breakdowns. Since breakdowns are judgments—and judgments, as we know, are declarations—we have the option of declaring something a breakdown. We don't have to wait for them to happen to us.

We can declare, for example, "This relationship I am in, as it is, is not working anymore. Either I change it or I end it." Or, "My daughter's grades at school are not acceptable to me. I will talk to her to see what we can do to improve them." Every time we declare "Enough!" we are in fact declaring a breakdown. Important differences in our lives result from the fact that there are those who, when faced with a similar situation, declare a break, while there are those who do not. There are those who do it earlier than others. Declaring a break is a fundamental resource in our life design. Depending on how competent or incompetent we are in doing so, our future will be different, as will our world and ourselves.

We can do this in any area of our lives, not just in our personal lives. In business, for example, the CEO can declare a break by saying, "In the future, it will no longer be satisfactory to receive the 10% return on investment that we have become accustomed to. From now on, we will not settle for less than 15%," or "We declare that the current design of our product must be significantly modified to better satisfy our customers," or "Instead of continuing to strive for a larger market share, we will focus on reducing costs and increasing profits."

In all these examples, something that was not a "problem" in the past was declared as such in the present, or something that was a "problem" (gaining a larger market share) ceased to be one through a declaration.

Two sources in the declaration of failures

We declare our satisfaction or dissatisfaction with the course of events. We do so triggered by some external event or as an autonomous, deliberate action. By declaring our dissatisfaction with the way things are, we now have the option to change them, to make them different. Breaks generally imply the judgment that things could have been different. They are based on not accepting the normal course of events—either we are dissatisfied with the way things are, or we envision ways to make them better.

This allows us to identify two ways in which breaks occur. The first, perhaps the most common, refers to situations in which the break appears as such; without our realizing it, it emerges from a judgment we make. These are situations in which there is a consensus in our community, often not even explicit, about what to expect. As a result, certain events are automatically considered breaks. Therefore, when these events take place, they do not seem to require us to make any judgment. The judgment preceded the event.

The same is true of the positive or negative sign of what we usually consider a break. If, for example, someone close to us dies or we win the lottery, we have the impression that the negative in one case or the positive in the other is accompanied by what happened and there seems to be no room for us to make any judgment. One event is negative, the other positive, period. What happens in this case is that the observer carries a judgment of rupture that belongs to the historical discourse of their community and, therefore, does so automatically, without even choosing to do so.

The second type of occurrence is one in which the break does not arise as a spontaneous expression of a particular historical discourse that affects the members of a community, but precisely because an individual decides to declare it. Within the historical constraints to which we are all subject, the individual has the capacity and autonomy to declare varying degrees of satisfaction or dissatisfaction. What may be perfectly acceptable to one person may be declared unacceptable to another. And the lives of both will be different according to how they use their capacity to declare breaks. A typical circumstance that allows us to appreciate the power of declaring a break occurs when we decide to learn something. Things happen as they happen, and we judge that we would do better if we had certain skills that we do not have. Therefore, we declare the normal course of events to be a break for us and commit ourselves to learning, to acquiring new skills that will allow us to be more effective in the future. Under the same circumstances, another person could perfectly well continue to perform as they always have. By declaring the break, we create a new space of possibilities for ourselves.

Based on what has been said, it is worth looking back and examining how what we have argued affects those two assumptions of our traditional conception. If we accept the proposed interpretation, it follows that we cannot assume the natural presence of the world of objects. This world as a world of objects and we as subjects in that world only constitute ourselves as such when there is a break in the transparent flow of life. Under the primary condition of transparency, the world is not revealed to us as a world of objects simply because it is there, before our eyes. Nor do we conceive of ourselves as subjects operating in a world.

The same can be said with regard to the assumption that human action is rational action. Without denying the experience of such action, it constitutes a type of derivative action. The primary form of human action is transparent action, where the role of consciousness, thought, or reason as a "guide" for the action we perform is not fulfilled. Only when this transparent action is interrupted does rational action emerge.

The rationalist model also assumes that the greater our level of competence, the greater the role of reason in our performance. Hubert L. Dreyfus, following Heidegger, points out that such a relationship is in fact the opposite. The more competent we are in what we do, the greater our level of transparency. Competence is associated not with the expansion of the domain of reason, but with the expansion of transparency. With what we call the ability to "embody" competencies in our performance.

Again, this does not deny the role of reason, but rather reevaluates and reposition it. From this perspective, Pete Sampras' response when asked, after winning the Wimbledon tennis championship, "What do you think about when you're playing?" is not surprising. Sampras replied, "When I play, I don't think. I just react."


 

Language and action


 

One of our recurring themes has been to reiterate that our traditional conception offers a narrow and restrictive interpretation of language. We have argued that language is neither passive nor descriptive; we have maintained that language is action. It is not surprising, then, that action seems to share center stage in the ontology of language. In one way or another, the reference to action has permeated many of the topics we have discussed.

Thus, for example, we have made constant reference to action when discussing different linguistic acts. In a different domain, we have also argued that our actions make us who we are. Elaborating on this point, we have pointed out that humans are not already constituted beings with fixed and permanent properties, but beings who are in constant change, and that this change is, to a large extent, the result of the actions they perform. We have therefore concluded that by changing the way we act, we change the way we are. We are what we do.

It is surprising, however, that when we examine the relationship between the person and the action, we normally only consider the aspect of it that regards the person as the agent producing the action and do not explore the relationship in the opposite sense—from the point of view of the actions that are producing the person. Our identity depends on the actions we perform.

What we wish to highlight here is the importance we have given to action by placing both language and human beings in reference to it. This has been one of the central operations of our argument. Through it, we have expanded and transformed our conception of language and developed new forms of discernment in relation to the human phenomenon. However, we see two problems arising from this, and we want to address them.

First, when we talk about action, we usually take action for granted. We assume we know what it is. Does anyone need to have action explained to them? Of course not. We all seem to know perfectly well what we are talking about. At least, that is what we assume. At a certain level, this is a valid assumption. We all have a certain understanding, thanks to common sense, of what action is.

But let's not forget that when we began our inquiry, we also had an understanding of language provided by common sense. However, we hope that at this point, as a result of our inquiry, we can agree that our previous conception of language could be questioned. Couldn't we say the same thing about action? This is part of what we would like to explore in this section.

Secondly, by referring language to action, one might assume that we have created a path that allows us to leave the sphere of language. By turning language into action, the illusion might arise that we have found a "measure of conversion" of language that transforms it into something non-linguistic. If so, one might think that it is possible for us to place ourselves outside of it. Action would appear as the final reference for what we are saying, and it could now be used to negate our first and fundamental postulate: human beings live in language. If this were the case, ours would be more of an ontology of action.

We maintain that there is no way to escape language, no possible exit. Human beings live trapped in language. And when we ask ourselves what action is, we find language everywhere. There is a hermeneutic circularity between language and action. Language is action but, at the same time, as we will see later, action is language.


 

Action as a linguistic distinction

What is action? The first thing we must admit when asking ourselves about the meaning of action is that action is a linguistic distinction. First and foremost, action is a distinction we make in language.

Every time we make a distinction, we separate a particular phenomenon from the rest of our experiences. Therefore, when we talk about action, we are making a distinction—that is, we are distinguishing or separating something from the background of human experience. Action exists as a distinction we make in language.

It is important to observe our distinctions as such and not merely as names of things. Things do not have names. We give them names. And the process of naming them often constitutes them as the things they are for us. By observing our distinctions as such, we are highlighting the operation of making the distinction. Every distinction is always the result of an operation of distinction. Distinctions are our doing. In making them, we specify the units and entities that populate our world. We cannot observe something for which we have no distinction. This is why we repeatedly say that although we see with our eyes, we observe with our distinctions. People with different sets of distinctions live in different worlds.

Certainly our senses play an important role in what we observe. If our biological structure does not detect certain sounds, it is unlikely that we will be able to generate the distinctions that allow us to observe them. In order to observe them, we must bring them, directly or indirectly, into the realm of our experience. Some instruments, such as the microscope or the telescope, have precisely this ability to expand the range of our experiences and allow our senses to capture what they could not under normal circumstances.

At the same time, our biological structure provides us with certain perceptual experiences that stimulate us to generate distinctions. When we observe a flash of light against a black background—as happens in the medical test that checks the breadth of our visual field—we need more than just the distinction of a flash of light. Having the distinction of a flash of light is not enough for us to observe it. A perceptual experience is needed. At the same time, we cannot observe a flash of light without having the distinction of a flash of light. We can see something, but what that something is for us will depend on our distinctions. Our biological structure and our linguistic distinctions often collaborate with each other in our observations.

An interesting example of the role our linguistic distinctions play is the way humans observed the sky in ancient times. Although we can say that the ancients were seeing the same thing as us from a biological structure point of view, their different distinctions produced worlds different from ours. For some, what we call stars were a kind of lit lamps hanging from a dark ceiling. For others, they were small holes in a dark ceiling through which one could see the light beyond the ceiling. And still others observed luminous objects in an open and infinite universe. Different distinctions, different worlds.

Many distinctions have no biological basis, that is, they do not correspond to any biological perception. They are generated on the basis of facilitating and structuring the experience of living with others and giving meaning to our lives. These distinctions are entirely linguistic. Most of our moral, political, religious, and metaphysical distinctions belong to this category. The distinctions we use when making judgments are of this kind. When we say "Benito is a visionary" or "Natalia is extremely industrious," the distinctions "visionary" and "industrious" have no biological basis whatsoever. It is a linguistic distinction that is entirely socially constructed.

Although every distinction already implies a first intervention of language on what is being talked about, it is not enough to point to the fact that something is a linguistic distinction to conclude that what is being pointed to by the distinction is linguistic in nature. That would be like arguing that because "dog" is a linguistic distinction, it proves that dogs are linguistic. In doing so, we ignore the need to distinguish between reference and meaning, connotation and denotation, sign and significance, and so on. We therefore confuse what belongs to the sphere of names with what belongs to the sphere of what they specify. Just as the linguistic distinction of sugar does not make sugar linguistic, neither does the fact that sugar is sweet make the distinction sugar sweet. What matters, therefore, is not only the recognition that the action is a distinction made by an observer, but the type of distinction that the action implies.


 

The uses of the distinction of action

What kind of distinction is the distinction of action? As we shall see, there is no simple answer to this question. To arrive at an answer, we need to examine how the distinction of action is used. Let us recall Ludwig Wittgenstein's statement, "The meaning of a word is its use in language." If we want to unveil the meaning of the distinction of action, we have to examine how this distinction is used.

The first use we detect is characterized by the use of the distinction of action in relation to natural phenomena. In this sense, we speak, for example, of the effects of "the action of a storm or an earthquake," "the action or reaction produced by certain chemicals." Just as we speak of action, the factor attributed with triggering such action is usually called an "agent." We therefore speak of certain "chemical agents."

We maintain that when the distinction of action is used to describe natural phenomena—such as those indicated above, whose use is metaphorical—we treat the supposed factor that produces the action as if it were a human being. Whenever a change is observed and a cause that produced it is referred to, the cause is usually called the "agent" and the change the "action." But in doing so, by extending the distinction to natural phenomena, the distinction becomes obscured when we use it to account for human behavior.

Let us therefore examine the uses of the distinction when it is used in relation to phenomena of human behavior. In fact, when we talk about human action, the two factors noted above are present: some observable change and the intervention of an agent. This time, however, the term "agent" is not used to refer to a cause, but to point to human beings who operate individually or collectively, endowed with will.

This has allowed for two different meanings of human action (two forms of the distinction of action when used in reference to human phenomena)—action as movement and action as intentional or purposeful behavior. The first meaning emphasizes change; the second emphasizes the deliberate intervention of the agent.

Based on the first meaning, we use the distinction of action when we see someone running, walking, moving their hands, breathing, and so on. Here, action appears independently of the intentions involved and even independently of the fact that we can attribute any intention or purpose to it.

Based on the second meaning, we speak of action when we can point to the agent's intention, even when there is no perceptible movement. Here, we can talk about actions such as "chasing someone," "getting married," etc., but also about actions such as "waiting for someone," "thinking," "remembering," or "forgetting." In these latter examples, it is sometimes difficult to associate the action with movement.

Finally, it is worth mentioning the use of the distinction of action applied this time to supernatural agents (gods, divine forces, etc.). In these cases, the same factors involved in human action are also present: an observable change and an intervening agent. It is, therefore, the same structure that we recognized in the case of human action, transplanted to supposed supernatural or superhuman agents.

Activity versus action

Having identified four different uses of the distinction of action, we will focus on the two that are related to human action. We will discard the first because, as we pointed out, we consider it a metaphorical use, and we will also discard the last because we consider it to be based on the same conditions that emerge when talking about human action. If we accept the existence of supernatural beings, the same things we say about human action would apply to them.

We are therefore left with two meanings of human action: action as movement and action as intentional or purposeful intervention. In fact, the Austrian economist Ludwig von Mises, one of the great thinkers on the subject, begins his work on human action by proclaiming, "Human action is purposeful behavior." For von Mises, it is purpose that defines human action. Human action is rational action.

We assume that, at this point, it will come as no surprise to the reader that we find this distinction between human action (action as movement and as purposeful behavior) problematic. This is for two reasons. First, because by accepting action as movement, insofar as we can speak of movement with respect to natural phenomena, we once again leave the door open to speak of action in a metaphorical sense. Second, because by accepting action as purposeful behavior, we are once again caught up in the issue of intentionality and in a rationalist interpretation of human action.

At the same time, we must recognize that the effort to draw a distinction between two different types of phenomena, expressed in the ideas of movement and purposeful behavior, is valid. To show why we find it valid, we will give an example. Let us imagine that, as often happens, I am walking in front of a group of students talking to them about the ontology of language. At a certain point, I ask Amalia, one of my students, "Amalia, how important is what I am saying?" If at that moment someone asked me what I was doing, there are two different ways to respond.

One is governed by the language of statements and makes assertions with which anyone present in the room would agree. Responding in this way, the following assertions could be made:

— I am walking;

— I am moving my arm toward Amalia;

— I am talking;

— I am looking at Amalia;

— I asked Amalia a question;

— I asked, "Amalia, how important is what I'm saying?";

— and so on.

All of these statements are accurate descriptions of what someone would observe me doing, and anyone who was there would agree that these were all actions I was performing. I suggest we use the distinction of activity rather than action when an action can be circumscribed in the language of statements.

But there is another way in which the question about what I was doing can also be answered. One could say, for example:

— I was teaching a group of students;

— I was earning a living as a teacher;

— I was concerned that they understood me;

— I was expanding the students' conception of language;

— I was making sure Amalia understood the importance of what I had said;

— etc.

This is a very different way of talking about my actions. All of these statements are closer to the language of judgments, since people can legitimately challenge them. Is this really teaching? Can we really say that what I get out of doing that can be called "earning a living"? Was I really concerned that they understood me? Was I really expanding the students' conception of language? Or was I perhaps confusing them? Was I really making sure that Amalia understood? Or was I perhaps exposing her to ridicule? People may have different opinions about each of these answers about the action I was performing.

Let's examine these statements more closely. We maintain that what characterizes them is the fact that they give meaning to what I was doing. This occurs when referring to what I was doing in relation to the domain of concerns. All of these statements interpret what I was doing and, in order to do so, assume that by acting, I am taking care of some concerns. Since they give meaning to what I was doing, we will call them semantic statements.

What characterizes them and differentiates them from the previous activities is the fact that, in addition to recognizing an activity, they add an interpretive component that justifies or explains the activity and, in order to do so, they make implicit reference to concerns. Performing an operation that we have already used several times in the course of our argument, we will say that human action is an activity that is interpreted by referring it to the domain of concerns [19]. Human action is activity plus interpretation.

We propose limiting the distinction of action to semantic assertions and, therefore, distinguishing activity from action. We postulate that semantic assertions are different from statements of activity. Every time we talk about action, we can ask ourselves, "What motivates that person to do what they are doing?"

However, we are not saying that a statement of activity cannot also be, in many cases, a statement of action. Very often we say, for example, "Alexander looked at me and sat down," and within the context of what is being said, we can explicitly or implicitly identify some concern on Alexander's part in doing so. In this case, as in any other where we can attribute concerns to the action, we are going to talk about action.

From the point of view of distinguishing activities, we could say, for example, "Juanita is running." This is what she is doing, and we will assume that we cannot attribute any concern to this fact. We enter the realm of action when, upon seeing her run, we say "Juanita was running away from the dog," "Juanita was chasing the kite," "Juanita was exercising," and so on. In all these circumstances, we are giving meaning to "running" and, therefore, we are making semantic assertions.

What is interesting about this way of observing action is the fact that it is the observer who makes the action meaningful. Therefore, different observers can make very different semantic assertions when talking about the same situation. This happens because they make their observations from their own distinct concerns. Depending on the concerns from which we make our observations, we will attribute different concerns to others.

The purpose, intention, or motivation postulated by the traditional conception of action are, we reiterate, nothing more than the agent's interpretations of their own actions. And as we pointed out earlier, such interpretations do not exclude the possibility of others, made by other people, which may prove to be even more powerful than those of the agent themselves.

But there is another additional advantage to accepting the approach we propose. This is that it is no longer necessary to postulate reason, consciousness, or thought as a precondition for action. With this, we make room within human action for all transparent forms of acting. With transparent actions, we can continue to interpret that by acting we are taking care of one or more concerns and can simultaneously accept the absence of prior deliberation.

Fernando Flores, in his doctoral thesis, provides us with an interesting example that illustrates what we are saying. Flores imagines someone typing on a certain day in the evening. A colleague calls him on the phone and asks, "What are you doing?" The character replies, "Right now, I'm writing a new version of chapter three." The phone rings again, and this time it's his editor, who asks the same question, "What are you doing?" Flores' character now replies, "I'm teaching a course on the mind and writing two books on intentionality, in which I propose a new notion that I believe will change the design of machines." Later, his character's wife appears and says, "I'm sorry I'm late, the car broke down and I couldn't let you know. I hope you're not angry." This time, Flores' character replies, "It's okay, I was waiting for you, but I was starting to worry about you."

The example offered by Flores shows us that the same observer, when in conversation with others, can give different interpretations to his own actions depending on his interpretation of the other person's concerns. All the answers given by his character are valid answers, and each one reinterprets what was being done in a different way. Only by understanding the role of interpretation in specifying action can we understand the profoundly linguistic nature of human action.


 

Direct and reflective action

One of the ways in which our common sense uses the distinction of action is by contrasting action with "talking about things" or "thinking about things," as if talking or thinking were not actions in themselves. In the same way, theory is contrasted with practice, and the realm of ideas is separated from the realm of doing. Our everyday language is full of expressions that reflect this distinction. We say, for example, "Actions speak louder than words!", "Stop talking and do something!", "Those are just words!", and so on.

When we speak in this way, our common sense points to a distinction that is clearly valid and that we will try to recover in this section. However, the way in which common sense makes this distinction reinforces the concealment of the fact that language is action. What is more, it also conceals the important practical consequences that often result from talking or thinking "about" something. And, therefore, it hides the responsibility that we have when we speak.

We sometimes hear phrases such as "I was just saying," as if saying something did not commit us to anything. Let us repeat once again: our speech is not trivial; it changes our world and shapes our identity. Nor is our speech innocent; we are responsible for the consequences of what we say and what we do not say. Our successes and failures are shaped in our conversations.

Therefore, when we separate talking or thinking about something from acting on it, we blind ourselves to the practical consequences that can result from talking and thinking. We often realize that by talking and thinking "about" something, we end up acting "on" it in a much more effective or even different way than our original intention. The practices of talking or thinking "about" are an integral part of the overall structure of human action.

We argued, however, that by separating talking from acting, our common sense reveals something that we should not dismiss. There is obviously an important difference between talking about something and doing it. The difference, however, is not what our common sense understands, attributing non-action on the one hand and action on the other. Action is found on both sides. The difference is that these are two different kinds of actions.

We have pointed out that human language is characterized by its recursiveness, by its ability to turn back on itself. Because of this, we are able to act on our actions. And since speaking is action, this includes speaking (which is acting) about our actions, acting on our speech (which is acting), and speaking (which is acting) about our speech (which is also acting). This recursive capacity, as we have said, is at the root of mental phenomena. Consciousness, reason, thought, and reflection are based on it.

Having questioned the rationalist interpretation of human action, we are now interested in rescuing the importance of mental phenomena and not subsuming them under a flat notion of human action that does not recognize the different levels of acting that result from the recursive capacity of language. Our criticism of the rationalist interpretation of human action does not lead us to a criticism of rationality, nor to a defense of irrationality. It only leads us to rethink the role we have traditionally assigned to reason or reflection.

It is no coincidence that we associate the term reflection with thought. We also speak of reflection when we see our image reflected in a mirror or in water. By seeing their own image, observers can observe themselves. This is precisely what language can do thanks to its recursive capacity. It can turn back on itself and talk about its own speech. It allows the observer to observe themselves. This is a fundamental and unique human ability. This is where, as a species, we differ from all others. This is what makes human language different, whether qualitatively or quantitatively, from the linguistic abilities of other living beings.

From this, we can make a distinction between two kinds of actions: direct and reflexive. These are functional or relational distinctions—they only make sense within a certain relationship. There are no direct or reflexive actions per se. We speak of reflexive action when we fix the action at a certain level and act on that action.

Let's take an example. We are engaged in the action of sculpting a rock. This is an action directed toward the rock. If we discover that we cannot make a certain cut, we can suspend what we are doing and retreat to speculate about other ways of doing it. This second action is not directed toward the rock but toward the action of sculpting it. In this example, sculpting the rock is the direct action, and speculating about other ways of doing it (acting on the action of sculpting it) is the reflective action.

It may happen, however, that we fail to discover a better way to make the cut in the rock. We can then say, "Hey, maybe I'm not reflecting effectively. I'm not concentrating well, etc." I can then decide to reflect, no longer on the action of sculpting the rock but on the action of reflecting on the action of sculpting the rock. As we can see, this can go on indefinitely. In doing so, reflecting on the way I was previously reflecting becomes reflective action. Furthermore, my previous reflection (which constituted reflective action in the previous example) takes on the role of direct action in this second reflection.

Recursion allows us infinite progression. We can become the observer of the observer that we are, and the observer of the observer of the observer that we are, and so on, in an endless recursion. Each time we move to a higher level, we transform what was previously a reflective action into direct action. That is why we say that these are functional or relational distinctions. Each of them makes sense in relation to the other or in relation to it.

By resorting to reflective action, we can increase our effectiveness at the level of direct action. This is the great benefit of reflective action, and it can occur in several ways. We will mention three different ways in which reflective action can serve direct action.

First, reflective action intervenes in the meaning of what we are doing and, as a result, can contribute, for example, to expanding or simply modifying it. When we act, we often do so based on a certain narrative within which we give meaning to our action. This narrative belongs, with respect to the action to which it gives meaning, to the reflective level.

Our capacity for action is dependent on the narrative from which we act, and we can have narratives that are more or less powerful, more or less coherent with other narratives we have. Therefore, by reflecting, we can increase the power of our narratives and, consequently, the meaning of our actions.

Secondly, we also reflect in order to examine and, eventually, broaden the horizon of possibilities within which we act. We always act within a certain horizon of possibilities. And it is from our horizons that we see a greater or lesser number of alternatives for ourselves. Let us not lose sight of the fact that when we talk about possibilities, we are always talking about the range of possible actions. Possibility always refers to possible actions. Reflecting on our horizons of possibilities is another way of intervening in our actions. Reflection allows us to invent the possible.

Thirdly, there is also the reflection we call design. Designing is an action that seeks better ways of using means to achieve our ends. It aims to create a guideline that guides our actions to ensure higher levels of effectiveness in achieving our goals. There are two main types of design—planning and strategy.

When we talk about planning, we refer to the design of actions within a relatively protected space in which the focus is on the efficient use of resources to achieve a specific objective. Generally, the objective is given, and what matters is the best way to use the available means to achieve it. It is technological in nature and is a common task for engineers.

When we talk about strategy, the conditions are very different. Strategy emerges as a way of designing our actions under conditions of reciprocal action. This time, the focus is not on the efficient use of resources, but on the actions that other agents with autonomous capacity for action may take, affecting the effectiveness of our own actions. This implies that our actions require us to anticipate the actions of others and be designed accordingly, while the actions of others also seek to anticipate ours and are designed accordingly. If planning is a fundamentally technological type of action design, strategy is essentially political. In strategic design, the problem of power is always at the center of what is defined as possible.

These three forms of reflection support and increase our capacity for direct action. And every time we judge that we are not being as effective as we would like to be, that something is wrong, an opportunity for reflection arises. When this happens, we should ask ourselves: Is my story about why I am doing what I am doing strong enough? Could I introduce new possibilities that I have not yet considered? Is there a better way to use my resources (means) to achieve my goals (ends)?

Reflective action, therefore, makes sense because it increases the power of direct action. It is an action that must always, sooner or later, return to the level of direct action where it originated, because that is where it is validated. This is its value and importance. In the end, we can always question reflective action from the level of direct action and ask ourselves: So what? Or how important is this really?

One of the problems we detect in reflective action is that it runs the risk of losing its effective link to the level of direct action it should serve. Often, reflective action becomes so autonomous that we lose sight of what it feeds. Or it translates into immobility at the level of direct action and, ultimately, at the level of the meaning of life. This is often what is meant when common sense exclaims, "Stop talking (or thinking) and act!" When we say this, we usually imply that we believe reflection has ceased to serve direct action.


 

Contingent and recurrent action

When talking about action (in addition to direct and reflective action), we can make another distinction. This is the distinction between contingent and recurrent action.

Human beings often act according to circumstances and respond in a nonspecific way to events that occur in their lives. We will call contingent action the action that is generated when we do not have an established way of acting, therefore, when there are no established rules for acting. This action is contingent on the nature of the existing circumstances. Many of the actions we perform belong to this category.

However, we also develop recurring ways of dealing with those concerns and disruptions that are permanent. We institutionalize certain ways of dealing with some events, of doing things, of addressing certain concerns and disruptions. To do this, human communities create a series of action structures that they execute repeatedly. These recurring actions differ from contingent actions. For one thing, we can foresee them. In a given society, we can expect people to behave in a certain way when faced with certain types of disruptions. We call these recurring actions social practices.

Social practices are historical. This means that they have not always existed and that they can vary from one historical period to another and from one society to another. Social practices occur within a particular historical drift. Sometimes social practices are designed; someone invents them and others apply them. Many other times they are generated by accident and are maintained simply because they work and because no one comes up with a better alternative.

One of the characteristics of social practices is that they often become transparent. People do things in the established way without even thinking about the actions they are performing. Very often, therefore, they are carried out as non-deliberative (unconscious) actions. This brings significant advantages and also some disadvantages.

On the positive side, social practices allow us to achieve a certain level of effectiveness, while remaining in the background of our actions. Their transparency allows us to build on them without paying too much attention to them and to focus on the contingent aspects of events or on those areas that can allow us to expand our capacity for action. One of the objectives of "practicing" certain skills is precisely to be able to make them transparent so that we can free up our attention to a different level of action.

When everyone in a community embodies the same social practices, the level of coordination of actions increases, since everyone acts from a shared background. There are many things that do not need to be made explicit. People move as if in a dance, knowing all the steps that go with the music.

Dance is, in fact, a good example of social practices. When people know how to dance, their actions and steps unfold transparently. They can "let themselves go," enjoy the dance, and focus on other things. The same thing happens in sports. It is only when we know the game, its rules, and how to perform the basic movements that we can "forget" ourselves and get results. This happens in all areas of our lives. There are social practices for greeting someone on the street, for talking on the phone, for listening to a lecture, for driving on the road, and so on.

The downside of social practices is that, as they become transparent, we tend to lose our ability to observe them. We become so accustomed to doing things in the established way that we assume it is the obvious, only, and natural way to do them. We simply do not realize that other people may approach the same situations very differently. We may miss the opportunity to deal with our challenges in a way that is perhaps much more efficient.

Our social practices become habits—the place from which we view the world and from which we act—sometimes to the point where we no longer see them. They are so close to us that we cease to perceive them. Social practices create blindness. Let us not forget, then, that being entrenched in social practices can often weaken us and diminish our ability to grow, learn, and innovate.

Organizations benefit from the power of social practices. Having many individuals operating transparently in a consistent manner has many advantages. An organization with few social practices (with few established systems) will spend more time doing what another will execute almost effortlessly. There is much to be gained by designing social practices that allow for more fluid coordination of actions among those who work together.

But at the same time, the organization will also benefit from observing and reflecting on its own social practices. Otherwise, it can become a powerful conservative force that resists change and can negatively impact the organization's competitive ability. The technologies of "reengineering" or process design, so fashionable these days in the field of business consulting, aim precisely to analyze, evaluate, and redesign the usual practices of an organization which, because they are habitual, are hidden and cease to be the object of evaluation and design.


 

The linguistic reconstruction of social practices

Another interesting way in which the connection between action and language appears is through the fact that we can proceed to a linguistic reconstruction of social practices. In doing so, we treat each social practice as if it were a game with certain objectives and rules that specify it. We can therefore say that social practices can be treated as language games.

We owe it to Ludwig Wittgenstein for postulating this relationship between language and games and for raising the possibility of examining language as linguistic games and, therefore, as social practices [20]. What we will do, however, is use Wittgenstein's proposal in reverse. For Wittgenstein, the distinction between language games allowed for an understanding of language by reference to social practices. For us, the same distinction between language games will allow us to better understand social practices by reference to language. It is in this sense that we speak of a linguistic reconstruction of social practices.

Games are useful for broadening our understanding of social practices, since they are themselves simplified social practices and have an explicit linguistic structure that we normally use when teaching the game to someone who does not know it. Another similar phenomenon is that of cooking recipes. They also represent an excellent example of a linguistic reconstruction of a particular social practice leading to the production of a particular food.

As we can see, the linguistic reconstruction of social practices is nothing new. We do it all the time in many different areas of life. It is what every instruction manual does: it uses language to introduce us to a particular practice. It guides us, step by step, through language, in learning a game. This therefore encompasses an entire concept of learning, an entire pedagogy. But that is a subject for another book.

What is not so common, however, is to use this same procedure in the opposite direction. Not to introduce us to a particular practice that is new to us, but as a way of evaluating and redesigning those practices that we constantly perform and, therefore, as a mechanism for transforming existing practices.

There are often those who believe that by treating social practices as games, we are trivializing them. There is nothing trivial about it. Games are social practices. Their great advantage is that they provide us with a model that we can use as a generalized expression of all social practices. This advantage arises from the fact that, since games are usually designed, their linguistic structure appears explicitly in the design process. And since many games need to be taught, this same linguistic structure is also made explicit during the teaching process.

A criticism that is often made of us relates to the "unreality" of games. "A game is just a game," we are told. The social practices that interest us have to do with life, not virtual worlds. If we learn anything fundamental from the axis that links Heraclitus to Nietzsche, it is precisely, to paraphrase Calderón de la Barca, that life is a game.

Only by understanding this can we enter the threshold of what Nietzsche calls "the innocence of becoming." Only then can we overcome the unbearable weight that, again in Nietzsche's words, comes from the "spirit of gravity." If ontology of language teaches us anything, it is to distrust those concepts that constitute the heart of metaphysics: Truth, Reality, Matter, Idea. If we learn anything from it, by understanding the relationship we postulate between language and existence, it is that virtuality governs reality.

Having said that, let us now focus on the process of linguistic reconstruction of social practices and how games are useful to us in this regard.

When we play a game, we can identify different kinds of rules. It should be noted that every rule represents a statement. This is its status as a linguistic act. These rules can be classified into five categories.


 

We can distinguish a first type of rules or constitutive statements. These state what players are pursuing when they act in the game and therefore define the objective or purpose of the game. Every social practice exists as a way of satisfying a particular purpose, and this purpose can be reconstructed as a particular rule or statement.

Normally, the objective of the game allows us to identify the concern that the game, like any social practice, addresses. But such a concern, in the context of the game, takes on a concrete expression and is related to a specific result that is sought. Take the example of soccer. The objective of the game, in terms of the concern it addresses, is to win the match. But there is a specific way to do this. To this end, it is necessary to have scored more goals than the opposing team at the end of the match. This is, therefore, the specific objective of soccer.

Next, we must distinguish a second type of rules or statements that constitute all social practices. We will call these statements of existence. As such, they specify the entities necessary to play the game, as well as the spatial and temporal scope within which the action takes place.

Once we know what the objective of the game is, it is important to determine the world in which the game will take place and, therefore, the entities that populate that world, as well as its spatial and temporal parameters. This involves performing a set of declarative acts that generate that world, its entities, its space, and its time. In the game of soccer, to continue with the same example, we therefore define a field of certain dimensions and with certain areas, the goals and their dimensions, two halves of 45 minutes each, the existence of two teams with eleven players each, the existence in each of these teams of a goalkeeper, a ball, a referee, and two linesmen, the goal, and so on.

Finally, we can distinguish a third type of rules or constitutive statements. These correspond to what we normally recognize as the laws of action of the game and, as such, define, like any law, what players are prohibited from doing, allowed to do, or required to do when acting.

In our soccer example, these rules include, for example, that only the goalkeeper can touch the ball with his hand when it is in play and only within his area, that a player from the opposing team cannot be hit, that the referee has the authority to declare when a goal has been scored, and so on. The rules of action normally include penalties for non-compliance.

No game or social practice can do without these three types of constitutive statements. This implies that every social practice can be reconstructed in terms of its objective or purpose, the world of entities it brings to hand, and the laws of action that regulate its development. These constitutive statements account for the fundamental linguistic structure of every social practice. When there are changes in any of them, we are faced with a different social practice. In other words, by changing the constitutive statements of an existing social practice, we generate new practices. This is a common procedure in the innovation processes of our social practices.

In addition to these constitutive statements, we can distinguish two additional types of statements. These are strategic rules and rules for conflict resolution.

As we have pointed out, strategic rules are not part of the constitutive statements of a game. This means that the game (or the social practice we are reconstructing) can be played without them. Their importance lies in the fact that they allow us to play better and, therefore, help us to be more effective in achieving the objective pursued by a given social practice. Strategic rules are usually the result of learning from past experiences in which we were able to observe the consequences (both positive and negative) of a particular action or mode of action.

In the case of soccer, for example, some teams may prefer to play the game according to the rules of 4-3-3, others may opt for 4-2-4, others for 4-4-2, and so on. Within the space defined by the constitutive rules, there can be infinite strategic ways of playing the game.

Rules for conflict resolution, while not essential, are often required for two different reasons. First, because any rule or constitutive statement can be interpreted differently by different players, and this raises the need for some means of resolving these differences. Second, because situations may arise that are not covered by the rules, situations that players do not know how to resolve and that require some authority to which they can appeal and to which they have, in advance, conferred the authority to intervene.

In the case of soccer, this is usually resolved through the creation of a Disciplinary Tribunal, dependent on a governing body (National Soccer Association, Regional Confederations, FIFA, etc.) that has authority over the teams participating in the game (soccer championships).

We can now use the above distinctions to reconstruct social practices. The objective of the game allows us to specify the concerns or breaches that this social practice addresses. The statements of existence allow us to identify the elements and space that are relevant to addressing these breaches and concerns within that social practice. The laws of action tell us what "can" be done, what "cannot" be done, and what "must" be done within it. Using these distinctions, we can reconstruct any social practice.

This linguistic reconstruction of social practices opens up a new domain of action—the domain of innovation and design. We can now reconstruct existing social practices. Once this is done, we can freely play with each of its components and examine whether, by changing one or more of them, we can find more effective ways to address the concerns and breaks that this social practice deals with. When we do this, we are innovating in existing social practices.

We now know that in order to design completely new practices, we must clearly establish the objective of the game (the concern or breakdown we are addressing), its statements of existence, and its laws of action. Once a social practice has been designed, we should observe how effectively it operates and judge what would need to be corrected to increase its effectiveness.

Every time we face a recurring disruption, if no social practice has been established to deal with it, we can hear a call to design one. By doing this, we stop dealing with recurring disruptions with contingent actions, which require greater effort and are more costly. Now we can deal with recurring disruptions with recurring actions.
