THE POWER OF CONVERSATIONS

In previous chapters, we have referred to the two facets of language: speaking and listening. As important as this distinction may be, it is, in a sense, an artificial one. We are by no means invalidating it; it points to two different dimensions of language. However, in the concrete experience of human communication, there is no such thing as speaking without listening or listening without speaking.

Every time someone speaks, there is someone listening. Usually, the listener is another person. But even when there is no other person—when we talk to ourselves—there is always the listening of the person who speaks. When we speak, we also listen to what we say. On the other hand, when we listen, there is always someone speaking. Again, this speaking may come from another person or from ourselves. Even when we listen to silences, there is the speaking of what we tell ourselves about them.

In communication, therefore, there is no speaking without listening and vice versa. When speaking and listening are interacting together, we are in the presence of a "conversation." A conversation, therefore, is the dance that takes place between speaking and listening, and between listening and speaking. Conversations are the effective components of linguistic interactions—the basic units of language. Therefore, whenever we deal with language, we are dealing, directly or indirectly, with conversations.

We can distinguish between several types of conversations. In fact, we have an infinite capacity to make distinctions within the vast domain of conversations, and we do so according to what we want to achieve, according to the concern that guides us. We can separate conversations according to their status (open or closed), the time they took place, the topic addressed, the person with whom we had the conversation, the importance or priority we give it, and so on. We can see that we have an endless capacity to make distinctions about conversations.

In this chapter, we will examine the relationship between conversations and breaks and explore the different types of conversations that can arise from a break. Based on this, we will offer a particular typology of conversations. Next, we will discuss how conversations shape our personal relationships. Finally, we will examine the importance of conversations in business organizations.


 

DESIGNING CONVERSATIONS

Breaks generally call for action. The way we deal with them is by taking action. It is through action that we restore broken transparency and deal with the consequences of the breakdown. However, the importance of action as a way of dealing with breakdowns is not obvious. This means that we often do not see the possibility of action as a way of responding to a breakdown. Very often we get "bogged down" in it. As a way of examining this connection between breaches and action, it is important to explore the various types of conversations that can follow a breach. These conversations will tell us whether we are moving toward action or have fallen into immobility. Based on these distinctions, we can then design the conversations that need to take place to address the breach effectively.

1. The personal judgment conversation

When faced with a breakdown, we usually resort to what we call "the conversation of personal judgments." In this conversation, we normally constitute what happened in a breakdown, although it also tends to extend beyond its constitution, almost spontaneously.

Let's take an example. I am driving my car with my dog in the back seat and I notice that I have a flat tire. My first reaction is an interjection (a statement). Some will say "Damn!", others will say "Shit!", others will exclaim "No way!" and there may be others who will say "Gosh!". What we say may vary from one community to another, but the fact is that a flat tire does not become a breakdown when it happens, but when someone, through a statement such as those above, makes it a breakdown, an interruption in the transparency of the flow of life.

Once the declaration of breakdown has been made, it is normal to enter into a chain of judgments through which we interpret what happened and the consequences that derive from it. We say, for example, "These things always happen to me!", "I'm so unlucky!", "This is my son's fault for not changing the tires when I asked him to!", "My day is ruined!", and so on.

The reaction we have described so far is what we understand as "the conversation of personal judgments." It is interesting to pause and examine it. The first thing to note is that this conversation is limited to judging the breakdown but does not yet move us to take responsibility for it. It implies a way of reacting (and, therefore, of acting) that does not move us away from the breakdown, that keeps us in it. And we could remain there for life, making one judgment after another and generating long stories based on them. If we proceed in this way, we insist, we do not generate the kind of action capable of restoring the satisfaction and transparency lost in the breakdown.

What's more, instead of taking responsibility for the breakdown, what we do is delve deeper into its explanation, its justification, its psychologization. We look for those responsible, those guilty, and, not satisfied with finding them, we now proceed to pass judgment on them. We usually have our favorite culprits. But those responsible are not only others; very often we are ourselves, and therefore we proceed with a chain of denigrating judgments about ourselves. We say, for example, "How stupid I am!", "When will I learn, idiot, to do things differently!", "This just proves once again how clumsy I am!" There are those who live their entire lives in a conversation of personal judgments, lamenting what happened, looking for those responsible, growing their psychologizing stories, and staying where they are.

The second aspect to note is that the same situation, or rather, the same type of setback, produces very different judgments in different people. Just because the event is the same, e.g., a flat tire, we cannot assume that the reactions will be the same. Psychologist Martin Seligman proposes observing the type of judgments (he calls them "explanatory styles") that people make when faced with a setback according to three different domains.

The first is what we might call the domain of responsibility (note that we are not using the terminology proposed by Seligman). The point to determine here is who one holds responsible for the setback: oneself or the world? There are those, Seligman tells us, who take responsibility for everything that happens to them. Each setback is further proof of how inadequate, incompetent, unintelligent, etc., they are. Others, on the other hand, rarely take responsibility themselves and usually blame others or attribute events to chance or bad luck. Depending on the type of judgments one makes in the domain of responsibility, one becomes a certain type of person and will live a certain type of life.

The second domain is what we might call the domain of inclusiveness. Every breakdown occurs in a particular domain of people's lives. Well, when we spontaneously proceed to make judgments triggered by the breakdown, there are those who, on the one hand, restrict their judgments to the particular domain that corresponds to what happened. Therefore, they assume that this is a situation that is limited to the domain of driving, or being punctual, or mathematics, or sociability with others, and so on, depending on the breakdown in question. But there are others who use the setback for a global disqualification, regardless of any domain, of those they hold responsible (themselves or others). Instead of saying, for example, "This proves that I am not competent in mathematics," they will say, "This proves once again how stupid I am." A different judgment, a different way of being, a different life.

The third and final domain is the domain of temporality. Every setback occurs in time and has consequences in time. Again, Seligman observes important differences in how time is involved in the judgments we make when facing a setback. For some, the consequences of the setback will be permanent and there is no way to change them. There are those who say, for example, "This destroys my life forever" or "It's clear that I'll never be able to do well in math." Never or always are the available alternatives. For others, however, the judgments they make based on the setback are only applicable to the occasion involved, and the consequences operate within a limited time frame. They will say, for example, "I'm going through a bad time" or "It's clear that I still don't know enough math." Very different judgments, different worlds and experiences. We can therefore argue: tell me how you judge your setbacks and I'll tell you who you are. This is, in fact, one of the central resources of "ontological coaching."

Let us not forget, however, that setbacks can be both negative and positive. The way we judge them as positive or negative can be very different, Seligman insists. Those who tend to take full responsibility when faced with a negative judgment may tend to blame others when faced with a positive setback. When faced with the negative, they will exclaim, "How stupid I am!"; when faced with the positive, "How lucky I was!" The opposite happens with those who tend to blame others for the negative. When faced with the positive, they may say, "My God, I did well!"

In the domain of inclusivity, those who tend to generalize the consequences of positive breaks can often maintain the particularity of negative ones. Conversely, those who limit the positive to the particular often generalize the negative. And in the domain of temporality, the same thing often happens. Many people who restrict the consequences of a positive break to the occasion on which it occurred may consider the negative ones to be permanent, and vice versa.

We are not arguing that we necessarily view positive and negative breaks in opposite ways. Many people make the same kind of judgments in both situations. What matters is to recognize that the way we judge negative breaks does not necessarily predict the way we will judge positive ones.

It is important to emphasize the consequences of remaining in the conversation of personal judgments and the consequent psychologization of what happened. Let's give an example. We ask one of our employees to obtain an important account for the company. The employee contacts the client, makes an offer, and it is refused. We consider this to be a significant setback for the company. The employee, however, writes a detailed report showing the results of his efforts and giving his interpretation of why his offer was not accepted. His report draws on an extensive characterization of the client, explaining why the client refused. We get a "long story" from him, possibly an intelligent and well-founded story. After the customer rejected the offer, the employee took action to make sense of what the customer did—and did a good job of it.

It is clear from this example that the action of making sense of the customer's action will not change their refusal. At least, not in itself. The action of "winning the account" stopped the moment the employee set out to make sense of the customer's refusal. At the level of "winning the account," the action of making sense of what happened translates into inaction. We could therefore call the employee aside and say, "Don't give me any excuses! I don't want explanations, I want results. If the customer said no to the offer we made, see what other offer would be acceptable to them. Now go back to your work and find other ways to get us that account."

This is an interesting example because it shows us that the act of making sense always involves retreating from the sphere of action in which we were previously engaged. It is a reflexive action. One of the dangers of seeking meaning is precisely the fact that, since it explains why things happened the way they did, we distance ourselves from the possibility of changing them. Making sense can often be a way of evading action, of reassuring ourselves, or even of disguising our resignation and eliminating the possibility of transforming the current state of affairs. When we seek meaning in what has happened, we run the risk of accepting things as they are. Stories can often be a powerful conservative force.

We have an infinite capacity for stories and personal judgments. We could construct countless endless explanations for a single event and blame people (including ourselves) for all kinds of things in countless ways. We could stay in this conversation for the rest of our lives. We become prisoners of our personal stories and judgments. We end up talking nonstop, sometimes to other people, sometimes to ourselves, without taking any other action. We are caught in a vicious circle.

How many times have we encountered people who are paralyzed, unable to take the actions that could free them from what causes them suffering? We see them possessed by their personal stories and judgments. They pass from one to another, in an endless chain. But very little else happens around them. Things tend to remain as they are. These people are victims of their inability to take action to overcome the breakdowns they face in life.


 

2. Conversation for coordinating actions

There are, however, other ways to deal with breakdowns, ways in which conversations lead us to act on them and allow us to overcome them. The conversation that acts directly on the breakdown is the "conversation for coordinating actions." This, as we shall see, is very different from the "conversation of personal judgments."

In the conversation for coordinating actions, we generate future actions to deal with the existing breakdown. Its goal is to make something happen, to intervene in the current state of affairs. When we engage in these conversations, we seek to change what is causing the breakdown or to deal with its consequences. We are modifying things with respect to their current state and, therefore, we are producing a reversal in the normal course of events. If we are successful, we can normally expect the breakdown to be overcome.

What are the actions associated with "conversations for coordinating actions"? The linguistic acts that allow new realities to emerge are requests, offers, promises, and statements. One of the most effective ways to deal with disruptions is to ask for help. Knowing how to ask for help is another fundamental linguistic skill in life. Many people often find it difficult to ask for help. If we inquire why, we usually discover that they have various judgments about the consequences associated with asking for help.

Some will say, "If I ask for help, I show weakness"; others, "They may say no, and I will feel rejected. Therefore, I prefer to manage on my own"; still others will argue, "If I ask for help, I commit myself to having to give it back, and with that I lose my independence," and so on. Judgments and more judgments. They determine what we define as possible for ourselves. The consequences of not asking for help are therefore often prolonged suffering, ineffectiveness, and isolation.

In today's world, it is not possible to live in complete self-sufficiency. We are dependent on each other. We must therefore learn to collaborate with others, to support each other, to coordinate actions together. We must learn to ask and also learn that when I ask, my request may be refused. We must know how to distinguish between refusing a request, a possibility that is always open when we ask among autonomous people, and personal rejection.

Distinguishing between conversations about personal judgments and conversations about coordinating actions is also crucial in the business world. If a new product does not sell as expected, we could fall into paralysis if we limit ourselves exclusively to conversations about personal judgments. However, we can change the situation if, for example, we request market research to find ways to make the product more attractive, or if we change some of its features and/or expand our advertising campaign. If the breakdown arises because a supplier cannot deliver parts on time, then we can request them from other producers, and so on.

Often, we do not see all the implications that exist between one type of conversation and another. Therefore, the power of conversations to change the state of affairs is not seen. When this difference is not recognized, language is used to describe and qualify what is happening, to unload interpretations about why the events occurred, and to assign responsibility for what happened. The active nature of language is not used to transform existing realities and generate new ones.

Sometimes, however, there are understandable reasons for not initiating "conversations for coordinating actions." We see two different kinds of reasons for this. On the one hand, this occurs when we do not know what action to take or what to do first. We do not know, therefore, what we could ask for in order to take charge of the breakdown. On the other hand, it also often happens that, although we do know what we could ask for, we judge that the person with whom we should have a "conversation for coordinating actions" is not open to having such a conversation. Or there may be a fear that this conversation will generate even more serious breakdowns if it is initiated. We will deal with these two situations separately.

We propose that even when it is not possible or convenient to directly engage in a "conversation to coordinate actions," there are other options before returning to the "conversation of personal judgments"—options that will allow us to manage our breakdowns more effectively and, ultimately, initiate a conversation to coordinate actions.

3. The conversation for possible actions

Let's take the first situation we mentioned above. When we don't know what actions to take to address a breakdown, we have the option of initiating another type of conversation. We call this the "conversation for possible actions."

This conversation does not directly address the coordination of actions to address the breakdown in question, but rather focuses on speculating about and exploring new possible actions, new possibilities that take us beyond what we are currently able to think of. This is a conversation aimed at expanding our horizon of possibilities.

In a way, this "conversation for possible actions" can look very similar to the "conversation of personal judgments." On the one hand, it is a type of retreat conversation, based on making new judgments and constructing new stories about what is possible. The main difference with the latter type of conversation is its commitment to finding ways to change the current course of events. This brings a completely different emotionality to the conversation. The conversation is no longer based on the desire to make sense of what has happened, but on the desire that, whatever has happened, it must be changed, even though it is not yet known what actions to take. What predominates within the horizon of this conversation is the need for action, to transform the existing state of affairs, rather than the need to make sense of it. This is a conversation about "what to do" rather than "why this happened."

When we don't know what to do, we can always resort to exploring new actions, either with others or alone. We can resort to speculating about new possible actions that we cannot yet articulate. All innovation is based on the ability to generate possibilities that were not previously articulated. Possibilities are not "out there," visible to anyone who looks at them. Possibilities are inventions that we generate in conversations. By designing "conversations for possible actions," we build a space for innovation and for expanding our possibilities. This is a fundamental conversation for any company that wants to remain competitive.

We are not saying that every "conversation for possible actions" will necessarily produce positive results. Nor are we saying that we should always have these conversations, as if automatically following a recipe. We are not interested in translating what we say into guaranteed recipes or techniques. What we are doing here is offering some distinctions that can expand our possibilities for action. It is up to each of us to judge which actions to take and which not to take.


 

4. The conversation for possible conversations

As we said earlier, a second reason for not having "conversations for coordinating actions" is our judgment that the person with whom we must have the conversation is not open to it. And if they are, we may judge that the conversation will go off track without producing positive results, or worse, causing more serious rifts. How many times have we found ourselves judging that the person we think we need to talk to in order to resolve an issue is not even willing to listen to what we have to say? That the conversation we want to have with this person is simply not possible? However, does this mean that we cannot take any other action and must return to resignation or personal judgments?

We propose that when this happens, there is still a way forward. There are still actions we can take. When we judge that we cannot have a certain conversation with someone, we can still have a conversation about the fact that we feel we cannot have that conversation. We can still have a conversation, not about the primitive breakdown that is at stake, but about the breakdown of not being able to open or conclude the conversation that, in our judgment, we should be having. We call this a "conversation for possible conversations."

When is this conversation appropriate? Let's imagine that we judge that our supervisor is not managing the project we are participating in in the most effective way. We therefore have a breakdown. However, every time we initiate a conversation to address this issue, we find that they react defensively, making the conversation difficult. How often do we find ourselves in similar situations with our parents, our partner, our children, our friends?

When this occurs, it may be appropriate to have a "conversation about potential conversations." We could, for example, approach our supervisor and say, "I have a concern that I would like to discuss with you. I feel that whenever I try to talk to you about how this project is being managed, you seem unwilling to discuss it. I could point to several instances where this has happened. I am becoming frustrated because I think the project could be managed more effectively. I know this is just my opinion, and I admit that you may disagree with it. I also recognize that, ultimately, you have the authority to decide how the project should be managed. However, I invite you to listen to what I have to say, as you may find some value in it. And, for my part, I am very open to hearing your point of view. Would you accept this invitation?"

It is important to note the mood of this conversation. As with any conversation, if our mood does not match the type of conversation we want to have, we will find it very difficult to engage in conversation. Every conversation, as Humberto Maturana points out, is a braid between language and emotion. If the emotion is not right, no matter how appropriate the language, the conversation will not be timely. This is a crucial aspect to observe in any conversation.

Sometimes it is our mood that prevents people from talking to us. In the conversation we gave as an example, the mood needs to be non-threatening. Otherwise, it would put the other person, from the outset, in the very attitude we are trying to dissolve. The "conversation for possible conversations" normally requires us to approach it from a place of mutual respect.

Diplomats must be highly skilled in conducting the type of conversations we are referring to. Often their task is precisely to open conversations with those with whom it was not possible to discuss certain issues. In South Africa, for example, many conversations took place between government officials and leaders of the African National Congress before President De Klerk and Nelson Mandela were able to sit down together to discuss their main differences. These preliminary conversations were called "conversations about conversations." Through these talks, they created the conditions that allowed both leaders to address these issues together and explore more effective ways of dealing with them. The same thing happened between representatives of the State of Israel and leaders of the Palestine Liberation Organization. Before they could discuss their differences, they had to design the conditions that would allow them to talk. These are all typical examples of "talks for possible talks."

Let's recap. We have been dealing with the conversations available when we have a breakdown. We have identified four different types of conversations:

1. "personal judgment talks"

2. "talks for coordinating actions"

3. "conversations for possible actions," and

4. "conversations for possible conversations."

The key point to note here is that whenever we face a breakdown, we should ask ourselves, "What conversation—and with whom—should I initiate in order to take the actions necessary to overcome this breakdown?" "What conversation can steer me away from the constant and unproductive recrimination I find myself in as a result of this breakdown?" Ultimately, "What conversation is missing in order for me to take charge of this breakdown?"

What we have tried to demonstrate is that there is always some conversation possible that will lead us, sooner or later, to take some kind of action in relation to the breakdown. If the "conversation for coordinating actions" is not available, then it is worth asking about the "conversation for possible actions" or the "conversation for possible conversations."

Knowing how to answer these questions, knowing what conversations we should have on different occasions, is the art of conversation design.

Some of the secrets of working and living with others have to do with being competent in designing these conversations, learning to judge when it is appropriate to open one conversation and close another, and knowing how to move from one conversation to another. These are fundamental skills in building harmonious families, couples capable of dealing with their breakdowns appropriately, and effective business organizations.

It is important to emphasize that we are not saying that the four conversations mentioned above are the only ones possible. Nor are we suggesting that other conversations are less important than those we have mentioned. As we indicated initially, there are infinite possibilities for distinguishing different conversations. Those we have mentioned so far are only those we consider relevant to the challenge of dealing with our breakdowns.

PERSONAL RELATIONSHIPS AND CONVERSATIONS

We postulate that our personal relationships are shaped by the conversations we have with others. We generally do not see the strong link between conversations and personal relationships. We think that we are in a relationship—or that we have a relationship with someone—and that within that relationship we have conversations. This interpretation separates the relationship from the conversations we have with the people we are in a relationship with. We fail to observe that the conversations we have (and those we don't have) are what are actually producing and reproducing the relationship. Failure to take into account the connection between personal relationships and conversations can have various consequences. This is how we find, for example, people who are concerned with having a good relationship but pay little or no attention to the conversations they have with their partners. They behave, therefore, as if conversations had nothing to do with the relationship. It is not surprising to find them later surprised that the relationship is not working.

We postulate that our conversations create the fabric in which our relationships live. Conversations and relationships are one and the same. We will maintain a relationship with someone as long as we are in open and continuous conversation with that person. That is what defines a relationship. If, for whatever reason, the conversation is interrupted or ends, the relationship is also interrupted or ends.

To know what kind of relationship two people have, just observe their conversations. More is needed. By observing their conversations with others, we determine what their relationships with them are like. We imagine that someone might immediately jump in and say, "Okay, I agree that conversations are important. But not everything in a relationship is conversation. For a couple, for example, their sex life may be as important as their conversations. Their income level may also be important, as well as many other factors. What's more, in a relationship there are many things that people don't say to each other, and that can be as important or more important than what they do say."

We agree that there is a lot of validity in this comment. A stimulating conversation is not necessarily a substitute for a satisfying sexual relationship or a higher income. Despite this, we insist that very few things other than conversations need to be examined in order to evaluate a relationship.

A poor sexual relationship, let's not forget, has nothing to do with the sexual activity itself, but with our judgment of it. A sexual relationship is considered bad because we judge it to be bad. A different couple, with similar sexual activity, might have a different judgment.

We are not saying that sexual activity itself is irrelevant to our judgment of it. Obviously not. Judgment is made by observing the actions to which it refers. In doing so, we always operate within a community that has certain standards by which the activities and actions of individuals are judged. Therefore, given a certain type of sexual activity and given the standards of the community within which we constitute ourselves as individuals, certain judgments can reasonably be expected.

Human beings do not have complete freedom to have whatever conversations they want. Most of the judgments we make do not originate from ourselves but from the community in which we live. Certainly, we can sometimes conform to more or less high social standards. But we can hardly do this with everything. No matter how "eccentric" (different from our community) we may be, we will always be a product of our social and historical conditions. Human beings are always social and historical beings.

However, to claim that sexual activity is deficient is a judgment, and in the realm of judgments there is always, theoretically, room for different opinions. Moreover, even in the case where one or both partners in a couple judge sexual activity to be deficient, the importance they attach to that judgment may be very different. In other words, it is not necessarily declared a breakup by everyone.

So far, however, we have treated sexual activity and the judgment of it as separate spheres. They are not. And they are not in relation to two different aspects. First, because sexual activity itself is, as such, a space for conversation, and the type of conversations that are had (or not had) before, during, and after the sexual dynamic itself affects the nature of the sexual experience.

Animals are sexual beings. Human beings add eroticism to the animal dimension of our sexuality. Eroticism points to the significance of sexuality because of our interpretive capacity, because of the fact that we are beings who live in language. Eroticism, to repeat a formula that is already familiar to us, is sexuality plus interpretation. The emphasis, however, is on interpretation, on conversational dynamics, on the virtuality of play. Hence, we can have very powerful erotic experiences that do not necessarily involve the consummation of the sexual act.

The second aspect to highlight is that by treating sexual activity and judgments separately, we are disregarding the power of conversations to intervene and redesign sexual activity with another. Nothing obliges us to take the type of sexual activity we have with another as a given fact on which we can only pass judgment. This implies, of course, resignation to the fact that things are as they have been and cannot change. If we judge that sexual activity with the other person does not satisfy us, we can declare a break and initiate conversations that will allow us to change it. Let us take our sexual life with the other person as a domain of design and rely on the power of conversation, on the generative capacity of language, to construct a new reality.

We can apply the same type of reasoning to income level or any other non-conversational factor that we believe affects our personal relationships. We insist that we are not saying that simply changing our conversations can change our relationships. But our conversations have the power to lead us to change what is not working.

Anyone who would object to our position on the role of conversations in our personal relationships would be saying something else that we have not yet taken into account. They would also be saying that "in a relationship, there are many things that people do not say to each other, and this can be as important as or even more important than what they do say." We also agree with this. When we talk about the importance of conversations, we are not limiting ourselves to what we say to each other, we are also referring to what we say to ourselves.

Not having a conversation with our partner does not make the conversation disappear. We just make it private. It now becomes a conversation with ourselves. And when we say that our conversations produce and reproduce our relationships, we are not just talking about the conversations we have with our partner. We also include here those private conversations we have in the context of that relationship and the way we draw the line between private and public conversations.

Conversations do not just provide us with a picture (a description) of a relationship. They constitute a kind of X-ray of that relationship. When we start a conversation with our partner, we also take action and, therefore, take responsibility for the breakdowns that occur in that relationship. Our conversations are always transforming our relationships, for better or for worse. By having certain conversations, we take action, and by taking action, some of the negative judgments we may have can be modified.


 

Breaks and conversations in personal relationships

Conversations that take place within a good relationship are not always or necessarily positive or optimistic. In every relationship, we must face breakdowns, and there will always be positive and negative things to address. There is nothing wrong with this. A good relationship does not have to exclude difficult and sometimes negative conversations. On the contrary, in a good relationship, there is always room for complaints, negativity, disagreements, and so on.

All of these are constitutive dimensions of a relationship between autonomous people, and it is important to accept this. When we do not accept it, we are idealizing what good relationships are, we are creating expectations that cannot be met and unrealistic standards that will generate permanent negative judgments. We may also end up undervaluing the positive aspects that exist in our relationships. And in doing so, we could end up compromising them.

A good relationship is not a relationship without breakdowns; it is a relationship that has developed the ability to take action to deal with them effectively. And the way we deal with breakdowns is through conversation. By talking, we act. By acting, we change the normal course of events and make things happen that would not happen if we did not act and if we did not have some conversations.

Again, therefore, we propose that in order to evaluate a relationship, we need to examine the conversations that produce it. One area to observe is the relationship's ability to generate conversations that address breakdowns—that allow members of that relationship to act and coordinate actions with others.


 

Public and private conversations

Another domain of observation when examining a relationship from the perspective of its conversations is provided by the distinction, already mentioned, between public and private conversations. We say that a conversation is public when we have it with another person. In my relationship with that person, this is a public conversation. A private conversation is one that we keep to ourselves—a conversation that we actually have but do not share.

The distinctions between public and private conversations only make sense here in the context of a particular relationship. For a specific relationship, conversation "x" could be public or private. It could well be that a conversation that is public for one relationship is private for another. That's what we do with secrets. When we tell someone a secret, what we are doing is opening a conversation—we are making it public in the context of that conversation. And we are also asking that person to commit to keeping the conversation private in the context of other relationships. In terms of the first conversation, it is public. In terms of the latter, the conversation is private.

Human beings are constantly chatting with ourselves. We make judgments about almost everything. We have conversations about our desires and about the future. Even when we are having public conversations, we are also having private conversations, and we undoubtedly have many private conversations while listening to others. When awake, we are constantly immersed in private conversations. However, we do not make them all public. We are always evaluating whether making this or that conversation public with someone is appropriate in the context of our relationships. Usually, we keep many conversations to ourselves. Again, there is nothing questionable about that.

From this perspective, a relationship is good if it strikes an appropriate balance between public and private conversations. This balance changes from one relationship to another. If we have a business relationship with someone, we might expect some conversations to be private. If someone brought them up, we might well say, "I'm sorry, but this is private." Here we have what we might call the "privilege of silence" or "the privilege of privacy." This is an expression of our autonomy as individuals.

However, we might be willing to share that same conversation in a more intimate relationship or perhaps in a conversation with a therapist or coach. In that context, invoking the privilege of privacy might be heard differently. A relationship is specified by the boundaries that define which conversations should be held in the relationship—which ones are made public and which ones are kept private.

Even though there are some social standards that govern what should be made public and what should be kept private in a relationship, the boundaries between the two are generally flexible. They are not strictly defined, and it is up to each party involved in the relationship to specify them. The boundaries vary or are more or less strict depending on different social environments. In England, for example, the boundaries between private and public are generally much stricter than in Mediterranean European countries. This produces in England not only a strong sense of privacy, but also a strong sense of publicness.

Therefore, a crucial issue in a relationship is being able to establish the division between the private and the public, that is, between our private and public conversations. Even in very intimate relationships, there is always a division to be made. What makes a relationship intimate is the fact that we are willing to share many conversations that we would normally keep private, not the fact that we make all our private conversations public.

What conversations should we keep private in an intimate relationship? It is not possible to draw the line from outside a relationship and from outside the context provided by the circumstances surrounding it. However, it is possible to make some considerations that help us make a decision in this regard.

We must admit that we all have many automatic conversations. We simply find ourselves in them. Even saying that we have them is a distortion of the phenomenon of automatic conversations. It would be more accurate to say that these conversations have us, since we have no control over them. Among these automatic conversations, there are, for example, many that are based on judgments about what is happening, judgments that, once outside of that conversation, can be proven to be unfounded.

Some believe that an intimate relationship "must" sustain any automatic conversation that arises. This means bringing them up indiscriminately, without thinking about the consequences this has for the relationship. The relationship is considered a repository for any automatic conversation. Some even go so far as to think that the relationship is not good if this does not happen. This attitude implies that the relationship is taken for granted. And no responsibility is taken for the way conversations shape our relationships.

Our personal relationships can be damaged by indiscriminately opening up all our private conversations to them. Every relationship needs care, attention, and cultivation. Every relationship is a space of mutual concerns. What anyone says or does is not only a meaningful action in terms of the concerns of the person acting, but also an action that intervenes in the domain of concerns of the person we are talking to or acting upon.

When opening a conversation in an intimate relationship, we must—if we assume responsibility for that relationship—judge how this conversation might affect our partner's concerns. We must assess what new reality we are revealing to this person and what this implies in terms of their concerns. Our decision to open a conversation, as well as to make a private conversation public, must be made while taking responsibility for how we are addressing the other person's concerns. Few things are more damaging to a relationship than taking it for granted.

However, keeping a private conversation private can also damage the relationship. Private conversations often interfere with our relationships with others, obstructing opportunities to coordinate actions together. This can happen, for example, when we have a complaint that we have not made public, or when we have an interpretation of the other person's actions that, in fact, closes off possibilities in that relationship. Issues that affect mutual trust between the parties in the relationship generally fall into this category.

When this occurs—that is, when there is a private conversation that does not dissipate and obstructs the relationship—it may be important to share that conversation for the sake of the relationship; to explore together some ways to address the concerns involved in that private conversation. When we do this, we generally put our concerns and interest in maintaining a healthy relationship above the interest of the other member of the relationship. By making this conversation public, we can clarify things. Furthermore, it is a way of taking responsibility for the relationship.


 

Beyond "talking as action"

We relate to others when we coordinate actions with them, when we judge that we have an open and continuous space to coordinate actions with someone. Language allows us to coordinate actions with others in order to coordinate actions with them. To this end, we make requests, offers, promises, statements, and assertions, and through these different linguistic acts, we participate with others in multiple language games.

At one point, our central interest was to show that relationships belong to the domain of coordinating actions. Language, we said, allows us to coordinate actions with others. The central formula was "speak => action." However, since we proposed this postulate, we have moved beyond it. This in no way means that we are denying the validity of our postulate. We fully support it, but it needs to be placed in a broader context.

Seeking to broaden our understanding of the relationship between human phenomena and language, it was necessary to take a few more steps. This led us progressively to go beyond the approach that speaking is acting. Hence, we pointed out that if we wanted to achieve a deeper understanding of language, we could not limit ourselves to speaking. Understanding speaking was not enough. Speaking, we said, only makes sense in relation to listening. An understanding of language that focuses on the aspect of speaking is biased. This is particularly important in interpersonal relationships. In this case, concern for listening is as important as concern for speaking.

Just as listening led us beyond speaking, we now want to move beyond a narrow understanding of action—at least beyond the notion that language is essentially and exclusively the coordination of actions. This does not deny that every time we speak, we act. The notion we wish to move beyond is that when we speak, we do so with the immediate goal of coordinating actions with others. The coordination of actions, while a primary dimension of language, is only one of the possibilities it offers us.

Once we generate language in the coordination of actions, language, beyond expanding our capacity to coordinate actions, transforms us into beings who use language to construct meaning. Language transforms human beings into beings who live in the torment of the search for meaning. This is the hallmark of our original condition that lies at the basis of the human phenomenon. From that moment on, an important reversal takes place. The mastery of meaning that results from our ability to coordinate action with others (e.g., our capacity for language) now becomes the backdrop from which we act and from which action itself is evaluated.


 

The importance of storytelling in human existence

In their quest for meaning, human beings invent and tell stories. As soon as children acquire minimal language skills, they begin to ask "Why." We all know how difficult it can be at times to satisfy their curiosity. From childhood, we all experience this basic urge to make sense of what is happening around us. If we examine this more closely, we realize that we are the only species that invents stories.

This compulsion to tell stories is not trivial. It is not something we do "in addition" to many other things. It is one of the most important things we do. If we are asked who we are, we tell a story. Our identity is constituted as a story we tell about ourselves. It is a story that positions us in the world. And when we are asked about the world, we tell another story. Our world is always a story about how things are around us.

We do not relate to our environment as if it were a collection of separate entities and events. Whatever entities and events we distinguish, we organize them, giving them an order that may be more or less complete, through stories that relate them to each other. Since we, as individuals—as personal identities—are a story about who we are, and since we all live in a world that is also a story, we could say that human beings are stories within stories, all of them produced by ourselves. We have been creators of myths since our earliest forms of social existence. This is constitutive of being human.

Sometimes, however, it seems to us that the mythmakers were our ancestors and not us. We think that they were the ones who lived in mythical worlds and that we abandoned that way of being some time ago. Unlike them, we maintain that we no longer need myths because we know how things are. To emphasize the point, we talk, for example, about our scientific explanations. But our ancestors also thought they knew how things were. They also considered their stories to be true representations of reality. If we look at our scientific explanations, we must admit that they are also stories. Stories that are more effective than others, stories that are founded in ways that we have come to accept as more powerful than others, but, in the end, scientific explanations are nothing more than narratives we produce about the world.

Generally, we do not see our myths as myths or our stories as stories. We do not realize that even what we say about our ancestors is a story. There is no way out. We cannot escape the fabric we create with our stories. Human beings live "in language": they live within the stories they construct to give meaning to themselves and the world around them. Martin Heidegger, who, as we have already pointed out, insisted that "language is the dwelling place of being," observed our stories as "buildings that shelter man." He recognized that man is not only the producer of his stories, but, first and foremost, the product of them. "Man acts," wrote Heidegger, "as if he were the architect and master of language, when in fact it is language that has remained the master of man."

Stories function as refuges for human beings. Every society is housed within certain fundamental structures composed of narratives. We call them metanarratives or meta-stories. We also call them historical discourses. They are essential components of a particular culture. When we look at the history, literature, religion, and philosophy of a given society, what we are doing is examining those meta-narratives that constitute one of the most important pillars of that society. They are the basic stories from which people derive meaning for their lives. Depending on how a human community gives meaning to its life, different forms of human existence emerge.


 

Stories and action

In some previous sections, we saw how stories often distract us from taking action. We wrote that by remaining in the stories that result from "personal judgment conversations" (in which we develop interpretations of why things are the way they are), we often distract ourselves from taking the actions that help us overcome our breakdowns. We fall into a passive attitude from which no commitments to change things arise. We have pointed out the negative role that stories can play with regard to action.

However, we now want to emphasize that some activities related to the creation of stories can also play a positive role in our capacity for action. In fact, this is the role that stories play in defining different forms of human life and giving meaning to existence. It is through the activity of inventing stories that we develop a vision of the future and, therefore, open up a horizon that will drive us to take action. It is also through the invention of stories that we develop the background that will give meaning to challenging the present and taking action. We often act on the basis that it makes sense to do so. Generally, it is the stories we have about ourselves and the world that provide the meaning from which action arises. Action never occurs in a vacuum. It occurs from the fabric of stories that give meaning to that action.

Social movements, those collective forces that have so often changed the course of history, are the product of compelling narratives that have had the power to unite people around a common cause. These stories operate, in the words of Antonio Gramsci, as the "cement" that holds together the individuals who make up the social movement. Social movements are just one example, among many others, of the power of stories, or the power of myths.


 

The power of storytelling in relationships

We postulate that in order to build close relationships, we need more than just mutual ways of coordinating actions. Certainly, effective coordination of actions is important, but it is not always enough. Close relationships, and especially close intimate relationships, are generally based on a shared basic background that gives them meaning. These relationships—in addition to joint actions—are capable of generating their own significance. They generate a sense of being together with those who participate in the relationship.

Again, this occurs decisively in the conversations that constitute that relationship. By engaging in conversation, the couple becomes involved in the process of constructing shared stories that will give meaning to their being together. Their conversations resemble the process of spinning, in which the fabric that sustains the relationship is produced. By engaging in conversation, both members of the couple enter into a process of mutual transformation. Their stories become intertwined. Then, depending on the quality of this fusion of stories, a shared background will develop, a space of consensus will emerge, a shared world will be created, and a shared sensibility will appear among those who make up the relationship.

All of this generates what biologist Humberto Maturana has called a process of mutual congruent transformation between the parties involved. Over time, they will observe how well they complement each other, how well they can even anticipate each other's actions and reactions. We have all witnessed this phenomenon in couples, friends, teams, and companies. We usually call this a good relationship. What has been produced is what we call a healthy culture for the relationship.

We have also witnessed the opposite. We have seen how some relationships have broken down and how the partners involved seem to grow increasingly distant as time goes by. When this happens, we might well talk about "incompatibility of characters" or different personalities. But in doing so, we are using the result of what happened (the fact that they appeared to be incompatible) as an explanation for why it happened. We are using the result as the cause. "Incompatibility" is not a given factor in a relationship. It is produced by the relationship itself and, therefore, by the conversations that shape that relationship.

We therefore wish to emphasize this aspect of our conversations: their ability to create a shared world in which each party sees the other as a partner in the invention of a common future. Conversations can create this. They can also destroy the possibility of achieving it. When they produce a healthy culture, we judge our relationships as "warm" and see our houses as "homes." They become better shelters, better buildings in which to live. These new structures have been produced by language, in conversations.

We don't often realize that our conversations produce positive and negative cultures. We find some people who do well in their relationships and others who do poorly. Some of us seem competent at building them, and others seem unable to do so. All of this happens as if it were determined by the existence of hidden personal talents. We postulate that, through the distinctions we have presented, we can observe the phenomenon of the construction of a shared world through conversations. This new observer can allow us to design our conversations in such a way as to make us responsible for the type of relationships we are generating.


 

CONVERSATIONS IN BUSINESS ORGANIZATIONS


 

Organizations as linguistic units

Recognizing the generative role of language allows for a new approach to understanding organizations in general, as well as companies and management activities. This approach shows us that organizations are linguistic phenomena: units constructed from specific conversations, which are based on the ability of human beings to make mutual commitments when they communicate with each other.

If we examine a company, for example, we realize that what constitutes it is not its name, nor the building where it is located, nor a specific product or set of products. All of these can change and the company can continue to exist.

A company is a stable network of conversations. As such, it generates an identity in the world that transcends its individual members. There are many ways in which companies go beyond the individuals who comprise them. Companies are agents of action and, as such, become socially responsible for their actions. Regardless of the individual responsibilities that may be involved, we hold Exxon responsible for the Exxon Valdez oil spill in Alaska, and we credit Sony with the invention of the Walkman. Companies can exist for fifty, a hundred, or more years. All of their individual members may change, but the company can remain the same entity.

Certainly, companies change over time, but the changes occur within a continuous identity. No matter how important an individual's role in the company may be, their identity is always different from the identity of the company. We know, for example, that Apple transcends Steve Jobs; that Ford transcends Henry Ford; and that IBM transcends Thomas J. Watson Jr., regardless of how important each of them may have been to the company.

Companies are a clear example of the power of language, the power of conversation. Without language, we could not build organizations. It is in conversation that companies constitute themselves as particular units, circumscribing their members into an entity. It is also in conversation that companies ensure their existence in the environment, mainly by making offers and accepting requests in the market.


 

The linguistic structure of organizations

Let us briefly examine how conversations integrate the individual members of an organization into a particular unit. There are at least four aspects worth exploring in relation to this.

First, we must accept that each unit is specified by its boundaries. What allows us to distinguish a unit is the fact that we can separate it from its environment. If we cannot delineate the boundaries of the unit, we simply cannot distinguish it. Now, if we look at the boundaries of an organization, we realize that they are linguistic. Who belongs to the organization and who does not is decided by a statement. Someone vested with the authority to make it formulates the statement.

The boundaries of a company correspond to a line drawn by language through someone's power to make a declaration. This is not a matter that is established once and for all. Individuals join and leave an organization. When they join, they do so because they have been hired, and this occurs by declaration. When they leave, it is because they resign or are fired, which also occurs by declaration.

Second, the structure of an organization is built as a network of mutual promises. Each person is bound to the organization by specific commitments and is responsible for fulfilling certain conditions of satisfaction. As we know, promises result from conversations. To have a promise, someone must make a request or an offer, and someone must declare their acceptance of what is requested or offered. In business organizations, people make promises in various domains. There are promises in the domains of production, sales, marketing, customer service, administration, general management, and so on. This internal network of promises is what allows a company to fulfill its own promises as an entity in the market.

And an organization is more than a network of autonomous individuals, linked by a declaration of belonging to the same social entity and by their mutual individual promises. A third dimension to consider has to do with the fact that members of an organization perform their actions on the basis of a shared background. This constitutes an important economic advantage for organizations. It is not necessary to tell people in an organization, every time they perform an action, exactly what to do and how to do it. By producing a shared background, the members of an organization generate synergistic conditions that save the company time and resources. This shared background is produced by a permanent thread of conversations. From this background, people's personal identities intermingle with the identity of the company.

This is what is known as the "culture" of a company. This "culture" allows for the development of social practices specific to the organization, common social standards from which each individual member makes judgments, shared ways of acting and dealing with circumstances to produce results. These social practices are even carried out transparently, as a set of habits, by those who are immersed in them.

There is a fourth dimension in which language unites the individual members of an organization. This aspect also belongs to the "culture" of the organization and constitutes part of the shared background from which the people who belong to an organization act. However, it differs from what we have pointed out above in that its emphasis is not on a shared past but, on the contrary, on sharing a common future. Organizations also develop synergistic conditions by circumscribing the actions of their members within a shared vision, a common story about what is possible, and a shared commitment to realize that vision together.

This is an important aspect for the functioning of a company. The shared future allows those who work in the company to carry out actions from a consensual basis, sharing common concerns and aspiring to common goals. This is often referred to as "agreement on direction." Shared direction makes it unnecessary to constantly spell out the concerns and goals of the company. Again, it saves time and resources and increases the productivity of the company.

Our main approach is that all this happens in conversations. If we want to understand a company, we must examine the conversations that constituted it in the past and those that constitute it today. The strength of a company will always lead us to the strength of its conversations. Its weaknesses are related to the weaknesses of its conversations or to the fact that some crucial conversations may be missing.

As a company builds positive communication habits, which increase its productivity, it also generates negative communication habits, thereby compromising its effectiveness. Habit breeds blindness, and people end up assuming that their business practices are the "normal" or "natural" way of doing things. They generally do not see beyond their own communicative background.

In reality, this points to one of the most important aspects of business consulting. What a consultant does is provide a more competent observer of business practices, an observer other than the one who generates the company's culture. This different observer can reveal blind spots that are not observed from within the company and can intervene in the conversations that constitute the company and change them.


 

Conversations as the foundation of business organizations

We propose that whatever problem a company is facing, it can be examined by observing its conversational structure. We would all agree that "some" problems in the company arise from communication failures (such as when the conditions for satisfaction have been heard differently by the people involved in making a promise). We are all familiar with the negative results that arise from these failures. We are also familiar with the problems that arise from a lack of persuasion when making a sale, or when reaching agreements takes too long, or when people do not keep their promises or keep them in a systematically deficient or untimely manner. We all admit that these problems arise from failures in our conversations and we can recognize how they harm the company's productivity.

However, we propose that any problem, whether strictly communicative or not, can be examined from the perspective of its underlying conversations. In making this proposition, we are not forgetting that some problems have significant non-conversational dimensions. We know, for example, that if production stops due to equipment failure, there is more to it than just a conversational failure.

However, it is also important to note that even these problems can be avoided, anticipated, or better addressed if certain conversations had taken place. We are referring, for example, to conversations at the time of equipment acquisition, or conversations about the proper use and maintenance of equipment, or about designing alternatives in case of failure. These conversations could have prevented the problem or could have modified its consequences. On the other hand, we must also recognize that the way we normally deal with a problem is through conversations (asking someone to repair the equipment, notifying our customers, etc.).

We therefore propose that a company's communication skills largely determine its success or failure. We propose that a company is a linguistic system and that everything that happens within it can be examined from the perspective of its conversations.


 

The conversational nature of management

There is no area of the company whose conversational nature is clearer than the managerial or management level. If we examine what executives and managers do inside and outside the company, we realize that their work consists fundamentally and almost exclusively of being in conversations. What executives and managers do is mainly talk, listen, communicate with others, promote some conversations in the company, and avoid others. Their work consists of nothing but conversations.

In fact, it is precisely through a communicative act, that is, by declaration, that a company is created. We also engage in conversations when we hire, promote, or fire staff, when we assign responsibilities, when we coordinate different activities, enter into contracts, set the company's goals and direction, when we make sales, build alliances, or advertise the company's products.

No area in the company is more dependent on communication skills than the executive and managerial ranks. The success or failure of an executive or manager is directly related to their conversational skills. It is striking, however, how little attention is paid to communication phenomena in the traditional understanding of business administration. If we examine traditional management education, we realize that the importance assigned to the development of communication skills is extremely limited. Executives and managers are not normally trained to view the company from the perspective of its conversations.

We have said that an organization is more than a space generated by declarative boundaries in which people are united by a network of mutual promises. An organization is also a space in which a certain culture is nurtured, a space in which people share a past, a collective way of doing things in the present, and a common sense of direction toward the future. Many other conversations, in addition to those examined in this work, must take place to produce this culture. These other conversations are essential to transcend mechanical forms of coordination of actions between individuals and to produce bonds of close cooperation and collaboration. They are important conversations for making organizations a space in which individuals find meaning in their work and their lives and achieve well-being in them.
