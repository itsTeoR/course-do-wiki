EMOTIONS AND MOODS

[22]

We have argued that although language constitutes human beings as the type of beings we are, we cannot ignore two other primary domains in our existence. The priority we assign to language does not ignore the fact that it is not the only domain relevant to understanding the whole of the human phenomenon. In addition to language, we point out and recognize the domain of the body and the domain of emotionality.

Although the phenomena of the body, emotionality, and language cannot be reduced to a domain other than the one they themselves constitute, it is nonetheless true that they establish relationships of coherence among themselves and, therefore, what happens in one of them conditions what will happen in the other. Physical postures, emotionality, and language behave in a congruent manner and influence each other.

This has at least three implications. The first relates to the need to explain the conditions that ensure the aforementioned coherence. It cannot be taken for granted, as if it did not need an explanation. In other words, we cannot consider it obvious or simply natural that these three domains maintain coherent relationships with each other. It is necessary to show why this happens and what mechanisms produce the influence of one phenomenal domain on another.

The second implication relates to the fact that, if such coherence exists, it opens up the possibility of using any domain to refer to the other two. In other words, it is possible to "translate" the phenomena of one domain into phenomena corresponding to the others. To the extent that physical postures, emotionality, and language "correspond" to each other, we can access each one through the others. We call this process of "translation" "reconstruction." This allows us to say, for example, that we can perform a "bodily reconstruction" of a particular language game, or undertake a "linguistic reconstruction" of a particular emotion.

The third implication refers to the fact that, beyond the aforementioned possibility of "translation" or "reconstruction," the coherence of these three domains opens up an important space for design and intervention. Given such coherence, we can expect that transformations produced in a given domain will translate into modifications in the others. There is, therefore, a whole field of indirect interventions through which, for example, an emotional change can perfectly well modify our conversations and our physical posture.

This third implication also has its downside. Because of the relationship of coherence between these three primary domains, on the one hand, the possibilities for intervention expand; but, on the other hand, they are also limited. This aspect is highly relevant to the practice of "ontological coaching" and its conditions for effectiveness.

Often, changes in one of the three domains cannot be sustained due to the pressure for coherence that comes from the other two. This very often requires simultaneous intervention in all three domains to ensure that the transformations produced in one of them are met with changes that are coherent with the others.

For example, it is not enough to induce a change in the type of conversations a person has if the emotionality in which they find themselves is consistent with past conversations. If emotionality is not addressed simultaneously, the change in conversations is in danger of not being sustained and, therefore, of being short-lived.

Human beings are constituted as such in our corporeality, our emotionality, and our capacity for language. We have a body, emotionality, and language, and in "having" them, as in the experiences they generate, these three phenomenal domains are irreducible to each other, regardless of the possibility of reconstructing them or indirect intervention. Hence, we call them primary domains.


 

Emotionality in our traditional conception

The subject of emotions has been difficult to address in our traditional conception of human beings. Based on the assumption that we are made up of different and separate substances, namely the mind and the body, our traditional conception of human beings has not been able to decide where emotions are located. Are they a bodily phenomenon? Are they mental? Where can we locate them? Most of the answers to these questions have been unsatisfactory. Emotional phenomena have been slipping away for a long time.

One of the ways in which our common sense has dealt with this dilemma has been to talk about emotions as "matters of the heart." By using this metaphor, a kind of middle ground was found: it was admitted that emotions do not arise from the head, where the mind was supposed to reside. Instead, they were assumed to be higher phenomena than other bodily phenomena. However, our traditional conception saw us as "rational beings"—following the interpretation proposed by Descartes more than 350 years ago. As we have already examined, this position considers human behavior to be rational, and the reasons of the mind are the key to making sense of human action.

From the outset, however, there were reactions against this conception of human beings, especially regarding the role assigned to our emotions—the so-called "matters of the heart." Blaise Pascal, a French philosopher and contemporary of Descartes, warned us that "the heart has its reasons which reason knows nothing of" ("le coeur a ses raisons que la raison ne connait point"). But despite the warning, Cartesianism became our predominant way of understanding the human phenomenon.

We maintain that in order to understand human action, we must pay careful attention to our emotional life. We postulate that our emotions are basic determinants of what we can or cannot achieve in the domains of work, learning, sociability, spirituality, and so on. Our emotional life is a crucial factor in every sphere of human action.


 

The distinction between moods and emotions

When we talk about our emotional life—what we have been calling emotionality—we can make a distinction between two kinds of phenomena: moods and emotions. This sometimes seems to be a very subtle distinction, since in some cases it is difficult to separate one from the other.

Every time we experience an interruption in the flow of life, emotions arise. We therefore associate them with breaks—that is, with interruptions in our transparency. When we act, we always do so within a certain space of possibilities. When an event leads us to significantly modify the boundaries of that space of possibilities, when we are led to change our judgment of what we can expect in the future, we speak of a break.

A break always implies a change in our space of possibilities. What we previously believed to be possible may no longer be so, and what we previously assumed to be improbable or even impossible may suddenly become probable or possible. Every time we judge that our space of possibilities has changed, whether positively or negatively, we are facing a break.

Let's take an example. When we are driving a car, we assume that certain actions are within the normal range of possibilities. Traffic lights can change from green to red; the car stops if we press the brake; we make the car move by changing gears and accelerating, and so on. This is what we call the normal flow of driving, and we often do it transparently, without paying much attention to it. All these actions are fully contemplated when we drive a car, and there are no emotional changes associated with them.

Now let's imagine that, when we see the traffic light turn red, we press the brake and the car does not stop. If this happens, we would say that the normal flow of driving has been interrupted. What was possible in the normal course of events has been broken, and a new space of possibilities has emerged. If the car does not stop when we press the brake, the possibility of an accident arises. Every time there is a change within our space of possibilities, emotions are generated.

Let's take another example. A client calls us to inform us that they have accepted our offer and will sign a contract that we consider very important for the future of the company. This fact changes our horizon of possibilities. The future ahead of us is now different. New emotions overwhelm us—happiness, optimism, excitement, and so on.

Emotion, therefore, is a distinction we make in language to refer to the change in our space of possibilities as a result of certain events (occurrences, happenings, or actions). When we talk about emotions, therefore, we can point to the particular circumstances that generate them. We can identify the events that trigger emotions. If those events disappear, the emotions that accompanied them will usually disappear as well. Emotions are specific and reactive. Events precede them. When we refer to emotions, we are often observing the way in which action (or certain events) modifies our horizon of possibilities.

The relationship between an event and emotion is not without importance. I often recount the example given by philosopher Michael Graves to illustrate this relationship between the uniqueness of an event and emotion. Graves imagined someone going out for a walk in the mountains on a particular day. As they walk, they observe nature, stopping to look at the trees and listen to the birds singing. In the distance, he also hears the sound of a river running between rocks. Not far away, in the transparency of the flow of his journey, he begins to think about a possible project that could be undertaken in the coming days. He feels happy, optimistic; the project excites him; he thinks that when he finishes it, he will be able to open doors that until now have been closed. He smiles. Then he notices a snake slithering toward him, in the middle of the path, a few meters from where he is. Suddenly, that perception leads him to a different experience. He is scared, his heart is beating faster; he wonders if he will have enough space to dodge the snake or if he should turn back. The space of possibilities in which he now finds himself is different. The emotion of fear has taken hold of him.

In Graves' example, the emotion of fear was triggered by the perception of the snake. An event triggered that emotion. I remember discussing Graves' example with Renato Orellana, who pointed out to me, "This implies that if we want to understand a particular emotion, it is important to refer it back to the triggering event. It is necessary, returning to Graves' example, to 'look for the snake'. Once we identify it, we will know that if we want to avoid repeating that emotion, we will have to "remove snakes from the path," and if we want that emotional experience to be repeated, we will have to make sure to place them there." When talking about emotions, recognizing the connection between the emotion and the event allows us not only to interpret emotional phenomena in a certain way, but also to take specific actions.

Indeed. If we are concerned, for example, about the fact that someone is often in a bad mood, we may wonder about the event or type of events that cause this emotional change. By identifying it, we open up a space for design. Now we can intervene not only in the factors that allow us to intervene in the bad mood that is triggered, but also in the occurrence of the type of event that triggers it.

Moods are very different from emotions. When we talk about moods, we are referring to an emotionality that does not necessarily refer to specific conditions and that, therefore, we cannot normally relate to specific events. Moods live in the background from which we act. They refer to those emotional states from which actions are performed.

Like all emotionality, and therefore like emotions, moods are also associated with a horizon of possibilities, a space of possible actions. However, in moods, the relationship between possibilities and action is reversed. We have said that emotions have to do with the way in which action modifies our horizon of possibilities. With moods, on the contrary, we are concerned with the way in which the horizon of possibilities in which we find ourselves, corresponding to the mood in question, conditions our actions.

Regardless of where we are and what we do, human beings are always in some state of mind that we do not usually choose or control—we simply find ourselves in it. Once we are in a certain state of mind, we behave within the parameters that such a state of mind specifies in us.

To the extent that emotionality conditions our actions, it also conditions the way we are while we are in it. In this sense, we cannot only say that we have moods; it is also true that our moods have us—we are possessed by our moods. We become our moods. Moods usually get ahead of us. By the time we notice them, we are already immersed in them.

Our language, the way we talk about our moods, often hides the fact that we are possessed by them. We usually say, "I feel happy" or "I am confused." By speaking in this way, we imply that the state of happiness or confusion is produced by the "I." That the "I" is the one who feels happiness. The "I" takes precedence. We do not realize that the truth is precisely the opposite. It is the mood that feels, it is the mood that is constituting the "I" in its present form of being.

Does this mean that there is nothing we can do about our moods? Must we resign ourselves to the moods that possess us? Not at all. We postulate that by changing the observer of moods that we are, we open up possibilities for action that normally remain hidden to the observer who is limited by common sense. Being able to observe moods as such allows us to intervene in their design. One of the goals of this work is to generate in the reader a new observer of moods and develop their ability to intervene in the moods in which we find ourselves immersed and design actions capable of changing them.

There is a close relationship between emotions and moods. On the one hand, what began as an emotion linked to a specific event can often become a mood if it remains with the person long enough and moves into the background from which they act. This usually happens with important events. For example, when we start a romance or when someone close to us dies. These experiences place us in an emotional state or space of possibilities that will manifest itself in all our actions: at home, at the office, with friends, and so on.

On the other hand, in addition to the differences indicated above, moods and emotions are very similar phenomena and allow us to reconstruct them in the same way. Most of what we are going to say below can be applied to both moods and emotions. However, to simplify our investigation, we are going to focus on moods. We leave it to the reader to draw conclusions to apply this analysis to emotions.


 

Moods are constitutive of human existence.

Human beings, wherever they are, wherever they live, are always immersed in certain moods. There is no way we can avoid finding ourselves in one and looking at life from outside some kind of emotional state, whatever it may be. Moods are constitutive of human existence. It is simply impossible to conceive of human existence without accepting that we are, inevitably, in some kind of mood. Moods are a fact of life, and we find them everywhere.

Different places seem to have different moods. Each city has its own. We can talk about the mood of San Francisco, Madrid, Mexico City, Buenos Aires, Santiago, and so on. Just by naming these cities, we evoke their mood. They are all very different. The same is true of countries. We can talk about an Italian, German, Russian mood, and so on. They are all very different. Tropical countries have a very different mood from, say, Nordic countries.

Climatic cycles also cause different moods, as we associate different possibilities with each of them. The seasons produce different moods. People tend to be in different moods in spring and winter. The days of the week cause different moods. People's moods on Mondays are commonly very different from those on Fridays or Sundays. We find ourselves in different moods at different times of the day. Our mood in the morning is not the same as in the afternoon; moreover, we know that there are "early birds" and "night owls."

Different ages are also associated with different moods. Teenagers have very different moods from older people. Finally, we can also talk about the moods of different historical periods. We know, for example, that "the mood of the 1960s" was very different from "the mood of the 1980s."


 

Moods and action

What kind of phenomena are moods? We have said that each mood (and the same can be said about emotions) specifies a space of possibilities. And when we talk about possibilities, we are referring to the space of events and, therefore, the space within which we act. A mood, therefore, defines a space of possible actions.

Maturana argues that emotions and moods are predispositions for action. The way an observer distinguishes the moods of other living beings, to the extent that they do not participate in the experience associated with their experience, is by making a judgment about the behavior of those living beings and, in particular, about what that behavior defines in terms of the possible actions that can be performed from it.

Let's take an example. When observing a dog, we say, "This dog is angry." In saying this, we are making a judgment about the dog through which we characterize its behavior and define what can be expected in terms of the actions the dog might perform in its current state. Anger, therefore, involves an observation of behavior, a judgment about it, and an assumption of consistency between the observed behavior and the behavior expected based on the former. This is how we talk about other people's emotions.

As we can see, this approach to emotionality is strongly behaviorist. We say nothing about what the dog may feel when it is in the emotional state we distinguish as anger. We say nothing, therefore, about the dog's own experience of anger. We say nothing and can say nothing because the experience of the emotion itself is not only unobservable when we observe the dog, but also, to a large extent, uncommunicable. This does not deny the experience in question, nor its importance to the person experiencing it, but it does warn us about what we cannot talk about meaningfully.

What we have recognized in the example of the dog is equally valid for human beings. There is, however, an important difference. Although our personal emotional experiences are ultimately equally incommunicable in themselves, because human beings share a generic way of being, we assume that when we distinguish equivalent emotions (by strict reference to the space of possible actions that they specify), the personal experience of such an emotional state is also equivalent.

But who can assure us that it is the same? Is the experience of the emotion of love that Romeo "feels" for Juliet the same as that which Juliet "feels" for Romeo?

Furthermore, is the experience of love we observe in other couples equivalent to the experience of love that Romeo or Juliet may have "felt"? Is the guilt Raskolnikov "feels" equivalent to the guilt anyone else "feels"? As a feeling, as a personal experience of living the emotion, we do not know. Only at the level of behaviors that are conducive or inconducive to other forms of behavior can we rigorously specify the phenomenal domain of emotionality.

From this perspective, to the extent that we accept that we do not observe others' personal feelings, just as we cannot fully communicate our own, when we talk about emotions and moods we are introducing a language to talk about action and, more particularly, the space of possibilities specified by the actions we observe. Talking about emotionality, therefore, involves making distinctions about the realm of possibility associated with action. Anger, love, guilt, rather than referring to what we feel, allude to the space of possibilities in which we find ourselves in our development in life.

Therefore, without denying our ability to attribute emotionality to our environment, time, and space, what we are doing is specifying the judgment of possibility made by a particular observer. Thursday does not have a different emotionality than Friday. It is always the observer who establishes a distinction between Thursday and Friday (Thursday and Friday do not exist independently of an observer who distinguishes them as such) and who associates with that distinction a space of different possibilities within a given community, who relates to these two days in terms of the possibilities he attributes to them. The emotion belongs to the observer, even though this observer may be making a judgment about the observations that another observer may be making by observing their behavior.

In summary, we can argue that depending on the mood we are in, certain actions are possible and others are not—some possibilities are open and others are closed. This is central to what constitutes emotions and moods. If we are predisposed to mistrust, the possibilities of coordinating our actions with someone else's are narrowed. In a state of enthusiasm, our horizon of possible future actions is broadened.

From early childhood, we learn about this connection between emotional states and action. As children, we quickly learned to check our parents' mood before asking them for something or telling them about a bad grade we got in school. If we thought our parents were in a bad mood, for example, we would postpone telling them that we had spilled ink on the bed or ask their permission to go skiing the following weekend. In doing so, we recognized that the actions that would follow as a result of engaging in that conversation with them would be very different depending on their mood.

Moods not only condition the actions that are possible or impossible to perform. They also condition the way in which we will carry out those actions. Athletes know this very well. Their moods determine their performance, and they know that if they change them, they can also change what they can achieve. When we want to coordinate an action with another person, we must ask ourselves whether that person's mood is conducive to the planned action. If we judge that their mood is not right, we may need to act differently than we had planned, perhaps changing our actions to others that, in our judgment, would be conducive to changing their mood. Instead of having a conversation to coordinate actions, we may have a conversation about possible actions, or a conversation about possible conversations.

When we talk, it is important to ensure that the mood of the conversation is right for achieving what we hope that conversation will produce. The prevailing mood will not only determine what can be achieved from that conversation, but, at a more basic level, it will condition the way people listen to what is being said. This is particularly important for conversations in which we want to arouse the other person's interest in what we have to offer (e.g., sales conversations, negotiations, romantic or business proposals, etc.).

Since moods define a space of possible actions, two dimensions immediately open up. The first is that moods specify a possible future, and the second is that they generate a certain world. Let's examine them in that order.

Our moods are like lenses through which we observe the future. Different moods are different openings to the future. If we happen to be in a good mood, the future will look bright. If we are in a bad mood, the future will look dark. Hasn't it often happened to us that we realize how the future changes simply because we go through a daily change in mood?

In the same way, our moods shape the world we live in. As we have emphasized before, human beings do not relate to their natural and social environment as it is, objectively. We do not know how it "really" is, we only know how it is for us, according to our concerns and the mood we are in. Each mood brings with it its own world. When Voltaire's Candide says, "Everything is for the best in the best of all possible worlds," we still don't know exactly what that world was like. But we definitely know what mood he was in. We know how broad the horizon of his possibilities must have seemed.

One of the problems we face is that moods are often transparent to us. We don't notice them, and therefore we judge that what belongs to our moods is the property of our world. We normally assume that the world "is" as we observe it, without stopping to examine the role that the observer plays in what he observes. If we happen to be in a bad mood, we judge that everything around us "is" negative. If we are in a good mood, everything "is" positive. We do not normally realize that these positive and negative characteristics do not belong to the world itself, as something separate from us, but to the observer that we are, depending on the mood we are in.

We are not suggesting that, since we are the ones who color the world with positive and negative shades, the way it appears is independent of world events or simply irrelevant. Very often, it can be quite understandable that we see the world in negative or positive terms. If we get a promotion or a raise, it is quite reasonable for our mood to be optimistic. We are expanding our future possibilities, and our world looks brighter. But even in those circumstances, it is important to admit that our world resides in the mood we are in. If the mood changes, the world will also change with it.


 

Two complementary domains of mood observation

We have argued that human existence recognizes three primary domains: the domains of the body, emotionality, and language. We have said that these represent irreducible phenomenal domains, in the sense that the phenomena corresponding to each cannot be reduced to a different domain without dissolving the phenomenon being described. We have also postulated that, despite the irreducible nature of these three domains, they maintain relationships of coherence with each other and that, because of this, it is possible to reconstruct the phenomena of one domain in terms of the phenomena of either of the other two, just as it is also possible to intervene indirectly in a particular domain by intervening in the others.

This means, therefore, that we can observe emotional phenomena and act on them from the domains of the body and language. This is what we will examine below. We will begin our inquiry with the primary domain of the body. However, we will do so by establishing a distinction between two different subdomains within the domain of the body. We will discuss, on the one hand, the domain of biology and, on the other hand, the domain of corporeality.

Let us clarify the distinction we are making between these two subdomains into which we divide the domain of the body: biology and corporeality. We call biology the domain of the components and relationships that constitute our biological structure. The way we are using the distinction biology refers to the way our different (biological) components behave and relate to each other to produce the biological unit that we are. What is at stake here is the behavior of our biological components. When we talk about biology, therefore, we are pointing to what happens at the level of the nervous, digestive, circulatory, and other systems; we are examining, for example, different hormonal conditions or different genetic configurations.

When we talk about corporeality, we are in another domain of observation. What is at stake here is the physical behavior of an individual (of the biological unit as a whole), the way in which this unit physically situates itself in its environment, and the physical relationships it establishes with the entities that constitute that environment. We are using the distinction of corporeality to refer to our gestures, our body postures, the way an individual moves in their environment, and so on.

The domains of biology and corporeality are related. What happens at the biological level conditions what happens at the bodily level and vice versa. But despite their interconnection, they remain different domains of observation, and we use different sets of distinctions to refer to them.


 

Observing moods from the subdomain of biology

We must admit that what we would call the biology of moods is in its infancy, and much of our knowledge could change as new discoveries emerge. However, recent research reports [23] suggest, for example, that there is a very strong link between the conformation of the brain and the activity of the frontal lobes, and people's emotions and moods.

Specifically, the results show that people who have greater activity in the left frontal area of the brain than in the right tend to have a more positive and optimistic character. For example, they react more cheerfully to people and situations. On the other hand, people who have more activity in the right frontal cortex shy away from encounters with other people and are easily affected negatively by difficulties.

It has been reported that there are many situations that cause happiness for those in the first group, and they are usually sociable and lively individuals. They have a strong sense of self-confidence and see themselves as involved with the world in a rewarding way. Their melancholic counterpart, the report continues, sees the world as fraught with difficulties, tensions, and threats. They are easily suspicious of people and consider themselves incapable of managing their world well. They tend to see catastrophes in the smallest things.

Dr. Richard Davidson of the University of Wisconsin has found this type of brain structure in children as young as 10 months old. These structures made it possible to predict which 10-month-old children would cry when their mothers left the room for a minute. The research showed that all the children who cried had increased right frontal activity. Those who did not cry had more activity on the left side.

Other research methods have shown that patients who have had their right frontal lobe surgically removed have experienced significant personality changes after the operation. They become more affectionate and are less bothered by negative events.

These findings are consistent with the approach of Russian neuropsychologist A.R. Luria. He observed that activity in the left frontal lobe helps to stop an unpleasant emotion once it has begun. Damage to certain areas of this zone, he observed, makes patients more prone to obsessing over disturbing events.

This does not mean, however, that there is a single causal direction between brain structure and emotional states. It does not mean that our brain structure determines our emotional patterns. It is also possible to change our brain structure by intervening at the level of our emotions and moods. "If you learn to better control your negative feelings," says Dr. Davidson, "you may also learn to activate the left frontal lobe."

The way biology relates to our moods goes beyond this relationship between brain structure and emotional states. On the one hand, we know that many other biological alterations, in addition to the patterns of our frontal lobes, can produce emotional changes. Hormonal imbalances often

cause mood swings, and these changes can in turn be modified through chemical medications. This has been well documented in cases of depression. Drugs are a good example of how people affect their emotional states by causing a chemical disturbance in their biological structure.

On the other hand, we also know that a change in our emotional state produces biological changes. A change in our mood can cause our heartbeat to slow down or speed up, the level of adrenaline in our blood to rise or fall, sweating, fatigue, stomach pain, and so on. Recent experimental research has shown that a patient's mood is an important factor in their ability to recover from a wide range of illnesses (severe headaches, cancer, heart disease, stress, nervous disorders, etc.).[24]


 

Observing moods from the subdomain of corporeality

We can all admit that we are always in one physical posture or another (in fact, we cannot avoid it). This physical posture is related to our emotional state. The connection between our body postures and our emotional life is so strong that we can tell a person's mood simply by observing their body posture. We can also predict the physical postures people will adopt if we know what emotional state they are in.

We can tell that someone is excited or disappointed by observing the way they cross a room, the way they sit or stand. The visual arts use this connection, allowing us to observe people's moods from their body postures. When we look at Rodin's "The Thinker," for example, we can discover his mood. Likewise, when we look at Michelangelo's "La Pietà," we can describe Mary's state of mind. Goya's portraits are an excellent example of what we are pointing out, as they masterfully reveal how much body language tells us about the emotional states of human beings. Actors also take advantage of the connection between body language and moods. Think of Greta Garbo, Humphrey Bogart, or James Dean. The way each of them moved, or "their postures," brought different moods to life.

When our moods change, our body postures often change as well. The interesting thing is that many times, by changing our body posture, we can also change our mood. A clear example of this is the effect of physical exercise (going for a walk, jogging, playing tennis) or other physical activities, such as dancing or yoga, on our mood.

In a new study [25], Dr. Richard Davidson and Dr. Paul Ekman of the University of California, San Francisco, show the relationship between certain types of smiles, brain activity, and corresponding emotionality. Of eighteen different types of smiles, one of them, called the "Duchenne smile"—named after the 19th-century French neurologist Benjamin Amand Duchenne—activates the left anterior region of the cerebral cortex, while producing feelings of joy and delight. The "Duchenne smile" is characterized by the engagement of two important facial muscles: the zygomaticus major, which runs from the cheeks to the corners of the lips, and the orbicularis oculi, which surrounds the eyes.

What the researchers discovered was that when the subjects in their experiment were asked to smile using these two muscles, the aforementioned activation in the cerebral cortex occurred and generated feelings of joy. Therefore, by intervening at the level of physicality, they indirectly intervened at both the biological and emotional levels.

In a different study, complementary to the previous one, Dr. Davidson points out, referring to a woman who was a subject in that study, that "when she was asked to put her facial muscles into an expression of sadness, she burst into tears."

Our physical postures are not innocent. Every time we adopt a certain physical posture, we reveal a particular way of being, the way we situate ourselves in the world—the way we stand and move in the world denotes the way we face the world. If we want to change our position in life, we must also change our physical postures.


 

Moods and language

However, it is the connection between moods and language that will be emphasized in this work. We say that, depending on the mood we are in, our conversations are different—we speak and listen differently. This allows us to discover the mood people are in by examining their conversations. Each conversation brings with it its own mood. Conversations are not emotionally innocent either. Biologist Humberto Maturana argues that conversations are not just a linguistic phenomenon. A conversation is always a combination of two basic factors—language and emotions.

We maintain that these two factors are not independent of each other. To engage in conversation, language and emotions must be balanced. There is a basic principle of coherence between them. We cannot randomly select a sequence of linguistic acts and add to them, also at random, whatever emotions we find at hand. Language and emotional states are closely related to each other.

We all know how our mood changes when someone gives us good or bad news. We have all experienced a change in mood as a result of having a business proposal accepted or an important request refused. We also all know that we can produce different moods in people by talking to them about the same topic in different ways. Every day we have multiple experiences in which we observe how what we say can influence people's moods.

The existence of this relationship between language and emotions is not a trivial matter. On the contrary, it is because this relationship exists that the most interesting possibility of intervening in the design of our moods arises. This is where the ability to master the shaping of our own and others' moods reaches its peak.

What do we gain from this relationship? Two important things. First, it allows us to perform a linguistic reconstruction of our moods and emotions. We can now translate our emotional states into a linguistic structure. We generate a language of emotions and moods. We maintain that, regardless of their biological and bodily support, moods can be presented and reconstructed in linguistic terms.

Second, we develop two domains of action and design. We can now intervene in the domain of our emotional states through action at the level of language and, conversely, we can intervene at the level of our linguistic acts by changing our emotional states. By combining these two domains of action, we can then design complete sequences of interventions in which language and emotions will modify each other.

Great seducers, whether they operate in the realm of love, politics, sales, religion, pedagogy, etc., are all people who know the power of language to modify emotions and, consequently, to redefine what is possible. They know that all linguistic exchange operates within an emotional context that conditions the effectiveness of communication, just as they know that whatever the original emotional context, it can be transformed as a result of communication itself. What was possible at the beginning of a conversation is not necessarily the same at the end of the conversation.

It is surprising to note how little attention is commonly paid to the connection between language and moods in conversation. We know that our communication with others is affected by the mood that existed before the conversation began and by the mood that the conversation itself generates, but we don't usually do much about it. Too often we move awkwardly in this field, as if we were blindfolded, and we do not take responsibility for the moods we create. We are generally far from realizing that our conversations can be a fundamental tool for changing other people's moods and that doing so opens up new areas of action. We become passive victims, both of pre-existing moods and of those we produce.


 

Towards a linguistic reconstruction of moods

Having examined the nature of moods, we realize that they play, in a different domain, a role equivalent to that played by the linguistic acts we have called judgments. Judgments, as we know, also define a space of possible actions. They also specify a possible future and, like moods, color the world we live in.

It is this link between moods and judgments that serves to ensure the coherent relationship between emotionality and language, and it is also what opens up the possibility of a linguistic reconstruction of the former. When we make a judgment, we relate to what we are judging from the point of view of our concerns and formulate it in terms of the possibilities it opens or closes for us. Judgments specify the world we live in and our horizon of future possibilities in the same way that moods do. This constitutes the basis for the linguistic reconstruction of moods.

We can now treat moods as automatic judgments of the world (and of the future possibilities that world brings us). We call them automatic because we know that they are not judgments that we make. In fact, we don't normally make them. Moods precede action; they are already there when we are about to act. We find ourselves immersed in them. Consequently, when we speak of moods as automatic judgments of the world, we are not describing what moods are, but rather we are doing a linguistic reconstruction, that is, a translation of the phenomenon of moods into a linguistic code.

Let's take some examples. If we are in a mood of enthusiasm, we can reconstruct this mood as the judgment we make when we say, "This is exciting!" This is a judgment that we can articulate—as with any judgment—as an "underlying linguistic structure" about the world. When we formulate the judgment, "This is exciting!", the underlying structure basically involves the following two linguistic acts:

— I assert that X is happening

— I judge that X will expand my possibilities in the future

This same structure is a valid linguistic reconstruction for the mood of enthusiasm. Therefore, enthusiasm is the mood that can be linguistically reconstructed as the underlying linguistic structure when we make the judgment that something is exciting. The way we name our moods is not arbitrary. It immediately creates the connection between moods and judgments, making this linguistic reconstruction possible.

Do we need anything else to linguistically reconstruct the mood of enthusiasm? We say no. This structure accounts for it in its entirety. In fact, when we put these two linguistic acts together, we generate the mood of enthusiasm. What is interesting about this linguistic structure is that it offers us the basic guidelines for generating or dissolving a state of enthusiasm. If we want to generate it, we must ensure that these two linguistic acts occur. If we want to dissolve it, we must ensure that those judgments are not made.

Now, if, on the contrary, we find ourselves in a mood of boredom, we can observe the underlying conversation implicit in the judgment, "This is boring!" The mood of boredom can then be reconstructed as the underlying linguistic structure corresponding to this judgment. In the case of boredom, the structure is as follows:

— I assert that X is happening

— I judge that X will not open or close any possibilities for me in the future.

Finally, let's take sadness. The question we should ask ourselves again is: what underlying linguistic structure appears when we say, "This is sad"? The mood can be reconstructed based on the following elements:

— I assert that X has happened (or is happening)

— I judge that X represents a loss for me (I judge that some possibilities have diminished).

— I accept that loss as a fact

As we can see, each state of mind generates a particular world. And that world will be very different if we are in a state of enthusiasm, boredom, or sadness. In general, when we are in "positive" moods (happiness, enthusiasm, admiration, etc.), we find ourselves in a world that is full of new possibilities for the future. When we are in "negative" moods (sadness, fear, anxiety, etc.), we find ourselves in a world that closes off our possibilities.

Any mood can be reconstructed by treating it as we have done, that is, as automatic judgments. Although we can establish a correspondence between moods and judgments, the opposite is not always possible. We cannot say that every judgment is connected to a different mood. For example, when we say, "Catherine is right" or "Benjamin is tall," or "Aurora is efficient," we are making judgments. However, in these cases, the mood in which we make these judgments is not evident from the judgments themselves. To determine the mood, we need to examine the context of the conversation in which these judgments are made.


 

Moods in social contexts

From what has been said so far, we might assume that moods are fundamentally an individual phenomenon. Certainly, individuals have moods. They are always in one mood or another. However, moods are highly contagious, and if we are among people in very negative moods, we should expect that we will soon be in a negative mood ourselves. Moreover, our individual moods always originate within the broader social mood of our community and our time. It is interesting to note, for example, how individual moods in the United States became gloomier during the period when the country was involved in the Persian Gulf War in 1991.

Communities determine the mood of the individuals who belong to them, but individuals also determine the mood of their communities. An individual's ability to determine the mood of a community is one of the important skills we associate with leadership. Leaders, among other things, are the designers of their communities' moods. They are the ones who generate new horizons of possibilities for their communities and the new actions that become possible.

In the same way that we talk about individual moods, we can also talk about the moods of very different social environments. Every community of people, whether it is a company, a family, a class at school, a sports team, a political party, etc., has its own particular moods. Sometimes, we only need to open the door to someone's house to know what mood the family is in.

The same is true for companies. That is why it is important in business to check the mood of all those who normally work on the front line of the company's activities, those who deal with customers and the general public on a recurring basis (salespeople, receptionists, executives, etc.). The mood of a company's front-line people is central to building its public identity.

But more important than concern for the company's public identity is the fact that its mood is a decisive factor in determining what the company will be able to achieve, how it will react to changes in its environment, how its individual members will respond to the challenge of creating a future for it, and so on.

Social moods are not an eccentricity. They are a fundamental component of our way of life and the quality of our lives. Social moods determine the horizon of possible actions for people and, therefore, are an important factor in predicting what they can or cannot achieve.

Athletes have recognized the importance of moods not only in individual performance but also in how the team performs. Social moods are so important that military strategists consider them one of the crucial factors in the design of military actions. They call it the "morale" of the troops. They know that morale is a factor of utmost importance in winning a battle, sometimes even more so than military manpower and the power of their military equipment. Napoleon wrote that "in war, morale is worth three-quarters; the balance between the strength of individuals and materials is only worth the remaining quarter."

We know the importance of moods in shaping economic behavior. Economists have long established the relationship between the expectations of economic agents (the space of possibility from which they operate and, therefore, their moods) and a nation's economic performance. This can be seen very clearly in the fluctuations that take place on the stock exchanges—which are a good indicator of the general mood of the country. We also know the importance of moods in the political arena. Mood was the critical factor in Jimmy Carter's political defeat in the United States, as it was in Ronald Reagan's subsequent success.

Social moods can be mobilized politically and turned into important historical forces. Hitler's power was built, for the most part, by capitalizing on the deep resentment that remained among the German people after the signing of the Treaty of Versailles at the end of World War I. The recent collapse of socialism in Eastern European countries was largely the result of the spread of a widespread mood of frustration. Revolutionaries of all stripes have always recognized the importance of the moods of the people. The power they achieve lies mainly in their ability to mobilize those moods to their own advantage.


 

Can we do anything with moods?

So far, we have seen the importance of moods in the lives of individuals. We have also seen the consequences of people's ability to generate moods conducive to what they want to achieve. Most of us, however, are carried along by the moods we find ourselves in and the moods that surround us, whether at home, at work, in our community, and so on. Our moods are defined by the drift of our lives, and in general, we do not have the ability to produce significant and stable changes in them.

Even when we recognize that some people are skilled at generating positive moods in others—moods that are conducive to achieving their goals or purposes, or simply moods that improve quality of life and make people happier—we assume that this is produced by hidden or mysterious talents, so mysterious that they seem unattainable to us.

We often find ourselves so powerless to change our moods and those of others that we do not even take responsibility for the moods we ourselves create. If someone complains about the mood we bring to work, we may say, "Well, that's just the way I am, and there's nothing I can do about it." This "take it or leave it" attitude can have disastrous consequences, not only in business but also in politics, in our personal relationships, in family life, and so on.

Many people, however, take responsibility for their moods and often realize that there are some actions they can take to change them. However, what such people usually fail to see is the possibility they have to intervene in changing the mood of the social environment in which they live. And when this mood becomes unsustainable, they leave, they withdraw from that social environment. They start the whole process over again, until they reach another point of saturation. This happens with relationships, with friends, with work, with cities, and so on. We move from one place to another, from one relationship to another, from one job to another, and so on. Our life never really stabilizes—we don't find a stable dwelling where we can live in peace.

We are not saying that we should not move from one social environment to another or that our relationships, friends, work, etc., should be for life. Nor are we saying that the moods that prevail in those places are our sole and exclusive responsibility. All of these are areas of shared responsibility with others. What we are saying, however, is that, in general, we do not even assume our fair share of responsibility. Therefore, we do not see that we can take action to change those moods. By not seeing this, we restrict our own possibilities in life. When no one assumes their share of responsibility for the moods we generate, we can anticipate the results that will be obtained.


 

Moods as a domain of design

We maintain that we can take an active position with regard to our personal moods and the moods of the social environment in which we participate. Instead of being carried away by them, we can participate in shaping those moods. We are not saying that we can totally control our moods, let alone those of other people. Our lives will always unfold amid events that are beyond our control.

However, in the drift of life, there is also room for design. When we talk about design, we recognize that our actions have the capacity, among many other factors, to intervene and modify the current course of events. As individuals, we are an active force in the drift.

We consider moods to be a particular domain of design. However, we need to seriously revise our traditional interpretation of human beings in order to participate in shaping our moods and those of the people around us. This interpretation limits our capacity for action and obscures our possibilities for designing moods. In this regard, we maintain that the interpretation of moods presented here increases the power we have as individuals, since it opens up possibilities for action that are difficult to see from outside it.

We have said that every mood specifies a space of possible actions, a horizon of possibilities. There is therefore a fundamental connection between our moods and our possibilities for action. This is perhaps the most important relationship for treating moods as a domain of design. If moods and possibilities are so closely connected, then by modifying our horizon of possibilities we modify our moods. This is a fundamental postulate. At this point, we already know that we have the power to change our horizon of possibilities through conversations and, therefore, we can accept that conversations are decisive tools for designing moods.

Which conversations have the power to produce changes in moods? All of them. Conversing is being in a process of mutual transformation, and this transformation includes our moods and emotions. This is what conversation is all about. A "conversation to coordinate actions" changes our horizon of possibilities by enabling us to carry out the proposed actions. But we also have conversations that directly aim at this change in the space of possibilities. These are "conversations for possible actions" and "conversations for possible conversations." They are the most effective tools for shaping our moods.

In a way, this is not new. We know it. We have all had countless experiences in which, by talking to people, we have changed their moods, or our mood has changed simply by talking to someone. We do it all the time, and these experiences are right there in front of our eyes. But have we really noticed it? Did we know it? It is difficult to answer these questions with a simple "yes or no." In some cases we knew, and in many others we did not. And we did not know because we often did not see our moods as moods, but as the way the world presents itself to us. We think that what is possible is only related to the world and not to us. We have often resigned ourselves to our moods and those of others, without seeing any possibility of changing them. Our traditional understanding of human beings has blinded us to the recognition of moods as a domain of design.


 

Some guidelines for designing moods

Based on what we have said above, here are some very general guidelines that may be useful for designing moods. Let's assume that we are dealing with our own moods. To deal with other people's moods, some corrections should be made, although the same structure and criteria are also valid. These are the guidelines:

a. Become an observer of moods. This involves starting by identifying them as moods and not as "attributes of the world" or "how things are." If we assume, as moods normally lead us to believe, that our judgments of the world really correspond to how the world is, we close off our possibilities for producing different moods. It is only when we develop the competence to observe our moods as such that we open ourselves up to design.

b. We are not responsible for the moods we find ourselves in. We did not "produce" that particular mood. We are simply in it. Recognizing that we are not the producers of our moods, but rather that moods produce us, allows us to intervene more easily—and in a lighter way—in the design of our moods. However, even though we are not responsible for the mood we find ourselves in, we are responsible for remaining in it.

c. Be careful with the stories we have fabricated around our moods. We tend to find our moods correct and can give endless reasons for the enormous significance of being in the mood we are in. It is important to remember that it is often not what the story says that produced that mood; it is the mood that produced the story.

d. Once the mood is identified, we should look for the judgments that correspond to it. Let's ask ourselves:

— How am I judging the world?

— How am I judging the people around me?

— What judgments do I have about myself?

— What judgments do I have about the future?

These questions should be sufficient to specify the state of mind we are in.

e. Once we have identified our state of mind as an automatic judgment, we can examine the linguistic structure underlying that judgment.

f. Once we have the underlying linguistic structure, we can examine whether the statements implied by this structure are true or false, whether the judgments it contains are well-founded or not, whether the statements it contains are valid or invalid, and so on. Based on the analysis of the underlying linguistic structure, we can discover whether or not the basis for that state of mind is sufficient. If it is not, we will be in a much better position to change it.

g. Once the underlying linguistic structure has been identified and we have examined the different linguistic acts it contains, we can look for actions to take to change those statements, judgments, etc. These actions may include conversations we can initiate to modify the underlying linguistic structure of the state of mind. We should not allow our mood to block action. This is what moods commonly do. However, it is generally our capacity for action that allows us to change our mood. By acting, we can change those statements and judgments, or we can make them irrelevant to the concerns of the people involved.

h. If we realize that we tend to fall into the same moods repeatedly, we can also take action to anticipate when the mood will reappear. By doing this, we can build "repertoires." These are courses of action that we prepare in a certain mood that would make it difficult for us to design, at that moment, the actions necessary to get out of it. We must commit to following these repertoires when we fall back into those moods (for example, by doing some physical exercise, calling a friend or colleague who has committed to helping us, etc.).

i. One of the actions we can take is to immerse ourselves among people with whom our moods do not have much room. Let us not forget that moods are contagious. Just as we transmit our moods to other people, they will also transmit theirs to us. This works both ways. If we choose to be with people who have little room for our mood, we will most likely benefit from their influence.

j. We must also not forget the link between our body and our moods. If we change our body posture, if we exercise, certain moods should disappear. Music is a very effective way of intervening at the bodily level to change our moods.

k. Most of the guidelines provided so far attempt to produce a change in our moods by changing our conversations and thus affecting the linguistic aspect of our moods. This line of action has its limits. If our mood is due to a biological condition, changing our conversations may not be enough in some cases. If this is the case, there is also the possibility of acting in a way that allows us to intervene directly at the biological level, as happens when a doctor prescribes medication that alters the biological basis of our emotions.

l. Let us not forget that we cannot avoid moods. Whatever we do, we will always be in one mood or another. There is nothing wrong with being in a mood. The purpose of the design is to open up the possibility of intervention, so that one can acquire a sense of responsibility for one's own moods and those of those around them, and then gain the skills to shape them according to one's own judgment of convenience.


 

Kairos: a brief commentary on human time

A characteristic feature of this phase of history that we call Modernity, which extends from the beginning of the 17th century to the present day, has been to uphold a conception of time that conceives it as linear, continuous, and homogeneous.

Two factors seem to have contributed to this conception. First, the importance that the natural sciences, and particularly physics, have acquired since the beginning of modernity. For a long time, they served as a model of rigorous thinking, and their assumptions were often imported uncritically into the realm of reflection on human phenomena. For a long period dominated by Newton's mechanical worldview, physical time was considered precisely as absolute and autonomous. The flow of time is conceived as a linear and continuous sequence of equivalent units. From the point of view of Newtonian physics, one minute was exactly the same as any other minute.

Added to the impact of the development of physics is a different but complementary factor. This is the invention of the mechanical clock in the 14th century and its impact on social coexistence. This invention completely changed the social concept of time, allowing human beings to synchronize the way they coordinate actions and, as a result, substantially increase the efficiency and productivity of joint actions. From that moment on, human time was referenced to the mechanical time of the clock, which obviously homogenized it. A minute is a minute for everyone, and this is the time it takes for the second hand to go around the clock face. It is the mechanical behavior of the second hand that defines the concept of time that governs human behavior.

In physics, the concept of time underwent a radical transformation with the theory of relativity developed by Einstein in the early 20th century. One of Einstein's main contributions was to question Newton's assumption of absolute time and his resulting concept of simultaneity. The curious thing about this, however, is that Einstein's new conception, rather than undermining the everyday concept of human time, which is strongly influenced by mechanical physics, separates itself from it, presenting itself as an abstract concept of time, alien to human time. We see, therefore, that human beings remain attached to a mechanical concept of time, even when physics itself has already abandoned it.

Based on the developments in this chapter on the subject of emotions and moods, we see that not every moment, not every unit of physical time, implies the same possibilities. For human beings, time is not homogeneous. What can happen in one minute is not equivalent to what can happen in any other. The density of life that a given minute can contain for one person is not the same as the density that same minute contains for another person. The flow of human time is a succession of discontinuous occasions, very different from one another and often very different for the different individuals involved.

The density of human time is heterogeneous, in that the same physical unit of time can contain very different possibilities. Something that could not be realized for years may open up as a possibility for a minute, only to close immediately afterward. That minute is very different from the one that preceded it or the one that will follow it. Human time, measured mechanically, is simply not the same.

Once we accept the above, we recognize that emotionality, insofar as it specifies different dispositions for action and, consequently, different spaces of possibility, is a fundamental factor in evaluating the different densities of human time. It is perhaps in this respect that our traditional conception, based on the model of rational action, shows one of its main shortcomings. The effectiveness of human action is not only a function of our ability to rationally articulate means for achieving certain objectives.

The effectiveness of our action is also a function of our ability to observe, evaluate, and design those emotional spaces that make possible what was not previously possible or that close possibilities that were previously open. The effectiveness of our actions is a function of the emotional conditions (our own and those of others) specific to the situation in which we operate. And there is no human action that escapes this emotional conditioning. At home, at work, at play, etc., what we can do, what we can achieve, will depend to a large extent on the existing emotional conditions.

This leads us to an important confrontation that took place in ancient Greece at the beginning of what we have called "the great metaphysical drift," which we see today in crisis, but of which we are still a part. The Greeks who preceded the fathers of metaphysics, Plato and Aristotle, had recognized the heterogeneous nature of human time. And they had a term through which they accounted for it. They spoke of kairos.

Kairos, for the Greeks, was that moment of opportune time, of appropriate time, of the occasion when possibility manifests itself in temporality and then disappears into it. Through kairos, it was recognized that the value of an action is realized in time and that not all time is equal. Sometimes one acts too early, other times too late. But sometimes one acts at just the right time. To refer to the latter, the Greeks coined the term kairos.

Kairos was a term commonly used by athletes to refer to the moment when they were given the opportunity to perform a certain action. In the sport of archery, for example, it was used to refer to the opening or opportunity to shoot, the cylinder through which the arrow must pass on its trajectory toward the target.

The same term was also used by horse-drawn carriage racers to refer to the moment when a space opened up for them to overtake their opponent. For much of the race, the drivers knew that they could only stay behind the one who had taken the lead. But there were often moments when an opening, an opportunity, arose to accelerate the horses and move ahead. They called such moments kairos.[27]

In a second meaning, as kairos, the term was associated with the art of weaving on a loom. It referred to that critical moment when the weaver must pull the thread through the gap (opening) that momentarily opens in the warp of the fabric being woven.

Gorgias, one of the great sophists and, therefore, a repeated target of criticism from metaphysicians, incorporates the term kairos as a central distinction in his theory of rhetoric. Gorgias is dedicated to investigating the power of language and its capacity for transformation. Language, he tells us, "can stop fear and banish suffering and create joy and nurture compassion."

Like the rest of the sophists, Gorgias is dedicated to educating young people in civic virtues, what the Greeks call areté. He understands that the nascent democratic practices taking place in Greece rest precisely on the linguistic skills of citizens and, more particularly, on the exercise of the art of persuasion. To this end, Gorgias insists, the speaker must always be attentive to the flow of conversation in order to detect opportunities (kairos) that arise to persuade the listener.

The emotional level of the conversation is a decisive factor in ensuring its success, both for the speaker (in which case we speak of ethos) and for the listener (in which case we speak of pathos). The speaker, therefore, must navigate the conversation as a sailor does, always attentive to the meaning and force of the waves and the direction of the wind, always ready to change the position of his sails. Truth is intrinsically related to its context. It will be what the community accepts as such and, in this sense, truth is seen by Gorgias as the very result of the art of persuasion.

Both Plato and Aristotle strongly oppose the positions adopted by Gorgias. For them, truth exists independently of individuals, although it is possible for them to access it through rational thought. Once such truth is attained, language is the instrument through which it is communicated to others. What matters in communication, according to Aristotle's position, is the truth content of what is said. Both the ethos of the speaker and the pathos of the audience must be subordinated to the logos. This accounts for the truth content and represents the rational aspect of communication.

With the predominance of the metaphysical program, recognition of the heterogeneous and discontinuous nature of human time gradually faded. With this, we forgot the importance of the notion of kairos proposed by the Greeks and incorporated into the teachings of nometaphysical philosophers such as Gorgias of Leontini.
