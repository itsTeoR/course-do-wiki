FOUR BASIC EMOTIONAL STATES

[28]

When I wrote the first draft of this text, I was not familiar with Stoic thought. I had some general references to it, but I was far from adequately understanding their positions. I was therefore greatly surprised when I delved into it and discovered that the text I had written had a marked Stoic sensibility. Since then, I have considered this text to be my own Stoic text.


 

By way of introduction: a note on the Stoics

Although the metaphysical program initiated by Plato and Aristotle ended up achieving unquestionable historical dominance for more than two thousand years, such influence was not the case during antiquity. On the contrary, although it exerted a strong influence from its inception, the metaphysical perspective they advocated had to coexist for many years with other philosophical positions that did not always accept its central postulates and, on the contrary, drew on those philosophers stigmatized by the metaphysicians. Among these divergent positions, it is worth mentioning the Hellenistic currents of the Stoics, Epicureans, Cynics, and Skeptics, who exerted their influence not only in Greece but also in Rome.

On the origin of the Stoics, I prefer to quote the words of KH. Sandbach, who points out:

In the northwest corner of the agora, the large central square of Athens, stood the Stoa Poikile, or painted colonnade, so named because of the murals by Polygnotus and other great artists of the 5th century BC that adorned it. There, during the early period of the 3rd century BC, one could often see the figure of a person sitting and addressing an audience; his name was Zeno, and his followers (...) were later called 'men of the Stoa' or, simply, 'Stoics.'

Zeno is the founder of the Stoics. He was born in 333 BC in Cyprus and moved to Athens in 312 BC, ten years after Aristotle's death. The representatives of this philosophical movement spread their influence throughout Greece and later throughout the Roman Empire. Emperor Hadrian was strongly influenced by their doctrines, and his successor, Emperor Marcus Aurelius, was an important representative of this school. Among its most prominent members were Seneca, the Hispano-Roman philosopher, and Epictetus, the slave philosopher from Hierapolis, Greece, both born during the first century AD.

The Stoics considered themselves followers of the teachings of Heraclitus. In their writings and reflections, Heraclitus occupies a prominent place, particularly because of their interest in the subject of change. Heraclitus had argued for the need to perceive things "according to their nature"

[29]. The Stoics adopted this precept and transformed it into the central criterion of their philosophy. This criterion is not only used with regard to knowledge, but also becomes an ethical guideline for good living. Heraclitus had stated that "a man's character is his destiny"

[30], and the Stoics made the theme of human character one of their main concerns.

The Stoics also considered themselves followers of Socrates. As such, however, they proposed a very different path from that followed by Plato and Aristotle. One of Socrates' most important contributions to the history of philosophy is to have reoriented it toward the great theme of human life. As Gregory Vlastos aptly argues, Socrates' philosophy is above all a moral philosophy in which the themes of human life and good living occupy a central place. Heraclitus had already initiated this shift toward the human soul. However, Socrates carried it out with unprecedented radicalism.

The pre-Socratic philosophers had devoted themselves entirely to an effort to understand the universe, what the Greeks called physis. Hence Aristotle later described them as physikoi (physicists) and, to differentiate himself from them, proposed the term metaphysics (that which is beyond physics). The Sophists, contemporaries of Socrates and part of the movement from which Socrates himself drew inspiration and from which he started, were concerned with the development of civic virtues (arete). Socrates, on the other hand, brings the concern of philosophy to the subject of life and the virtues that allow human beings not only to be effective citizens but, above all, to live well. This is the core of Socratic concern.

It is no less effective, however, that the way Socrates conducts his philosophical inquiry into life—that is, the type of questions he asks, the type of argumentation he follows, and the type of answers he accepts—will sow the seed that will later become, in Plato's hands, the birth of metaphysics. But Socrates, as Vlastos convincingly demonstrates, was not a metaphysician, he was not a philosopher centrally committed to the apprehension of being and its truth, as Plato and Aristotle would be. His fundamental philosophical commitment consisted of the rigorous inquiry into the good life.

It is in this vein that Socrates would be an important influence on the Stoics. The latter never abandoned their interest in the domain of ethics, in the inquiry into the meaning of life, and in discussing the conditions that allow for the good life. It is true that they also ventured into other domains and, in doing so, made proposals in the fields of physics and knowledge. It is also true that some of them show a significant influence from the metaphysical positions adopted by Plato and Aristotle. But this never led them to abandon the Socratic program aimed at discerning the conditions for the good life.

The goal of the Stoics will be very similar to that later proposed by Nietzsche. It is not for nothing that he will proclaim himself "the last of the Stoics." What Nietzsche proposes is precisely to take up the great Socratic theme of human life, strip it of the metaphysical wrapping with which Socrates had covered it—and which, according to Nietzsche, leads him astray—and provide it, instead, with a Heraclitean basis. It is not surprising, therefore, to note his great affinity with the Stoics. They had set themselves an equivalent program.


 

The moral philosophy of Epictetus

Among all the Stoics, the one with whom I feel the greatest affinity is Epictetus, the Greek philosopher who was born a slave and died a free man. Epictetus did not have a philosophical system per se. His teachings, collected by his disciple Arrian, constitute a set of recommendations for good living. Of the Stoics, he is the furthest removed from the logical or even metaphysical interests of some of his predecessors. His Discourses are advice aimed at guiding men to live wisely. This is the wisdom that Heraclitus had already spoken to us about. Hence, Epictetus is one of the most Heraclitean of the Stoics.

Heraclitus had warned us that "most men do not think things as they find them, nor do they recognize what results from their own experience, but rather stick to their own opinions" [31]. Epictetus goes further. For him, men's opinions not only define the world they inhabit, but also determine their own lives. According to the judgments that human beings make about what happens to them, they generate one type of life or another and determine the happiness and peace they will find in it. Let us remember that Epictetus pointed out that "it is not what has happened that upsets a man, since it may not upset another. It is his judgment of what has happened."

Among the judgments that human beings make are those that refer to change and define what is possible. Few judgments are as important as these. Heraclitus had already argued that "he who does not expect will not find the unexpected, for it is difficult to discover and impossible to attain." Epictetus, once again, offers us a similar recommendation when he points out that "we must measure both the size of our stride and the extent of our hope according to what is possible." What is possible therefore defines what we can expect and how much we can achieve in life.

The way Epictetus specifies "what is possible" is by resorting to the aforementioned Heraclitean criterion of establishing "agreement with nature." By determining what is in accordance with the nature of anything, we determine the limits of what we can expect with respect to it, the limits of what is possible and what is not possible to change. If we discover that something "belongs to the nature" of a particular thing, it means that we cannot expect that thing to go beyond, to transcend the limit defined by its nature. The criterion of nature therefore defines the space of possible change that can be expected from whatever the case may be.

This criterion is characteristic of all Stoic thinkers and has a central place in Epictetus' philosophy. Hence, he insists that before embarking on any task, we must always ask ourselves about its nature. Epictetus argues: "whatever we do, whatever we enjoy, whatever we find useful or appreciate, we must ask ourselves from the outset, 'What is its nature?'" Similarly, Epictetus reiterates, "when one is about to undertake something, remember the nature of what one is about to undertake." The criterion of nature defines "what will be possible" to achieve in the task at hand.

Striving to achieve what is not possible by nature is a source of unnecessary suffering. Therefore, much suffering can be avoided by asking ourselves about the nature of what we are undertaking and by examining, according to this criterion, what we expect from our actions and those of others, from things, from people. Once we determine what is "according to the nature" of things, we adjust our expectations accordingly and protect ourselves from absolutely unnecessary disappointments.

This allows Epictetus to introduce the topic of personal power. The power we have is limited by the criterion of nature. By definition, human beings do not have the power to change what is determined by the nature of things. The criterion of nature, therefore, simultaneously defines the limits of our power to transform and the limits of what we can and cannot change.

True to this, Epictetus' Manual opens with the following paragraph:


 

"Of all existing things, some are in our power and some are not in our power. In our power are our thoughts, our impulses, our will to achieve and our will to avoid, and, in a word, everything that corresponds to what we can do. Things that are not in our power include our body, our property, our reputation, our position, and, in a word, everything that does not correspond to our doing. Things within our power are by nature free, unhindered, unimpeded; things not within our power are weak, servile, subject to hindrance, dependent on others."

To live well, it is necessary to focus on the things that are within our power and not waste time on those things over which we have no power. Epictetus repeatedly urges us: "Exercise yourself in what is within your power."

A common interpretation of Stoic thought attributes to them the preaching of resignation, promoting an easy acceptance that the existing state of affairs cannot be changed. This implies a distortion of their thinking. As we can see in the case of Epictetus, he insists that we learn to discern what can and cannot be changed and, once that has been determined, to commit ourselves fully to change. The worst thing that can happen to us is to be trapped in an expectation of change that contravenes the nature of things. Knowing how to use the criterion of harmony with nature, according to Epictetus, translates into wisdom of life.


 

The criterion of harmony with nature and judgments

Stoic thought exerted an important influence on the early stages of Christianity's development. It was an important source of inspiration for Christian slaves struggling to survive and expand their influence during the Roman Empire. This influence was also taken up by Christian theology, and we recognize it in the field of natural law, where the criterion of conformity with nature survives, although it is often presented in Aristotelian terms.

What natural law maintains is that human behavior must be governed according to certain guidelines, as these are consistent with the nature of whatever the case may be. This obviously poses a problem for which different types of solutions have been offered, different theological interpretations and legal philosophies.

The problem consists in accepting that human beings can behave in a way that contradicts natural law, in a way that is therefore inconsistent with their nature. The question then arises: how is it possible to maintain that there is a certain way of behaving that is in accordance with nature and simultaneously recognize that human beings can behave differently? Was not the natural criterion the one that defined the limits of what was possible?

We consider that the criterion of harmony with nature is the great Achilles heel of Stoic thought. Although taken from Heraclitus himself, it represents the place where the metaphysical approach can filter through. It is also the place that allows the reappropriation of Stoic doctrines by the metaphysical program. Once again, it is a question of postulating an absolute point of support from which to sustain "the being of things" and the possibility of accessing it. As with the metaphysical approach itself, it is a question of introducing a resource of violence into social coexistence. The concept of Nature runs the risk of taking us back to the metaphysical territory of Being, Truth, and Reality, terms used to authoritatively deny the legitimacy of others.

How, then, can we recover the wisdom contained in Stoic thought, and particularly in the teachings of Epictetus, without falling into the need to postulate the criterion of nature? Is it possible to reconstruct this criterion in a different way, so as to avoid the metaphysical danger? Our answer is yes.

What the Stoics defined as agreement with nature is, in our opinion, the expression of the ability to establish two different judgments. We call these judgments of facticity and possibility. The convergence of these two judgments generates what the Stoics referred to when they spoke of nature.


 

Judgments of facticity and possibility

Based on our capacity to make judgments, we humans can make a fundamental distinction between two different areas. On the one hand, there is everything that we judge cannot be changed. However we act, whatever we do, things will remain as they are. We have coined an expression to talk about this—the so-called "facts of life." We will call this the realm of facticity.

On the other hand, there are all those situations that make up what we consider to be changeable. If we act effectively, everything that belongs to this area could be different in the future. This is a space that offers us possibilities for action. We will call this area the domain of possibility.

We postulate that a fundamental skill in life is one that allows us to make this distinction in a well-founded way, so that the separation between "facticity" and "possibility" is solid.


 

Ontological facticity

When we examine the "facticity" of life, we can identify at least two domains that are permanent dimensions of human existence. Whatever the historical circumstances, human beings cannot change these domains. They comprise what we call "ontological facticity." By ontological, we simply mean that we judge them to be constitutive of the human way of being. Whatever the circumstances, human beings do not have the capacity to change them.

The first domain of ontological facticity is the finitude of our body. Human beings, as a constitutive factor of our own condition, are limited, and our finitude is a fundamental dimension of our mode of being. Our actions, for example, will always be restricted by the limits of our biology. As Maturana insists, human beings can only do what we are biologically allowed to do. And what the body allows us to do also changes as we age. Over time, many things that were once possible at a young age disappear and cease to be possible. We have what are called "critical periods" within which certain possibilities exist, and beyond them, these possibilities are restricted or simply closed off. We cannot, for example, become a ballet star at the age of fifty. Perhaps that possibility is open at fifteen, but it disappears at fifty. Nor can we learn to speak a foreign language without an accent beyond a certain age. These are ontological facticity, inherent to our human condition. In another area, it is also not possible for us to avoid the end of our lives. Human life is finite and, therefore, death is inevitable. Therein lies another ontological facticity.

A second domain of ontological facticity points to the fact that we cannot change the occurrence of past events. Agathon, a Greek who lived in the fifth century BC, quoted by Aristotle, points out: "Not even God can change the past." This does not mean, of course, that we cannot change the way we interpret the past. Our interpretation of what happened can change many times, as evidenced by so many historical studies that modify our interpretations of our history. But what no one can change is the occurrence of events that have already taken place. The fact that something happened is a facticity, and there is nothing we can do to change it. We can reinterpret the event, just as we can also deal with its consequences, but we cannot eliminate its occurrence.


 

Historical facticity

There is a type of facticity that differs from what we have called ontological facticity. We call it historical facticity. We often consider that certain situations cannot be changed, not because this is inherent to our human condition, but because of the historical conditions in which we live. When this happens, we accept that if historical conditions were to change, it would be possible to change what previously seemed unchangeable.

For example, a couple of centuries ago, a person living in Mexico could not communicate instantly with another person living in Chile. This, however, was a historical impossibility and not an ontological one. With subsequent technological development, what was not possible then became possible later, to the point that if someone in Mexico tells us that they have just spoken to someone in Chile, we are not surprised.

Let's take a different example. The Catholic Church does not allow priests to marry. Today, this possibility is closed to Catholic priests as long as they want to remain priests. But it is not an ontological impossibility, it is a historical one. It was not always so in the past and it does not necessarily have to be so in the future. Historical facticity, unlike ontological facticity, is characterized by the fact that it can dissolve as facticity with the change of historical conditions. In this example, the Vatican would need to change its rules on the priesthood in relation to the issue of priests' marriage. There is evidence that there are currently some forces within the Church that are pushing for these changes. To the extent that those pushing for such changes become more powerful within the Church, we cannot rule out that what is not possible today may become possible in the future. A change in historical conditions transforms a situation of historical facticity into a situation of possibility.

It is important to recognize that when we speak of facticity, whether ontological or historical, we are not referring to what is usually called an "objective" condition. Facticity is always a judgment made by an observer about an event. As such, it can always be modified. This is an important point because history shows us that many judgments held up as judgments of ontological facticity have, over time, been shown to point to historical facticity. Taking our previous example, it is not strange to think that two centuries ago, instant communication between a person in Mexico and another in Chile might have been thought of as an absolute impossibility and, therefore, an ontological facticity.

To the extent that we recognize that facticity is a judgment, we also recognize that what one person might consider a historical facticity may well be considered by another as an area of possible transformation. This difference is one of the most important features of the phenomenon of leadership. Leaders are people who normally declare as possible things that other people consider impossible. This is precisely what makes them leaders. Our capacity for innovation conditions our judgment about what is historically possible, just as our judgments of facticity and possibility condition our capacity for innovation.

What we say is possible is always a judgment of the capacity of our actions to change what is given. As we have said before, this, in itself, is not given. It is not something established once and for all. Judgments of historical facticity or possibility are themselves historical. In other words, what separates historical facticity from historical possibility is closely related both to the statements we make and to the actions that such statements enable.

When we understand that what is possible is a judgment, we recognize that all possibility is constituted by the observer who makes that judgment. We also recognize that possibilities never exist independently of the observer who defines them as such. They are not "there" waiting to be "discovered." What is possible is always a function of human "invention." Hence, what is impossible for some can become possible once someone declares it to be so and acts accordingly.

A good example of this is major innovations in science and business. The fact that some people make certain statements and move in the direction indicated by them creates new historical circumstances, opening up possibilities for some and closing them for others. Recall, for example, the space program developed by the Kennedy administration. The power of those statements allowed us to realize one of humanity's oldest dreams—to reach the moon.

It is important to issue a warning. The above does not imply that everything we declare possible is so simply because it has been declared. That would imply a dangerous linguistic voluntarism. The declaration of possibility is a judgment and as such requires justification. The problem with innovations is that the past does not provide us with the elements to justify them, and these elements need to be provided in the very process that transforms the possible into reality.


 

We can accept or reject judgments of facticity and possibility.

Once we recognize that the distinction between facticity and possibility is the result of a judgment, we also recognize, based on what we have learned about judgments, that not everyone makes this distinction in the same way. Different people may differ significantly in how they draw this distinction. And the way they do so will generate different observers of what is possible, which, in turn, will determine both differences in the actions each will be willing to take and, obviously, in the consequences that will result in each person's life as a result of such actions. Since we can make different judgments about the same thing, someone may agree or disagree—they may accept or reject the way another person makes the distinction between facticity and possibility. A may believe that what B considers open to change is not, and that what B considers impossible to change could well be modified. This makes A and B different observers—that is, observers who may accept or disagree with each other's judgments.

One way to evaluate the validity of other people's judgments in distinguishing between facticity and possibility is to take as a basis the grounds they provide. We argue that many people judge something to be possible without substantiating their judgments. In doing so, they develop a kind of blindness in their lives, since they insist on changing what others, with more grounds, judge to be impossible, or declare something immutable that others consider possible to change.

It is common to encounter people who live by repeatedly saying things like, "If only that hadn't happened, I could have done this or that." Without denying that what they say may be valid, living in that conversation often shows us a fixation on a situation that can no longer be changed. There are others whose repeated attitude corresponds to saying, "Why should I bother doing that if I know nothing will change?" while we judge that the situation could be completely reversed if the decision to act existed. The important thing is to recognize the immense suffering that can result from such conversations. The way we make our judgments of possibility (or impossibility) has a significant impact on our lives.

The state of mind of resentment

When human beings fight against what we cannot change, when we show an inability to accept what we have called the facts of life, we create a space within which resentment can easily develop. We are not saying that resentment is only resistance to the facts of life. It takes more than rejection of what cannot be changed to create resentment. However, when we do not accept the facts of life, the mood of resentment finds fertile ground in which to develop.

What is resentment? This state of mind can be reconstructed in terms of an underlying conversation in which we interpret that we have been victims of an unjust action. A conversation that argues that we had the moral right to obtain something that was denied to us or that we simply deserved better than what we got. Someone stood in our way, preventing us from getting what we deserved, denying us possibilities that we believe we were entitled to. Someone, therefore, appears in our interpretation as guilty for what happens to us. In its linguistic reconstruction, we detect the judgment that someone closed off certain possibilities in our lives, as well as the judgment that this is unfair. This someone could be a person, a group of people, an entire class of individuals (for example, all men, all women, bosses, immigrants, Hispanics, Jews, Blacks, Gypsies, etc.). One could even blame the whole world or life as a whole.

But resentment usually does not stop there. In addition to the underlying judgments in which we can reconstruct it, we also discover a statement (or a promise we make to ourselves). Whoever we hold responsible for the injustice done to us will pay sooner or later. As soon as it is permissible, justice will be done. It may take time, but the moment will come when we will take revenge or someone (divine justice!) will avenge us. The spirit of revenge is a common byproduct of resentment.

The state of mind of resentment is close to that of anger. The main difference, however, is that anger is openly expressed. Resentment, on the other hand, remains hidden. It is kept as a private conversation. It grows in silence and is rarely expressed directly.

Resentment feeds on two sources: promises and expectations considered legitimate, which in both cases are not fulfilled. Both contribute to giving us the "right" to expect certain behavior from others towards us. It is from this "right" that resentment appears as an invocation of justice in the face of the injustice of what has happened. Promises, as we well know, generate duties and rights. Those who make promises commit themselves to fulfilling them. Those who receive a promise effectively acquire the right to expect it to be fulfilled.

But we are not only bound by our promises. In many cases, it is not enough to say "But I didn't promise!" to avoid our responsibility. In every community, we are also bound by the existence of certain social standards of behavior. There are certain actions that we are obliged to do and others that are forbidden to us, regardless of the promises we make.

It is not acceptable, for example, to say, "I never promised anyone that I would not physically abuse my wife," or "I never promised that I would take care of my children." Although in both cases no promises may have been made, if someone physically abuses his wife or does not assume the responsibilities that are socially expected of him (or her) as a father (or mother), this may generate resentment on the part of both the wife and the children affected. In that community, they have legitimate expectations of different behavior than what they got. In another community, social standards may be different, and the same actions will not necessarily generate resentment.

However, it is not enough to simply break a promise or fail to meet a legitimate expectation. Not every breach of these necessarily generates resentment. For resentment to develop, there must be a situation that obstructs or prevents us from expressing our anger or making a complaint. As we will see later, complaining is the most effective remedy against resentment.

Resentment, therefore, requires conditions that, in one way or another, block the possibility of making public the private conversation that feeds those who suffer the consequences of the breach of a promise or a legitimate expectation. For example, we may judge that we will not be understood by others or that if we make a public complaint we will face even worse consequences, and so on.

This is precisely where this "resistance to the facts of life" comes in. On the one hand, we tenaciously oppose the current state of affairs, which, we argue, has made us victims of injustice. On the other hand, we judge that there is nothing we can do to change it. We are therefore caught between the judgment of what was not only possible but also expected and "fair," and the judgment of facticity that there is nothing we can do to change it.

Resentment emerges from powerlessness and often reproduces it. An important reason for not expressing our anger and allowing resentment to develop is the belief that we are in a precarious position of power. We harbor resentment against someone who humiliates us by abusing their position of power, while judging that if we complained, we would be subjected to even worse "abuse." If we complained, we would "have to pay the consequences." We feel at the mercy of another's power. Resentment flourishes easily in situations of unequal distribution of power.

It is not surprising, therefore, that resentment often arises after a military defeat, when the victorious forces impose conditions on the vanquished that they consider extremely humiliating or that greatly exceed what is thought to be fair. Furthermore, where fear reigns, there is room for resentment. When the coordination of actions is based on fear, resentment can develop very easily.

The link between power distribution and resentment is critical, since the unequal distribution of power is almost an axiom of all forms of social life. It is practically impossible to imagine a social order in which power is equally distributed. The factors behind the distribution of power can change. Sometimes they may be based on physical strength; other times, they may stem from differences in financial resources, skills, political support, and so on. There is unequal distribution of power between parents and children, between teachers and students. It also exists in relationships with other people and peer groups, and definitely in organizations. In organizations, power is, almost by definition, distributed unequally.

Only strong discipline and intense socialization of an organization's highest goals allow subordinates to accept practices based on a highly hierarchical structure with a skewed distribution of power. Military organizations are a good example of what it takes to create special conditions of obedience and get people to accept rules of conduct such as those described by Lord Tennyson when he wrote The Charge of the Light Brigade: "Theirs not to reason why, theirs not to question why, theirs but to do and die." it is only for them to do and die").

The state of mind of resentment is extremely corrosive to social coexistence, so that it often acts in favor of the parties involved to find ways to make it disappear. The person "in" resentment is affected by a penetrating and often almost permanent suffering, which manifests itself in multiple domains of life. There is no joy, no true happiness for people who live in resentment.

Even more important is the fact that resentment severely obstructs or restricts our possibilities for action. To the extent that we are absorbed in a conversation that refuses to accept the loss we have suffered and feeds our judgment of injustice and our accusation of guilt, the past reigns over the present and narrows the space of the future. When we think about the future, it appears imbued with the judgments that characterize resentment. We normally live under the judgment that we will continue to be treated unfairly from now on. Our future possibilities become possibilities for incubating more resentment.

Those who are the object of others' resentment, on the other hand, find themselves surrounded by "quicksand" and a hostile environment even when they do not perceive it. Their attempts to coordinate actions are constantly thwarted. They face the unspoken promise that "at some point, somewhere, in some way" they will pay for what they are silently accused of. It is only a matter of finding the right opportunity. In any social space where resentment prevails, productivity and the quality of coexistence are affected.

We maintain that every state of mind can be reconstructed through its corresponding underlying linguistic structure. Therefore, what is the linguistic structure in which the state of mind of resentment can be reconstructed? Based on the analysis developed above, we propose the following linguistic structure for the state of mind of resentment:

— I affirm that X happened (or did not happen)

— I judge that this implies the breach of a promise or a legitimate expectation

— I judge that X caused me harm and restricted my current possibilities

— I judge that this is unfair

— I declare that "A" is responsible for this ("A" can be a person, a group of people, an institution, etc.)

— I judge that there is nothing I can do now to make "A" repair the damage caused to me

— I declare that this is not right (it should not be, or it should be different)

— I declare that at some point, somewhere, in some way, "A" will pay for this

Some variations on this structure are also acceptable. What is interesting about it, however, is that we can clearly see the tension between the judgment "I can't do anything now" and the statement "things should be different." For resentment to exist, these two elements must be present. It implies a tension between a judgment of facticity and a simultaneous judgment of possibility. As we will see below, this linguistic structure will guide us in designing paths that allow us to move away from resentment.

Nietzsche has been the great philosopher on the subject of resentment. According to him, resentment poisons life and corrodes coexistence with others. But above all, it is an emotion that chains human beings, robbing them of their freedom. Resentment, Nietzsche tells us, makes us slaves. Slavery is not only, or even primarily, a political or legal issue. It is, above all, a condition of the human soul. Someone who is legally a slave can at the same time be a free soul, as Epictetus shows us.

But those who are legally free and live in resentment lose their freedom and transform the object of their resentment into the master of their existence. Resentment makes us live in function of the person (or persons) we resent. What we judge to be an injustice becomes a guide and an obsession in our lives. The consistency of what we do is defined by our hatred of the other and our thirst for revenge. The resentful person moves, Nietzsche tells us, like a tarantula, waiting for the right moment to unleash their venom.

As we previously stated when examining the declaration of forgiveness, resentment hides its own nature, even from those who suffer from it. By drawing a line between the evil of the other and the sense of victimhood with regard to oneself, the resentful person gives themselves an alibi by placing themselves on the side of good. This is precisely the trap. It is difficult to recognize how hatred toward another could make them our master. But this is what resentment does. Through hatred, the other, even without knowing it, gains power over our lives. Resentment, we have said, deepens our powerlessness and robs us of our freedom. It is in this sense that we insist on the liberating power of forgiveness.


 

The state of mind of acceptance and peace

By acceptance or peace, we characterize the state of mind opposite to resentment and which, therefore, accounts for a diametrically different emotionality resulting from the same situation. What defines the state of mind of acceptance is the expression of reconciliation that it exhibits with reality. We say we are at peace when we accept to live in harmony with the possibilities that were closed to us. We are at peace when we accept the losses that are beyond our control.

In this way, for example, we can relate to our past from a place of resentment or acceptance. When we do so from a place of acceptance, it does not imply, for example, refusing to acknowledge the mistakes we may have made. But we are able to live in peace because what happened in the past does not necessarily have to be repeated in the future. We view those mistakes as expressions of precariousness that we can eventually correct in the future. Acceptance does not ignore the mistakes or harmful actions of others, nor does it ignore the desire that they not be repeated. What acceptance fundamentally "accepts" is the fact that we cannot change what has already happened and, as such, declares it "closed." As Epictetus tells us, acceptance allows us to focus on the task of changing what can be changed, without being consumed by useless regret for what we cannot do anything about. Acceptance puts us on the path to transforming the future.


 

From resentment to acceptance

It is important to examine how it is possible to move from a state of resentment to one of acceptance. The key to doing so is often found in topics we have discussed previously. A first step in this shift can be achieved by identifying the judgments that appear in the linguistic reconstruction of resentment and examining their foundations. For example, we could examine the foundations we find for making the judgments that lead us to make the accusation involved in resentment. In doing so, we may discover that, upon re-examining the situation, the person we accuse is not entirely responsible for what happened. How many times have we held a grudge for a long time only to realize that our accusation, viewed from a new perspective, had no basis whatsoever?

However, the above procedure is often unsuccessful. When examining the basis of the original situation, we often find that our examination confirms the grounds that lead us to hold someone responsible and accuse them for the consequences that resulted from their behavior. Does this mean that we have no way of overcoming our resentment? We do not believe so.

In these circumstances, the options available to us for moving from resentment to acceptance are related to our ability to make a statement that closes the past. We must examine whether or not we can end those private conversations that have been haunting us for so long. And there are different possible alternatives for closing those private conversations.

One alternative is to examine whether our decision not to speak and to keep our accusation silent is well-founded. If we decide to speak, a common way to do so is through recrimination or complaint. Recrimination is a variant of what we previously called "a conversation of personal judgments." When we recriminate someone, what we do is blame the other person for what happened and overwhelm them with judgments. This can serve as a release and help us free ourselves from our anger.

However, when we choose recrimination, what we often get in response from the other person is a rejection of our interpretation of events, an attempt to show us our own guilt in what happened, and another avalanche of personal judgments. A conversation based on mutual recrimination tends to heat up quickly and often fails to generate any remedial action. In the process, the relationship between the two people usually ends up deteriorating.

There is another way to deal with our resentment when we speak. Unlike recrimination, which was a variant of "the conversation of personal judgments," this alternative is a variant of "the conversation for the coordination of actions." We are referring to the complaint. The complaint seeks to take actions that are conducive to eliminating the cause of the resentment. Doing this has the power to dissolve it.

The complaint is a particular language game, made up of several linguistic acts. It combines at least statements, assertions, and requests. If successful, it usually ends in promises of actions that address the damage done. Let's examine the canonical structure of the complaint.

Statement: "I have to make a complaint."

Assertion: "You promised me you would do x by y time."

Assertion: "You did not keep your promise."

Statement: "As a result of your failure to comply, I have been harmed."

:... [Affirmations may be provided to support the above judgment].

Statement: "I hold you responsible for these damages."

Request: "As a way of assuming responsibility for the damages you have caused me, I ask you for "a," "b," and/or "c."

: ... [Response]

Statement: [If the response is positive] Thank you.

Let's examine each of these steps. The first statement sets the context for the complaint conversation. It alerts the listener to the nature of the conversation that is about to take place and usually helps to establish the right frame of mind for it.

The next two steps also provide contextual elements, but this time with the purpose of justifying the complaint, of giving it legitimacy. The listener may respond to our first statement by saying, "Excuse me, but I didn't make that promise to you. What I said was...," or "What I promised you was not what you say, but...," or "The completion date is not what you claim, but...". In all these cases, we may discover that there was a misunderstanding and perhaps conclude that we need to improve the way we make promises with this person in the future. But it is very likely that these responses will dissolve the need for the complaint and, with it, the source of our resentment.

The same can happen with the statement that constitutes the third step. Our listener may well say, "Wait a second. I gave the report you're referring to your secretary before the deadline we had agreed upon. I have fulfilled my promise to you." What we need to recognize here is that our assumption that the promise made was not kept may be false and that the listener may be able to show us that he or she did indeed fulfill the promise. Again, if that happens, the complaint loses its meaning and our resentment dissolves. In fact, we often resent because we make assumptions that later turn out to be untrue. By talking about what we resent, we open up the possibility of verifying whether our assumptions were true or false.

If our listener agrees with our previous statements, we can legitimately get to the heart of the complaint. We can, therefore, declare the harm we have suffered, declare the responsibility of the person who failed to fulfill their promise, and proceed to ask for some redress for what happened.

If what we consider to be adequate redress is accepted by the listener, the reason for the complaint dissolves and with it our resentment will also dissolve. When this happens, the complaint conversation ends with a closing statement. This is what the final step of "Thank you" represents.

If our listener does not accept what we are asking for, several things can happen. We can, for example, enter into a negotiation on the terms of the redress, in which the listener can make counteroffers until an agreement is reached. It may also happen that we come to understand and accept the listener's reasons for not complying and declare the matter closed anyway, without the need for reparation. And finally, if we do not obtain adequate satisfaction, we may be forced to reevaluate our relationship with that person based on the resulting loss of trust. But in all these cases, we have managed to move toward closing that private conversation that fed our resentment.

The important thing is to be able to compare the consequences of keeping our accusation silent, entering into a conversation of mutual recrimination, or making a claim. One of the results of the latter is that the parties involved open up the possibility of moving on from it, when an agreement is reached, regaining the dignity that was previously considered affected. What is important to note here is that the complaint is made with the aim of closing an open conversation that comes from the past.

Many people do not know how to make a complaint in order to effectively bring closure to the past. Some believe that by recriminating the other person, they are doing everything they need to do. And they go from one recrimination to another.

There are circumstances, however, where the damage done seems irreparable. Or, even if there is the possibility of obtaining some reparation, it does not compensate for the loss incurred as a result of the other person's action or inaction. When this happens, we have another alternative to end the suffering. It is the declaration of forgiveness.

Human beings make mistakes. Nor do we measure all the consequences of our actions. The reasons we give ourselves for acting in one way or another are subject to the precariousness of all our interpretations. Acceptance of this fact gives rise to what is understood in English as compassion. Compassion in the English language (unlike in Spanish, where it is associated with pity) is the full acceptance of the other, with their limitations, blindness, and incompetence. From the emotionality of compassion, the space for forgiveness expands.

There are those who are willing to forgive, but on one condition: that the person responsible for the action we resent declares their repentance. This implies that they make themselves worthy of our forgiveness by asking for it. If there is no repentance, it is argued, there is no reason to forgive. Forgiveness is paid for, at least, and particularly when there is no possibility of repairing the damage, with repentance.

This way of conditioning forgiveness, while often valid, cannot be established as an unquestionable principle. We must not forget that forgiveness not only frees the person responsible for the damage done from the guilt we attribute to them, but also frees the resentful person from their own resentment and the consequences it has on their existence. We insist: the main beneficiary of forgiveness is not the forgiven, but the one who forgives.

Forgiveness is not an act that frees the person who committed a certain action that we consider to have wronged us from responsibility. On the contrary, forgiveness is the act that frees us from resentment when we have grounds to blame someone for their behavior. Only then does forgiveness emerge as an alternative. With forgiveness, we declare that we will not allow our resentment and, therefore, our interpretation of the harm that person may have inflicted on us in the past, to interfere with our chances of coexisting and continuing to coordinate actions in the future. This is often an important consideration, particularly when we are forced to share the same social space and live together.

As we said earlier, when we forgive, we are not committing ourselves to forgetting. We cannot commit ourselves to forgetting. This is beyond our capabilities. When we forgive, we are only committing ourselves to closing a certain conversation about the past and not using it against a certain person in the future.

There is a third alternative. Sometimes we may judge that the harm done to us is so unacceptable that it makes no sense to maintain a relationship with those people. The magnitude of the harm done (the responsibility of the person who caused it, the systematic recurrence of the same type of actions, the deep mistrust we have developed toward a person, etc.) may lead us to conclude that neither complaint nor forgiveness will be enough to restore our relationship with them. We can forgive, but we are not willing to continue a relationship with that person. We simply do not see an acceptable future in maintaining the relationship. When that is the case, the way to end our conversation about resentment may be to declare the relationship over. Sometimes that is precisely what we have to do, as a way of taking charge of our dignity.

Every relationship involves at least two entities that relate to each other. So far, we have examined ways of dealing with resentment through actions taken by the person suffering from resentment. There are also actions that can be taken from the other end, that is, from the side of the person generating the resentment or from the system that causes it. We are referring to the design of environments that can anticipate the possibility of resentment and in which certain measures can be taken to avoid it. This is a fundamental aspect of organizational design, in which the issue of unequal distribution of power can permanently generate a mood of resentment.


 

Make clear promises

We maintain that there are two areas where we can intervene to prevent resentment in organizations. The first has to do with the way we make promises. As we stated earlier, resentment often arises because people feel that the promises made to them were not kept. The way we make promises is a basic area of design if we want to avoid resentment. Resentment often arises because both parties heard the promise in very different ways. Whenever we make promises that give rise to unfounded assumptions or expectations about the agreed conditions of satisfaction and the standards implicit in those conditions of satisfaction, we run the risk of creating resentment.

Resentment is also often caused by people's unequal expectations, or because the fulfillment of commitments is evaluated using different standards. When this happens, it means that different contexts are being used. To avoid this, it is therefore important to make the contexts in which we operate explicit and not assume that we all have the same expectations or standards. Before starting a relationship, it is often advisable to take the time to create a common context and state, for example, "If you accept the position we are offering you, you can expect that if x conditions are met, y will happen. However, we warn you not to expect w, as long as the rules are those we have agreed upon."

Poor communication is a constant source of resentment. If people took care from the very beginning to make clear promises, many causes of resentment would disappear. For this to be possible, it is important to verify that everyone involved hears the promises in the same way and to make very clear the commitments that each party makes to fulfill their promises. Ambiguity in making promises is always a potential source of resentment.

However, no matter how careful we are when making promises, we can never be absolutely sure how they were heard. At this point, we know that there is no such thing as perfect listening. No matter what we do to verify how each person understood what was said, we will always listen from our own perspective and historical background. We cannot avoid this. Therefore, a certain degree of ambiguity is part of the reality of human communication.

Commit to sharing some private conversations and allow for complaints

This brings us to the second area where we can intervene to reduce the risk of resentment and manage the potential misalignment of action that it implies. Another way to address resentment is by not allowing it to grow when it arises. How can we do this? We can do so by mutually agreeing (as a basic commitment in our relationship) to share any private conversation about each other that we judge may interfere with how we coordinate our joint actions. This involves keeping a space open for complaint.

This commitment allows us to identify resentment at its source and address it as soon as it arises. Of course, for this to be possible, it is essential that each party does not feel threatened when sharing a private conversation. The fear of sharing these conversations must be eliminated. This means that those who talk about their private conversations or complain will not suffer negative consequences.

We are not saying that people should make public any private conversations they may have. As autonomous beings, we have the privilege of privacy, and this privilege must be fully respected. The conversations we commit to sharing are those that, in our judgment, have the power to interfere with our coordination of actions within the organization.


 

The state of mind of resignation

As we have reiterated, what happened in the past cannot be changed. It is already determined, and the present can only recognize it as such. This is an ontological fact. The future, however, is characterized by offering us a space of indeterminacy, a space subject to our capacity for action. The future may be different from the present. And it may be different both because of the actions of others and because of our own actions.

We observe that someone is in a state of resignation when that person behaves, in a certain domain, as if something cannot change, while we believe the opposite. We recognize the state of resignation when we can produce an underlying conversation that would question the opinion that something cannot be changed, that is, when we judge that what someone considers immutable can change. What characterizes a person in a state of resignation is the fact that, unlike others, they do not see the future as a space for intervention that allows them, through their own actions, to transform the present.

However, the resigned person generally does not observe their state of mind of resignation as such. For that person, resignation appears as well-founded realism. Let us not forget that people not only have states of mind, they also have a tendency to justify them.

We often admit that we are resigned in some area of our lives. We can observe our own resignation. When this happens, a tension arises again between judgments of possibility and judgments of facticity. On the one hand, we recognize that things could be different. But on the other hand, we are possessed by the judgment that things will not change, no matter what we do. This often leads to admitting that theoretically things could change. At the same time, it is not clear to us how to execute the change. On a very concrete level, we do not know what to do and therefore do nothing. This is a very common and widespread manifestation of resignation.


 

The state of mind of ambition

The state of mind of resignation is contrasted with that of ambition. The latter is characterized by doing the opposite of what the former did. While resignation was defined by the closure of future possibilities, ambition stands out for identifying broad spaces for intervention that carry the seeds of change. Through its linguistic reconstruction, ambition corresponds to a judgment that speaks to the way a person faces the future. As such, ambition can be reconstructed as a different view of the future. A view in which the future is seen as a vast space of possibilities for action and where actions have a great generative capacity and, therefore, the capacity to construct new realities. An ambitious person understands that the present builds the future and, in doing so, transcends what exists today. As a disposition, it is identified with what Nietzsche calls the "will to power."

It should be noted, however, that the term ambition has very different connotations depending on the historical discourses that predominate in different communities. In the Anglo-Saxon world, it is a positive attribute. In contrast, in those communities where, for example, the predominant historical discourse is Catholicism, ambition has a strong pejorative connotation. Being "ambitious" is usually seen as despicable. What is important to note is that these are differences that refer to the predominant historical discourses.

From our perspective, we can linguistically reconstruct the state of mind of ambition by reference to the judgment we make of a person, in the sense that they see possibilities for action where others normally do not and commit themselves to carrying out such actions.


 

From resignation to ambition

When we identify an area in which we suspect that a state of mind of resignation may be hidden behind our "realism," certain actions can also be taken. We now understand that resignation can be reconstructed as an underlying linguistic structure in which we declare that "Nothing can be done here" or "Whatever I do, nothing will change," in circumstances where others may refute our judgment.

Again, one way to deal with this mood of resignation is to examine the basis for these judgments. We may discover, upon closer examination, that the obstacles we assumed were preventing us from acting effectively do not exist or could be overcome. It often happens that we do not act because we assume that if we made a certain request, it would be denied. Or we assume that if we entered into a conversation to explore a possibility of interest to us, it would come to nothing.

However, it is not uncommon to discover, when we engage in that conversation, that our initial assumption was unfounded. Sometimes we also discover that our assumptions are based on the fact that we are incompetent at making requests or conducting those conversations in such a way that the other person can see the implicit possibilities. Therefore, if we commit ourselves to learning how to have those conversations that we thought were impossible, circumstances may change.

Sometimes we may declare an entire domain of action to be immutable, not because the judgments we have made about the obstacles to action make them insurmountable, but simply because we do not visualize effective actions that can be taken to bring about change. In these circumstances, what is lacking is not an examination of the basis for our judgments, but rather a need to engage in conversations about possible actions. To do this, we may want to talk to other people, read about the subject, and follow those "repertoires" of actions that allow us to enter a state of mind conducive to fertile speculation.

When we find ourselves torn between conflicting judgments of possibility and facticity (as described above), we can also find space to take actions that move us away from resignation. When we admit that things could theoretically change, but we don't know what to do about it, we can always resort to "reflexive" actions. If we don't know what to do next, we could still take actions that would lead us to the actions we should take to get where we want to go.

What action can we take when we don't know what actions to take? We can learn. We can take action to expand our capacity for action. Learning is one of the most important ways to keep people from becoming resigned. Learning makes what may have seemed impossible seem achievable. Through learning, we transform our judgments of facticity into judgments of possibility.
