TOWARDS AN ONTOLOGY OF THE PERSON

Elsewhere we have said that human beings share a particular way of being: the human way of being. As human beings, we are different from other animals and plants. We are also very different from rivers, mountains, clouds, rocks, rain, starfish, and so on. Human beings share a common way of being.

However, we have also said that this human way of being allows for infinite forms of realization. We can be human in infinite ways. All of these ways, of course, share some basic constitutive or generic aspects that belong to all human beings (what we call the ontological dimension) and, at the same time, give rise to infinite variety. This particular way of being that we are as individuals (within the already particular way of being that we are as human beings) is what we call the person [33]. The person represents our particular way of being human.


 

What constitutes human beings

It seems legitimate to ask what makes humans the way they are. Throughout the history of ideas, many answers have been given to this question. Aristotle, for example, argued that human beings are, by nature, political animals. Carlyle, many years later, argued that human beings are animals that use tools. "Without tools," he said, "they are nothing; with tools, they are everything." Both Aristotle and Carlyle seem to be pointing to indisputable dimensions of human beings.

But, we wonder, could we not say with equal validity that human beings are, for example, poetic animals? And could we not also say that they are humorous beings? Or religious beings? Or beings who ask questions? Do we know of any other beings that possess these attributes? Is any of these approaches more valid than the others? The point is that if we look for the traits that characterize human beings, even those that characterize them exclusively, we will end up with a long, very long list.

Among that long list of human traits, we ask ourselves again, is there one that, once identified, allows us to derive all the others from it? Could we find something like the "primary constitutive condition" of human beings which, once identified, would allow us to conclude that being political, poetic, humorous, religious, asking questions, using tools, etc., are all "derived conditions" that result from it? If this were possible, it is clear that such an attribute, such a primary condition, would contain all the others.

Following this line of thought, in the search for this primary condition, the prevailing interpretation has been to point out that human beings are rational animals: animals, therefore, endowed with reason. This fundamental postulate has taken various forms. In one way or another, it is contained in those statements that say that what makes us who we are is that we possess a consciousness, a mind, a spirit, a soul, and so on.


 

All of these are usually variations on this basic approach that interprets us as rational beings.

Compared to the previous statements, this one has the great advantage of operating as a constitutive condition, from which all the others could be derived.

It would make sense to say, for example, that human beings are political, use tools, are poetic, ask questions, etc., because we are endowed with reason (or mind, or any other term of this kind that we might wish to use).

This interpretation, however, presents some significant problems from our perspective. First, it is based on postulating an entity, attribute, or property (reason, spirit, consciousness, mind, etc.) whose own constitutive conditions are difficult to establish. What do we mean by this? That although this interpretation may explain how other specifically human traits may have been generated (being political, using tools, being poetic, etc.), taken as the primary generative factor of the human way of being, it closes itself off to explaining how it itself was generated.

Secondly, this interpretation induces a rationalist understanding of human beings and therefore excludes fundamental aspects of human behavior that do not follow the guidelines of the rationalist model. We have already referred to the latter in previous chapters.

Returning to the first objection, we believe that the interpretation that we are rational beings contributes to other interpretations that separate human beings from the evolutionary process from which we have understood the development of different forms of life. We might ask ourselves, how did humans come to have a spirit? If we support this interpretation, we will normally find that there is a "missing link" in the evolutionary chain that leads to explaining the emergence of rational or spiritual beings.

That link, if it existed, would explain the conditions that allowed for the constitution of the human mind. At the same time, it would be able to identify the conditions that generated those conditions, based on the biological traits possessed by our evolutionary ancestors.

Therefore, by positing the mind as the "primary constitutive condition" of human beings, we have historically been pushed toward two subordinate explanations, two interpretations that are attempts to give consistency to this dominant interpretation. First, the interpretation of the mind as separate from the body and, therefore, from the process of biological evolution. At best, the body serves to assign the mind its "place of residence." Until now, it has been said that its "usual place of residence" is somewhere in the brain. Descartes placed it in the pituitary gland.

The second interpretation refers us to the argument of divine creation. It assumes that God has endowed us with this special entity or property. Since we have not been able to demonstrate how the mind arises from the process of evolution, and yet we realize that we have obtained it, God seems a reasonable source to point to. Throughout history, we have attributed to God many of the phenomena whose generation we are unable to explain.

These interpretations have, over time, produced some difficulties. One important area where problems have been accumulating is within the biological sciences. With the expansion of the evolutionary understanding of life and, especially, with the research that has been developed on the functioning and structure of the brain (take, for example, among others, the work of Norman Geschwind), the assumption that the mind is separate from the body has become increasingly indefensible. Today, biology recognizes the close relationship between biological and mental phenomena.

From biology itself, however, there has been a significant shift away from the notion that what defines us as human beings is reason. This shift comes from those biologists most directly involved with Darwinian evolutionary theory and systems theory. Precisely at the intersection of evolutionary theory and systems thinking, a different proposition of what constitutes us as human beings has emerged from biology itself.

In 1963, the prominent theoretical biologist Ernst Mayr, in his book Animal Species and Evolution, recognized that a fundamental evolutionary step taken by our ancestors was bipedalism, the upright position that allowed primates to walk on two legs.

"Bipedal locomotion," writes Mayr, "especially in its early stages, must have been a rather inefficient form of locomotion for a quadrupedal mammal. Its greatest selective advantage was presumably that it freed the forelimbs for new behavioral tasks. It allowed the use of the hands for efficient tool manipulation, for wielding weapons (sticks and rocks), and for carrying food. It is possible that the beginnings of bipedalism date back to the early hominid line..."

A couple of pages later, however, Mayr refers to what he considers the constitutive trait that characterizes human beings. He writes:

"The ability to speak is the most distinctive human characteristic, and it is quite likely that speech was the key invention that triggered the transition from hominid to man. It enabled community structure and allowed man to become a truly social organism. As such, man developed the need for mechanisms that promoted social homeostasis, communal rights, myths and beliefs, and, ultimately, primitive religions. This chain of developments included a number of positive feedback mechanisms; thus, each advance exerted, in turn, a selection pressure in favor of even greater brain development."

This involves a significant change in the understanding of human beings. From an evolutionary point of view, Ernst Mayr posits that language can be considered the key transformation that led to the emergence of human beings. The ability to speak is a biological capacity and can be treated in strictly biological terms. It refers to the structure of the human nervous system and the particular features of the vocal and auditory organs.

However, what is even more important in this interpretative proposal is the fact that language also allows us to explain the emergence of mental phenomena. The mind, reason, consciousness, spirit, soul, etc., can now be returned to the process of evolution. The separation between body and mind is no longer necessary, and we are not inevitably forced to seek transcendental explanations to account for our ability to access spiritual experiences. This postulate, which places language at the center of our understanding of human beings, has been developed in greater depth by other biologists. It is, for example, one of the cornerstones of Humberto Maturana's biology.

The ontology of language is developed from this proposal. One of its central postulates is that what constitutes human beings, what makes them the kind of beings they are, is language. Human beings, we postulate, are linguistic beings, beings who live in language. Language, we maintain, is the key to understanding human phenomena. And it is from this perspective that we will examine what a person is.


 

Our traditional understanding of the person

Our traditional understanding assumes that we all have certain fixed properties. We all "are" in one way or another, and how we "are" is how we act in life. We assume, therefore, that each of us is endowed at birth with a particular mind, spirit, soul, etc., and that this defines who we are. Everything we do in life "reveals" who we really are. The person is considered to possess an unchanging core. An essence that nothing can change.

Different experiences can, as with metals, mold, temper, polish, and wear down a person, but iron will always be iron, and gold will always be gold.

The person we are resides somewhere that is not always directly visible to others or even to ourselves. One of the challenges we often face in life is to "discover" who we are and what we "really" want in life. We talk about processes of "self-discovery."

The same is true of others. It often takes time to get to know who the other person really is. In romantic relationships, we often end up "discovering," sometimes after years of living together, that despite our original hopes, the truth is that we were really incompatible. By this we mean that the other person's way of being does not complement our own way of being. Once we discover this, there seems to be very little we can do except suffer together for the rest of our lives or separate.

Something similar often happens in other areas of social coexistence. In education, teachers characterize their students in a certain way and, in doing so, define in advance what they can expect from them. Students do the same. They characterize their teachers and what they can expect from them. The situation is similar at work. Bosses wonder if they have hired the right staff, and employees develop their own interpretations of what the boss is "like." Once these characterizations have been made, they regulate working relationships. We live in a world where we accept as normal explanations based on the notion that "this happened because this person is this way or that way" or "that's just how he (or she) is."

Even more important is the way we characterize ourselves. We usually assume that we are one way or another and, therefore, there are things we would never do, since "we know ourselves well," or "we know what we want," or "we know what we can and cannot do," and so on. We tell others that "this is how we are" as a way of announcing what can be expected of us. By characterizing ourselves in a certain way, we define what is possible for us in life and shape our future. We generally take our self-characterizations as descriptions of our way of being. With them, we often point to what we assume to be immutable in ourselves.

We call this way of understanding the person metaphysical psychology. It assumes that we possess a particular way of being that defines us, a particular way of being that has its permanent and unchanging essence deep within ourselves. We call it this because its roots come from Aristotle's ancient metaphysical understanding of being. Within this understanding, the being of something is what is permanent and unchanging in that something. Its attributes may change, but its being always remains the same.

Moving away from metaphysical psychology: critical reconstruction of characterizations

Our understanding of judgments gives us the first clue to dissolving this metaphysical interpretation of the person. Every characterization we make of ourselves (and others) is a judgment. When we say, for example, "George has a bad temper" or "Mary is shy" or "Carmen writes beautiful stories," we seem to be characterizing George, Mary, and Carmen. It is as if we were saying what they are like and, therefore, describing their being. We also assume that, since we know what they are like, we know what to expect from them in terms of their behavior. These characterizations seem to tell us how they will act.

However, once we realize that these are all judgments, we also realize that we are not describing anyone. What we are doing is ascribing certain properties to them. Judgments do not describe, since they are not statements. They ascribe.

What are the processes and conditions that lead us to make judgments? How do we come to make them? We all know that judgments are not arbitrary and that we are communicating something when we make them. Just because they are ascriptions does not mean we should conclude that they are meaningless. On the contrary, they are meaningful. And so, therefore, are personal characterizations. It is not, therefore, a matter of denying the value of characterizations; it is a matter of reinterpreting them.

In accordance with what we have previously stated about judgments, we maintain that we make judgments about individuals based on how they act. If they act in one way or another, we can say, "Jorge has a bad temper," "Maria is shy," and "Carmen writes beautiful stories." If we were asked why we say this, we would say that we have seen them act in a way that allows us to make those judgments.

It is important to pause here, as we are pointing to something interesting. Just as judgments are based on actions we have observed, we know that every action always takes place at a specific time and within a specific domain. The curious thing, however, is that when we make our characterizations, we assume that we are describing what people are like. We are, therefore, making a general conclusion, which purports to speak to an individual's permanent way of being, based on attributions made in particular instances. We also forget that this supposed description is the result of observing specific actions, and we end up assuming that we have come to know the unchanging essence of people (and not their actions). And we take this even further. Since we assume we know what individuals are like, we conclude that we can predict how they will act.

Let us return, then, to the process of characterization that we examined earlier. Upon closer inspection, we see that a fundamental reversal has taken place. What were initially judgments about people's actions that allowed us to assume what they are like is then considered an understanding of people's nature, which allows us to determine how they will act.

Is there anything wrong with this? From our perspective, we have to answer yes and no simultaneously. Let us consider, first, why we say that there may be nothing wrong with proceeding in this way. If what we are doing is observing people's actions in the past or present to anticipate how they might act in the future, there is nothing wrong with that. We are simply doing what a judgment always does. And as we shall see, there are grounds for expecting that the way people have acted in the past may well be repeated in the future. But when we do this, we are dealing with actions—past, present, and future actions—and nothing more. The fact that someone has acted in one way or another in the past does not entail the description of some permanent or fixed property of the person acting.

Therefore, we make an invalid leap when we take those judgments as permanent characteristics of the person being evaluated (including ourselves). There is nothing that legitimately authorizes us to conclude that we have arrived at something permanent or fixed. However, this is precisely how we normally proceed. We operate within the traditional understanding of the person by assuming, first, that human beings have an unchanging way of being. Personal characterizations are generally considered to be just that: accurate descriptions of our permanent way of being.

We are what we do

The traditional understanding of the person assumes that we act according to how we are. Actions, therefore, are taken as manifestations of our being, and our being is the determining factor in understanding how we act. Within this interpretation, it is assumed that personal characterizations therefore tell us what people are like. Knowing what they are like, we can know how they will act. If they happen to act in an unexpected way, it seems to us that we did not know them well.

Our understanding of the person is different. We postulate that, without denying that we act according to who we are, we are also who we are according to how we act. Personal characterizations are constructs that we produce from the actions we perform, in order to anticipate how we might act in the future. Actions are not only a manifestation of our being, but also the way in which it is constituted in a process of permanent transformation. We generate the way we are by acting in one way or another.

We do not deny the possibility of talking about our particular way of being, but we postulate that our actions are the generative principle of who we are. Through our actions, we create ourselves. We, within the social environment in which we live, are active participants in the creation of our way of being.


 

The person as an explanatory principle

From this perspective, and as a first approximation (since we will expand on this understanding later), we can say that the person is an explanation based on the actions undertaken by the individual. We postulate that the person is an explanatory principle, a principle that gives coherence to the actions we perform.

If we look at what we do when we develop our understanding of someone's personhood, we realize that when we observe how someone acts, we make some judgments based on those actions and weave them into a story or narrative that produces a principle of coherence about the person being observed. It is nothing more than this. Seen from the point of view of what we do when we produce the distinction of personhood, we realize that personhood is a story about who we are based on the actions we perform.

Let's take some examples. If we were asked to say who we are, we would probably come up with a story based on some judgments that we would consider basic to account for who we are (judgments generated as a result of many actions performed in the past). We could include some past actions to which we attach special significance and, possibly, some of the actions we are performing in the present. If the actions we have performed in the past or are performing in the present make sense in terms of a project we want to achieve in the future, we could also talk about it. Talking about ourselves, therefore, means bringing up a narrative that integrates everything capable of giving meaning and coherence to our actions.

In relation to what we have just said, the biologist Humberto Maturana has formulated a fundamental postulate. He has said that "the mind is not in the brain, the mind is in [people's] behavior." This is a postulate that completely distances us from the interpretation we were originally discussing, which held that the human mind has its "place of residence" in the brain. If we accept that the mind is an explanatory principle that we use to make sense of people's actions, we will realize how absurd it is to postulate that it resides in the brain. Certainly, to produce a story we need language, and language is based on the biological capabilities of the human brain. But recognizing this is very different from suggesting that the mind is rooted in the brain.


 

Who has the correct story?

The story we tell ourselves about ourselves is our private persona, our private identity. The stories other people tell about us make up our public persona and belong to the domain of our public identity. These two kinds of stories are based on our actions, and therefore we are responsible for both. People, however, observe our actions from different positions, with very different concerns and interests and with very different distinctions. This makes them different observers of who we are. Different observers will produce different stories.

Who has the correct story? Who are we really? If we accept that the person is a story or narrative, we must also accept that there is no "existing entity" that the story is talking about. The metaphysical interpretation of the person is misleading precisely because it posits an existing entity that transcends the person as a story. This is why it needs the Mind, Spirit, or Soul. For us, both the mind and the spirit and soul belong to the realm of our personal experiences, but they are not reified into any metaphysical entity. There can be no true story about the person, since the person is the story itself. The only references in the story are the actions that are performed.

Let us remember that the distinctions between true and false belong to the realm of statements. Our story of the person may contain statements, and these may be true or false. But the story of the person cannot be made up of statements alone. It is always organized around certain master judgments.

The different stories that make up a person may be more or less well-founded, depending on how they relate to the universe of actions to which they must give meaning. These different stories can also be more or less powerful, depending on the possibilities they open up for the future and the actions that may derive from them. But there is no real, correct, or true story of the person. And no one, not even oneself, has a privileged position from which to construct the real story of the person we are.


 

The person as an evolutionary trait

We cannot completely break with the traditional understanding of the person unless we produce some satisfactory explanation of how it is generated. To say that the person is an explanatory principle (a story we invent to make sense of our actions) is not enough. That is not enough to dispel the mystery of the person contained in our traditional understanding. What we have done so far is simply to move the mystery of the person one step higher, but it is still there.

Therefore, someone might well ask us, "If, as persons, we are the story we tell ourselves, there must necessarily be someone who tells the story. Who is the person telling the story that gives meaning to one's own action? Isn't a person needed to produce the story of the person?" Or, "If the story refers to the actions performed, who is acting?" Or, going even further, "Why would we need that story?" "Why is it necessary to give meaning to the actions we perform?"

Here we have different questions that we cannot avoid addressing. And they are of very different types. Let's start with the last one, why the story of the person is necessary. The best answer we can give to this question is, in fact, to question it, to debate it. If we take an evolutionary point of view, we will realize that this is a question that can only lead us to confusing answers. Why is there a need for human beings to exist? Once we accept their existence, why is it necessary for them to have five toes on each foot and not seven?

These types of questions assume that evolution occurs in response to the imperative of necessity. It is the old idea that function creates the organ. For centuries we have assumed that they were created to serve a purpose. Every time we observe something new, we ask, "Who made it?" and "For what purpose?"

In our opinion, no one has addressed these questions better than Nietzsche. Regarding the first question, "Who made it?", as mentioned above, his position is that it is wrong to assume that there must be a subject to account for the event. "The actor is a fiction," Nietzsche tells us, "the action is everything." Nietzsche points out that once we abandon the presumption of an effective subject, we also abandon the object on which the effects are produced. Regarding the second question, "for what purpose?", his position is equally categorical:

"As soon as we imagine someone who is responsible for us being this way or that way, etc. (God, nature) and therefore attribute to them the intention that we should exist and be happy or torn apart, what we are doing is corrupting for ourselves the innocence of becoming." (The Will to Power, III, No. 552).


 

Or:

"No one is responsible for existing or for being constituted as he is, or for living under the circumstances and surroundings in which he lives. (...) He is not the result of a special design, a will, a purpose; he is not the result of an effort to achieve an 'ideal man' or an 'ideal of happiness' or an 'ideal of morality'. It is absurd to want to transfer his nature to one purpose or another. We invent the concept of "purpose"; in reality, purpose is absent." (The Twilight of the Idols).

If we examine the assumption of purpose from an evolutionary point of view, we arrive at a conclusion similar to that offered by Nietzsche. Evolution is a purposeless process. Living organisms and their characteristics evolve by accident, and once they have emerged, they are selected according to their adaptive advantages. Characteristics that provide an adaptive advantage (which, simply put, means that they increase the organism's reproductive capacity) remain, while others may disappear from the evolutionary process. Characteristics do not emerge because they are necessary; they emerge by accident. They remain within the evolutionary process either because they provided an adaptive advantage or simply because they did not provide any adaptive disadvantage. We may find this evolutionary logic disappointing, we may not like it, but unfortunately that will not change it, just as our judgment of the force of gravity does not affect it or make it any less effective.

We often think that when we ask a question, such as those we asked earlier, the next step is simply to answer it. However, we do not always stop to examine the validity of the question. As we have reiterated, we have created great and insoluble problems for ourselves by asking "Why?" every time something happens, thereby implying the existence of a purpose or intention behind a phenomenon. In this way, we asked ourselves "Why is it raining?" in the same way that one usually asks "Why are you looking at me like that?" This question, thus posed, assumes the existence of a purpose that is not directly revealed in the event. But the assumption of the existence of such a hidden purpose is taken for granted by the question and is therefore not always questioned. However, it is very different to ask "Why?" in search of a purpose than to ask the same question in search of the mechanisms that generated the phenomenon in question. The power of science consisted precisely in moving from one type of question to the other.

Only when we deal with deliberate action does the question of purpose or intention make sense. We can then ask, "Why did you do that?" What needs to be emphasized, therefore, is that while the person is the foundation of deliberate or purposeful action, the very existence of the person is not itself the result of any purposeful action. In other words, the existence of the person serves no purpose, but it allows human purpose to exist.

This is, for us, a fundamental distinction. What Darwin achieved with his theory of evolution was precisely a scientific interpretation of the emergence of different forms of life, an explanation that does not require the presupposition of purpose or intention. The same thing had happened before in the field of physics and astronomy with Newton. In Aristotelian physics, movement could not be totally separated from intention. Things moved to reach the place they were supposed to occupy, according to their nature. This changed completely with the physics of Galileo and Newton. Just as physics had done before, the theory of evolution sought generative mechanisms, not intentions. This same distinction is also decisive in Humberto Maturana's biology of knowledge.

Returning to our original question about the need for a history of the person, we say that the fact that human beings actually constitute a person makes sense within an evolutionary and ontogenetic drift. It occurred within their history of evolution as living beings and must have had adaptive advantages or, at least, must not have had adaptive disadvantages. What these might be is a question we leave open, as exploring it would take us away from the topic at hand.


 

How is the person constituted?

It is one thing, therefore, to ask about the need to have a person and quite another to ask about the mechanisms that allow for its emergence. We can avoid the first question, but we cannot avoid the second. If we agree to accept that there is a phenomenon we call a person (and some may even want to refute that), we must also accept that there are conditions that produce it, there are mechanisms that generate it. When referring to the mechanisms that generate the person, we will answer the question, "What is it that produces the story or narrative that constitutes the person?" We will say that what allows and is behind this phenomenon is human language.

The person is not only a linguistic phenomenon in the sense of a story we tell about ourselves and others. The person is a product of the recursive capacity of language. Thanks to the extraordinary plasticity of the human nervous system, human beings can not only coordinate actions with each other, but also coordinate actions to coordinate other actions. And this, which is already recursive in itself, can be carried out in further recursive turns, in an open progression. This is what characterizes human language.

What we call mind, reason, spirit, consciousness, and so on, are all phenomena based on the recursive capacity of language. The higher we move up the chain of recursion, the more capable we are of observing our life as a whole, the closer we get to the mystery of life, the more spiritual our experiences become. The more we talk "about" our experiences, or "about" the experience of talking about our experiences, the more reflective we become.

Both spirit and reflection are based on the recursive capacity of language. Sometimes the words spirit and reflection have been used to refer to the same phenomenon (as in Hegel's philosophy, in which spirit, mind, and reason are often interchangeable terms). Sometimes they are considered different. The spiritual is seen as an experience in which we use the recursive capacity of language to move away from speaking itself (as in Zen meditation), while reflection is understood as recursive speaking.

But there is more to it than that. All those distinctions (mind, reason, etc.)—and specifically when we talk about the person—are not only implying recursive activity, but are also assuming that there is some unity that sustains that recursive activity.

They are not just names we give to a collection of mental actions. They also imply that this plurality of actions constitutes a unity and, therefore, refer to a singularity.

How is it that we, as human beings, do this? We can offer many possible interpretations, and at some point we may be able to produce a scientific explanation. For now, we can only speculate based on the fact that we observe that our actions arise from a continuous biological unit (our body). If we accept this interpretation, it would mean that it is the body that provides unity to all the actions we perform. The body would be the fundamental substrate for the unity of the mind.

However, this interpretation is missing something important. What is missing is the recognition that the person experiences themselves as a unity because they live as a unity of experience. This leads us to the phenomenon of subjectivity. The very recursiveness of language, this capacity that turns language back on itself, allows us not only to live the present in language, but also to remember the past. Because we are linguistic beings, human beings are animals with memory. Our human memory is a linguistic memory. Whatever ways other animals have of connecting with their past, they have no way of relating to their past experiences through language. Memory, as we know it, is a human phenomenon.

Again, based on the recursiveness of language, we can not only reflect on the actions we have performed, but also on the possible actions we may eventually perform. In this way, language not only connects us to the past, but also projects us into the future. Humans are the only animals we know of that can anticipate and design their future. We can do this because we are linguistic beings.

Language, therefore, enables us to live in the present in a way that connects us to past experiences and projects us toward possible future experiences. The recursiveness of language allows us to live as a unit of experience with continuity over time. This same recursiveness allows us to connect one experience to another, as a flow of interdependent actions and events. If we add to this the recognition of our biological continuity, we have a solid foundation from which the distinction of personhood can emerge.


 

The pitfalls of language

Language, being the generative principle of the person, is also responsible for our misunderstandings about the person. The generation of the person occurs in language and takes the form of the "I" distinction. The "I" is the unit of my actions.

However, once we see ourselves as a person, as "I," we can say, for example, "I eat," "I drive my car," "I work at The Newfield Group," "I write documents," "I design courses," and so on. By speaking in this way, we are recognizing our person as the unit of our actions. But at the same time, we are doing something else. By speaking in this way, we are also separating the "I" from the actions we perform, and because of the grammatical structure of our speech, every time we say, for example, "I drive my car," we are giving priority to the "I" over the action of driving the car. In this way, we create the illusion of an already constituted "I" who, among many other possible actions, "drives a car."

This being the case, it is not surprising that we easily end up accepting that we act according to who we are, according to the "I" that we are, and that we hide the inverse relationship: the fact that we are according to how we act. The grammatical structure described above invites us to ask questions about the "I" as an entity constituted independently of the actions it performs.

Language often leads us into different traps, and this is one of them. The traditional understanding of the person is not an arbitrary understanding. It is an understanding that makes sense and is based on the way we speak. This, however, is not enough to make it immune to criticism.


 

The person as an active principle of coherence

The person should not be considered only an explanatory principle that gives coherence to what we do. There is another side to this coin. The person is, at the same time, an active principle of coherence that is projected in the actions we perform.

What we are saying is that the person is not just another story we tell about ourselves, in the sense that it is a story that has no effect on the events of our lives. On the contrary, that story seems to take on a life of its own and develops the power to specify the actions we perform. Not only do we construct a story about ourselves that gives coherence to the actions we perform, but the actions we perform are also consistent with the story we have about ourselves. The story of the person we are is not a trivial story. It has generative powers.

The traditional understanding of the person realizes this. This is what gives it the basis for postulating an entity that guides the actions of individuals. After all, this is very similar to what we are saying. If we realize that people do not act randomly, first one way and then the opposite way; if we realize that people's behavior follows some basic coherences; if we recognize that coherence is not only in the story we tell about ourselves but also in the actions we take, then it becomes difficult not to conclude that there must be something that selects our actions and provides the basic coherence we seem to observe.

But instead of observing this phenomenon as consisting of an active circularity between actions and narratives (or stories), the traditional understanding of the person invents an immanent being: a being within human beings to which it gives various names. We have referred to this when discussing the fallacy of the homunculus.

What is the difference between interpreting the person according to our traditional understanding and, instead, as a narrative that provides us with a basic principle of coherence? The main difference lies in the domain of action. Just as with the story we sustain about our person, the possible actions that arise from both interpretations are substantially different. Our space for intervention at the level of the person is very different if we sustain one interpretation or the other.

The social construction of the person

Everything we have said so far could give the impression that we have been talking about a process that takes place within the individual. It might seem that the person is the result of a process that occurs in language within the limits of particular individuals. Again, our position is exactly the opposite.

As we have previously argued, language is not an individual phenomenon, it is a social phenomenon. A single individual, by themselves, cannot produce language. Language arises in the process of social interaction, in the collective play of individuals coordinating actions together. Language was not created by a person. It is not the invention of a single individual. On the contrary, the person is a creation of language. We become who we are as a result of a language that precedes us. At birth, we are thrown into an already constituted language. And we become whoever we become within the language in which we grow up.

This does not mean that we, as individuals, play no role in the development of language. We certainly do, or at least we can. But long before we can take an active part in its development, we need to be constituted as persons, as a result of already existing in language.

Wittgenstein wrote in his Philosophical Investigations that "to imagine a language is to imagine a form of life." Few ways allow us to grasp this proposition better than considering the way we are constituted as persons. Communities with different languages constitute different persons. The different ways in which individuals coordinate actions in language make those individuals what they are as persons. Wittgenstein called these different ways of coordinating actions in language language games.

Depending on the games we play in language, words take on different meanings and players develop different identities. They constitute themselves as the people they are by playing these linguistic games. But they do not only constitute themselves. Their language also constitutes the world in which they live. Every language brings a world with it.

I would like to relate Wittgenstein's postulate to a personal experience. The first version of this chapter was written while I was spending a few days with Yaven, my wife, in Asilomar, California. She had brought with her the autobiographical book by Mexican-American author Richard Rodríguez, Hunger of Memory. During one of my breaks from the computer, since Yaven was reading something else, I picked up Rodríguez's book and started reading it. To my surprise, I realized that, in a very different way, Rodríguez, in recounting his personal experience, was addressing the same issue that I was grappling with and trying to develop in my text.

Rodríguez warns the reader early on that this is "a book about language." In it, he recounts his experience of growing up as a child in a Spanish-speaking home and the profound personal transformation that entering the world of his English-speaking school meant for him. Rodríguez leads us, step by step, through this process and shows us how the world of the English school shapes a new identity in him.

He recounts how this same process creates and widens the gaps between the person he was becoming and the Spanish-speaking world of his Mexican parents, the world of his childhood. Rodríguez's autobiography has the great merit of showing us, as only literature can, the validity of Wittgenstein's statement.

When we say that a person involves a principle of coherence, we must realize that what is coherent within one community—within a community that lives within a certain language—may be very different in another community that lives within another language. When we say that something is coherent, we are making a judgment and we use different social standards to do so, according to the community to which we belong. Being coherent is a linguistic game that is played differently within different communities.

When we talk about the person, we normally think of them according to the parameters of contemporary Western societies. Therefore, our conception of the person carries a strong individualistic charge. Furthermore, we often think that this type of individualistic person has existed throughout history, and we even think that history is the result of the actions of individualistic people. What we don't usually see is the fact that this individualistic person is itself a historical result, a very recent product of Western history.

For most of history, the relationship between the person and the community was very close, and individuals saw themselves as constituted by the communities to which they belonged. This close link between the community and the person was visible to all members of the community and did not remain hidden as it often is today. The separation between the individual and the community, as we know it, is an extremely recent historical phenomenon.

Take the example of an artist in the late Middle Ages. Today, we assume that artists are, in general, people who are strongly rooted in their individuality. But this was not the case back then. At that time, an artist belonged to a guild, and the guild established strict rules of conduct regarding the subjects that could be painted, how they should be presented, what materials and colors should be used, how large the different figures could be, and so on. There was little room for individual freedom of expression. The range of actions was strongly limited and subject to strict social norms. Their individual identities were strictly determined by the community.

This is still the case in many Eastern societies. In many Eastern countries, it is still the community that decides the basic rules of marriage, and it is the family that takes responsibility for making it work. Individuals have little or nothing to say in these matters. People operate in a highly constrained social environment. When we Westerners look at these behaviors, we tend to consider them aberrant, without realizing that we are observing them from very different historical conditions, which are, moreover, historically very recent.

The relationship between language and community

It is interesting to explore the relationship between community and language. We maintain that in order to belong to a community, we must share a language. Language, as the coordination of actions, as a set of linguistic games, is what constitutes a community. When we speak of language, therefore, we are not referring only to English, Spanish, French, Chinese, or Indian, although we can easily associate different worlds with each of these languages.

Of course, each of our language games is played within one of these broad linguistic traditions, and we must be within a tradition to be able to play any possible language game. But speaking English does not guarantee that the language games we play are the same. For example, the British play a language game called monarchy, which Americans do not play. For Spanish speakers in Latin America, the word Presidente refers to different language games. It refers to one game in Venezuela and another in Argentina.

But there are also communities within different countries. If we want to belong to the community of chemists, we must speak the language of chemistry; if we want to be part of the community of mathematicians, we must speak the language of mathematics; and if we want to belong to the community of real estate brokers, we must speak the language of real estate brokerage. Some communities protect the privilege of their members by raising additional barriers to playing their games. They will require, for example, some form of accreditation before accepting someone as a member. These barriers can work as long as those communities have the power to enforce them. However, we have all seen people who make their way, despite the barriers, simply by mastering the language of the relevant community and showing competence in the games played within it.

Based on his personal experience, Richard Rodríguez has become a strong opponent of forcing bilingual education in the American school system. He argues that bilingual education does not facilitate the full integration of immigrants into the public games of American society. Rodríguez recognizes that language is the critical factor in achieving full membership in the community. Bilingualism, Rodríguez argues, delays the process of full social integration of immigrants and maintains pockets of second-class citizens.

We have said that language, understood as the consensus of a set of distinctions for coordinating joint actions on a stable basis, is what constitutes a community. A community is organized as a system for coordinating actions among its members, based on a shared language. When there are no integrative games, when individuals cease to coordinate actions among themselves, the community, by definition, disintegrates.

The five basic domains of the person

Based on what we have said so far, we can identify the person as a set of different domains of observation. We will call them the experiential, discursive, performative, moral, and emotional domains.


 

1. The person as an experiential domain

We have said that a person, first and foremost, lives as such, as a person. In this sense, it is a domain of experience. Our experiences are the basic components of our lives. Life is the sequence of our experiences. Whatever we do, we do it "from" our experiences. Certainly, we can reflect on our experiences and generate interpretations about why certain things happened to us and why we experienced them the way we did. But regardless of what we say about them, they stand on their own as something given, and we cannot deny them. It is in this sense that we conceive of them as the basic components of our lives. Just as we cannot deny our own experiences, any intervention at the personal level cannot deny the experiences in other people's lives either. We simply live our experiences the way we live them, and any intervention must start from there. Reflection can teach us to experience future events differently. It can also teach us to reinterpret what has already happened to us. But what we have been through and how we have been through it is unquestionable. And there is nothing we can do to change this. Regardless of what reflection may add to the experience of life, experiences will remain as they are.

This is fundamental to the practice of anthological coaching. It is based on the full acceptance of people's experiences. We cannot say, for example, "I do not accept that this is a setback for you" or "I do not accept that this experience you have just told me about has caused you suffering," and so on. If we have no reason to doubt the sincerity of what the person undergoing coaching tells us about their experience, we must accept that they have complete privilege over their own experiences. This is regardless of whether we judge that we would have experienced the same events very differently. Anthological coaching can only be done on the basis of accepting the legitimacy of others' personal experiences.


 

2. The person as a discursive domain

We have also said that the person is an explanatory principle that gives coherence to the actions we perform. In this sense, we have spoken of the person as a narrative or story we tell about who we are. Such a story is always based on some fundamental judgments that we have called master judgments and that constitute us as the person we are. These judgments usually deal with ourselves, others, the world, and the future. These are, in general, the four cardinal points that define the structure of our master judgments.

To access what we call someone's soul, that is, their particular way of being, the basic structure of their person, we must look for the master judgments that govern their actions (direct or reflective). Once, through an interpretive process, we can claim to have apprehended them, we realize that what individuals do or do not do (including what they say or do not say) results from those master judgments. Conversely, what individuals do or do not do (including what they say or do not say) are ways of revealing those master judgments and, in that sense, using them as windows into the human soul. Anthological coaching always makes use of the above, using the way people act to capture their master judgments and, through them, the basic structure of their particular way of being.

It is important to note that we are not saying that people have formulated these core judgments. They may never have done so. They may not even be aware that they have them. However, the way they act (always including the way we speak, since speaking is acting) reveals that they live according to them. These core judgments constitute them as the type of human beings they are.

At this level, the person's story (or the person as story)—and the distinctions and judgments upon which the story is constructed—generates a world of meaning. It should be noted that our world is always an interpretation. It is in this sense that we speak of a world of meaning. A person always reveals a world of interpretations. By considering our interpretations as "ours," we come to observe what kind of person we are. And by changing our interpretations, we modify the person we are.

The story that we are is always a story within a story or, to be more precise, within a set of different stories. We do not invent the stories that we are out of thin air, but from the stories held by the community to which we belong. We have called these stories, which have been passed down from one generation to another, historical discourses.

Many of them come from fundamental books, as is the case with most religions. Others are passed down orally from generation to generation. Some are sub-stories within larger ones, as is the case, for example, with the story of Adam and Eve in the Bible. Others are relatively independent of each other. Still others intersect with each other. Our stories are not fixed; we return to them again and again. We often reinterpret them.

Each community generates its own stories about itself, and individuals develop their own personal stories within them. By knowing the stories from which our personal stories come—the historical discourses—we gain a greater understanding of the people who constitute them.

We have said that our stories are not innocent. They generate different worlds of meaning and also specify the way we act. This is true both for our historical discourses and for our particular personal stories about ourselves. Stories do not exist only as stories "we talk about." They specify different types of lives. When we know that someone is an Indian from Chiapas in Mexico, a child of Lebanese immigrants in Argentina, or a teenager with wealthy parents in a North American metropolis, we already know something about them, even before meeting them. When we know that someone is French, Spanish, or Japanese, we already know something important about them. We know, in broad strokes, the stories that give meaning to the stories they have about themselves and the world: the stories that will guide their actions.

It is important to emphasize this point. Stories go beyond the mere act of telling them. They constitute active principles of coherence from which we act and interpret ourselves. They project themselves onto what we see as possible and what we find acceptable. They condition our future.

3. The person as a performative domain

The way we act cannot always be inferred from a story or group of stories that generate it. Not every action is based on a story. As we pointed out earlier, we do many things simply because that is the way we have seen people do them. We are generally not even aware that the way we do things, the way we face life, is only one possible way of doing them. We take them as the "obvious and natural" way of doing them. We take them for granted.

When we talk about ourselves, we rarely think to talk about the way we do certain things. This usually escapes the story we tell about ourselves. Therefore, in terms of our private identity, this may not play an important role. It does, however, play a role in terms of our public identity and the way people talk about who we are.

Seen from the perspective of someone who has a different way of doing things, a different way of dealing with life, our way of doing things does not go unnoticed. It shapes the way we will be seen and judged publicly. When we realize that we take our way of doing things for granted, that our way of approaching life is only one of many possible ways, we can then incorporate those transparent actions of ours into a story we have about ourselves.

This is one of the experiences we face when we go to a foreign country. We realize that our natural way of doing things is just the way our community does them. In our community, therefore, that way of approaching things might have gone unnoticed, but it does not go unnoticed in a different community. We also realize that, for people who belong to another community, our actions trigger judgments that we could hardly anticipate. My wife always insists that she really discovered she was American once she married me.

To communities that are closed to our world, we might be seen as "barbarians"—as people who do not behave the way they should. To communities open to a wider world, we might simply appear as foreigners: as people who behave differently, in a way that is not our own and that is strange to us. Conversely, for those who go to foreign countries with a strong sense that their way of doing things is "the proper way," as many British people did when traveling throughout the Empire, the inhabitants of the host community will be seen as strange or exotic human beings. Another way of referring to those who are different from us is to call them "natives." In a global and multifaceted world, we pay a high price for not recognizing that our ways of doing things are only one of many possible ways. And not always, as we have come to learn, the most effective ones.

Just as in the previous section we talked about historical discourses, in the performative domain we must refer to "social practices" or what sociologists often call "social institutions." These account for the particular way of doing things that exists in a given community.


 

4. The person as a moral domain

Both narratives (stories and discourses) and social actions and practices give rise to a new domain of the person. We call this the moral domain. From this domain, the person is conceived as a set of statements about the actions that can, cannot, and should be performed in certain circumstances. These statements can be traced back to narratives and practices, but they can still have some degree of autonomy. Individuals who come from the same historical discourses and share the same social practices may have different moral boundaries. Therefore, if we only ask ourselves about each individual's personal history, we may fail to observe these boundaries.

As we pointed out, moral boundaries can always be reconstructed as statements about what one and others can, cannot, and must do under certain circumstances. They are therefore defined by what is permitted, prohibited, and obligatory.

Their importance lies in the fact that they specify different ways of being and define the range of possibilities for action available to individuals (even when such individuals observe other possibilities). What is possible in the realm of observation is not necessarily what is possible in the realm of action. Individuals do not act in a certain way simply because they do not see other possibilities, but because they do not see them as possibilities for themselves. Something that we might consider possible may not be acceptable to us.

In a coaching interaction, it is important to understand the person's moral limits. Very often, we realize that individuals take their moral limits for granted, without giving them much thought. Very often, particularly under the current historical circumstances of a world that has entered postmodernism, individuals come into conflict with their own moral limits, for example, with the way they define good and evil. The moral criteria they uphold are at odds with the ethical imperative to give meaning to existence. We have the experience of living in two different worlds: one defined by our moral boundaries and another that generates possibilities for action for which we are not morally equipped and which, at the same time, we are unable to dismiss.

We often find ourselves, for example, faced with challenges that seem to require actions that we are not morally equipped to take. Under these conditions, the choice seems to be either to respond to the challenge or to dismiss it. The problem lies, and in saying this we are merely acknowledging the fact, in that neither of these options is capable of providing us with peace and sparing us suffering. This is particularly acute when we have turned the moral distinction between good and evil into a distinction between the sacred and the diabolical, when we have turned the moral into the religious.

Human beings are moral beings. The domain of morality is constitutive of human existence and, in that sense, is anthological. But morality is nothing more than a subdomain of a higher-level aspect: ethics. As we have already pointed out, ethics is related to the way we respond to the challenge that every human being faces with regard to the meaning of life. It is in response to this primary challenge that results from being linguistic beings and, therefore, from facing life from the semantic perspective of meaning, that we establish values and, within them, define good and evil. We do this thanks to the power of judgment that language gives us.

We cannot escape our condition as moral beings. This is not an option open to us as human beings. We cannot behave, as other animals do, outside the imperative of the meaning of life and our capacity to make judgments. Immorality, seen as liberation from moral domination and not as contravening certain particular moral guidelines, is not an available option.

But there is an option that we do not always detect. This is the one proposed to us, once again, by Nietzsche. It is the option of committing ourselves to the reevaluation of our values, to the judgment of our moral judgments, and to the design of new guidelines of meaning, of new ways of interpreting good and evil. We can never place ourselves outside of good and evil, but we can transcend them, we can go beyond the particular way in which they have historically been defined and which we have made our own. This necessarily involves reexamining the sacred and the diabolical as expressions of the distinction between good and evil. This is equivalent to rehumanizing morality.

We cannot avoid having moral limits. They may be broader or narrower, but we all have them. Without moral limits, social life, life together with others, is impossible. The problem arises when we become prisoners of our own moral limits, without the possibility of reflecting on them and their consequences in our lives: when we close ourselves off to the possibility of redesigning them according to our desires in life.


 

5. The person as an emotional domain

The person is a specific emotional space or field. Depending on our way of being, there are things we will not see and actions we will not be able to perform. We are a set of predispositions and possible actions. But we are also a set of actions that are not possible for us.

When we characterize people, we usually refer to the emotional space they occupy. When asked about someone, we might say, for example, "Anita is a delicate woman," "Camilo is such a sweet little boy," "Alberto is a depressive guy," "Constanza is a happy girl." All of these characterizations involve emotional factors.

As we have said elsewhere, emotions can be reconstructed linguistically and can also change due to linguistic interactions. We consider them, however, to be a domain distinct from language. Language can affect emotions, just as emotions can affect language. Due to our emotional state, we will engage in certain conversations and be unavailable for others.

Every person understands a particular emotional field. We find people who are more optimistic or more pessimistic, more ambitious or more resigned, more fluid or more aggressive with others, and so on. Our possibility for change will be different depending on our emotional field. Each emotional field defines different limits for the transformation of the person.

We consider the emotional aspect of the person to be the most important aspect when it comes to, for example, learning and coaching. The emotional field of the person is the factor that defines their limits for change and self-improvement. We are not saying that the emotional side of the person determines, once and for all, how far it would be possible for someone to change. Our inherent possibilities for change are practically infinite, and in this sense, we are not subject to immovable limitations.

But without changing the emotional state in which the person finds themselves, our opportunities to bring about change will be very limited. However, if we take care to first change the emotional context in which learning or coaching can take place, we can immediately expand someone's willingness to transform. In other words, even though a person's emotional field sets limits on their transformation, that field itself can be transformed in order to expand those limits.

If someone is not in the right emotional state, they will not see the new possibilities that are presented to them. In order for them to see those possibilities, it is often necessary to modify their emotional state first. However, once this is achieved and that person sees possibilities that they did not see before, it may still be the case that their emotional state does not allow them to take the actions that they now see as possible. The emotionality that allows us to observe something is not necessarily the same that will lead us to act within that space of possibilities. Often, another emotional change is required to generate the disposition that will lead to those actions. The anthological coach has the responsibility of designing all these emotional interventions.


 

The conservative forces of the person

We have said that the person can be considered a principle of coherence in the actions we take. As such, it implies a great conservative force, a force that resists the possibility of moving toward a different way of being. We tend to become coherent; it is only as a secondary option that we are willing to choose a global process of transformation. Rather, we remain within the limits of the same stories and patterns of behavior, merely adding anything new to the old principles we have used to articulate our past actions.

From this point of view, the person can be considered as an addition of actions to a story, as a unit with a certain pattern of behavior. It is this conservative force that allows us to characterize individuals (according to the actions they have taken in the past) and anticipate how they will perform in the future. It is this same conservative force that has mistakenly led us to see the person as an immutable being who will remain constant, regardless of the changes (which are always assumed to be superficial) that occur. It is precisely here that the metaphysical understanding of the person finds one of its roots.

Without denying the fact that the person is in a permanent process of transformation and self-creation, the principle of coherence and the conservative forces that the person unleashes on themselves allow us to observe the person not only as a process of becoming but also as a certain way of "being." It allows us to observe the person as the place from which we act, as a certain "soul."

These conservative forces within the individual are an important aspect to consider in anthological coaching. If the possibilities for change that arise in a coaching interaction do not affect the core coherence of the person undergoing coaching, there will probably be no problem in accepting them. However, greater resistance can be expected if those possibilities challenge the basic principle of coherence that defines that person's way of being.

The transformative forces of the person

Just as we refer to the conservative forces of the person (from which the coach can expect resistance), there are also conditions that facilitate coaching, which the coach can count on. We will call them transformative forces and point out two of them here: one, which is always contingent, and the other, which is anthological, constitutive of our human way of being.

The normal starting point for coaching is a break. Individuals seek a coach because they declare that "something" in their lives is not going well and they realize that they need a different observer to help them resolve that break: someone who possesses distinctions and competencies that they do not have. They realize that the observer that they are does not allow them to take charge of the break.

Every break that opens a coaching interaction shows us two sides. On the one hand, we find ourselves, due to our own coherences (and often, recurrently), in certain life situations. On the other hand, because of our own coherences, such situations are not acceptable to us and we declare them to be a break. Therefore, both generating the situation in which we find ourselves committed and not being satisfied with it are manifestations of the coherence that we are. When we request coaching, we also recognize something else: the fact that the coherence we are does not allow us to resolve the breakdown, and we declare that we need an observer to help us get out of it, to help us observe what we cannot observe and to take the actions we do not know how to take.

Often, the rupture is experienced as something that is interfering with the principle of coherence that we are, and our initial tendency may be to try to save or protect it. It is the coach's task to show the person who requested coaching how that rupture is often an expression of the same principle of coherence. Therefore, every rupture contains powerful forces of transformation that are the coach's natural allies in carrying out their work. The coach must return to this rupture again and again to keep alive the forces of transformation that coaching requires.

The second transformative force is constitutive of our human nature. To be human is to live in a process of incompleteness as a person. This manifests itself, for example, in the way we relate to time. As Milan Kundera points out in The Book of Laughter and Forgetting, "the only reason individuals want to be masters of the future is to change the past. They fight for access to laboratories where photographs are retouched and biographies and stories are rewritten." Being human means being forced to take a position regarding our being. We have a permanent need to take care of ourselves. If we do not, we risk our existence.

Sometimes we may be satisfied with the way we take care of ourselves, and yet we cannot stop doing so. When this happens, it is usually a passing moment. Even a satisfying present is rarely an expression of complete fulfillment in the present. A satisfying present is usually so because we envision a future full of possibilities. But this very fact, this need for future possibilities, speaks to the sense of incompleteness with which we humans go through life.

This sense of incompleteness leads us to a permanent process of transcendence with respect to ourselves. We are always trying to achieve some kind of fulfillment that we never fully attain. To be human is to be in a process of permanent recognition of our limitations, restrictions, and imperfections. We are always recognizing our incompetence. To be human is to be in a continuous process of becoming.

This sense of transcendence that constitutes human beings is at the basis of our spiritual quests and experiences. But we transcend ourselves in very different ways. In addition to our spiritual life, we also transcend through politics, aesthetics, work, family life, volunteer work in the community, and so on. Love, for example, gives us a sense of completeness that we cannot achieve alone. This impulse toward transcendence is a great transformative force for the individual. When activated, it has immense power and can far outweigh the resistance that will come from the conservative forces within the individual.


 

Life and literature

Literature shows us, in a beautiful way, how the construction of people relates to much of what we have discussed in this chapter. Literary characters are fictional people we create from the power and generative magic of language. By playing with language, authors bring fictional people, worlds, and experiences to life. Mexican novelist Carlos Fuentes tells us about his experience of writing: "When I sit down to write, I am master of the world. For a brief moment, I am God. I create reality."

If we look at the great literary characters, we find that what makes them special is not the events of their literary lives, but the depth of their personalities. The characters in modern literature are characterized by not being very different from the rest of us and, normally, by not having extraordinary experiences either.

One of the most important modern literary characters is Flaubert's Madame Bovary. In her, we find an ordinary person with an extremely common lifestyle in a small town in France. Nothing in her life is different from that of the hundreds of people who live in a town like hers. What makes her so special, as Flaubert so masterfully shows, is the depth of the literary design of her character. From what the work tells us, the character of Emma Bovary comes to life. We begin to see Madame Bovary when we meet other people. We can say how Madame Bovary would have acted under certain circumstances. She becomes someone in our social circle, whom we know almost better than our closest friends.

Dostoevsky is another literary genius in character construction. It is difficult to read his novels without being left, for the rest of our lives, with the characters he presents to us. How could we forget Prince Myshkin, Raskolnikov, or each of the Karamazov brothers? This is the genius of the great literary authors.
