THE LANGUAGE OF POWER

[34]

Much of what we have done so far has consisted of postulating and demonstrating the power of language. Therefore, it is not necessary to introduce power in this chapter. It has been constantly present in our arguments. We have seen it appear, for example, when we postulated that language is generative and therefore creates realities. We also spoke of it when we argued that power is the main criterion for choosing between different interpretations. We spoke of the power that results from each and every linguistic act. We referred to the power of conversations. We saw how power is associated with our ability to make judgments. Later on, the topic of power also arose when we addressed the domain of emotionality and, specifically, when we observed the loss of power that results from resentment or the gain of power that produces the emotionality of ambition. And with that, we have not exhausted the times when power has already been at the center of what we have argued. It is time, therefore, to move from referring to power when we talk about language and emotionality to examining its meaning. If we spoke earlier about the power of language, we will now reverse the relationship and talk about the language of power.


 

The traditional conception of power

It is interesting to examine how we traditionally understand power. Perhaps its main characteristic is its reification. Our traditional conception treats power as a substance, as "something" that is there, independently of the individuals who observe it. It seems to be a mysterious "something," highly elusive, which at times seems to be within our grasp, only to discover soon after that it has slipped through our fingers. It gives the impression that power is something that individuals "access." It is not uncommon, therefore, to hear expressions that refer to the "seizure" or "conquest" of power, as if it were there, waiting to be grabbed.

Sometimes power seems to be associated with the image of a mountain that we can climb and at whose "summit" there is only room for a few. When some reach it, others are forced to descend. At other times, power tends to be associated with the image of a fluid, which can be distributed differently and passes from one person to another, as if they were operating as a cup into which power can be poured. In certain situations, it seems to be "concentrated" in the hands of a few; in others, it is distributed or "diluted" more or less equally. It even gives the impression that this substance is governed by the laws of thermodynamics. Its quantity seems to be fixed, and when someone cedes power to another, they lose the power they cede, with a precision typical of Archimedes' postulate.

The way in which the social sciences, and particularly sociology, habitually refer to power is characteristic of this reifying tendency. This is not surprising. Sociological language is characterized by being a language of reifications, and the treatment it

it confers on power is just one example among many others. We maintain that as long as sociology fails to recognize that every social phenomenon is a linguistic phenomenon and, therefore, as long as it does not place language at the center of its concerns, its reifying tendency will be practically uncounterable, and its "power" as a discipline will remain as limited and precarious as we know it to be. Human sociability is based on language, which is the key to its intelligibility. As long as this is ignored, the social sciences will continue to move in reified and phantasmagorical worlds.

Reification, however, is only one feature of our traditional conception of power. Another feature just as important as the previous one is the strong negative ethical charge often associated with power. This is not a homogeneous phenomenon and is much greater in some historical discourses than in others. The negative assessment of power is, for example, strongly visible both in Catholic discourse and in the old Marxist discourse, now in the process of extinction.

Within the Western world, the development of Protestant ethics represents an important correction to this tendency, giving rise, not without contradictions, to a conception that confers a positive value on both work and power. This does not prevent, however, even within the historical Protestant discourse, the persistence of regressive and undermining elements that make power a degrading factor. We maintain that the devaluation of power is a general feature of the metaphysical program, now in crisis, that sustained Western historical development for centuries.

In its clearest expression, this negative ethical assessment of power is recognized in the judgments that "power is evil" and that "power corrupts." Such judgments therefore suggest avoiding it, not becoming contaminated by it. Both, therefore, recommend not following "the path of power" and choosing other paths. Power is thus associated with the threat of distancing us from the path of virtue and exposing us to the dangers of vice.

Our position in this regard is that any interpretation that devalues power as a general phenomenon ultimately implies a degradation of life itself. Power, we postulate, is consubstantial with human life. For human beings, to live is to be inevitably thrown onto the path of power. To understand this, however, it is necessary to abandon the notion of power that results from the metaphysical program and proceed to develop a different conception.

Before doing so, we must first ask ourselves: What are the alternative paths within the metaphysical program? What other paths are available in life? Fundamentally, there are two: the path of truth and the path of salvation.


 

The path of truth

As we have argued elsewhere, the metaphysical program begins with a movement, a particular argument made by Socrates in ancient Greece. Committed to the great theme of virtues, which lead human beings to live well, Socrates takes the position that all virtue is a function of truth. This movement will have profound consequences in the history of humanity.

What is interesting about this movement, which in itself places truth at the center of ethics, and therefore of the profound meaning of life, is nevertheless what results from it. The truth of which Socrates speaks is not a distinction that arises from human action and behavior, nor does it arise from the concrete life of men. The truth to which Socrates alludes is an absolute, abstract, and universal reference point that transcends and, therefore, lies beyond human life itself. Truth does not belong to the world of men.

Moreover, for Socrates, truth not only transcends the world of human beings, it even transcends the sacred world of the gods. Virtues are not an expression of what the gods do; what the gods do is an expression of abstract and universal virtues that are independent of them. These virtues, therefore, are established independently of men and gods. Being independent of them, human beings can access them through reason. Reason is the vehicle that is capable of leading us along the path of truth.

It is this argument proposed by Socrates that will give rise to the birth of metaphysics, initiated immediately afterwards by Plato and Aristotle. Unlike Socrates, the latter abandon the theme of good living as their central concern and commit themselves fundamentally to the search for the transcendent Truth that is deduced from the aforementioned Socratic position.

It is precisely this commitment to the transcendent world of truth that constitutes them as metaphysicians, as thinkers who project themselves beyond the surrounding physical world that would amaze the pre-Socratic philosophers and whose constitutive principle (arche) they would seek to apprehend. As we argued previously, in the transcendent world of metaphysicians, Truth converges with Being, in the total stillness of immobility. Being is immutable.

It is not surprising, therefore, that metaphysicians would declare as their main enemies two philosophical currents that flourished before and even simultaneously with them. We refer, first, as already mentioned, to the philosophy of Heraclitus, who maintained that everything is in a process of permanent becoming, that nothing is immutable. Secondly, to the Sophists who, following Protagoras, subscribed to a very different position on truth.

Protagoras, perhaps the most notable of the Sophists, was a personal friend of Pericles and achieved great influence at a time when Athens was at the height of its splendor. Protagoras developed the doctrine that would later become known as Homo mensura. It holds that "man is the measure of all things." Within this doctrine, it makes no sense to seek truth in the cold and transcendent world of abstract and universal ideas. For the Sophists, as Gorgias, another of their great representatives, also understood, truth cannot be dissociated from the social power of language within a given community.

No one has shown us better than Nietzsche the consequences that result from what he calls "the pathos of truth" proposed by metaphysicians. As Nietzsche rightly points out, the assumptions on which the whole metaphysical edifice rests, by relying on the privileged value conferred on truth and, with it, on the transcendent world of ideas, result in the degradation of human life and the world. The world of human experience is transformed into a world of misleading appearances whose truth lies always beyond itself, in the cold stillness of Being.

The true world of metaphysicians is not the world where the experience of life takes place. The world in which life takes place is, in the image proposed by Plato, equivalent to the interior of a cave that receives light from the outside, to which we do not have direct access. Inside the cave, we only see the movement of distorted shadows. These are our appearances: mere forms that do not reflect the true and inimitable being of things.


 

The path to salvation

Something similar happens with the proposal made by Christian discourse and, particularly, with its fusion with the metaphysical program carried out after integrating with Roman imperial power and becoming the central political reference point throughout the Middle Ages.

The Christian ideal is not defined, as it was for the early metaphysicians, by a commitment to achieving truth through the transcendent power of reason. Its ideal is the salvation of the human soul. In this sense, it represents an effort to respond to the suffering and limitations of existence. Christianity tells us that human beings are torn in their existence, living the great tragedy of their finitude while attaining the ideal of infinity. Their souls are predestined for death, making it impossible for them to achieve eternity, which they can nevertheless dream of.

Jesus Christ, however, has shown a way to salvation for the human soul. He has shown the existence of an eternal world, in another life, beyond death. This world is Paradise. Not everyone, however, will be able to access the eternal life offered by Paradise. The right to it must be earned in this earthly life. The meaning of existence for Christians, therefore, is to ensure the salvation of their souls and earn eternal life in Paradise.

From this perspective, it is "the afterlife" that gives meaning to the concrete life of human beings on Earth.

As Milan Kundera argues, speaking not of Christianity but of socialism, but referring in general to this same existential stance, "life is elsewhere." What really matters is not this life, but the other one. This triggers yet another form of devaluation, of degradation, of concrete human existence.

This is our central point of criticism of the proposal of the path to salvation: its degradation of concrete human life. And it is only when we observe that the path to salvation appears to be associated with the degradation of human life that our criticism of it is relevant. We do not, therefore, criticize the proposal of another life itself, nor the very idea of a path to salvation, insofar as we can support them without degrading human life. Our point of criticism, we insist, is directed at the assumption that in order to save the soul, it is necessary to sacrifice life. We only oppose the idea of human sacrifice in pursuit of the ideal of salvation.

Within the prevailing interpretation of the path to salvation, it is often assumed that, to the extent that the meaning of this life is conferred by the next, anything that signifies the full affirmation of this life, regardless of the effort to attain salvation, is at least suspect. Anything that does not subordinate the value of our concrete existence to the higher values of eternal life is often interpreted as a departure from the path of virtue. Human happiness results from the satisfaction of having earned the merits necessary for transcendent life. All other forms of happiness or enjoyment are seen as temptations that incite Christians to sin and therefore need to be avoided. The goal of this life is basically one: the Christian goal of salvation.

From this perspective, any form of affirmation of earthly life for life's sake, and therefore alien to the ideal of salvation, is usually negatively sanctioned. When this happens, Christians not only live with the anguish that is inherent in all forms of human existence, but also with the anguish that results from having to respond to two different existential logics: that which corresponds to this, their concrete human life, and that of the afterlife. From this ethical discourse, affirming the immanent value of human life often generates guilt and fear of compromising salvation.

For centuries, this ethical discourse was one of the predominant discourses in the Western world. Its influence was preserved, among other reasons, because it was not challenged by different discourses. Today, however, this discourse has entered a critical phase. Several factors contribute to this. Among these, at least three are worth mentioning.

First, the growing power and greater autonomy that individuals have achieved vis-à-vis institutional organizations in general and, more particularly, vis-à-vis the authority of the Church. Today, submission to the authority of the Church is a matter of choice. This was not the case in the past, when the Church had a power over individuals that it no longer possesses today.

Second, in an interdependent world, exposure to the influence of multiple different discourses, multiple different ways of life, and multiple options for giving meaning to life. Today, "the ungodly" have gained a degree of respectability that they never had in the past, and their own paths to salvation and their different ethics of life no longer seem aberrant to us. In many cases, we even find them attractive.

Finally, it is also worth mentioning the great crisis that all metadiscourses in general face today in their goal of guaranteeing a stable meaning of life for human beings. This is what has come to be characterized as the postmodern condition. In this context, Christian discourse itself and its proposal for the path to salvation are historically insufficient to fully assure us of the meaning we must provide ourselves with in order to make the transition through this human life. All of this creates conditions that allow us to rethink the meaning of life and, from there, to rethink the problem of power.

The hidden paths of power

Both from the path of truth of the metaphysicians and from the path of salvation proposed by Christianity, both pillars of our Western conception, what we will call the pathos of power has been attacked, insofar as it is not subordinate to the objectives of truth or salvation, respectively. This is the root of the notion that power is evil and corrupt. Power for power's sake is despicable. It is only accepted when it is subordinated to a "higher cause" which, in both cases, refers us to transcendent worlds. This, therefore, degrades power.

What we do not always observe, however, is that these two paths have historically been evident paths of power and, moreover, of a type of power that is often exclusionary, abusive, and denies the mutual respect that we consider fundamental to establishing the basis for an adequate ethic of social coexistence today. From both paths, truth has often been used to cover up what we can now consider abusive uses of power. By resorting to the criterion of truth, they concealed, even from themselves, their relationship with power.

Let us reiterate once again what Humberto Maturana points out: every invocation of truth always contains a demand for obedience. In other words, the criterion of truth offers us an alibi that legitimizes our expectations of being obeyed. In doing so, the other is denied the space in which to establish the legitimacy of their difference from us and the right to act autonomously on the basis of their own interpretations. By legitimizing our expectations of being obeyed, the invocation of truth also legitimizes the reprisals we may take in the event that such obedience is not fulfilled. Truth, therefore, confers legitimacy on the use of violence against the other; it legitimizes the absence of mutual respect.

It is only when we question the legitimacy of invoking truth that we open up a space where the other can emerge as a different, legitimate, and autonomous being. It is only then that we see the emergence of a different interpretation of power that does not necessarily refer to the idea that power is despicable, evil, and corrupt. On the contrary, we discover that one of the conditions associated with the abusive exercise of power is precisely the invocation of truth that appeared to distance itself from it or confer upon it a purifying mantle.

We do not intend to deny the abusive use of power, much less defend it. On the contrary, our entire approach is aimed at giving the subject of power a solid ethical basis consistent with the value of mutual respect. To do so, however, we believe it is essential to question the way in which it is conceived within the metaphysical program and the links that this conception has with the central assumptions of that program.

The relationship between truth and power is not new. We find it in the metaphysical program itself from its origins. Plato sees no problem, after arguing that philosophers are those individuals committed to the search for truth, in asking for political power for them in his ideal of a republic. If they have, by definition, privileged access to truth, who could be better equipped than them to exercise political power over the rest of the members of the community? After all, for metaphysicians, all human virtues are based precisely on truth.

We are so accustomed to the ideal of transcendent truth proposed by the metaphysical program that we cannot imagine how we could do without it. Similarly, it is difficult for us to imagine the possibility of thinking about the issue of power on ethical bases other than those offered by metaphysics.

However, we do not have to go very far to discover within our own Western history concrete, highly successful historical experiences based on very different ideals. We forget, for example, that the most brilliant period in the history of Greece, the one with the greatest cultural wealth, the one in which democracy flourished, was precisely the one that developed before the emergence of metaphysics and which we now identify with the century of Pericles. As Nietzsche rightly points out, the birth of metaphysics marks the beginning of the decline of Athens and, with it, that of the Hellenic world.

But what is more, the history of Rome did not take place under the predominance of metaphysical thought either. Although Plato and Aristotle were known in Rome, they never became the main guides or inspirers of the Romans. It is no coincidence that we do not count any notable metaphysicians among the Romans. On the contrary, the greatest philosophical influence during the Roman period was exerted by the Stoics. And one of the most outstanding periods, considered to be the height of the Roman Empire's power, coincided with the reign of Emperor Hadrian, during which Stoicism exerted its greatest influence.

The great obsession of the Romans was not truth but power. And, in general, they exercised it based on the acceptance of human diversity and respect for those who thought and believed differently from them. Once again, when Christianity gained access to the highest spheres of influence in the Empire, Rome began its decline. It is interesting to follow Gibbons' masterful interpretation in this regard.


 

Power as a linguistic phenomenon

Having broadly characterized the traditional conception of power and its discursive sources of inspiration, it is appropriate to begin developing our own conception of power. To do so, the first thing that interests us is to move toward the dereification that we denounced in the traditional conception. This implies moving away from the assumption that power constitutes some kind of substance.

Our central postulate regarding power is that it is a phenomenon that emerges, as such, from the capacity for language of human beings. Without language, the phenomenon of power does not exist.

Only when we accept this are we in a position to dissolve the notion of power as a substance. From our perspective, power does not refer to any substance or property that is independent of our observations. Without an observer equipped with language, power as such cannot be seen. However, it is not that it cannot be seen because it is hidden, concealed, or covered up, waiting to be revealed or discovered. It is not observed because it is the observer himself who constitutes it as the phenomenon that it is. Power, therefore, is not a phenomenon independent of the observer. The very process of observation constitutes it as a phenomenon.

Power, then, is not something that is "out there," something we can point to with our finger, take with our hands, or put in our pocket. It is not something over which we can claim ownership, for example, in the way we do with our car or our house.

To properly understand what we are saying, it is important to examine our premise that power is a linguistic phenomenon and to examine the different ways in which power appears to be related to language. This is what we will do next.

Power as a linguistic distinction

The first relationship between power and language—and undoubtedly the most superficial—arises from the recognition that power is a linguistic distinction, a distinction we make in language.

We say that this is the most superficial relationship because every time we ask about something, we can strictly speaking respond that it is a linguistic distinction. Everything we can ask about, given that asking necessarily occurs in language, will always be the result of some process of distinction. Only as a result of a distinction can we bring something to hand and ask what that something is that we are able to differentiate from the rest. We cannot ask about what we do not distinguish. Hence the importance of our distinctions.

However, we have different types of distinctions. At one extreme, we can recognize some distinctions that are strongly determined by our biological structure. We can therefore venture that every human being, provided with normal biological conditions for the species, will distinguish a certain range of phenomena for strictly biological reasons.

One example is the perception of certain noises. Given how we humans are, certain noises will be registered within our perceptual field. The same can be said with regard to the experience of certain physical obstacles or the experience of emptiness. In these cases, it is to be expected that different human communities, regardless of the languages that constitute them as communities, will have linguistic distinctions for these phenomena.

At the other extreme, we have distinctions that are the exclusive result of linguistic operations. These do not arise as an expression of our biological structure's response to what happens in our environment, but are the product of the power of language to generate observations and experiences that we could not have without it. Mathematical distinctions are a good example of what we are pointing out, and within them a typical case is the distinction of "zero." Since different distinctions have different powers, those that prove to be more powerful will tend to endure, will be transmitted from one community to another, and in many cases are invented independently by different communities.

Our distinctions, among other factors, constitute us as the type of observer we are. Equipped with a vast set of distinctions—whose roots may be more or less biological, more or less linguistic, but which as such, as distinctions, always live in language—we constitute ourselves as a particular type of observer and open ourselves up to different experiences. Hence, we say that we do not only observe with our senses, but above all, we observe with our linguistic distinctions.

Once we have established ourselves as a particular observer within a given community, we can make a new distinction about the nature of our distinctions. This is the distinction between observing as description and observing as ascription.

When we describe, what we distinguish belongs to what the observer observes. The center of gravity in this case is placed on what is observed or, if you will, on the world. When we ascribe, on the other hand, it is the observer himself who confers on what is observed that which constitutes the process of ascription. The center of gravity is now on the observer himself.

As can be seen, this distinction between description and ascription is equivalent to the one made earlier between statements and judgments. Statements, we say, are descriptions; judgments are ascriptions.

When we make judgments, we confer on the world and its constituent entities features that result from the way we relate to them. Judgments, therefore, speak not only of the entities to which they refer, but also of the relationship we establish with them. Judgments, therefore, always speak as much about the person making them as about the world being judged. Consequently, judgments operate as synthesizers of the way we are in the world, or what Heidegger calls Dasein.

Based on the above, it is then worth asking: What kind of distinction is power? We have already provided part of the answer. Power, being linguistic like all distinctions, is a distinction whose foundation is not biological, but linguistic. Language constitutes it as such. But when we talk about power, are we making a description or an ascription? Is there an affirmation or a judgment at its root?


 

Power is a judgment

Power, we argue, is a judgment. As a linguistic distinction, power does not refer to a substance whose existence we can assume to be independent of us. On the contrary, power always exists as a judgment we make. Without our ability to make judgments, it would not be possible for us to talk about power, recognize it as a phenomenon, or experience it.

By recognizing it as a judgment, we are pointing out that when we talk about power, we are making an attribution, we are attributing something to a particular entity, something that does not belong as such to the entity but to the way in which we, as observers, relate to it. As with any judgment, with any attribution, it is the observer who "confer" a particular trait on a particular entity.

What kind of judgment is power? Power is constituted as a phenomenon based on a judgment made by an observer about the greater capacity of a particular entity to generate action. By saying greater, we recognize that we are comparing the capacity to generate action of the entity we are examining with the more restricted capacity to generate action of other equivalent entities. Let us briefly examine some of the elements contained in this postulate.

Power, as a judgment, refers to "a particular entity." Why do we talk about "entity"? To make it clear that we make the judgment of power for any unit that we presume to have the capacity for action. We can distinguish three types of entities: agents, machines, and tools.

We call entities with autonomous capacity for action agents. We can attribute (interpret) a certain concern to their actions. In the case of agents, we can say that they act as a way of addressing a concern. Agents can be individuals or collective agents (organizations, communities, etc.).

We call machines those artifacts that are capable of performing some activity on their own, but that require an agent to program, start, or stop them. With the development of various feedback mechanisms, the distinction between autonomous agents and machines has become increasingly blurred and problematic, as shown by the discussion surrounding the topic of artificial intelligence.

We also have entities that do not have autonomous capacity for action on their own, but which increase an agent's capacity for action. We call them tools. A tool normally expands an agent's capacity for action and, in doing so, increases its power. This allows us to talk about more or less powerful tools. When we do so, however, we use the distinction of power by reference to the agent that makes use of the tool. As such, tools do not possess power. They increase the power of agents.

Power, we have said, is a judgment about "capacity to generate action." Action, therefore, is the basic referent of the judgment of power. The greater an entity's capacity for action, the more power we can say it has.

In this regard, it is important to make two points. The first is to distinguish between capacity for action and the judgment of power. These are two different phenomena. Entities possess the capacity for action that they possess, and this can often be independent of the judgment that an observer may make. In this sense, it is obviously not the judgment that confers (or ascribes) the capacity for action that they possess. However, given that capacity for action, we can make the judgment that the entity is powerful. Power, therefore, lives in the judgment that is made and not in the capacity for action that is judged.

The second point relates to the fact that the judgment of power is not always a judgment on actions taken, but on the "capacity to generate action." By this we are pointing out that it is not necessary for the action to be carried out in order to make a well-founded judgment of power. Even if the entity does not act, if we can maintain that it has the "capacity" to do so, we can still make the judgment of power. Having power is different from exercising it. What this scope shows us is that the judgment of power is not about action, but about the mastery of the possible. If I judge that someone could do something, whether they do it or not, I can still maintain that they have the power to do so. We will return to this point later.

Finally, it is worth referring to the element of "differentiated" capacity for action. We could also have said "relative" or "comparative." What are we getting at with this? We maintain that we do not make judgments of power by examining only an entity's capacity to generate action, but by comparing that capacity for action with some other capacity. The judgment of power differentiates the capacity for action of the entity being judged by reference to something else. And it is in the difference that the judgment of power is made as a judgment.

The point of reference can often be an entity similar to the one being judged. It can also be a reference to certain social standards that allows us to argue that an entity is powerful. The important thing to recognize, in this sense, is that isolated observation of an entity's capacity for action does not lead us to a judgment of power. If, after observing a single entity, without reference to others or to certain standards, we say that it has power, that makes no sense. The judgment of power always involves reference to another equivalent entity or to certain social standards.

This allows us to understand why a judgment of power made under certain historical conditions can lose its meaning if those conditions change. Social standards change historically, and what was powerful at one point in time ceases to be so later on. This happens in the fields of sports, technology, education, business, and so on. It also happens that the same performance may be considered very powerful by one community and go unnoticed by another. This confirms the linguistic nature of power.

The power of language

If we accept that power is a judgment regarding differentiated capacity for action and if we also accept the generative nature of language—and, therefore, that language is action—we must recognize another level in the relationship between power and language. To the extent that language is action, language is a source of power. The way we act in language is therefore a crucial aspect in assessing how powerful we are in life.

Based on the above, as we suggested at the beginning of this chapter, it is therefore possible to look back and examine everything we have discussed so far from the perspective of power. Each specific linguistic competence we have referred to can now be recognized as a possible domain of power.

And in a way, when talking about them, we have not been able to avoid discussing, for example, the power of saying "No," the power of making our own judgments and knowing how to justify them, the power of knowing how to make promises and keep them, the power of complaint, and so on. In each of these cases, we are faced with a particular domain in which we can perform better or worse and, therefore, be more or less powerful.

When we examine language as a whole, we can distinguish four different domains of linguistic competence and, consequently, four different sources of power. The first is the domain of distinctions. Our distinctions make us different observers, and depending on the type of observer we are, our possibilities for action will be different. Without a certain distinction, there will be phenomena that we will not observe and, therefore, we will be very blind to the possibility of intervening in them.

The second domain of linguistic competence is the one developed extensively in this book: the domain of speech acts. We will not examine them again. But every speech act and every modality and aspect within them is a source of power.

Within the set of speech acts, there are two in particular that are traditionally the most important indicators of power. We are referring to declarations and requests. We say that the President of the Republic of a given country has power because, unlike other citizens of the country, what he declares carries the authority to change the state of affairs and because his requests are accepted and fulfilled. There may be many different reasons for this, but his power is manifested centrally in the exercise of these two speech acts. His power is the power of the word.

Now let's take an opposite example: that of a mugger on the street who asks us to hand over our wallet. Again, there may be many reasons that lead us to behave in one way or another with respect to his request. The fact that he has a knife in his hand and that we speculate about the consequences of saying no are some of them. However, his power will manifest itself in his ability to make us accept his request. His power is, likewise, the power of words.

This brings us to a point that we consider to be of the utmost importance: power over others, which is exercised by imposing one's own word and making others comply with it. This form of power ultimately always depends not on the person who asks or declares, but on the person who accepts. There may be very justified reasons for granting such acceptance; among others, as in the case of the assailant, the judgment of not wanting to risk one's life. We are not making a moral judgment in this regard. But that does not prevent us from recognizing that, ultimately, the source of this type of power always lies with those who accept the word of others. This form of power is always a concession made by those over whom power is exercised.

We often refer to the power of money or the power of weapons. However, we do not always recognize that both money and force are usually factors of power insofar as they influence the power of the word. Money allows us to ask, and to the extent that we are willing to part with it in the amount stipulated by the owner of what we are asking for, we will get what we ask for. In doing so, we leave the other in possession of our money and, therefore, with the ability to initiate this same game on their own. To the extent that money leads to the acceptance of our requests, we say that it is an instrument that socially embodies the ability to obtain promises.

When talking about force, it is important to make a distinction. The use of force as an instrument of subjugation is different from the use of force as an instrument of destruction. Obviously, the effectiveness of force as an instrument of subjugation rests on its destructive capacity, but its uses in one capacity or the other are different. In the case of subjugation, force is used to back up the power of words. In this case, therefore, the resulting power depends on who agrees to submit. But it is very different from the power of force on the battlefield. Here, power is evaluated by its destructive capacity. We must not lose sight of the fact, however, that the destructive power of force is generally used to obtain submission. Ultimately, the goal of war is political, as von Clausewitz recognized.

When examining the domain of linguistic competencies related to speech acts, it is important to remember that it is not enough to consider the latter in isolation. Speech acts are also components of more complex language games that can integrate several of them in different ways.

A third domain of linguistic competencies related to power concerns narratives. These are interpretive linguistic constructs that, as such, seek to generate meaning and establish relationships between entities, actions, and events in our world of experience. Narratives provide us with a basis from which we act in the world. Depending on the type of narrative we uphold, our possibilities for action will be different. Hence, we can also speak of the power of language by referring to the power of our narratives. A recurring example of a type of narrative with great power is science. Science offers us a particular type of explanation of phenomena that allows us to generate or reproduce them.

Finally, a fourth domain of linguistic competencies also related to power is the domain of conversation. As already explained, through conversation we can not only act directly and modify the current state of affairs. We can also modify the state of what is possible, so that we can subsequently intervene directly.

We have insisted that power does not result solely from our effective performance, but also involves a judgment about our possibilities for action, regardless of the actions taken. Among the different types of conversations we have presented, there are two that are directly linked to our ability to expand our horizon of possibilities. We are referring to conversations about possible actions and possible conversations. Achieving competence in these is an important source of personal power. In general, the greater the reflective component of our conversations, the greater their capacity to expand what is possible, insofar as this allows us to examine the possible space that other conversations take for granted.


 

The facticity of power


 

From what has already been pointed out, it is clear how absurd it is to take a position contrary to power. Particularly after we accept that power is not a substance that is out there and on which it is relevant to take a position for or against. Once we recognize power as a judgment on the capacity to generate action, and once we recognize that individuals and groups of individuals inevitably have and will have different capacities to generate actions, we discover that opposing power is not only pointless, since this will be a judgment we will continue to make, but it is also self-limiting.

Opposing power, as such, leads us down the path of powerlessness. Powerless is the one who has no power. It is someone who suffers through life, without being able or willing to intervene in it. And by not intervening, they make others the masters of their own existence. The powerless live in resignation from which nothing is possible, no action makes sense. The powerless are a breeding ground for resentment, as their inaction cannot stop the actions of others or the effects of those actions on themselves. Often, however, those who claim to oppose power are not exactly choosing powerlessness, but rather expressing their resentment of a power they do not know how to counteract.

Power is a fact of life. No human being can do without it. To oppose power is to already be inside the power games. And while we can legitimately question and oppose the way power is played out socially or the social foundations that confer power on some and restrict it to others, we cannot therefore escape participating in the power game that is life.

The most appropriate way to oppose forms of power that we reject is precisely by playing the power game. Power results from the capacity for action of human beings and from the fact that this capacity for action is not and cannot be the same for everyone because, as individuals, we are and will always be different. The unequal distribution of power is a fact of social coexistence.

A common way of opposing the facticity of power is to assert the ideal of equality. Insofar as the judgment of power is always a judgment of inequality, of differential capacity to generate action, all forms of power, viewed from the ideal of equality, are always suspect. The important thing in this regard is to examine what kind of equality we are talking about. History, insofar as it increases the process of individuation of human beings, makes us increasingly different and more autonomous. We were never equal, and we are becoming less and less so.

The only equality that is consistent within this process of historical individuation is that which guarantees everyone the basic conditions to participate in the social games of power. It is equality of opportunity to participate in such games. It is that which questions, on ethical grounds, that the power games of some deny others participation. But it is a participation whose meaning is not to be equal, but rather the expansion of different individual possibilities.

Michel Foucault has been one of the most important contemporary thinkers, showing us how power permeates, without exception, the whole of social life. His great contribution has been precisely to reveal how power is present in every institution, in every discourse, in every social relationship. His great weakness, however, is that he does so from a position of denunciation, without managing to accept the facticity of power. Every time he points his finger at power, one hears an accusation. Foucault resents power.

We are not arguing that all forms of power are acceptable from an ethical point of view. But it is not enough to expose the presence of power for that, in itself, to be sufficient grounds for challenging it. Foucault's position is based on an anarchic ideal of social life (the idea that social coexistence without power is possible) and on a consistent ethic that makes power a sinful element. For Foucault, what is acceptable, what guarantees social peace, is the absence of power.

We disagree with Foucault. For us, acceptance and peace are opposed to the resentment that arises from powerlessness and are allies in overcoming the resignation that denies the possibility of action. Therefore, acceptance and peace are identified with the commitment to expand what is possible and increase power. They need to be complemented by ambition, by what Nietzsche calls "the will to power." Just as Heraclitus posits that "rest is achieved in change," [35] we similarly maintain that peace is achieved in action and in the expansion of our possibilities in life.


 

Learning as a strategy of power

We have posited that power is the differential capacity of an entity to generate action. Until now, we have conceived of the term differential (or comparative) in relation to another entity or to certain social standards. However, we can also do so by comparing the capacity for action generation of the same entity at two different points in time. When we do this, we are faced with a particular form of power that we usually call learning.

When we argue that the same entity can perform effective actions at a certain point in its development, actions that the same entity could not perform in the past, we say that the entity has learned. Learning is, therefore, a judgment of power. What was not possible before in terms of effective action becomes possible afterwards.

It is interesting to examine this from the point of view of the type of concept of learning it presupposes. As we argued with regard to power, learning, we maintain, is a judgment. We can do what we do, but we can only say that there was learning when we make the judgment that what we can do in the present, we could not do in the past. It is, therefore, a judgment that refers to our capacity for action. There is no learning, just as there is no knowledge, that does not refer, in one way or another, to our capacity for effective action.

To know is to do, just as to do is to know. And to learn is to be able to do what we could not do before. Does this imply a narrow pragmatism? Pragmatism, undoubtedly; whether it is narrow is debatable. By referring all learning and all knowledge to our capacity for action, we are not suggesting that certain situations that we previously conceived of as learning, or as knowledge, should now be excluded from the list of things learned or known. We are not restricting our notions of learning and knowledge. What we are doing is modifying their meaning.

But aren't there expressions of knowledge that are not expressed in capacity for action? If someone knows that it rained yesterday, what does this have to do with our capacity for action? If someone knows which house Napoleon slept in on the night of August 5, 1806, isn't it the same? Or when someone learns literature, where is the action?

To better answer all these questions, it is convenient to turn the problem around and ask ourselves: how, in each of these cases, can we maintain that someone knows or has learned something? On what basis, then, do we base our judgment of learning? On the fact that if we ask, these people will give us the answer we consider appropriate. And from our perspective, in which we understand that speaking is acting, responding appropriately is acting effectively. It is having the competence to function appropriately in the language game that we open with the question we ask.

And this is precisely how we usually evaluate learning. The only thing that has changed, from our point of view, is that we now accept that responding is acting and responding appropriately is effective action.

Why do we talk about "effective" action? Because obviously not every form of action (or not every response) allows us to make a judgment about learning. Only some actions (only some responses) form the basis for a judgment about learning. This implies that in addition to acting, those who are in the process of learning submit themselves to the judgment of someone who is given the authority to determine whether their action (or response) is effective. As was the case when we referred to the power of an individual's word over others, the judgment of learning is "conferred." It is conferred on those whom we invest with the authority to do so. Learning, as Humberto Maturana often tells us, is a gift (a judgment) that the teacher gives (confer) to the student, based on the actions performed by the latter.

Based on the above, we see that when we learn something, we expand our capacity for action and, therefore, increase our power. Every time we acquire new skills, we gain power. Learning allows us to differentiate ourselves from how we were in the past in terms of our capacity for action. By differentiating ourselves from our past capacity for action, we accelerate our process of becoming and transform ourselves into different human beings. We are what we do: action generates being. Learning, as a form of power, is a crucial part of the process of becoming that we are exposed to in life.


 

Seduction, institutional authority, and force as strategies of power

We have argued that power is associated not only with the actions we take but, above all, with the space of possibilities available to us to act. When comparing one person with others, we can judge that they have a wide range of possibilities for action. We will therefore say that this person has power. However, it is worth considering that this person may not make use of the space of possibilities available to them and, consequently, may not carry out the actions that are possible for them.

Power, therefore, refers to the space of possibilities, the space of possible actions, rather than to the actions actually carried out. The latter correspond to the exercise of power that is specified at the level of the space of possibilities. The greater our possibilities for action are, in comparative terms, the greater our power will be. This defines different possible strategies for increasing our power.

One of them, as we have seen, is learning. Through learning, we increase our capacity for action—and, therefore, our space of possibilities—by gaining new skills that allow us to do what we could not do before. The center of gravity of learning as a strategy of power is the capacity for action of the person in question.

What is possible for us, however, depends not only on what we are capable of doing, but also on the judgment others have of us. We must not forget that possibility is constituted as such in a judgment. Like any judgment, it may be well-founded or unfounded, and the process of establishing its validity refers us to conditions that lie beyond the judgment itself. But this does not prevent us from recognizing that possibility is constituted as such through our judgment that something is or was possible.

The relationship between power, possibility, and judgment has important practical consequences. These are most clearly seen when we examine the power that results from our relationship with others and that is manifested in the way they respond to our statements and requests. We have already examined the influence of these two aspects on our personal power over others.

What we want to highlight here is that the power we achieve over others to command authority for our statements and acceptance of our requests depends not only on our skills, but also, and very particularly, on the judgments others make about us. These judgments contribute decisively to shaping the scope of our possibilities for action with them and are therefore central factors in our power. In other words, our personal power is not only a function of what we are capable of doing, but also of the judgment others make about what we are capable of doing.

This defines three additional strategies of personal power, based on seduction, institutional authority, or force. The strategy of seduction rests on our ability to generate in others the judgment that we are a possibility for them. To the extent that we elicit such a judgment, we expand our capacity for action with them, finding them more willing to confer authority on what we declare and to accept our requests.

A common form of seduction is to observe ourselves in courtship, in the games of love. To the extent that the other person judges us to be a romantic possibility for him or her, our own possibilities with him or her will expand. One of my students once pointed out to me: "I understand little about power, but I recognize the power I have over men: having something they desire."

Our interpretation of seduction, however, transcends the realm of love games. Every time we manage to make the other person judge that we are a possibility for them, we are playing the game of seduction. And we can be a possibility in terms of our professional skills, our interpretations of life, the fact that we have a product that addresses their concerns, and so on.

Seduction, therefore, constitutes an anthological skill that can take on different concrete forms. Among these, we can mention, for example, marketing and sales, political proselytizing, missionary work by religious figures, pedagogy, and so on. In all of them, the objective of the game in question is to modify the other person's space of possibility, based on what we have to offer. Our ability to make offers—indeed, to be an offer to the other—is at the heart of seduction as a strategy of power.

A particular case of seduction is persuasion. Persuasion consists of the ability to generate in others the judgment that our interpretations are the most powerful and, therefore, that they expand the possibilities of those who accept them. The development of the art of persuasion was one of the main concerns of the Greek sophists and is contained in their teachings on rhetoric. Gorgias is said to have been a master at persuading an audience on virtually any subject. He attached great importance to rhetoric in the education of young people, as it contributed to the formation of individuals' public identities and was an important source of the power they attained in the community.

A particular form of persuasion is logic, this great invention of the metaphysicians and developed particularly by Aristotle. Logic focuses its persuasive power on the coherence between the different propositions within an argument. Among the different elements of the art of rhetoric, logic, consistent with its metaphysical origin, emphasizes truth content and argumentative coherence (Logos), reducing the importance of emotional elements (ethos and pathos).

The second strategy of personal power is related to institutional authority. In this case, our statements and requests are socially accepted, not because of the possibility we represent for others as individuals, but because we are invested with the authority that society confers on a particular institutional position. As long as we hold that institutional position, we have access to the power that the community confers on it. The moment we leave the position, we lose the power we previously possessed. Power does not result from a person's capacity for action as such, but from the capacity for action conferred on us by the position we occupy within certain institutional games established by the community.

The third strategy of personal power is that which rests on force, on our capacity for destruction as a means of subjugating or deterring others. We have already referred to this. As in the previous cases, force in these cases is placed at the service of a judgment of possibility on the part of the other. The power we obtain results from the other's judgment of the possible consequences of not obeying our word or the consequences that could result from our ability to respond to the use of their own destructive capacity.

In all these cases, in strategies of seduction, institutional power, and force, our personal power is a function of the other's judgment of possibility.

The judgment of possibility: Chrysippus of Soli

Power, we have insisted, is based on the judgment of the possible. When we talk about the possible, it is interesting to look back at the Stoics. Among them, we should now highlight the figure of Chrysippus, perhaps the most influential of the Stoics in antiquity. He was also one of those who stood furthest from the influence of the metaphysicians. Chrysippus was born in Soli, Cilicia, in the southern part of Asia Minor. He lived from 281 to 208 BC. Of his abundant written output, only a few fragments have survived.

Chrysippus opposed the notion of the possible, previously proposed by Diodorus, according to which the possible is that which is or will be true. Such a notion of the possible, Chrysippus argued, makes it indistinguishable from the necessary. For Chrysippus, there are events that, while possible, will not happen. It is possible, he argues, for a jewel to break, even though this never happens. In the same way that he takes sides against Diodorus, Chrysippus also confronts the approach of Cleanthes, a Stoic who preceded him, who had argued that statements about the past could not be necessary because, if they were, they would not have been possible. In other words, while Diodorus applied the criterion of necessity to both the past and the future, Cleanthes denied such necessity and argued that both the past and the future were possible.

Opposing both, Chrysippus takes a position that we can summarize by saying: the past belongs to the realm of the necessary; the future, to the realm of the possible. According to Chrysippus, all true things of the past are necessary insofar as they do not admit of change and because the past cannot change from what happened to what did not happen. What has already happened ceases to be possible insofar as the possible is that which could happen, whether it actually happens or not.

According to Chrysippus, what actually happens is what we call destiny. One of the factors that separates the possible from destiny is what Chrysippus calls what is "in our power." Through what is "in their power," individuals participate in the generation of destiny. Destiny is not independent of the actions of individuals. This reminds us of Heraclitus, who told us, "Our character is our destiny."

The judgment that conceives of the past as necessary contains a strong ethical charge. First, it represents a powerful antidote to resentment, that emotionality that arises from impotence, from lack of power. When we look at the past through the prism of necessity, it regains its innocence. Things happened as they did because there were no conditions for them to happen differently. In that sense, they were necessary. Those who lament past experiences often forget that the person lamenting is already a different individual from the one who lived those experiences, as they are a product of them. The person who lived those experiences was a different being from the one lamenting, as they had not yet gone through them.

Resenting the past not only compromises the past, it also compromises the present and, in doing so, compromises the future as well, insofar as the present is its prelude. This is part of the corrosive effect of resentment. Any form of resentment of the past reveals that we do not fully accept who we are, that we do not love ourselves enough, for who we are today is what that past made us. Full acceptance of who we are rests on full acceptance of our past, on the ability to look at it and accept its necessity, without stripping it of its innocence. This is what Nietzsche refers to when he talks about the importance of amor fati, love of fate, love of the actual happening of things. What does not kill us, Nietzsche tells us, makes us stronger.

Accepting the past does not mean ceasing to take responsibility for our actions or demanding that others take responsibility for theirs. The way we take responsibility for what we did or how we were is by projecting ourselves into the future, so that the actions we are capable of taking take charge of our necessary past and avoid the consequences of our past behavior. Nothing is more useless, nor does anything produce such unnecessary suffering, than tearing ourselves apart over a past we cannot change. Nothing is more absurd than fighting against the necessary.

Just as it is necessary to learn to accept the necessary past as a way of learning to live well, it is equally important to learn to exercise what is "in our power" because, in this way, we participate in shaping destiny. Entering the future means participating in the art of the possible.


 

Power and emotionality

By focusing our interpretation of power on the judgment of the possible, we cannot help but turn our gaze to the domain of emotionality. The possible, we argue, is defined both at the level of our judgments and at the level of our emotions and moods. It is not surprising, therefore, that we have been forced to return to the subject of resentment.

Depending on the emotionality we find ourselves in, what is possible for us will be different. Emotionality defines the possible range of actions we can take. Conversely, depending on our judgment of possibility, our emotionality will be different. When we see our possibilities expanding, we will move toward positive emotionalities; when we see our possibilities shrinking, we will move toward negative emotionalities. Every emotion and every state of mind can be linguistically reconstructed in terms of judgments of possibility.

Emotionality is an inherent factor in any configuration of power and, therefore, in any capacity for action. The same person or the same group of people will be more or less powerful depending on their emotionality. This, which was already pointed out when we discussed the topic of emotionality, must now be reiterated when addressing the topic of power. If what interests us is to intervene in the effective capacity for action of an individual or an organization, if what concerns us is to increase their power, we cannot fail to observe and intervene in the domain of emotionality.

Hence, for example, the most competent people are not necessarily the most powerful. How often do we see that the best student in school or university is not the one who achieves the most success in work or in life? Or that an organization run by a highly qualified executive team is surpassed by another that, on paper, had less trained executives? We all know that the best teacher is not necessarily the one who knows the most, but the one who best relates what they know to the ability to expand their students' horizons of possibility.

More important than the training that increases our skills is the emotionality that defines the possible spaces within which we act. With a lower level of competence, someone can exhibit greater capacity for action and, therefore, more power, because they are in an emotional substrate that specifies a range of possibilities that may not be available to someone more competent.

The affirmation of life and the path of power

We have posited that action generates us, that it makes us the kind of beings we are. We have said that action generates being and, in doing so, allows us to transcend ourselves and participate in the process of our own creation. By acting, we cease to be who we were and access new ways of being: we become. Who we were yesterday, we cease to be today, only to be different again tomorrow.

Nietzsche reiterates to us again and again, "Man is something that must be overcome." This is perhaps the most important teaching of his Zarathustra. And Nietzsche calls that human being who is capable of overcoming himself the superman. Few of Nietzsche's concepts have triggered so many misunderstandings, so many misinterpretations. The superman is the one who makes life a path of constant self-improvement. "Man," Nietzsche tells us, "is a rope stretched between the beast and the superman—a rope over an abyss." "What is great in man," he insists, "is that he is a bridge and not an end."[36]

Action—and with it, language—constitutes us. Therefore, when we act in the same way, we remain fundamentally within the same way of being. Only by constantly expanding our capacity for action can we ensure the path of our self-improvement as human beings. It is not only the capacity for action that defines human beings. It is, above all, the capacity to expand our capacity for action. The key to this is the recursive and expansive capacity of language. Heraclitus already pointed this out to us: "The soul has a language (Logos) that expands."

The capacity to expand our capacity for action is what we have called power. Choosing to affirm the supreme value of life, choosing to recognize the priority of this life and this world over all other transcendent forms of life and worlds, is choosing the path of power as a way of life. The path of power does not deny transcendence. On the contrary, it is the expression of our commitment (Nietzsche speaks of will) to transcend ourselves permanently. It is the absence of any resignation with ourselves, of resignation to continuing to be as we are, without aspiring to achieve fuller forms of being.

Transcendence in the context of the path of power is no longer a resource that degrades human life, confronting it with a higher reference point outside itself. On the contrary, it is the full affirmation of the supreme value of life. Transcendence now becomes an immanent resource of life itself. It is not human life that must be transcended, it is the being that we are in life that opens itself to transcendence. "What can be loved in man," Zarathustra tells us, "is that he is an opening."

In today's world, in a world where our meta-narratives, our transcendent discourses, have ceased to adequately nourish us with the meaning we need to live, we have no other option for living well than to open ourselves to the path of power, to the path of the permanent expansion of our possibilities for action in life. Any affirmation of transcendent worlds, however legitimate and even necessary it may be, cannot be based, as in the past, on the subordination of the value of human life.

If we do not choose the path of power, Nietzsche warns us, we will not be able to overcome the great disease of the soul of our time: nihilism. Nihilism is precisely the experience of the meaninglessness of life, of living as an experience devoid of value, in a historical context characterized by the retreat of the power once exercised by metanarratives, transcendent discourses.

Nihilism is the experience that results from expecting the meaning of life to come to us, as an act of grace, without yet understanding that, today, it is we who must participate in its creation, as if we had not realized that metadiscourses are in the process of exhaustion. In the current historical conditions, we cannot consider the meaning of life as a given, nor can we expect, when we lose it, that it will come to us without us going out to conquer it. It is in this sense that the path of power presents itself to us as the option that allows us to confront nihilism.


 

The politics of the soul

Approaching life from the path of power allows us to relate to it from the realm of politics. We maintain that human beings face their lives in terms of the political game. The type of behavior we observe in great politicians serves as a reference point for us to better understand how we should approach life in order to live it well.

It should come as no surprise that, having postulated the importance and necessity of the path of power as a way of life, we find ourselves needing to use politics as a reference point for what this postulate implies. Politics has been, until now, the domain par excellence in which we test our capacity for action in power games. In this regard, we would like to mention five different aspects that we see as associated with the games of politics and that will allow us to explore the way in which human beings relate to themselves when they choose the path of power.

1. Politics as government.

Choosing the path of power means taking full possession of our soul and assuming responsibility both for its actions and for the type of person we become by acting in the way we do. This taking possession of our soul leads us to authenticity, to the ability to live our lives under the sovereignty of our own judgments, being masters and lords of our actions.

The subject that we are, Nietzsche warns us, is a multiplicity. It is a coexistence of interpretations that are not always consistent, emotions that are not always congruent. Part of our responsibility as human beings is to ensure not only coherence within this multiplicity, but above all the kind of coherence we wish to achieve. The human being who embarks on the path of power becomes the guarantor of the order of his soul. The free man, Nietzsche tells us, is a state and a society of individuals.

2. Politics as an exercise of freedom.

The path of power is not open to captive souls, it is not the path for enslaved souls, nor is it the path of the herd. The path of power is only available to free human beings, to individuals capable of asserting their full autonomy, their capacity for self-action. Only free human beings can cross the rope that leads from the beast to the superman. Those who are not free will fall into the abyss and will not be able to make the crossing.

3. Politics as the art of the possible.

The above phrase was coined by Bismarck and allows us to explore a fundamental aspect of choosing the path of power. Power, we argued, is defined not only by the actions we take, but above all by the space of the possible. The possible, as Crisipo le Soli, the Stoic, argued, represents a fundamental criterion from which we project ourselves into the future.

Here it is important to make a distinction, because understanding politics as the art of the possible allows for two different interpretations. The first consists of knowing how to act within what is possible and, therefore, takes the possible as a given. The fundamental concern here is to "determine" what is possible in order to act better. The emphasis here is on intervening within the possible to ensure what happens. To the extent that not everything that is possible happens, those who govern their souls at this level seek to ensure that what does happen is what allows for their greatest development as persons. At this level, however, we are still at the level of the administration or government of the soul.

The second interpretation conceives of politics not so much as the art of acting "within" the possible, but as the art of participating in its "invention." The possible, as we have repeatedly insisted, does not exist as such independently of the observer that we are. The possible is always a declarative act, a judgment, made by an observer. The linguistic act of the possible, without excluding the need to submit to certain conditions that allow it to be founded, creates the world. When the path of power is conceived as a way of "inventing" the possible, we move from the administration of the soul to the phenomenon of leadership. Leaders are those who engage in the invention or expansion of the possible.

4. Politics as leadership.

In arriving at the figure of the leader, we have once again approached the figure of the superman. The superman, we said, is the one who makes life the path of his permanent self-improvement, of his self-transcendence. However, when we return to the distinction of the superman through the figure of the leader, a new interpretive element emerges. We now realize that self-transcendence as a person, as the way we are as individuals, cannot exist without also transcending the limits of our individuality and subsuming ourselves once again into the social space within which we constitute ourselves as individuals. In the leader, both processes of transcendence complement each other.

The leader is not only someone who actively participates in the invention of himself; in doing so, he transforms the social space of his community and creates an environment in which, at the same time, others gain access to new ways of being. The leader, therefore, represents a space for the embodiment of the social being of the community of which we are members. All forms of leadership involve abandoning a narrow individualistic view of oneself. It involves the return of the individual to their social being. It is about the realization of the individual being in their social being.

Let us not forget what we said earlier: the individual is a social phenomenon. To the extent that the individual transcends his or her individual way of being, he or she simultaneously transforms the community to which he or she belongs and opens up new spaces of possibility for other individuals.

It should be noted, however, that when we refer to leadership as an expression of choosing the path of power, we are not concerned with responding to a social need. We are not suggesting the need for leaders capable of subjugating and leading a vast contingent of individuals around them. Our conception of leadership does not correspond to the social need for an authoritarian figure, but rather to a particular ideal of individual development.

Obviously, individuals who rise as leaders will influence their social environment. However, the perspective we uphold stems from an individual ethic committed to ensuring the meaning of life and expanding the possibilities of human existence. From this perspective, then, it is not contradictory to consider a society made up of a multiplicity of leaders, influencing each other and generating mutual possibilities in different domains of existence.

5. Politics as a space for the development of entrepreneurial individuals.

The figure that, consequently, most faithfully responds to what we uphold is that of the individual as an entrepreneur, in a community of free and autonomous human beings, open to encouraging individual entrepreneurial capacity. This is a community committed, on the one hand, to providing maximum openness to all its members so that they can participate, with mutual respect, in the development of their respective paths to power and, on the other hand, to accepting the differences that result from following such paths. In short, it is a community based on the recognition of human life and, therefore, the basic conditions of subsistence as fundamental values.


 

Life as a work of art

From the path of power, human beings are defined not as contemplative beings who delight in observing truth, nor as souls in torment who travel a path of trials and suffering, but as creators of their own lives. The fundamental attribute of human beings is their capacity to act and, through it, their capacity to participate in the creation of themselves and their world.

Of all the things that human beings can create, nothing is as important as the ability to participate in the creation of their own lives. Every other form of creation serves this, their main work: life. From this perspective, understanding human beings is not exhausted by conceiving of them through the figure of the politician. In fact, this is completely insufficient. Above this figure, another now rises: that of the human being as an artist, as a participant in the creation of their life, as an initiate into the miracle and mystery of the invention of themselves.

The path to power is, therefore, the path of creation. Human beings are, above all, creative beings. As creators, Nietzsche tells us, human beings transcend themselves and cease to be their own contemporaries. But in creation, another important aspect emerges: they become free beings. Creation and freedom are mutually dependent. Our capacity for creation makes us free. But just as creation is the exercise of freedom, the latter only emerges in the creative act. Freedom, in the deepest sense, is not a legal condition, but a condition of the human soul.

Conceiving of human beings as artists who make their lives their great work of art leads us to make some additional considerations.

The first of these relates to making room for the destructive forces that accompany all creation. If we accept creation, we must also accept destruction. As Heraclitus teaches us, there is no one without the other. We cannot transcend ourselves and attain other forms of being without ceasing to be who we were, without abandoning our previous forms of being. And this results in a crucial challenge in life. To transcend ourselves, we must be willing to sacrifice our present forms of being. There is no transcendence without sacrifice, without being willing to let go of what seems to hold us back, without first finding a new foothold.

Those who cannot detach themselves from themselves restrict their possibilities for transcendence. All transcendence, therefore, presents itself to us as a leap into the void, as a plunge into nothingness, into the principle of dissolution of the being that we are, in order to re-emerge from there into the waters of life. The path of power and, consequently, of creation is the path of risk, of life as a gamble.

Choosing the path of power therefore implies ensuring the emotional conditions necessary to allow both creation and destruction, both of which are inseparable aspects of the dynamics of self-transcendence. This implies overcoming what Nietzsche calls "the spirit of gravity." This binds us to existing norms of being, introduces heaviness into our movement through life, and prevents us from taking off in our leap into the void.

The spirit of gravity must be overcome with opposing forces: those that arise from the innocence of play. Drawing on images provided by Heraclitus, Nietzsche identifies this creative capacity with the figure of the child who builds sandcastles on the beach only to see them immediately destroyed by the waves and then builds new ones. For Nietzsche, the child is the favorite figure of the higher forms of power, for it shows us a power that unfolds from the innocence of play, from the absence of gravity.

Nietzsche contrasts this image of the power of the child with two different, important, but also inferior forms of power. The image of the beast of burden, which he illustrates with the figure of the camel. And the image of the beasts of prey, which corresponds to the figure of the lion. The camel is characterized by its endurance, its ability to absorb adversity, and its ability to carry others on its back. It is a form of power that cannot be despised. Many times in life we will have to resort to the power of the camel. The lion, on the other hand, is the one who rebels, who asserts his own power over others, who declares the inviolability of his territory. Life also often requires us to be lions.

But neither the camel nor the lion has the power of the child, who creates from the innocence of play. There is no higher form of power than this. From the figure of the child, we can see how far we have strayed from our traditional conception of power and how distant we are from the judgment that power is evil and a source of corruption.

The second consideration to make is that which relates the creative power of human beings to Nietzsche's interpretation of Greek tragedy. In conceiving of human beings as artists, Nietzsche argues that we need art as a disposition because only through it can we distance ourselves from the meaninglessness of life. Art makes life bearable. And only art is capable of giving life the meaning that it does not provide us with on its own, that it is worth living. From the artist's disposition, we "invent" the meaning of life, without which it is not possible for us to live it. This is what, according to Nietzsche, Greek tragedy undertakes.

Greek tragedy is based on the victory of beauty over knowledge. Human beings move through it, avoiding submission to the principle of truth and seeking instead the fulfillment of life from an aesthetic perspective. It is not surprising, therefore, to see how Socrates reacted against art.

Art allows us to see ourselves heroically, and this is necessary for life. Only art allows us to forget our limitations. We need the tragic sense of the hero to respond to the challenges of self-transcendence. The tragic hero can endure life because he has committed himself to making it a work of art. And as works of art, human beings attain their highest dignity.
